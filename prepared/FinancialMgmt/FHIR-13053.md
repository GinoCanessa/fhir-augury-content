## Summary

The reporter identifies five distinct issues with the Contract Signer Type Codes value set. These include inconsistent spelling of the Latin term "ad litem" across multiple entries, apparently swapped definitions for Grantee and Grantor, an overly narrow definition of Interpreter that excludes sign language, a conceptual mismatch where Healthcare Power of Attorney and Special Power of Attorney are defined as persons rather than legal documents, and potential semantic duplication between Named Insured and Covered Party codes. A commenter notes that the related ticket FHIR-20344 (which proposes that Contract.signer should use the Signature datatype directly) should be considered in conjunction with this work.

## Details

**Description:**

1) The spellings *ad Lidem*, *ad lidem*, and *ad litem* are used at Dependent and Guardian ad litem. They all seem to mean the same thing and should be represented consistently. Googling the terms, *ad litem* is used far more frequently than the others. *Ad Lidem* seems to be used primarily on law practice Web sites in a few states, and *ad lidem* without upper case seems not to be used very much.

2) It appears that the definitions of Grantee and Grantor are switched. If this has not been corrected as a typo it should be handled through the committee process.

3) The definition of Interpreter does not seem to include sign language interpreters as it only addresses written and spoken language.

4) Healthcare Power of Attorney and Special Power of Attorney are defined as persons, whereas powers of attorney are commonly defined as the legal document that grants the power. There should be another term for the person that has been granted the power.

5) Named Insured vs. Covered Party. These two concepts seem to be identical. It is unusual to have two codes that represent exactly the same concept. If there is a semantic distinction, this should be explicitly identified in their definitions.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Accept all five clarifications and apply the following changes to the Contract Signer Type Codes value set:

1. Standardize all occurrences of "ad Lidem" and "ad lidem" to "ad litem" in the Dependent and Guardian ad litem entries.
2. Swap the definitions of Grantee and Grantor to correct the apparent reversal.
3. Expand the definition of Interpreter to explicitly include sign language and other non-spoken/non-written modalities.
4. Revise Healthcare Power of Attorney and Special Power of Attorney definitions to distinguish between the legal document and the person granted the power, adding appropriate codes for each concept.
5. Either differentiate Named Insured and Covered Party with explicit distinguishing definitions or deprecate one code in favor of the other.

Coordinate with FHIR-20344 (Contract.signer should use the Signature datatype) to ensure consistency.

#### Justification

All five issues identify genuine terminology defects — inconsistent spelling, swapped definitions, incomplete scope, conceptual confusion, and semantic duplication — that reduce clarity and interoperability. These are well-established terminology quality issues that should be corrected to maintain the integrity of the value set. The changes are non-breaking for implementations that rely on codes rather than display names, and they improve the usability of the Contract resource.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Accept items 1–3 (spelling standardization, Grantee/Grantor swap, Interpreter expansion) as straightforward corrections, but defer items 4 and 5 (Power of Attorney person vs. document distinction, Named Insured vs. Covered Party overlap) pending a broader review of the Contract Signer Type value set in coordination with FHIR-20344. Create a new tracker item specifically scoped to the person-vs-document and duplication issues so they can be addressed holistically alongside the structural changes proposed in FHIR-20344.

#### Justification

Items 1–3 are clear-cut corrections with minimal risk and no design implications. Items 4 and 5, however, involve deeper semantic modeling questions — whether to add new codes, deprecate existing ones, or restructure relationships — that benefit from the broader context of FHIR-20344's proposal to restructure Contract.signer. Splitting the work reduces risk and ensures the more complex changes receive adequate design consideration.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline this request on the grounds that the issues raised, while valid observations, are insufficiently specified to drive normative changes. The reporter identifies problems but does not provide concrete proposed definitions, code values, or value set modifications. Request that the submitter resubmit with specific proposed changes to the value set content.

#### Justification

Change requests to terminology value sets benefit from precise proposed wording to ensure the work group can evaluate the exact impact. The current ticket describes problems but leaves the solutions open-ended, which could lead to extended design discussions. Asking for a more specific proposal would streamline the review process. However, this disposition risks losing the substantive feedback provided, especially given the ticket has been open since 2017.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

Disposition B strikes the best balance between making progress and managing risk. The first three issues — spelling inconsistency of "ad litem," the Grantee/Grantor definition swap, and the Interpreter scope gap — are clear, low-risk corrections that can be applied immediately without controversy. They represent straightforward terminology quality improvements. The remaining two issues (Power of Attorney person-vs-document distinction and Named Insured vs. Covered Party overlap) involve more complex semantic modeling decisions that intersect with the structural changes proposed in FHIR-20344, which suggests Contract.signer should adopt the Signature datatype. Addressing those deeper questions in isolation from FHIR-20344 risks creating inconsistencies or requiring rework. By accepting the simple fixes now and deferring the complex ones for coordinated resolution, the work group makes meaningful progress on a ticket that has been open since 2017 while ensuring the harder design questions receive the thorough treatment they deserve.
