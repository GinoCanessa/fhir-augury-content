## Problem Statement

The reporter identifies an ambiguity in the AI Transparency IG's introductory text, which states it is useful to know "when a FHIR resource has been inferred, in whole or part, by an AI." The reporter points out that this framing conflates two materially different scenarios:

1. **AI-generated content, traditionally assembled FHIR resource:** An AI system (e.g., an LLM) produces a piece of clinical information (such as a patient summary in free text), and then a traditional rule-based system constructs the FHIR resource wrapper around that AI-generated content.

2. **Deterministically generated content, AI-assembled FHIR resource:** A piece of data is produced through deterministic/conventional means, but an AI system generates or assembles the FHIR resource structure around it.

The reporter notes that the current model provides no mechanism to encode these two situations differently, and the IG text as written does not acknowledge the distinction. This matters because the provenance implications, trustworthiness assessments, and validation requirements differ significantly between the two scenarios.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Persuasive with Modification — Clarify Text and Add Provenance Guidance

**Resolution:** Accept the concern and update the IG introductory text to explicitly acknowledge both scenarios (AI-generated content wrapped in a traditionally assembled FHIR resource, and deterministically generated content wrapped in an AI-assembled FHIR resource). Add narrative guidance in the IG explaining how existing Provenance mechanisms (specifically `Provenance.entity.role` and `Provenance.agent`) can be used to distinguish these two cases. Do not add new structural elements at this time.

**Rationale:** This addresses the ambiguity identified by the reporter without requiring model-level changes. The existing Provenance resource already has the expressiveness to capture whether AI was involved at the content level or the resource-assembly level through agent roles and entity descriptions. Adding clear guidance and examples would resolve the documentation gap while keeping the IG's scope manageable for this ballot cycle. This aligns with the "Documentation & Clarity" categorization from the triage.

### Disposition B: Persuasive — Extend the Model to Distinguish Content-Level vs. Resource-Level AI Involvement

**Resolution:** Accept the concern fully and introduce a coded element or extension (e.g., on Provenance or as a tag) that explicitly distinguishes between content-level AI generation and resource-level AI assembly. Update the IG text, profiles, and examples to support both scenarios with distinct encoding paths.

**Rationale:** The reporter correctly identifies that the current model cannot encode these situations differently. A structural solution (such as a code in the AI provenance tagging system or a new extension) would provide implementers with a clear, computable way to distinguish the two scenarios. However, this is a significant scope expansion for a ballot-cycle change, would require consensus on the exact modeling approach, and may benefit from broader implementer feedback. The related tickets (FHIR-54118, FHIR-54211, FHIR-54244) suggest a cluster of model-expansion requests from the same reporter that should ideally be considered together.

### Disposition C: Not Persuasive — Current Model Is Sufficient

**Resolution:** Reject the change request on the basis that the existing Provenance-based model already supports the distinction implicitly, and adding explicit differentiation would add complexity without clear implementer demand.

**Rationale:** The current IG model uses Provenance to tag AI involvement at the resource level. Implementers can already use Provenance.entity and Provenance.agent to describe whether AI was involved in content generation vs. resource construction. The distinction, while intellectually valid, may not be practically significant for most real-world implementations. However, this disposition risks leaving a genuine ambiguity unaddressed — the reporter's point that the text specifically says "FHIR resource has been inferred" while the more common scenario may be AI-generated content within a conventionally constructed resource is a valid observation.

## Recommendation

**Disposition A: Persuasive with Modification** is recommended.

The reporter identifies a genuine and practically important ambiguity. The two scenarios have meaningfully different provenance and trust implications. However, introducing new structural model elements (Disposition B) may be premature given the ballot stage and the cluster of related model-expansion requests (FHIR-54118, FHIR-54211, FHIR-54244) that should be evaluated holistically. Clarifying the IG text to explicitly acknowledge both scenarios and providing guidance on using existing Provenance patterns to distinguish them is the most balanced approach — it addresses the reporter's concern, improves IG clarity, and leaves the door open for structural changes in a future ballot cycle if implementer demand warrants it.
