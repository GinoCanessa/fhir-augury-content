The submitter requests that IEEE 11073-10101 unit nomenclature codes be permitted as an alternative to UCUM codes in Section 3.3 of the PHD IG. The argument is that for a transparent PHG (Personal Health Gateway) accepting a new device, mapping 11073 unit codes to UCUM may be impractical, and 11073 codes could be used consistently instead. The submitter characterizes this as a business rule rather than a protocol issue.

This ticket was previously resolved as "Considered for Future Use" and deferred to the next revision of the PHD IG per Devices WG review in November 2025. It was re-opened by lynn_laakso on 2026-03-31 post STU2 publication for reconsideration.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Option A: Not Persuasive

**Rationale:** The FHIR ecosystem has standardized on UCUM for units of measure. The PHD IG's UCUM requirement was deliberately established through prior ballot feedback (FHIR-15506 and FHIR-15411, both resolved as Persuasive with Modification in 2018). All current IEEE 11073-10101 unit codes have defined UCUM equivalents, making the mapping feasible. As Erik Moll noted on the closely related FHIR-53363, use of UCUM increases interoperability and usability of uploaded data. Allowing 11073 unit codes would fragment the ecosystem and reduce interoperability with FHIR-based consumers that expect UCUM.

**Suggested Response:** The PHD IG's requirement to map IEEE 11073-10101 unit codes to UCUM aligns with FHIR core guidance and was established through prior ballot consensus. All current 11073 units have UCUM equivalents, and this mapping is a key element of interoperability. The committee does not find sufficient justification to relax this requirement.

### Option B: Persuasive with Modification

**Rationale:** While UCUM is the preferred unit system in FHIR, the submitter raises a valid implementation concern for transparent PHG implementations that may receive data from previously unknown devices. Allow 11073 unit codes as a secondary option when UCUM mapping is not available, while maintaining UCUM as the preferred and recommended approach. This would require updating Section 3.3 to define a fallback mechanism.

**Suggested Response:** The committee agrees that transparent PHG implementations may face practical challenges mapping unfamiliar 11073 unit codes to UCUM in real-time. Section 3.3 will be updated to permit 11073 unit codes as a fallback when the PHG cannot determine the appropriate UCUM mapping, while retaining UCUM as the preferred and recommended approach. A translation extension or secondary coding should be used to carry the original 11073 code alongside the UCUM code where possible.

### Option C: Considered for Future Use

**Rationale:** The concern about transparent PHG implementations deserves further analysis, but the current UCUM requirement is well-established and changing it carries interoperability risk. Defer to allow the community to gather implementation experience with STU2 and evaluate whether the UCUM mapping requirement causes practical problems for transparent gateways.

**Suggested Response:** The committee acknowledges the concern regarding transparent PHG implementations. This item is deferred to gather further implementation experience with the STU2 publication and assess whether the UCUM mapping requirement creates practical barriers. Implementers are encouraged to provide concrete examples of mapping difficulties for future consideration.

## Recommendation

**Option A: Not Persuasive**

The UCUM requirement in the PHD IG was a deliberate design decision established through prior ballot consensus (FHIR-15506, FHIR-15411). All current IEEE 11073-10101 unit codes have defined UCUM equivalents, as confirmed by Erik Moll's comment on the closely related FHIR-53363. The submitter's concern about "impracticality" for transparent PHGs is not substantiated with specific examples of units that cannot be mapped. Relaxing the UCUM requirement would reduce interoperability with the broader FHIR ecosystem and contradict FHIR core guidance. This ticket should be coordinated with FHIR-53363, which raises the same underlying concern.
