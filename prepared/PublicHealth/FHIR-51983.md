## Summary

This ticket identifies a tension within the Helios Query and Response IG between two pieces of guidance on query design. The "Minimization of Queries" section advises using broad FHIR categories (e.g., vital signs, lab) to retrieve larger datasets and then post-filter results. However, this approach appears to conflict with the "Minimum Necessary" principle and with guidance elsewhere in the IG (e.g., in "FHIR Query Basics") that advises being as specific as possible—such as not requesting STI lab results when following up on flu cases. The reporter requests a focused discussion of the pros and cons of each approach (broad retrieval with post-filtering vs. targeted queries) so implementers can make informed decisions.

The resolution accepted the concern as persuasive with modification, and language was updated in the IG as recommended by Craig Newman.

## Details

### Description

> In the Minimization of Queries section, it talks about using the category (vital signs or lab) to retrieve a larger set of data, but this seems at odds with the concept of Minimum Necessary and elsewhere in the document where the advice is to be as specific as possible (such as in FHIR Query Basics where it says that flu follow ups shouldn't ask for data related to STI lab results). I think there should be a focused discussion of the pros and cons of each approach.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept (Persuasive with Modification)

**Rationale:** The resolution is appropriate. The IG's guidance was internally contradictory—advising broad category-based retrieval in one section while counseling specificity and minimum necessary compliance elsewhere. Adding a focused discussion of the trade-offs between broad retrieval with post-filtering (fewer queries, simpler implementation, but more data transferred) versus targeted queries (minimum necessary compliance, less data exposure, but more complex query construction and dependent on server capability) gives implementers the information they need to make context-appropriate decisions. Craig Newman's updated language directly addresses the gap he identified.

**Action:** Accept the applied resolution. Verify that the updated language clearly articulates:
1. When broad category queries are acceptable (e.g., when server search capabilities are limited per FHIR-52209).
2. When targeted queries should be preferred (e.g., when specific codes are available and the server supports fine-grained search parameters).
3. That the choice should be guided by the HIPAA Minimum Necessary principle.

### Disposition B: Accept with Additional Guidance

**Rationale:** While the resolution addresses the immediate contradiction, the IG could be strengthened by cross-referencing the related introduction consolidation work in FHIR-51978. A single authoritative section on Minimum Necessary—referenced from both the Minimization of Queries and Query Specificity sections—would prevent future inconsistencies and give implementers one place to understand the principle.

**Action:** Accept the applied resolution and additionally recommend that the Minimum Necessary discussion be consolidated as proposed in FHIR-51978, with the trade-off discussion from this ticket incorporated into that consolidated section.

### Disposition C: Decline (Not Persuasive)

**Rationale:** The existing guidance could be viewed as complementary rather than contradictory: the Minimization of Queries section describes a practical fallback when servers lack fine-grained search support, while the Query Specificity section describes the ideal. However, this interpretation requires readers to synthesize across sections, which is not ideal for an implementation guide.

**Action:** Decline the change. Document that the two sections address different scenarios (constrained vs. capable servers) and that no further wording changes are needed.

## Recommendation

**Disposition A: Accept (Persuasive with Modification)** is recommended. The resolution directly addresses a genuine internal inconsistency in the IG that could confuse implementers about whether to prioritize query minimization or query specificity. The updated language recommended by Craig Newman resolves the contradiction by providing a balanced discussion of the trade-offs, which is exactly what implementers need to make informed decisions in their specific contexts. The resolution has already been applied and aligns with the related work in FHIR-52209 on acknowledging API scope limitations.
