## Summary

This ticket raises the question of how to distinguish average blood pressure values that are calculated from measurements taken at a single body site versus measurements taken at multiple different body sites. The issue was reported by Floyd Eisenberg against the US Vital Signs with Qualifying Extensions specification and is categorized as a technical correction. ## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested
#### Proposal
Add guidance to the US Vital Signs with Qualifying Extensions specification clarifying how to represent average blood pressure observations and distinguish whether the average was computed from measurements taken at a single body site or from multiple body sites. This could be accomplished by defining qualifying extensions or by specifying the use of existing Observation elements (e.g., `bodySite`, `method`, `component`) to convey whether the averaged values originated from one site or from different sites.
#### Justification
The question identifies a genuine ambiguity in the current specification. Clinicians and systems that compute average blood pressure values need a standardized way to indicate the source site(s) of the constituent measurements. Without this guidance, interoperability is compromised because receiving systems cannot determine whether an average reflects repeated measurements at one location (a common clinical practice to reduce variability) or measurements across different locations (which may indicate a diagnostic comparison). Providing explicit guidance addresses a real clinical workflow need.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach
#### Proposal
Rather than adding new guidance or extensions to the vital signs profile, recommend that implementers use the existing Observation `derivedFrom` element to reference the individual blood pressure Observations that contributed to the average. Each referenced Observation would carry its own `bodySite`, making it possible for consumers to determine whether the average was computed from one site or multiple sites. Add a non-normative note to the specification illustrating this pattern.
#### Justification
This approach leverages existing FHIR infrastructure without introducing new extensions or constraints. The `derivedFrom` element is specifically designed to link an Observation to the source data from which it was derived. By referencing the individual measurements, the distinction between single-site and multi-site averages is inherently captured without additional complexity. A non-normative note would provide implementer guidance without imposing new conformance requirements.

---

### Disposition C: Decline
#### Proposal
Close the ticket as out of scope for the US Vital Signs with Qualifying Extensions specification, noting that the standard FHIR Observation resource already provides sufficient mechanisms (e.g., `bodySite`, `derivedFrom`, `method`) for implementers to address this use case without specification-level changes.
#### Justification
The ticket lacks a description, has no comments or community discussion, and has remained unassigned since 2020. The absence of engagement suggests limited demand for a specification-level solution. The base FHIR Observation resource already offers elements that can represent how an average was computed and from which body sites, and implementers can address this through implementation guides or local profiling without modifying the specification itself.

---

### Recommendation
**Recommended disposition:** B
Disposition B strikes the best balance between addressing the legitimate clinical question and minimizing specification complexity. The `derivedFrom` pattern is already part of the FHIR Observation resource and is well-suited for linking an averaged blood pressure to its constituent measurements, each of which carries its own body site. This approach avoids the overhead of defining new extensions or normative constraints while still providing clear, actionable guidance to implementers. A non-normative note would help raise awareness of the pattern without imposing new conformance burdens on a specification that has not seen active community engagement on this topic since 2020.
