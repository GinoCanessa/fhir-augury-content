## Summary

The submitter raises concerns that the FHIR Breast Cancer BRCA testing model incorrectly assumes BRCA testing is performed on breast tissue specimens. In clinical practice, BRCA testing is typically performed on whole blood specimens using NGS or other genetic sequencing techniques, not on breast tissue. The ticket identifies three specific issues: (1) the model should allow non-breast specimen types such as whole blood, as documented by major reference laboratories including LabCorp, ARUP, and Mayo; (2) BRCA testing methodology aligns with Clinical Genetics (CG) Work Group modeling and ACMG reporting standards, suggesting the work should be coordinated with CG WG rather than modeled independently; and (3) BRCA results should not be oversimplified to a single positive/negative value, as clinical reports contain multiple observations including Variants of Unknown/Uncertain Significance (VUS), which would be better represented using a DiagnosticReport-based approach. The resolution was "Persuasive with Modification," acknowledging that LOINC codes for BRCA testing could be made more generic to accommodate different specimen types or expanded to cover variations.

## Details

**Description:**

This model for BRCA testing and results seems to assume that the specimen tested is a Breast Cancer Specimen. Several concerns with the model:

1. Non breast specimens need to be allowed. BRCA testing is typically NOT performed on Breast Tissue or tissue (in some cases IHC stains may be performed, but not as common.) BRCA testing is commonly performed on whole blood specimens like other clinical laboratory testing. See BRCA CLIA Specimen Collection Manual information/Test compendiums at Mayo, ARUP, and LabCorp for examples below.

   - https://www.labcorp.com/test-menu/21366/ibrca1-2-i-comprehensive-analysis-brcaissure-i%C2%AE
   - http://ltd.aruplab.com/Tests/Pub/2011954
   - https://www.mayomedicallaboratories.com/test-catalog/Specimen/64283

2. Most BRCA test methods are performed with NGS or other types of genetic sequencing techniques and reported in accordance with ACMG recommendations. Why isn't this part of CG WG modeling & reporting efforts as the data originate? If not aligned, interoperability will not result without manual intervention as the models are different. (Fyi, CAP Cancer Protocols or Biomarker Template do not address BRCA testing as it is typically not performed on a breast pathology specimen, but rather blood.)

3. Given these results are typically reported in a report with many observations (see example above), BRCA results should not be limited to a single positive or negative value, as the CG reports related to Diagnostic Report would be more clinically appropriate so information is not lost. It appears the model is trying to oversimplify the results and may not be able to accommodate results such as Variants of Unknown/Uncertain Significance (VUS) found during BRCA testing, as a result.

**Resolution Description:**

Duly noted. Clinical LOINC codes for BRCA testing could be made either more generic to account for different specimen types, or we will need to include a greater set of LOINC codes for the variations.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested
#### Proposal
Update the BRCA testing model to: (1) broaden the Specimen profile to allow non-breast specimen types, particularly whole blood, by relaxing any specimen-type constraints currently limited to breast tissue; (2) align BRCA result modeling with Clinical Genetics Work Group patterns by adopting a DiagnosticReport-based structure that can carry multiple Observation results, including VUS classifications; and (3) expand the LOINC value set for BRCA testing to include codes appropriate for various specimen types and sequencing-based test methods.
#### Justification
The submitter's concerns are well-founded and supported by clinical laboratory practice. Major reference laboratories (LabCorp, ARUP, Mayo) confirm that BRCA testing is performed on whole blood, not breast tissue. The current model's assumptions about specimen type and result simplification do not reflect actual clinical workflows, which could impede interoperability.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach
#### Proposal
Rather than modifying the existing breast cancer staging model, defer BRCA genetic testing modeling to the Clinical Genetics Work Group, where genomic variant reporting patterns (including DiagnosticReport with multiple Observations, ACMG classifications, and VUS handling) are already being developed. Remove BRCA-specific testing elements from the breast cancer staging specification and reference the CG WG's genomic reporting profiles instead, ensuring specimen type flexibility is handled through the standard Specimen resource without breast-specific constraints.
#### Justification
BRCA testing is fundamentally a genomic test, not a breast pathology test. The Clinical Genetics Work Group has established patterns for genomic variant reporting that naturally accommodate the concerns raised—multiple observations per report, VUS classifications, and specimen-type agnosticism. Delegating this modeling avoids duplication of effort and ensures consistency across genomic testing use cases beyond breast cancer (e.g., ovarian, pancreatic, prostate).

---

### Disposition C: Decline
#### Proposal
Decline the request on the basis that the specification (US Breast Cancer Staging) is now deprecated.
#### Justification
The specification targeted by this change request is listed as deprecated. Investing effort in modifying a deprecated specification may not provide long-term value. Successor specifications or the Clinical Genetics Work Group's genomic reporting profiles may already address these concerns. If the submitter's use case is still active, it could be re-filed against the appropriate current specification.

---

### Recommendation
**Recommended disposition:** B
The strongest path forward is to defer BRCA genetic testing modeling to the Clinical Genetics Work Group. The submitter's core argument—that BRCA testing is a genomic sequencing test performed on blood, not a breast pathology test—directly supports this approach. The CG WG's genomic reporting profiles are purpose-built for the exact patterns needed: DiagnosticReport with multiple Observations, ACMG variant classifications, VUS handling, and specimen-type flexibility. Since the breast cancer staging specification is deprecated, modifying it (Disposition A) would be wasted effort. Declining outright (Disposition C) would ignore legitimate clinical concerns. Disposition B acknowledges the validity of the request while directing the work to the appropriate domain experts and active specifications.
