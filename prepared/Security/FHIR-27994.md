This ticket asks how jurisdictions will communicate their confidentiality protection levels in a computable fashion, and whether this requires additional work within HL7 or at a federal/state level. It was originally resolved as "Considered for Future Use" in August 2022 (voted 4-0-0, Mohammad Jafari / John Moehrke), with the rationale that computable communication of confidentiality policy was not in scope for the current version of the IG but should be included in the roadmap. However, lynn_laakso reopened it in April 2023 post-STU1 publication, reverting the previous resolution.

The ticket references the `#how-to-assign-a-security-label` section of the DS4P IG, which describes how the appropriate level of confidentiality protection is determined.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Persuasive — Define a Roadmap Section for Computable Jurisdictional Confidentiality Policy

**Action:** Add a new section to the DS4P IG (or expand the existing `#how-to-assign-a-security-label` section) that outlines a roadmap for computable communication of jurisdictional confidentiality protection levels. This section should:

1. Acknowledge that jurisdictions (federal, state, organizational) set varying confidentiality protection levels.
2. Describe the intended approach for representing these policies computably (e.g., via ValueSets, ConceptMaps, or policy-profile resources tied to jurisdictional contexts).
3. Reference related ongoing work such as SMART Fine Grain Access and the HL7 Privacy and Security Healthcare Classification System.
4. Include placeholder guidance for implementers on how to handle jurisdictional variation in the interim.

**Rationale:** The ticket was reopened post-STU1 specifically to be addressed in a future cycle. Adding a roadmap section would fulfill the prior resolution's commitment while scoping the work appropriately for the next ballot.

### Disposition B: Persuasive with Modification — Add Informative Guidance Without Normative Requirements

**Action:** Add an informative (non-normative) subsection under `#how-to-assign-a-security-label` that:

1. Explains that jurisdictional confidentiality protection levels are currently communicated through out-of-band mechanisms (trust frameworks, policy documents, regulatory references).
2. Notes that computable representation of jurisdictional policies is an area of active development within HL7.
3. Points implementers to the HCS (Healthcare Classification System) value sets and the Confidentiality code system as the current building blocks.
4. Recommends that implementers consult jurisdictional legal counsel for protection level determination until a computable standard is available.

**Rationale:** This provides immediate actionable guidance without committing to normative requirements that may be premature. It addresses the commenter's question while acknowledging the current state of the art.

### Disposition C: Considered for Future Use — Defer to a Future Ballot Cycle

**Action:** Re-apply the original "Considered for Future Use" resolution. Acknowledge that computable communication of jurisdictional confidentiality policy remains out of scope for the current version of the IG. Add a tracker item to the DS4P roadmap to address this in a future STU cycle, potentially in coordination with SMART Fine Grain Access and jurisdiction-specific IGs.

**Rationale:** The fundamental challenge — that jurisdictional confidentiality policies are inherently varied and complex — has not changed since the original resolution. The work required to define a computable representation is substantial and cross-cutting (involving regulatory bodies, not just HL7). Deferral allows the specification to mature without premature normative constraints.

## Recommendation

**Disposition B: Persuasive with Modification** is recommended. The ticket has been open since 2020 and was intentionally reopened post-STU1 to be addressed, so deferring again (Disposition C) would leave a highest-priority ticket unresolved for a third cycle. However, defining a full computable framework for jurisdictional policies (Disposition A) is likely too ambitious for a single ballot cycle given the cross-organizational coordination required. Disposition B strikes the right balance: it provides immediate, useful guidance to implementers about how to handle jurisdictional confidentiality variation today, while signaling the direction for future normative work. This approach aligns with the pattern established by FHIR-30868 (CDex), which adopted similar interim guidance language.
