## Description

This ticket, submitted by the Kassenärztliche Bundesvereinigung (KBV — the National Association of Statutory Health Insurance Physicians in Germany), raises two concerns about the EU Laboratory Report specification:

1. **Entities receiving data (Page 6, Purpose section):** The specification notes that "another entity" may include employers, life insurers, health insurers, pharmaceutical and other industries (e.g., market research), universities, and other research or statistical institutions. The commenter asks: how are patients informed about the transfer of their data and how do patients consent to this data transfer, particularly when this is not regulated through the electronic patient record?

2. **Waiver of medical confidentiality (Page 9, Use Case No. 8):** The described cross-border use cases do not clearly define whether data flows to a health professional in country B originate from the electronic patient file or directly from a health professional in country A. In the latter case, the medical confidentiality of the health professional in country A must be waived in writing by the patient. The commenter notes that in many cases the bureaucratic costs of obtaining such a waiver can exceed the costs saved by avoiding a new clinical laboratory analysis.

## Analysis

This is a **Comment-type** ticket raising policy and governance concerns rather than requesting a technical change to the FHIR specification. The two issues raised are:

- **Patient consent for data sharing:** This is a legitimate concern about how the EU Laboratory Report IG addresses (or defers to jurisdictional policy) the question of informing patients and obtaining consent when data flows to third-party entities such as insurers, employers, or research institutions. The FHIR specification typically does not prescribe consent mechanisms but references applicable legal frameworks (e.g., GDPR in the EU). The IG may already consider this out of scope.

- **Cross-border medical confidentiality:** The question of whether data flows between health professionals across national borders require an explicit waiver of professional secrecy is a valid concern rooted in national medical law (particularly German law). However, this is a jurisdictional policy matter rather than something the FHIR IG can normatively resolve. The IG could clarify its assumptions about data flow origins in the cross-border use cases.

The ticket has been in "Triaged" status since November 2023 and remains unassigned, suggesting it has not been actively prioritized.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Resolved — No Change (Out of Scope)
**Rationale:** The concerns raised are about national and EU-level privacy/consent policy and professional secrecy obligations, which are outside the scope of the FHIR IG. The EU Laboratory Report specification defines a technical interoperability standard for laboratory data exchange, not consent management or confidentiality policy. These governance aspects are addressed by applicable legal frameworks (GDPR, national medical confidentiality laws) and are the responsibility of implementing jurisdictions.

**Response text:** "Thank you for the comments from KBV. The EU Laboratory Report IG defines a technical data exchange format and does not prescribe consent mechanisms or confidentiality waiver processes, which are governed by applicable EU and national legislation (e.g., GDPR, national medical secrecy laws). The cross-border use cases describe data exchange patterns but do not impose specific data flow routing decisions — these are implementation choices governed by jurisdictional policy. No change to the specification is required."

### Disposition B: Resolved — No Change (with Informative Note)
**Rationale:** While the IG cannot normatively address consent and confidentiality policy, the specification could benefit from a brief informative note acknowledging that implementing jurisdictions must address patient consent and professional confidentiality requirements according to applicable law. This does not change any conformance requirements but improves clarity.

**Response text:** "The concerns are acknowledged. The EU Laboratory Report IG is a technical interoperability specification and does not prescribe consent or confidentiality waiver processes. However, we will consider adding a brief informative note in the scope/purpose section clarifying that implementing jurisdictions are responsible for ensuring compliance with applicable privacy and consent requirements (e.g., GDPR, national medical confidentiality laws) when exchanging laboratory data, including in cross-border scenarios."

### Disposition C: Persuasive (Clarify Use Case Data Flow)
**Rationale:** The second concern about unclear data flow in use case No. 8 is partially a specification clarity issue. The use case descriptions could be improved to explicitly state the expected data flow origin (electronic patient file vs. direct professional-to-professional transfer) so that implementers understand the assumptions and can apply appropriate confidentiality and consent requirements.

**Response text:** "The comment regarding patient consent for data transfer to third-party entities is noted but is outside the scope of this IG, as it is governed by applicable legal frameworks. However, the concern about unclear data flow in Use Case No. 8 is accepted. The use case descriptions will be clarified to specify whether data flows to health professionals in another country originate from the electronic patient record or directly from the referring health professional, so implementers can apply appropriate confidentiality and consent requirements."

## Recommendation

**Disposition B: Resolved — No Change (with Informative Note)**

This disposition strikes the right balance. The core concerns about consent and confidentiality are genuinely outside the scope of a FHIR data exchange IG, so a normative change is not warranted. However, the concerns from KBV are substantive and reflect real implementation challenges in cross-border health data exchange within the EU. Adding a brief informative note acknowledging jurisdictional responsibilities for privacy and consent would improve the specification's usability without changing its conformance requirements, and would signal that these concerns have been heard and considered.
