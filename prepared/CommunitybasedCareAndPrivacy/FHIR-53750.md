## Description

The submitter asks whether "consent" in the Scalable Consent Management IG also covers **opt-out workflows**, such as those used by some public health jurisdictions for certain types of reporting. Specifically, the question is:

- Would an opt-out be modeled as a **withdrawal of an implied (default) consent**?
- Or as a **de novo consent** (a new, separate consent record)?

## Resolution Description (from Jira)

> Consent in terms of this guide can be either a Permit or a Deny. We are going to add a section in the scope to discuss Implied-Consent and Explicit-Consent along with Permit and Deny and how that is not in scope of this guide since that is part of determining the overall consent for the patient.

## Analysis

This ticket raises a legitimate scope question. The Scalable Consent Management IG focuses on managing consent records (Permit or Deny) but does not address the **policy determination logic** that decides whether a patient's consent status is implied, explicit, or withdrawn. The reporter's question — whether an opt-out is a withdrawal of implied consent or a new de novo consent — touches on consent policy semantics that are intentionally outside the IG's scope.

The proposed resolution acknowledges this gap and commits to adding clarifying scope language about Implied-Consent vs. Explicit-Consent and their relationship to Permit/Deny. This is consistent with historical FHIR Core discussions (FHIR-10693, FHIR-10697) that highlighted the ambiguity of "opt-in/opt-out" terminology and ultimately led to the adoption of Permit/Deny as the canonical terms.

The resolution is also aligned with FHIR-53886, which independently asked for clarity on how opt-out scenarios interact with `policyBasis` and `provision.type = 'deny'`.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept as Persuasive (Recommended)
**Action:** Accept the resolution as stated. Add a scope section to the IG home page or introduction that:
1. Defines Implied-Consent and Explicit-Consent in the context of the guide
2. Explains that the guide models consent decisions as Permit or Deny records
3. Clarifies that determining the *overall* consent posture (implied, explicit, opt-out, withdrawal) is out of scope and belongs to policy determination systems
4. Optionally references the historical FHIR Core discussions (FHIR-10693) for background on terminology choices

**Rationale:** The resolution directly addresses the submitter's question, is consistent with established FHIR consent modeling principles, and improves the IG's clarity for implementers encountering opt-out use cases. Adding a scope section prevents future similar questions.

### Disposition B: Persuasive with Modification — Expand with Examples
**Action:** Accept the resolution but request the added scope section also include a brief worked example showing how a public health opt-out scenario would be represented using the IG's Consent profiles (e.g., a Consent resource with `decision = #deny` and appropriate `provision` entries for specific public health reporting categories).

**Rationale:** While the resolution correctly identifies that policy determination is out of scope, implementers coming from public health jurisdictions may still need guidance on *how* to record the outcome of an opt-out decision using this IG's profiles. A concrete example bridges the gap between policy and data modeling.

### Disposition C: Not Persuasive — No Change Needed
**Action:** Close without modification. The IG's existing use of Permit/Deny in Consent.decision and Consent.provision.type is sufficient for implementers to model opt-out outcomes. Adding scope language about implied vs. explicit consent risks expanding the IG's conceptual scope unnecessarily.

**Rationale:** The Consent resource already supports deny provisions. Implementers familiar with FHIR Consent should be able to model opt-out as a deny without additional guidance.

## Recommendation

**Disposition A: Accept as Persuasive.** The resolution is well-reasoned and addresses a genuine source of confusion. Adding explicit scope language about implied/explicit consent and its relationship to permit/deny is a low-effort, high-value improvement that prevents repeated questions of this nature. The historical record (FHIR-10693, FHIR-10697) shows this terminology confusion is long-standing in the FHIR community, making proactive clarification especially worthwhile.
