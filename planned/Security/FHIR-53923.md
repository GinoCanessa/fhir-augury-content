## Feature Proposal

### Problem Statement

Between R4 and R6 the Security specification page was bulk-edited so
that every lowercase "may"/"should" became uppercase RFC-2119 keywords
("MAY"/"SHOULD"). The reporter observed that this CTRL-F-style rewrite
miscategorises sentences whose "may" was *not* a conformance keyword but
ordinary English used to make a statement of fact about the world.

The cited example is the second item of the "Communicate Securely → Data
Management Policies" bullet list on `source/security.html`:

> Not all the capabilities that FHIR enables MAY be appropriate or legal
> for use in some combinations of context and jurisdiction …

Here "MAY" is nonsensical as a conformance keyword: the sentence is not
granting an implementer optional behaviour, it is making a factual claim
about jurisdictional and contextual constraints. Rendering it as the
RFC-2119 keyword "MAY" therefore misleads readers about the conformance
weight of the statement.

### Proposed Change

Edit `source/security.html` in `HL7/fhir` so that the sentence at line
44 reads:

> Not all the capabilities that FHIR enables **are** appropriate or
> legal for use in some combinations of context and jurisdiction (e.g.,
> HIPAA, GDPR).

Concretely:

- File: `source/security.html`
- Locate the `<li>` block beginning with `<b>Data Management
  Policies</b>` (line 43 in the current clone).
- Inside that bullet, replace the substring `MAY be` with `are` on line
  44, leaving the rest of the sentence — including the parenthetical
  examples and the trailing sentence about implementer responsibility —
  unchanged.

The resulting line should read exactly:

```html
    Not all the capabilities that FHIR enables are appropriate or legal for use in some combinations of context and jurisdiction (e.g., HIPAA, GDPR).
```

No other normative verbs on the page are touched by this resolution.

### Design Rationale

- The reporter's analysis (the original "may" was ordinary English, not
  RFC-2119) is correct: the sentence describes external reality
  ("appropriateness" and "legality" are not implementer choices), so
  replacing it with the indicative "are" both removes the false
  conformance signal and reads naturally.
- "Are" was chosen over reverting to lowercase "may" because (a) it is
  unambiguous — no future reader will mistake it for a normative keyword
  — and (b) it is what the resolver and the reporter explicitly agreed
  to in the resolution description and the 2026-03-17 comment.
- A page-wide re-audit of every "MAY"/"SHOULD"/"SHALL" was considered
  (the title hints at it) but rejected by the work group: the resolution
  text scopes the change to this single sentence, and a broader re-edit
  would require a fresh round of review over each individual verb. That
  broader work, if desired, belongs in a follow-up ticket.
