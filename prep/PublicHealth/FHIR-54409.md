## Summary

The reporter (entered on behalf of a NAACCR community member) is challenging the
reporting cadence implied by the encounter-based triggers defined in the US
Central Cancer Registry Reporting Content IG. After the initial T0 bundle is
sent, they argue that Central Cancer Registries (CCRs) do not need report
updates more frequently than every 30 days. They are particularly concerned
about the laboratory-result triggers: patients undergoing chemotherapy may have
labs (CBCs, chemistry panels, etc.) drawn multiple times per week, which —
under the current trigger set — could generate a very high volume of essentially
duplicative reports. The reporter frames this as inconsistent with the stated
purpose of the USCDI+ Cancer Registry data list, which is to define a *minimum*
data set so CCRs receive added value rather than additional operational burden.
The ask is, in effect, for the IG to throttle post-T0 reporting (likely via a
≥30-day debounce) and/or to remove or restrict the lab-result-based triggers.

No related Jira tickets were surfaced via cross-references. A loosely related
Zulip thread on `#implementers` covered ballot comments on
`Bundle.timestamp` / `Meta.lastUpdated` for the same IG and touches on what
"trigger event" means; that discussion is contextual, not a duplicate.

Wendy Blumenthal opened this thread on 2026-04-07 to gather implementer

`Bundle.timestamp` to `1..1` MS, and (b) tightening `Meta.lastUpdated` to

(Grahame, Eric Haas) but pushed back on (b): Lloyd McKenzie and Gino Canessa
explained that `Meta.lastUpdated` reflects server-side write time, not a
clinically meaningful event time, and is unreliable on facade servers; Cooper
Thompson and Lloyd then pivoted the discussion to "clinically effective dates"
(e.g., `Procedure.performedDateTime`, `MedicationAdministration.effectiveDateTime`)
as the right way to date a *trigger event*. Wendy concluded the team would
likely find the `Meta.lastUpdated` request "not persuasive." The relevance to
FHIR-54409 is indirect but useful: the same Use-Case-Triggers-and-Reporting
section is the locus of both tickets, and the implementer consensus that
"trigger events need clinically meaningful dates" supports any disposition that
debounces or filters lab-result triggers using effective dates rather than
record-update times.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Amend the IG's *Use Case: Triggers and Reporting* page (and any normative
supporting profiles) to introduce an explicit **minimum reporting interval of
30 days** for follow-up reports after the T0 bundle has been submitted for a
given case. Concretely:

1. Add narrative on `usecases.html#use-case-triggers-and-reporting` stating
   that, after T0, an EHR/sender SHALL NOT submit a follow-up Central Cancer
   Registry Reporting Bundle for the same `PrimaryCancerCondition` more often
   than once per 30 days, regardless of how many trigger events fire during
   that window. Trigger events that fire inside the window are accumulated and
   included in the next scheduled bundle.
2. Remove (or downgrade to "optional / sender-may-suppress") the
   laboratory-result triggers from the encounter-based trigger set, citing the
   chemotherapy-monitoring example as the rationale.
3. Add a conformance statement on the Reporting Bundle profile (or the
   Composition profile) saying senders MUST debounce per the rule above.

#### Justification

This is the most direct response to the reporter's stated concern and is
consistent with the IG's own framing of triggers as a *volume-reducing*
mechanism rather than a real-time stream. The Public Health WG has already
heard implementer feedback (see the related Zulip thread) that
"trigger events" need a clinically meaningful date — a 30-day debounce keyed off
effective dates is straightforward to express and to implement. It also aligns
with the USCDI+ Cancer Registry data-list framing of a *minimum* data set:
high-frequency lab churn is unlikely to add registry value between
chemotherapy cycles. The cost is a single substantive change in one IG, with
no cross-spec impact.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Keep the trigger catalog intact (including lab-result triggers), but introduce
a **two-tier reporting model** in the IG:

1. **Event tier (existing behavior):** triggers continue to fire on the events
   currently listed in *Use Case: Triggers and Reporting*; senders MAY emit a
   bundle on each fire if they wish.
2. **Minimum-cadence tier (new):** define a normative SHOULD that senders
   batch trigger events into a single Central Cancer Registry Reporting Bundle
   no more than once per 30-day window per case after T0, with the bundle's
   `Composition` enumerating which trigger events fired in that window.
3. Add an IG-level extension (e.g., `cccr-trigger-suppression-policy`) on the
   `MessageHeader`/`Bundle` that lets the receiving CCR advertise its preferred
   cadence (e.g., daily, weekly, 30-day), so vendors who already implement
   per-event reporting can keep doing so for registries that want it, while
   defaulting to the lower-burden cadence for those that do not.
4. Specifically for `Observation`-based lab triggers, add narrative noting
   that senders SHOULD use clinically-effective dates
   (`Observation.effectiveDateTime`) — not server `meta.lastUpdated` — when
   deciding whether a lab result is "new" within the window, mirroring the
   guidance reached in the related Zulip discussion.

#### Justification

This preserves the existing trigger catalog (so implementers who have already
built against it are not stranded) while still delivering the burden reduction
the reporter is asking for. It also gives CCRs a way to express their
operational preference rather than baking a single number (30 days) into the
spec for everyone. Trade-off vs. Disposition A: more complex to specify and
test; benefit is that it is more durable as different registries mature. It
also picks up the implementer consensus from the Zulip thread that effective
dates — not `meta.lastUpdated` — should drive trigger semantics.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Mark the ticket *Not Persuasive*. Do not change the trigger catalog or the
post-T0 reporting cadence. Add narrative to the *Use Case: Triggers and
Reporting* page clarifying that the IG defines *what* events MAY trigger a
report and *what* must be in the resulting bundle; the *operational cadence*
of how often a sender chooses to actually transmit follow-up bundles is an
implementation/deployment concern between the sending EHR and the receiving
CCR, not a normative IG conformance rule. Reference any sender/receiver
trading-partner-agreement language already in the IG.

#### Justification

Defensible if the WG concludes that (a) the comment is a deployment-policy
concern rather than a content-IG concern, and (b) hard-coding a 30-day window
in a content IG would over-reach into operational policy that varies by state
registry, by EHR vendor, and by use case (e.g., outbreak monitoring scenarios
might legitimately need tighter cadence). The risk is that without any
guidance, the reporter's burden concern remains real and the IG could be
perceived as forcing high-frequency lab reporting it never intended; that risk
is what the recommendation below weighs.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

Disposition B best serves both the reporter's burden concern and the existing
implementer base. Disposition A solves the burden problem cleanly but is
prescriptive: a single hard-coded 30-day window is a policy choice that
arguably belongs in the trading-partner agreement between sender and CCR, and
some registries (or future use cases) may want a tighter or looser cadence.
Disposition C is the easiest path for the WG but leaves the reporter's
substantive concern — that the IG's own trigger catalog implies real-time
lab reporting — unaddressed and risks the same comment returning at the next
ballot. Disposition B keeps the catalog stable, codifies the
"minimum-cadence + clinically-effective dates" guidance the implementer
community already converged on in the related Zulip thread, and gives CCRs
an explicit knob for operational tuning. It also avoids deleting the
lab-result triggers outright, which preserves optionality for registries that
*do* want signal-rich reporting (e.g., clinical-trial-adjacent reporting).
The WG should confirm the `repo-analysis` briefing for
`HL7/fhir-central-cancer-registry-reporting-ig` is current before scoping the
edit, since that briefing is not in the local cache today.
