## Summary

This ticket asks the SMART Health Cards: Vaccination and Testing (FHIR) IG
to explicitly call out `Resource.language` in its profiles and recommend
that implementers populate it (e.g., `"language":"en"`). The request
originates from a suggestion by Grahame Grieve on the `smart/health-cards`
Zulip stream during the December 7, 2021 SHC FHIR IG conference call, in
the broader context of cross-border / international verifier workflows
where display text (patient names, code displays such as
`Immunization.vaccineCode.coding.display`) may appear in languages the
verifier cannot infer from context. Max Masnick filed the ticket to
capture any discussion. The title as filed contains a typo
("langauge" → "language"). There are no explicit cross-referenced Jira
tickets; cross-references only surface the two Zulip threads that framed
and re-surfaced the request for the January 4, 2022 IG meeting.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Update the SMART Health Cards: Vaccination and Testing (FHIR) IG
profiles (at minimum the `Immunization`, `Patient`, and lab
`Observation` profiles, plus any `Bundle` profile used as the SHC
payload) to add guidance recommending that issuers populate
`Resource.language` with a BCP-47 code (e.g., `"language":"en"`) on
every resource in the Health Card, and to add a narrative section
("Language") to the IG that describes why (cross-border verifier
apps, disambiguation of `*.display` strings and `Patient.name.*`
free-text values). The recommendation would be at the `SHOULD` level —
not a profile invariant or required element — so that issuers with
extreme payload-size constraints can still omit it. No changes to the
underlying base FHIR definition of `Resource.language` are needed.

#### Justification

The request is small in scope, touches only IG-level narrative and
profile guidance, and directly addresses a concrete interoperability
gap raised by a voting committee member (Grahame Grieve) on behalf of
cross-border / international verifier use cases. `Resource.language`
is already a standard FHIR element with well-defined BCP-47 semantics;
recommending (rather than requiring) its use keeps the change
backward-compatible with existing SHC issuers and preserves the ~17
byte cost as optional. The Zulip discussion from Dec 7 surfaced
concrete examples (non-US `vaccineCode.coding.display` values,
multi-script `Patient.name`) where knowing the language materially
improves verifier UX.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Rather than recommending `Resource.language` on every resource in the
Health Card, add guidance in the IG that language be conveyed once, at
the `Bundle` level only, via `Bundle.language` (plus optionally a
narrative note that per-resource `Resource.language` MAY be populated
when the SHC mixes languages across resources — an atypical case).
Additionally, where the real concern is the language of specific
display text (e.g., `Immunization.vaccineCode.coding.display`,
`Patient.name.text`), point implementers to the existing
`http://hl7.org/fhir/StructureDefinition/language` extension on the
specific element and show a worked example in the IG narrative.

#### Justification

This keeps the byte cost to the minimum (~17 bytes per Health Card
rather than ~17 bytes per resource in the Health Card, which is
materially different for a multi-resource Bundle in a QR-constrained
payload — Josh Mandel's concern on Zulip). It also targets the actual
problem more precisely: verifier apps care about the language of
specific display strings, not a whole-resource language tag, and FHIR
already defines a per-string `language` extension for that case.
Trade-off vs. A: requires slightly more implementer education (two
mechanisms to understand instead of one), and does not follow
Grahame's literal suggestion.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline the request as out of scope for the current SHC
Vaccination & Testing IG: SMART Health Cards are a size-constrained,
largely machine-processed credential whose content is tightly scoped
to a small set of US-oriented profiles, and per-card language is
already implicitly known by the verifier from the
`SMART Health Card` issuer identity and context. If international /
cross-border use cases materialize, they should be handled in
jurisdictional profiles (per the IG's existing internationalization
guidance) rather than adding payload-size overhead to the base IG.

#### Justification

Josh Mandel's Zulip objection stands on its own terms: payload budget
in a QR-encoded SHC is scarce, and `Resource.language` × N resources
is a non-trivial fraction of it, particularly under compressed sizes
that accrete across many small additions. The current IG scope is
US-focused, and the examples Grahame gave (multi-script names,
non-English `vaccineCode.coding.display`) are best addressed by
jurisdictional profiles that already need to diverge from the US
profile anyway. Declining here does not prevent a future
jurisdictional profile from requiring `Resource.language`.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** A

Accepting the request at `SHOULD` level is the minimum-risk,
minimum-controversy outcome: it directly answers the committee
member's suggestion that prompted the ticket, it is backward
compatible, it costs the implementer nothing if they choose not to
populate the element, and it gives international SHC consumers a
predictable place to look for the language of display text. Josh
Mandel's payload-size concern is legitimate but is addressed by
keeping the recommendation at `SHOULD` rather than `SHALL` —
size-constrained issuers can still omit the element. Disposition B
(Bundle-level + per-element language extension) is technically more
elegant but introduces two mechanisms the IG does not otherwise use
and diverges from the literal request without clear community
mandate; it should be considered only if the workgroup concludes that
per-resource `Resource.language` is materially too expensive.
Disposition C ignores a reasoned, on-the-record request from a FHIR
core author and leaves a real (if narrow) interoperability gap for
the cross-border verifier case.
