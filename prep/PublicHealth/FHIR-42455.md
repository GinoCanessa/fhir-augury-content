## Summary

Lloyd McKenzie (commenting as imported Comment 6 from a prior ballot review)
flags a sentence in the BSeR (US Bidirectional Services eReferrals) FHIR IG
that is unclear and difficult to parse: *"Key concepts adopted in the design
of the BSeR FHIR Profiles are specific fit to purpose, parsimony, and
closed-loop feedback to the referral initiating provider."* The reporter
states the intent of the sentence is opaque and asks the work group to
clarify the wording. The ticket has already been triaged with a resolution
of **Persuasive with Modification**: the BSeR authors have proposed
replacement narrative that recasts the paragraph in terms of supporting the
full life cycle of eReferrals (clinical and non-clinical), facilitating
information exchange for preventative and therapeutic services, and aligning
with HIPAA minimum-necessary disclosure expectations. There are no
cross-referenced Jira tickets, Zulip threads, or GitHub items connected to
this issue in the augury index.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Adopt the BSeR authors' already-drafted replacement wording verbatim in the
narrative page that currently contains the "Key concepts adopted in the
design of the BSeR FHIR Profiles…" sentence (typically the IG home page
or an "About"/"Background" page in the BSeR IG). Replace the entire
offending paragraph with:

> The artifacts in the implementation guide are designed to support and
> implement the full life cycle of eReferrals for clinical and non-clinical
> providers, facilitating the exchange of information for preventative and
> therapeutic services while ensuring coordination, data exchange, and
> efficient referral management. Under the HIPAA minimum necessary
> standard, covered entities must make reasonable efforts to ensure that
> access to protected health information (PHI) is limited, per the HIPAA
> Privacy Rule, to the minimum amount of information necessary to fulfill
> or satisfy the intended purpose of a particular disclosure, request, or
> use.

This is a narrative-only change — no profiles, value sets, examples, or
StructureDefinitions are touched. The disposition resolution
(*Persuasive with Modification*) and replacement text are already recorded
on the ticket, so applying them is a mechanical edit to the IG's narrative
markdown/HTML source.

#### Justification

The reporter's complaint is purely about clarity of prose; the technical
content of the IG is not in dispute. The replacement paragraph proposed by
the BSeR authors directly addresses the original ambiguity (jargon-heavy
phrasing about "parsimony" and "closed-loop feedback") with concrete,
reader-friendly language about what the IG actually supports (the full
eReferral life cycle, both clinical and non-clinical providers, and HIPAA
minimum-necessary alignment). Editorial fixes that make an IG easier to
understand carry effectively zero implementer risk and are exactly the
kind of change a *Persuasive with Modification* resolution is designed to
green-light.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Accept the spirit of the rewrite but split the replacement into two
paragraphs and tighten the HIPAA sentence so the IG narrative does not
appear to restate HIPAA regulatory text inside a normative-looking IG
paragraph. Concretely:

1. **Paragraph 1 (purpose of the IG):**
   > The artifacts in this implementation guide support the full life
   > cycle of eReferrals for clinical and non-clinical providers,
   > facilitating the exchange of information for preventative and
   > therapeutic services and enabling coordination, data exchange, and
   > efficient referral management.

2. **Paragraph 2 (privacy guidance, kept clearly advisory):**
   > Implementers operating as HIPAA covered entities are reminded that
   > the HIPAA Privacy Rule's *minimum necessary* standard applies to
   > exchanges built using this IG: PHI disclosed, requested, or used in
   > a referral exchange should be limited to the minimum information
   > necessary for the intended purpose. See the HHS guidance on the
   > minimum necessary standard for details.

Optionally also retire the "fit to purpose / parsimony / closed-loop
feedback" terminology entirely from the IG glossary if it appears
elsewhere, so the rewrite is self-consistent.

#### Justification

The author-proposed replacement (Disposition A) is a single long paragraph
that mixes two distinct ideas — what the IG *is for* and a HIPAA
compliance reminder. Splitting them improves scannability and avoids the
appearance that the IG is *defining* HIPAA obligations (it isn't; it's
flagging them). It also frames the HIPAA sentence as guidance to
implementers rather than asserted policy, which is editorially safer for
an IG that will be read by non-lawyers. The trade-off vs. Disposition A
is that this is a slightly larger edit than what the resolution literally
records, so it would typically be applied as "Persuasive with
Modification" with the modification being this two-paragraph structure
rather than the single-paragraph wording.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Close the ticket as **Not Persuasive** on the grounds that the existing
sentence, while terse, is intelligible to the IG's intended audience
(BSeR implementers familiar with referral workflows) and does not contain
a defect in normative content. Leave the current "Key concepts adopted in
the design of the BSeR FHIR Profiles…" paragraph in place.

#### Justification

This rationale is defensible only on the narrow grounds that ballot
comments are typically reserved for substantive defects, and a single
unclear sentence in introductory narrative does not break conformance,
break interoperability, or mislead implementers about a profile's
behavior. However, this position is weak: the reporter is a senior FHIR
participant, the work group has already drafted a clearer replacement,
and the cost of merging the rewrite is trivial. Decline is documented
here only for completeness.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

The work group has already agreed (via the recorded *Persuasive with
Modification* resolution) that the original wording is unclear and
should be replaced, so Disposition C is not a serious option. Disposition
A simply lands the resolution as written and is the lowest-effort path.
Disposition B is recommended because the resolution text bundles two
distinct ideas — "what the IG is for" and "HIPAA minimum-necessary
applies" — into one long paragraph, and the second of those reads as a
quasi-regulatory statement embedded in IG prose. Splitting them into a
purpose paragraph and a clearly-advisory privacy paragraph preserves the
intent of the resolution, removes the original ambiguity that the
reporter flagged, and avoids the editorial risk of looking like the IG
is defining HIPAA. The marginal extra editing cost is small, and the
rewrite still falls within "Persuasive with Modification" because the
modification is editorial structure, not technical content.
