## Summary

This ticket ([FHIR-42452](https://jira.hl7.org/browse/FHIR-42452)) is an
imported "Comment 3" from Lloyd McKenzie addressed to the US
Bidirectional Services eReferrals (BSeR) IG concerning how the guide
should describe and constrain "intermediaries" that sit between the
referral initiator and recipient. Lloyd's position is that **true
pass-through intermediaries are merely routing exercises and should not
have distinct functionality defined for them in BSeR** — implementers
should instead be pointed at the FAST (FHIR at Scale Taskforce)
exchange patterns. He emphasizes that pass-throughs have no data
"obligations": they never persist content and all operations remain
synchronous. The ticket is filed under the `BSeR_INTERMEDIARIES` label,
indicating it is part of a broader cluster of comments the work group
needs to triage about how (and whether) BSeR should specify
intermediary behavior. No cross-referenced Jira tickets, Zulip
discussions, or GitHub items were surfaced for this ticket via Augury;
the related companion comments in the `BSeR_INTERMEDIARIES` cluster
are not linked to this one in the ingested data set.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Adopt Lloyd's position verbatim in the BSeR IG: do **not** define any
distinct conformance, capability, or operational requirements for
"true pass-through" intermediaries. Add a short narrative section
(e.g., in the BSeR architecture / exchange-patterns page) that:

1. Defines a pass-through intermediary as an actor that routes BSeR
   exchanges synchronously without persisting any payload and without
   altering the semantics of the request or response.
2. States that BSeR imposes no additional obligations on such
   intermediaries beyond those of the underlying transport.
3. Refers implementers to the FAST exchange / directory work for
   guidance on building and discovering routing infrastructure.
4. Explicitly scopes the rest of the intermediaries discussion in
   BSeR to **non-pass-through** intermediaries (e.g., those that
   queue, transform, or aggregate), which retain data obligations.

#### Justification

This matches Lloyd's stated rationale: pass-throughs are
indistinguishable from transport from BSeR's perspective, so any
BSeR-specific conformance language about them would either restate
generic FHIR/HTTP behavior or improperly constrain transport
infrastructure. Pointing to FAST keeps BSeR aligned with the broader
HL7 architecture for FHIR-at-scale exchange and avoids duplicating
work. It also sharpens the IG's scope, freeing the "managing
intermediaries" discussion to focus on the cases where BSeR genuinely
has something to say (store-and-forward, async handoff, payload
modification).

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Rather than removing all language about pass-throughs, **define a
small classification of intermediary roles** in BSeR and assign
conformance only where it adds value:

- **Pass-through router** — synchronous, no persistence, no payload
  inspection beyond routing headers. *No BSeR-specific obligations*;
  cross-reference FAST.
- **Store-and-forward intermediary** — may persist, may operate
  asynchronously. *Has* obligations (e.g., correlation of
  request/response, audit, retention boundaries, ack semantics).
- **Transforming intermediary** — alters payload (e.g., terminology
  translation). *Has* obligations (e.g., provenance, declared
  transformations, conformance to BSeR profiles on output).

For each role, the IG would call out which BSeR CapabilityStatements
and AuditEvent / Provenance expectations apply. The pass-through case
collapses to "see FAST", which preserves the spirit of the request,
while the IG still gives implementers a clear taxonomy.

#### Justification

The BSeR_INTERMEDIARIES label suggests the work group is grappling
with multiple intermediary patterns, not just pass-throughs. A
classification anchors the discussion and makes it obvious why
pass-throughs deserve no extra conformance text — they're one cell of
a larger matrix where the other cells *do* need BSeR-specific rules.
This reduces the risk that future implementers read "BSeR says
nothing about intermediaries" and assume that includes
store-and-forward brokers, which would create real interoperability
gaps. The trade-off vs. Disposition A is more authoring effort and a
slightly larger surface area to maintain, but the conformance burden
on pass-through implementers is identical (zero).

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline to adopt this comment as a normative change to the IG.
Acknowledge Lloyd's input in the disposition log, mark the ticket
"Persuasive — No change" (or "Not Persuasive" depending on WG
consensus), and consolidate the broader `BSeR_INTERMEDIARIES`
discussion into a single design ticket that the WG can plan against,
rather than acting on individual imported comments piecemeal.

#### Justification

The ticket is an imported comment, not a concrete change request: it
states a design opinion ("don't define distinct functionality") but
does not name a specific page, section, profile, capability
statement, or constraint to add or remove. Acting on it in isolation
risks producing IG text that is incoherent with whatever the WG
decides about the *non*-pass-through cases tagged with the same
label. Declining (with rationale) and rolling the substance into the
consolidated intermediaries design work is the lowest-risk way to
keep the BSeR architecture story internally consistent.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

Disposition A faithfully captures Lloyd's stated position, but on its
own it leaves the IG silent about the intermediary patterns that
*do* need BSeR-specific guidance — and the `BSeR_INTERMEDIARIES`
label strongly implies the work group already knows those patterns
exist. Disposition C is procedurally tidy but defers a decision the
WG will have to make anyway. Disposition B operationalizes Lloyd's
guidance (pass-throughs ⇒ point at FAST, no extra obligations) while
giving the IG a structural place to put the obligations that *do*
apply to store-and-forward and transforming intermediaries. That
keeps the BSeR architecture chapter coherent and gives implementers
a clear, role-based answer to "what does BSeR require of me as an
intermediary?". The work group should consider B as the starting
point and fall back to A only if it concludes the non-pass-through
cases are already adequately covered elsewhere in the IG.
