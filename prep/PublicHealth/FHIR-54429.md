## Summary

The reporter questions why "Vital Status" (the `deceased[x]` element on the
Patient profile in the US Central Cancer Registry Reporting Content IG) is
required and additionally requires a data-absent reason when not populated.
The reporter notes that the element is **not** part of USCDI+Cancer, that the
reporting use case is largely **ambulatory practice** workflows where an
encounter implicitly indicates the patient is alive, and that **central
cancer registries** rely primarily on death certificate information for
accurate vital status and date-of-death capture rather than on submitted
EHR-derived data. The IG project team confirmed that the IG covers more than
USCDI+ workflows and cancer-incidence reporting, but agreed the constraint
is too strict; their proposed resolution is to add the **data-absent-reason
extension** to `deceased[x]` so submitters that genuinely lack the
information can still produce conformant instances. Cardinality cannot be
relaxed below 1..1 because the IG's Patient profile inherits the
`mustSupport`/cardinality posture from a parent profile. No cross-referenced
Jira tickets, Zulip threads, or GitHub items were surfaced for this ticket.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Adopt the project team's proposed change verbatim: on the IG's Patient
profile, leave the cardinality of `deceased[x]` at the inherited 1..1
but explicitly permit the standard `data-absent-reason` extension on
`deceased[x]` (typically by adding it to the element's allowed
extensions and noting `mustSupport = true` for the extension itself, or
by referencing the canonical
`http://hl7.org/fhir/StructureDefinition/data-absent-reason` extension).
Implementers that cannot determine vital status (e.g., ambulatory EHR
extracts where death is not tracked) populate `deceased[x].extension`
with a DAR code such as `unknown` or `not-applicable` instead of a
boolean/dateTime value. Update the IG's Patient profile narrative,
mustSupport guidance, and any conformance examples / IG-publisher
expectations to describe the DAR usage pattern, and add a validation
note clarifying that a DAR-populated `deceased[x]` satisfies the 1..1
requirement.

#### Justification

This is the minimum change that resolves the reporter's complaint
without breaking the inheritance contract from the parent profile. The
`data-absent-reason` extension is the FHIR-canonical mechanism for
exactly this situation — a required element whose value is genuinely
unknown / not-applicable for a subset of submitters — and applying it
here is consistent with widespread practice in US Core and other
HL7-published profiles. It accommodates both the ambulatory-practice
sender (who can assert `unknown`) and the central-registry receiver
(who continues to consume actual death information from death
certificates as today), without forcing every implementer to fabricate
a `deceased = false` assertion they cannot vouch for.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Same DAR mechanism as Disposition A, but additionally constrain the
allowed DAR codes (e.g., to `unknown`, `not-asked`, `temp-unknown`,
`not-applicable`) and add explicit IG guidance distinguishing the two
conformance audiences:

- **Ambulatory / EHR submitters** SHOULD populate `deceased[x]` with a
  boolean or dateTime when known; otherwise SHALL populate
  `deceased[x].extension[data-absent-reason]` with `unknown` (or
  `not-asked`).
- **Central registry receivers** SHALL accept either form and SHOULD
  reconcile against authoritative death-certificate data before
  publishing vital status downstream.

Optionally, file a follow-up against the parent profile to consider a
0..1 cardinality there, which would propagate cleanly to this IG in a
future ballot cycle and make the DAR workaround unnecessary.

#### Justification

Restricting the DAR value set prevents `masked` / `error` /
`asked-declined` from being used in ways the receiving registries
cannot interpret meaningfully, and explicit per-audience guidance heads
off the next round of confusion (which the reporter implicitly
anticipates by noting registries "rely on death certificate information
primarily"). Filing the parent-profile follow-up is the only path to a
cleaner long-term fix; raising it now keeps the architectural concern
visible without blocking this ballot's resolution.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Reject the request and keep `deceased[x]` as a required element with no
DAR allowance. Document in the IG that submitters SHALL determine and
assert vital status (boolean `false` if the patient was seen recently /
is otherwise believed alive, or boolean `true` / dateTime when known
deceased) for every reported patient.

#### Justification

Vital status is foundational to cancer surveillance and survival
analysis, and an explicit `deceased = false` from a recent ambulatory
encounter carries more information than `unknown` for downstream
analytics. Allowing DAR risks degrading data quality across the
registry feed, since the path of least resistance becomes "always send
`unknown`." If the reporter's only objection is workflow burden, the
correct remedy is implementer guidance, not a conformance loosening.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

The reporter's underlying complaint is real — central cancer registries
do treat death-certificate data as authoritative, and forcing every
ambulatory submitter to assert `deceased = false` would either degrade
data quality or create a workflow burden disproportionate to the value
of the assertion. Disposition C ignores that reality. Disposition A
(the project team's stated resolution) solves the conformance problem
cleanly, and on its own would already be defensible. Disposition B
adds two pieces of rigour that the workgroup should consider before
ballot close: (1) constraining the DAR value set so receivers can rely
on the meaning of an absent value, and (2) raising a follow-up against
the parent profile so the inheritance constraint that forced this
workaround is at least visible in the longer-term roadmap. Both
additions are low-cost relative to A and materially improve the
predictability of the feed for central registries.
