## Summary

This ticket bundles three ballot questions (and the submitter's proposed
responses) for the US Bidirectional Services eReferrals (BSeR) IG, all of
which concern how the IG should describe behaviour when an intermediary
(CBO Hub or HIE) sits between the referring provider and a community-based
organization (CBO). Specifically, it asks (1) whether CBOs can realistically
offer secure FHIR Subscriptions to data in their systems and how that
works when an intermediary is involved, (2) whether the "Notify of
Referral" step in the *Referral – Notify and Pull* transaction should
carry only the `Task` or both the `Task` and the `ServiceRequest`, and
(3) whether both intermediary modes — "intermediary acts on behalf of the
CBO and exposes web access to data" and "intermediary as pure pass-through
with full functionality at the CBO" — should be documented in the IG for
both *Referral – Push* and *Referral – Notify and Pull* use cases. The
reporter's proposed answers are: subscriptions are only realistic via a
CBO Network service (e.g., Unite Us, FindHelp/AuntBertha); both `Task`
and `ServiceRequest` should be sent in Notify of Referral; and yes, both
intermediary patterns should be represented in the IG. No cross-references
to other Jira tickets, Zulip threads, or GitHub items were found, so the
disposition decision rests on these answers and BSeR editorial judgement.

## Details

**Description:**

- Is it realistic for community-based organizations (CBOs) to offer secure subscriptions to data in their systems? How would that work when there is an intermediary? When the intermediary is a pure pass through?
  RESPONSE: It is not realistic at this point to expect CBOs to offer secure subscriptions to data in their systems EXCEPT if the CBO is served by a CBO Network service (such as Unite Us or FindHelp/AuntBertha). In those cases, the CBO Network could get the information from the CBO and then offer it to others (like providers) that are connected to that CBO Network and need information about a CBO in that network.
- In the Notify of Referral step in Referral – Notify and Pull, should both the Task and ServiceRequest be sent, or should just the Task be sent?
  RESPONSE: BOTH the Task and ServiceRequest should be sent, to complete the referral loop and support the information needed by the referring entity.
- We believe that sometimes the Intermediary (CBO Hub or Healthcare Information Exchange (HIE)) will act on behalf of the CBO and will present the CBO with web access to the data at the intermediary, and sometimes the intermediary will act as a "pass through" and full functionality will exist at the CBO. Should we represent both of these possibilities for the two different transaction (Referral – Push and Referral – Notify and Pull) use cases?
  RESPONSE: Yes, both of the approaches/possibilities should be represented in the IG.

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Adopt all three reporter responses verbatim into the BSeR IG narrative:

1. **Subscriptions / Intermediaries.** Update the BSeR IG narrative
   describing CBO-side capabilities to state that direct FHIR
   Subscription support at individual CBOs is *not* expected in the
   current IG version. Add an explicit pattern in which a CBO Network
   service (e.g., Unite Us, FindHelp/AuntBertha) acts as the
   Subscribable endpoint on behalf of its member CBOs, and forwards
   relevant referral state to subscribed providers. Note in the same
   section that pure pass-through intermediaries do not change this:
   the Subscription is still served by the CBO Network, not by the
   underlying CBO.
2. **Notify of Referral payload.** In the *Referral – Notify and Pull*
   transaction definition, change the Notify of Referral step to
   specify that **both** the `Task` (referral request workflow) and the
   referenced `ServiceRequest` (clinical/social request being
   actioned) MUST be conveyed, so the receiving party has enough
   context to decide whether to pull the remainder. Update the
   transaction's expected-resources list and any sequence diagrams
   accordingly.
3. **Two intermediary modes.** For both *Referral – Push* and
   *Referral – Notify and Pull*, add narrative (and ideally a sequence
   diagram per mode) covering (a) the intermediary as agent — CBO uses
   intermediary's web UI / data store, intermediary holds the FHIR
   resources — and (b) the intermediary as pass-through — full FHIR
   functionality at the CBO endpoint, intermediary only relays.

#### Justification

The responses were authored by the BSeR team itself in answer to ballot
questions, so they already represent the editors' considered position.
Sending both `Task` and `ServiceRequest` in Notify of Referral is
consistent with how FHIR workflow uses `Task.focus` to point at the
work to be done — receivers benefit from getting the referenced
`ServiceRequest` in the same exchange instead of having to issue an
extra read. Documenting both intermediary modes reflects the actual
deployed reality of CBO Hubs and HIEs and avoids implying a single
architecture is mandated. The Subscription guidance is appropriately
conservative: it acknowledges current CBO capability constraints while
still leaving the door open via CBO Networks.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Accept the substance of the three responses, but split the work and
tighten the conformance language so the IG is testable rather than
purely descriptive:

1. **Subscriptions.** Instead of only narrative, define a *capability
   tier* in BSeR — e.g., "CBO-direct" vs. "CBO-Network-mediated" — and
   state SHALL/SHOULD requirements per tier (CBO-Network-mediated
   actors SHALL support `Subscription` (R5 backport / R4
   topic-based as appropriate); CBO-direct actors are NOT REQUIRED to
   support `Subscription`). Avoid naming specific commercial CBO
   Networks (Unite Us, FindHelp/AuntBertha) in normative text;
   reference them only as non-normative examples.
2. **Notify of Referral payload.** Require the `Task` and make
   inclusion of the referenced `ServiceRequest` SHOULD rather than
   MUST, but require that when the `ServiceRequest` is *not* included,
   the `Task.focus` reference MUST resolve via a documented
   read/search interaction the receiver can rely on. This preserves
   the intent (receiver has access to the request) while accommodating
   intermediaries that genuinely cannot duplicate clinical/social
   payloads.
3. **Two intermediary modes.** Document the two modes as named
   *deployment patterns* (e.g., "Intermediary-Hosted" and
   "Pass-Through Intermediary") with conformance expectations per
   pattern, rather than as free-form narrative. Apply the patterns
   consistently to both transactions and link each pattern to the
   actor/capability statements it implies.

#### Justification

The reporter's responses are sound directionally, but as written they
are narrative answers to ballot questions, not conformance statements.
Turning them into actor tiers, SHALL/SHOULD wording, and named
deployment patterns gives implementers and testers something concrete
to conform to and avoids the common IG failure mode where ballot
responses get pasted into prose and then are difficult to validate
against later. Removing vendor names from normative text protects the
IG from coupling to specific commercial offerings while still allowing
the examples to appear in non-normative guidance.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline to apply this ticket as a single change set. Close it as
"Persuasive with mod / Won't Fix as filed" and require the submitter
to break the three questions into one ticket per question, each with
the specific page, section, and proposed text edit identified.

#### Justification

This ticket is structurally three independent change requests rolled
into one (subscriptions architecture, transaction payload contents,
deployment-pattern documentation). Each touches a different section of
the IG, has a different audience, and has different conformance
implications. Tracking acceptance, dissent, and post-ballot edits
across all three under a single Jira key makes it hard to attribute
ballot comments and disposition decisions cleanly, and risks one
contentious sub-question blocking two uncontroversial ones. Splitting
also forces the submitter to identify the exact location of each edit,
which the current ticket does not.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

The reporter's three answers are the right answers, so outright
declining (C) would just defer work the work group has already
effectively decided. However, accepting them verbatim (A) bakes prose
ballot responses into the IG without the conformance scaffolding that
makes BSeR usable by implementers, and it embeds vendor names (Unite
Us, FindHelp/AuntBertha) in normative-adjacent text. Disposition B
keeps the substance — Subscriptions only at the CBO-Network tier, both
`Task` and `ServiceRequest` available to the receiver, both
intermediary modes documented — but expresses each as actor
capabilities, SHALL/SHOULD requirements, and named deployment
patterns. That gives implementers something to build to, gives testers
something to assert against, and keeps the IG independent of any
particular CBO Network vendor. It is also the disposition that best
positions the work group to handle any one of the three sub-issues
being challenged on the floor, because each is now a discrete
conformance decision rather than a paragraph of narrative.
