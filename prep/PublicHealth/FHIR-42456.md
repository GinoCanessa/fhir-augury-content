## Summary

This ticket is a Question raised by Lloyd McKenzie (imported as
"Comment 7") against the US Bidirectional Services eReferrals (BSeR)
implementation guide. It asks how a referral initiator can know, with
confidence, the complete set of information that a referral recipient
will need in order to adequately deliver the requested service. The
question probes who acts as the arbiter of "what is necessary," whether
the design accounts for the fact that the HIPAA "minimum necessary"
standard does not apply when the patient has consented to the
disclosure, how rules governing package-content expectations evolve over
time as data availability, granularity, standardization, and clinical
evidence mature, and how the system behaves when participants have
differing capabilities or differing expectations about the contents of a
referral package.

The ticket carries the label `BSeR_NOTIFY_SEND_CONTENTS`, reflecting
that it is part of a thematic cluster of BSeR comments concerning the
notify/send package contents. There are no cross-referenced Jira
tickets, Zulip threads, or GitHub items associated with this issue. It
has been triaged with the resolution "Considered - Question answered,"
and the work group has recorded a substantive answer (reproduced in the
Details section below) that frames CDC programs as the authority that
defines the necessary content for each referral pathway.

## Details

**Description:**

> How can the referral initiator know with confidence the complete set
> of information the referral recipient will need to know to adequately
> deliver the service? Who is the arbiter of what is necessary? Does
> the design take into account the fact that minimum necessary does not
> apply if the patient consents to information being shared? How do the
> rules for package content expectations evolve (as data availability,
> data granularity/standardization, clinical expectations/evidence,
> etc. change)? What happens when different particpants have different
> capabilities or different expectations in terms of package content?
>
> (**Comment 7 - imported by: Lloyd McKenzie**)

**Resolution Description (recorded by Public Health WG):**

- In terms of BSeR all the programs that are involved specify the
  information needed for the recipient to adequately deliver the
  service.
- The arbiter of what is necessary is the CDC program.
- Under the HIPAA minimum necessary standard, covered entities must
  make reasonable efforts to ensure that access to protected health
  information (PHI) is limited, per the HIPAA Privacy Rule, to the
  minimum amount of information necessary to fulfill or satisfy the
  intended purpose of a particular disclosure, request, or use.
- As data availability, data granularity/standardization, clinical
  expectations/evidence, etc. change, the rules for package content
  expectations would be updated based on what the specific CDC program
  decides is necessary.
- In terms of BSeR, the different participants that are involved are
  all working closely with CDC and expected to be able to receive the
  content.

## Proposed Dispositions

The ticket has already been resolved by Public Health as "Considered -
Question answered." The dispositions below are framed against that
existing resolution so the work group can confirm, refine, or revisit
it.

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Confirm the existing resolution ("Considered - Question answered") and
close the ticket with no normative change to the BSeR IG. The recorded
answer is treated as the authoritative response to all five sub-questions:
(1) referral package contents are specified by each participating CDC
program; (2) the CDC program is the arbiter of what is necessary;
(3) HIPAA minimum-necessary applies as written and is not weakened by
patient consent in the BSeR design — consent broadens what *may* be
shared but does not change what *must* be sent for the program;
(4) package-content rules evolve via the relevant CDC program's update
cycle; (5) participants are onboarded through CDC and are expected to
be able to receive the program-defined content.

#### Justification

The question is informational rather than normative, the work group has
already reached a position, and the answer is internally consistent
with how BSeR is governed (CDC-program-driven content profiling rather
than IG-level cross-program harmonization). Closing as
"Considered - Question answered" matches HL7's standard disposition for
questions that have been substantively addressed without requiring
spec changes, and avoids re-opening a settled governance question.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Keep the resolution but use this ticket as the trigger to add a short
**"Package content determination"** narrative section to the BSeR IG
(in the existing "Notify / Send Contents" area indicated by the
`BSeR_NOTIFY_SEND_CONTENTS` label). The new section would codify the
five-bullet answer already recorded on the ticket: program-driven
content scoping, CDC program as arbiter, the unchanged role of HIPAA
minimum-necessary regardless of patient consent, the evolution path for
content rules, and the onboarding expectation that participants can
receive the program-defined package. Cross-link the section from each
program-specific referral profile.

#### Justification

The same question keeps surfacing in BSeR review (this is "Comment 7"
of an imported batch and shares a label with other notify/send-contents
tickets), which suggests the answer belongs *in the IG* rather than
only on a Jira resolution. Documenting it once, in the IG, reduces
recurrence of the same question, gives implementers a citable
reference, and makes Public Health's governance model explicit without
changing any conformance requirements. Because the change is purely
narrative and cross-referencing, it does not perturb existing profiles
or examples.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Reverse the current resolution and decline to act on the question on
the grounds that it is out of scope for the BSeR IG itself: the
substantive answer points entirely at *external* governance (CDC
programs, HIPAA Privacy Rule), none of which the BSeR work group can
normatively constrain through the IG. Mark as "Not Persuasive — out of
scope" and leave existing IG text unchanged.

#### Justification

A defensible read of the answer is that BSeR is a transport/structural
specification, while the determination of "what's in the package" is a
program-policy concern owned by CDC and by covered entities operating
under HIPAA. On that view, embedding governance narrative in the IG
risks giving the impression that the IG itself constrains
minimum-necessary or program scoping when it does not. Declining keeps
the IG's scope tight and pushes the question back to the CDC program
documentation where the authoritative answer lives.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

The work group has already produced a clear, defensible answer on the
ticket; that work should not be lost in Jira. The same question is
likely to recur — the ticket is one of a labeled cluster
(`BSeR_NOTIFY_SEND_CONTENTS`) and the underlying issues
(minimum-necessary vs. consent, evolving program content, capability
mismatch) are perennial implementer questions in cross-jurisdictional
referral work. Disposition A is the lowest-effort path but leaves the
answer hidden in Jira; Disposition C contradicts the work group's own
stated position and would undo a substantive resolution. Disposition B
preserves the existing resolution while paying down the documentation
debt that produced the question in the first place: a short narrative
section in the BSeR notify/send-contents area, cross-linked from the
program-specific profiles, restating the five-bullet answer verbatim.
This is a low-risk, narrative-only change that should be straightforward
to schedule into the next BSeR maintenance release.
