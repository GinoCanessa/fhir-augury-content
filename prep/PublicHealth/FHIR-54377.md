## Summary

The ticket, filed on behalf of a NAACCR community member against the US
Central Cancer Registry Reporting (CCRR) Content IG, calls out an
ambiguity in the guide's statement that "After 12 consecutive months of
no trigger criteria or upon patient death, reporting will stop." The
reporter asks whether patient death itself is (or should be) a trigger
that produces a final report before reporting ceases. They argue that
if patient death does not currently trigger a report, it should — so
that the central cancer registry receives a definitive end-of-life
record rather than simply having the reporting stream go silent.

There are no cross-referenced Jira tickets, Zulip threads, or GitHub
items, so the request stands on its own and must be evaluated on the
merits of the CCRR IG's stated trigger logic.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Update the CCRR IG so that patient death is an explicit **trigger event
that produces a final report**, not merely a stop condition. Concretely:

- Amend the "Trigger Events" / reporting rules section that currently
  reads "After 12 consecutive months of no trigger criteria or upon
  patient death, reporting will stop" to clearly distinguish
  *terminating* events from *triggering* events.
- Add "Patient death" (detected via `Patient.deceasedBoolean = true`,
  `Patient.deceasedDateTime` being set, or a `Condition` /
  `Observation` / encounter-discharge disposition indicating death) to
  the list of events that SHALL cause the source system to produce and
  transmit a CCRR report for any patient with a reportable cancer
  history.
- Clarify that the final report is sent *on* patient death, and that
  reporting then ceases (so the existing "reporting will stop"
  language remains true, but only after the final death-triggered
  report is sent).
- Include a worked example / narrative in the IG showing the sequence:
  reportable condition established → periodic reports on trigger
  events → patient death detected → final death-triggered report →
  reporting stops.

#### Justification

Central cancer registries treat vital status, and specifically date of
death, as one of the most important data points in the case record;
NAACCR's raison d'être is longitudinal cancer surveillance through
end of life. A guide that says reporting "stops … upon patient death"
without specifying that a final report is produced leaves registries
to reconcile dates of death out-of-band, which is the exact gap
FHIR-based CCRR reporting is intended to close. Making death an
explicit trigger aligns the IG with established NAACCR data
expectations and with analogous FHIR public-health reporting IGs
(e.g., eCR / Case Reporting), where clinically significant status
changes are triggers, not silent terminators.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Rather than adding "patient death" as a brand-new trigger event,
**re-characterize death as a mandatory final-report condition layered on
the existing trigger framework**. Specifically:

- Keep the current trigger-criteria list unchanged.
- Add an IG-level SHALL requiring that, when a source system becomes
  aware of the death of any patient who has previously been the
  subject of a CCRR report (or who meets reportability criteria at
  time of death), it transmits exactly one final CCRR report whose
  payload includes the date/cause of death (e.g., `Patient.deceased[x]`,
  and/or a cause-of-death `Observation` / `Condition`).
- After that final report, the 12-month silence rule and the "stop on
  death" language continue to apply as-is.
- Optionally profile a `reportReason` extension or a `Composition.type`
  slice value specifically for "final / death" reports so registries
  can recognize and store them distinctly from interim reports.

#### Justification

This preserves the conceptual cleanliness of the IG's existing trigger
model (triggers are clinical/encounter events that indicate ongoing
care) while still guaranteeing that registries receive a
death-attributed record. It lowers implementation burden because
systems don't have to retrofit a new trigger pathway — they reuse the
existing reporting pipeline, just with a distinguished reason code and
a requirement to fire it exactly once. It also makes the
death-triggered report distinguishable at the registry end, which is
useful for registry QA and for detecting the difference between "we
received a final report because the patient died" vs. "the feed went
silent for 12 months."

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline the change on the basis that the CCRR IG intentionally relies
on **out-of-band vital-status ascertainment** (state vital records,
NDI linkage, etc.) rather than EHR-sourced death notification, and
that requiring EHRs to trigger a FHIR report on death would place the
obligation on a system (the EHR) that is often not the first or most
reliable source of death information.

Close as "Won't fix — existing mechanism addresses need," with an IG
clarification only: restate in the narrative that death-of-patient
information is obtained by registries through vital-records linkage,
and that the "reporting will stop upon patient death" wording refers
to cessation of *EHR-sourced* CCRR reporting, not to the registry's
overall case record.

#### Justification

Cancer registries in practice reconcile vital status via state death
certificates and NDI rather than trusting clinical systems to know
when a patient has died outside of a hospital encounter. An EHR that
never learns of an out-of-facility death cannot send a
death-triggered report, so making it a SHALL could either be
unenforceable (system simply never fires it) or produce worse data
than the current vital-records-linkage baseline. Declining keeps the
IG scoped to what EHRs can reliably observe and avoids a requirement
that is trivially bypassed.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

Disposition B best balances the reporter's concern with the realities
of EHR-sourced cancer reporting. The reporter is correct that the
current wording is ambiguous and that registries benefit from
receiving a final, death-attributed FHIR report; Disposition C is too
dismissive of that benefit and essentially leaves the IG's wording
problem unresolved. Disposition A solves the problem but risks
over-promising, because it elevates death to a trigger on par with
clinical encounters even though many EHRs only opportunistically
learn of death. Disposition B threads the needle: it mandates a final
report when the EHR *does* become aware of death, makes that report
distinguishable from routine trigger-driven reports, and leaves the
existing trigger framework and the 12-month silence rule intact. It
also gives the work group a concrete spec hook (a death/final report
reason or Composition type) that a CCRR IG editor can map onto the
actual authoring sources once the work group has selected the
approach.
