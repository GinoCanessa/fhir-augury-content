## Summary

The reporter (entered on behalf of a NAACCR community member) asks the
US Central Cancer Registry Reporting (Central Cancer Registry / "CCR")
Content IG authors to **re-introduce time-based (TB) triggers** into
the IG's reporting use case. Current event-based (EB) and non-event-based
(NEB) triggers fire only when discrete clinical events occur, but the
reporter notes that some clinically relevant information — for example,
medications added to a patient's medication list **between** scheduled
updates — may never surface an EB or NEB trigger and would therefore
never be reported. A periodic TB trigger (e.g., a report at 1 year after
T0, with subsequent annual reports gated by other criteria) would close
that gap and catch information that is otherwise missed. The ticket asks
the workgroup to re-evaluate the earlier decision to remove TB triggers
from the use case in light of these missed-information scenarios.

There are no cross-referenced Jira tickets, Zulip threads, or GitHub
items recorded for this ticket.

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Re-introduce **time-based (TB) triggers** as a first-class trigger
category in the US CCRR IG use case alongside the existing event-based
(EB) and non-event-based (NEB) triggers. Concretely:

- Add a TB trigger definition to the IG's "Triggering Events" /
  "Reporting Workflow" narrative pages, defining it as a periodic
  trigger anchored on **T0** (the initial reportable event for the
  patient/case).
- Specify a default cadence of **1 year after T0**, with subsequent
  annual recurrences conditional on case status (e.g., case still open,
  patient still under active surveillance, or other criteria the WG
  agrees on).
- Update the PlanDefinition (or equivalent triggering artifact) used
  by the IG to include a `PlanDefinition.action.trigger` of type
  `periodic` with a 1-year period and a starting boundary of T0.
- Update the use-case examples and any conformance/test scenarios so
  that a TB-triggered report is exercised end-to-end.
- Update the IG ballot/change-history note to record that TB triggers
  were re-introduced after being removed in a prior cycle, with a
  pointer to this ticket.

#### Justification

The reporter cites a concrete failure mode — medications added between
EB/NEB-triggering events never appear in a report — and argues there
are likely other, currently-unenumerated, gaps with the same root cause:
EB and NEB triggers can only fire when *something the IG knows to watch
for* changes. A periodic TB trigger is the standard backstop that
cancer registries (and most public-health reporting programs) rely on
to catch silent state drift. NAACCR's involvement (per comment 69594)
indicates this is community feedback from the registry side, not a
solo opinion. The change is additive — implementers that only support
EB/NEB are not broken — and it restores symmetry with related
public-health reporting IGs that already define a periodic-reporting
trigger.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Instead of re-introducing TB triggers as a peer of EB/NEB in the core
use case, address the underlying "missed information" concern by:

1. **Strengthening the EB/NEB trigger set** — explicitly enumerate
   medication-list updates (and any other gap scenarios the WG can
   identify with NAACCR) as EB triggers, so that the medication
   scenario in the ticket would in fact fire today.
2. **Defining an optional "Periodic Reconciliation Report" companion
   profile** (rather than a new trigger on the main report) that a
   sender MAY produce on a registry-defined cadence, and whose
   conformance is `SHOULD` rather than `SHALL`. This keeps the
   primary submission driven by clinical events but gives registries a
   sanctioned way to ask for a periodic snapshot when they need it.
3. Document the trade-off explicitly in the IG so future readers
   understand why TB was deliberately *not* re-added to the core
   trigger set.

#### Justification

The previous removal of TB triggers presumably had reasons — most
commonly, that periodic reports without a clinical change generate
high-volume, low-value submissions that registries then have to
deduplicate. Approach B preserves that earlier decision while still
plugging the specific gap the reporter raised: if medication-list
changes are the actual concern, treating them as an EB trigger is more
targeted than re-adding a generic annual report. The optional companion
profile gives registries a way to opt into periodic reconciliation
without forcing every implementer to support TB triggers in their
primary reporting pipeline. This is more incremental and lower-risk
than (A), at the cost of being a less direct response to the literal
ask.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline to re-introduce TB triggers. Rationale category: **already
addressed by existing mechanism / scope creep risk**. Document in the
IG that periodic reconciliation between providers and the central
cancer registry is expected to be handled by the registry's existing
data-quality / follow-up processes (out-of-band requests for
abstraction updates, NAACCR's standard follow-back workflow), not by
re-triggering a fresh CCRR FHIR report. Suggest the reporter raise
specific gap scenarios (e.g., medications-added-between-updates) as
their own EB/NEB tickets so they can be evaluated individually.

#### Justification

The IG was originally drafted with TB triggers and they were
intentionally removed; reversing that decision based on a single
example (medication list changes) and an open-ended "there may also
be other scenarios we haven't thought of" risks re-litigating a
settled design choice without new evidence. Periodic backstop
reporting is a real registry need but is typically a
registry-operations problem rather than an IG-conformance problem,
and conflating the two tends to push the IG toward larger,
lower-signal payloads. Declining keeps the EB/NEB-only model intact
and forces concrete gap scenarios to be addressed on their own merits.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** A

The reporter's argument is structurally strong: EB/NEB triggers are
inherently a *whitelist* of changes the IG knows to watch for, and any
data element not on that whitelist (medications added between updates
being the worked example) is invisible to the registry until the next
event happens to fire — which may be never. A periodic TB trigger
anchored on T0 is the standard, well-understood remedy and is already
the pattern used in adjacent public-health reporting work. NAACCR's
endorsement (the ticket was entered on their behalf) gives this real
operational weight from the registry side, which is the consumer the
IG exists to serve. Disposition B's "enumerate the missing EB
triggers" approach is appealing but unbounded — the reporter
explicitly flags that there are likely scenarios "we haven't thought
of," which is exactly the failure mode TB triggers exist to cover.
Disposition C is the safest in terms of scope but leaves the
registry-side gap unaddressed and pushes the cost onto out-of-band
follow-back, which is what the IG was meant to reduce. Recommend
accepting (A), with the caveat that the workgroup should record the
specific cadence and the gating criteria for "subsequent years" before
ballot, since the ticket leaves both deliberately open.
