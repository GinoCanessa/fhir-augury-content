## Summary

Lloyd McKenzie (commenting on a BSeR ballot review — this entry was
imported from "Comment 31" of an upstream ballot tracker) flags that
the data elements selected across the BSeR service-specific referral
profiles appear inconsistent and incomplete. Specifically, contact
preferences such as "best day to call", "best time to call", and
"leave message" are captured only for the tobacco-cessation referral
flow even though such logistics are universally relevant to outreach
for any referral program. Conversely, allergies are captured for the
diabetes referral but not for early-childhood-nutrition referrals, even
though allergy information is plainly relevant to nutrition counselling
as well. The reporter also asks where the various SDOH (Social
Determinants of Health) measures fit in, since several of them are
relevant to multiple of the BSeR referral programs and do not appear
to have a coherent home in the current set. The ticket is essentially
a request for the Public Health WG to revisit and rationalise the
per-program data-element matrix in BSeR so that elements appear in
every program where they are clinically/operationally meaningful,
rather than only in the program where they happened to be added first.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Treat this as a directive to harmonise the BSeR per-program data-element
matrix. Concretely:

1. Promote the contact-logistics elements currently scoped to the
   tobacco-cessation referral — "best day to call", "best time to
   call", and "leave message / voicemail OK" — into a **common
   referral-recipient contact-preferences slice** (most naturally as a
   set of `Patient.contact` extensions, or as a small `Observation`
   panel with a shared `BSeRContactPreferences` profile) and reference
   that slice from **every** BSeR service-specific referral profile
   (tobacco, diabetes prevention, hypertension, early-childhood
   nutrition, arthritis, obesity, etc.).
2. Promote `AllergyIntolerance` from the diabetes-prevention referral
   bundle into the **shared** Bundle/profile that backs every BSeR
   referral whose downstream service may involve diet, supplements, or
   medication (diabetes, early-childhood nutrition, obesity,
   hypertension, arthritis); leave it optional on referrals where it
   is not plausibly relevant.
3. For SDOH, define a **shared SDOH observation slice** referencing
   the HL7 SDOHCC value sets / observation profiles (food insecurity,
   housing instability, transportation, etc.) and include it as
   *optional* on every BSeR referral profile, with per-program guidance
   describing which SDOH measures are most clinically relevant for
   that program.
4. Document the rationale in a new "Data-element scoping" page in the
   BSeR IG: an element appears in a program's bundle iff it is
   plausibly relevant to that program; absence is not a signal of
   irrelevance unless explicitly stated.

#### Justification

The reporter's observation is correct on its face: the inconsistencies
are accidents of how the IG was authored program-by-program rather
than principled distinctions. Contact-preferences elements are about
*how to reach the patient*, which is orthogonal to the clinical
program; they belong on the patient/contact layer, not on a
program-specific profile. Allergy information is similarly relevant
wherever a downstream service may recommend foods, supplements, or
medications — the diabetes-only scoping is hard to defend. SDOHCC
already provides the canonical observation profiles, so referencing
them keeps BSeR aligned with the broader US Realm SDOH ecosystem
rather than re-inventing program-specific equivalents.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Rather than promoting individual elements one-by-one, restructure
BSeR around a **layered profile model**:

- A `BSeRReferralBase` Bundle/profile that requires
  `Patient`, `ServiceRequest`, `Coverage`, and an *optional* slice
  for: contact preferences, `AllergyIntolerance`, SDOH observations,
  and "common social history" observations.
- Each program-specific profile (`BSeRTobaccoCessationReferral`,
  `BSeRDiabetesPreventionReferral`,
  `BSeREarlyChildhoodNutritionReferral`, …) **derives from**
  `BSeRReferralBase`, leaves the shared slices as `MS` (must-support)
  where appropriate for that program, and only adds *additional*
  truly program-specific observations (e.g., pack-years for tobacco;
  HbA1c/fasting glucose for diabetes prevention; WIC eligibility data
  for early-childhood nutrition).
- Provide a single `BSeRDataElementMatrix` page (table view) so
  reviewers can see at a glance which elements are required /
  must-support / optional in each program, replacing prose discovery
  with a published matrix.

#### Justification

This approach addresses the *root cause* of the reporter's complaint —
the lack of a principled inheritance model between BSeR programs —
rather than only patching the specific examples cited. It makes
future additions (a new referral program; a new SDOH measure)
straightforward and consistent by construction, and the published
matrix makes inconsistencies immediately visible to reviewers and
implementers. The cost is non-trivial: it is a refactor of the IG's
profile hierarchy, likely with downstream effects on existing
implementer code and on referenced examples, and would constitute a
substantive (likely STU-level) change.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Close as **Not Persuasive** with the rationale that BSeR is a
program-specific eReferral IG whose data elements were chosen by the
sponsoring program subject-matter experts to reflect *what those
specific outreach programs actually collect and act on* in production
deployments today. Instruct implementers that any element they wish
to send beyond a profile's required/MS set may be included as
permitted-by-default FHIR extensibility (open profiles, additional
`Observation`s in the Bundle), and that program-specific scoping
decisions should be raised against the sponsoring program rather than
re-litigated in the base IG.

#### Justification

This is defensible only if the Public Health WG has explicit evidence
that the per-program scoping reflects *deliberate* SME-driven choices
(e.g., tobacco-cessation programs have telephonic-coaching workflows
that genuinely depend on contact-preferences data, while other
programs studied do not). It also relies on FHIR's open extensibility
to fill the gaps the reporter identifies. The weakness is that this
position does not address the *consistency* concern at all: a
reviewer reading the IG cannot distinguish "deliberately omitted" from
"accidentally omitted", which is precisely the problem the ticket
raises. Decline is therefore the weakest of the three options unless
the WG can produce that documented SME rationale and is willing to
commit to publishing it.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** A (with B as a stretch goal)

The reporter has identified a genuine and embarrassing inconsistency
in the BSeR data-element matrix that cannot be defended on clinical
or operational grounds: contact preferences are not a tobacco-only
concern, allergies are not a diabetes-only concern, and SDOH measures
plainly cut across multiple programs. Disposition A delivers a
pragmatic fix in a single editorial pass — promote the
already-modelled elements to a shared layer and add SDOHCC references
where program-relevant — without requiring a wholesale refactor of
the IG's profile hierarchy. Disposition B is the *right* long-term
target architecture and the WG should plan toward it for a future
release, but accepting it as the answer to *this* ticket risks
turning a tractable harmonisation pass into an open-ended refactor.
Disposition C should only be entertained if the WG produces explicit
SME documentation showing the current per-program scoping is
intentional; in the absence of that documentation, declining will
read to balloters as the WG defending an accident.
