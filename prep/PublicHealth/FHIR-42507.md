## Summary

The ticket (originally imported as Comment 58 on a larger BSeR ballot
bundle by Lloyd McKenzie) challenges a modeling assumption baked into
the BSeR referral workflow: that any given referral will (a) always
result in an encounter/visit, and (b) always result in exactly one
encounter. The reporter argues neither assumption is safe — a referral
may be declined, may be satisfied without a face-to-face visit, or may
span multiple visits over time. He also points out that details about
scheduling are frequently not information the requester of the referral
needs or should receive. The implication is that BSeR's current
coupling of scheduling/encounter data to the referral `Task` is too
tight and should be revisited. No Jira, Zulip, or GitHub
cross-references were surfaced by the cross-reference index, so this
summary is drawn entirely from the ticket text itself.

## Proposed Dispositions

Because the cross-reference index surfaced no related GitHub repos,
these dispositions are framed against the BSeR IG in the abstract. Any
accepted change will need to be landed in the BSeR IG authoring repo
(typically the `HL7/bser` IG source under the `input/` authoring root,
with profiles under `input/profiles/`, examples under `input/examples/`,
and narrative under `input/pagecontent/`) — the exact paths should be
confirmed against a current `repo-analysis` briefing before
implementation.

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Decouple encounter and scheduling information from the BSeR referral
`Task`. Concretely:

1. In the BSeR referral `Task` profile, relax any cardinality or
   invariant that implicitly assumes a single fulfilling `Encounter` —
   e.g., drop or re-cardinality any `Task.output` / `Task.focus`
   slice that points at exactly one `Encounter`.
2. Allow zero-to-many fulfilling `Encounter` resources to be
   associated with the referral, carried via `Encounter.basedOn`
   pointing back at the `Task` (or via a `ServiceRequest` fulfilled
   by the `Task`), rather than being embedded in the `Task` itself.
3. Explicitly state in the BSeR narrative that a referral may be
   fulfilled without any encounter, via a single encounter, or via a
   series of encounters, and that the referral status machinery
   (`Task.status` / `businessStatus`) is what signals fulfillment —
   not the presence of an `Encounter`.
4. Move `Appointment` / scheduling details out of the referral `Task`
   payload the requester consumes. Scheduling data should live on
   `Appointment` / `Encounter` resources at the performing side and be
   exchanged (if at all) via a distinct interaction, with clear
   guidance that requesters are not guaranteed to receive them.

#### Justification

This matches the FHIR workflow pattern (`Task` orchestrates, and the
fulfilling clinical resources are separate artifacts linked via
`basedOn` / `partOf`). Real-world referrals frequently resolve
without a visit (phone consult, declined referral, care plan update
only) or span multiple visits (e.g., a tobacco-cessation referral
with recurring sessions). Keeping scheduling detail on `Appointment` /
`Encounter` — rather than folded into the `Task` payload — also aligns
with the reporter's privacy point: requesters typically do not need,
and in some jurisdictions should not receive, the performer's
scheduling internals.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Rather than re-architecting the `Task` payload, keep the `Task` shape
largely intact but:

1. Add an explicit `0..*` extension (or profile-defined element) on the
   BSeR `Task` profile that references fulfilling `Encounter`
   resources, replacing any current `0..1` assumption — documented as
   "informational, may be absent, may be many."
2. Introduce a BSeR-defined CodeableConcept slice on
   `Task.businessStatus` to represent "fulfilled without encounter",
   "fulfilled by single encounter", and "fulfilled by multiple
   encounters", so the requester can reason about fulfillment without
   needing the encounter list.
3. Leave `Appointment` out of the referral payload entirely and add
   narrative explicitly stating that scheduling information is a
   performer-side concern and is *not* part of the BSeR exchange
   unless a specific use case requires it (documented separately).

#### Justification

This preserves backward compatibility for existing BSeR implementers
who have built against the current `Task`-centric model while still
fixing the two problems the reporter raises: the "always exactly one
encounter" assumption (addressed by 1 and 2) and the leakage of
scheduling detail to the requester (addressed by 3). It also avoids
churn in the fulfilling resources' profiles, which tends to be where
most implementer effort is concentrated.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Close as "Not Persuasive" / already addressed. Rationale: BSeR's
referral `Task` profile does not *mandate* exactly one `Encounter` —
the issue, if any, is with IG narrative examples rather than the
underlying model, and the base FHIR workflow pattern already permits
`Encounter.basedOn` → `ServiceRequest` linkage for zero-or-many
fulfilling encounters. Scheduling visibility is a deployment-time
policy concern rather than a conformance concern.

#### Justification

If on review the BSeR `Task` profile is found to already tolerate
zero-to-many fulfilling encounters and the concern is purely narrative,
a decline (with a pointer to the existing mechanism and a minor
narrative clarification handled editorially) is defensible and avoids a
normative change. This is only a tenable position if a line-by-line
audit of the current BSeR `Task` profile and examples confirms the
model is already permissive enough.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

Disposition B is the right balance. The reporter's concerns are real —
referrals genuinely do not always produce exactly one encounter, and
scheduling detail genuinely is often inappropriate for the requester —
so Disposition C (decline) is unlikely to satisfy the workgroup,
especially given the ticket's "Highest" priority and Lloyd McKenzie's
explicit `BSeR_discuss_Lloyd` label flagging it for discussion.
Disposition A fixes the model cleanly but carries real implementer
cost, since BSeR already has production-leaning implementations and
reshaping `Task.output` / `Task.focus` cardinalities is a breaking
change. Disposition B achieves the same behavioral outcome (zero-to-
many fulfilling encounters, scheduling kept off the requester path)
via additive profile constraints and narrative, which is the pattern
BSeR has historically favored for STU-era refinements. It also lets
the workgroup close the ticket with a concrete, auditable change
without reopening the broader `Task`-vs-`ServiceRequest` debate that
Disposition A could trigger.
