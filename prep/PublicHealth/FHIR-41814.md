## Summary

The ticket asks the BSeR (US Bidirectional Services eReferrals) IG editors to
formally define the "Requirements Repository" concept. The reporter (Brett
Marquard) observes that this term appears in several figures throughout the
implementation guide but is never explained in the narrative. He proposes two
acceptable resolutions: either (a) add a formal definition for the repository
along with concrete examples of its content and usage, or (b) remove the
references entirely. He further notes a substantive concern: if there are not
yet well-agreed requirements for these eReferral exchanges, then assuming a
"knowledge repository" exists to support the interaction is optimistic and may
mislead implementers about the maturity of the supporting infrastructure.

There are no cross-referenced Jira tickets, Zulip threads, or GitHub items
linked to this ticket in the indexed sources, so this review is grounded
solely in the ticket text and its BSeR-IG context.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Add a formal definition of the **Requirements Repository** to the BSeR IG
narrative. Concretely:

1. Add a new subsection (e.g., "Requirements Repository") under the BSeR
   architectural overview / actors page where the figures referenced are
   introduced.
2. Define the Requirements Repository as a logical actor/system that holds
   service-specific referral requirements (e.g., the data elements,
   prerequisites, intake forms, and consent items that the receiving service
   provider expects in order to triage and act on a referral). Make explicit
   whether it is a separately-deployable system, a logical view onto another
   system, or a conceptual placeholder.
3. State its responsibilities (publish requirements, version them, allow
   discovery by referring providers) and its non-responsibilities (it does
   not store patient data, does not adjudicate referrals).
4. Add at least one worked example: e.g., the requirements published by a
   diabetes-prevention program describing the intake `Questionnaire`,
   eligibility criteria, and any prerequisite `Observation`s.
5. Update each figure that currently mentions the Requirements Repository to
   reference the new definition by anchor.

#### Justification

Every actor or system that appears in an IG figure should be defined in the
narrative; otherwise figures cannot be read in isolation and implementers
must guess the intended scope. Adding a definition is the lowest-risk
resolution and preserves the existing diagrams without invalidating any
in-flight implementations that may have been built against the published
figures. It also gives the WG a place to record the maturity of the concept
(e.g., flag it as informative/illustrative if no normative behavior is yet
defined), which directly addresses the reporter's concern that the
infrastructure may be "optimistic."

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Rather than defining a new actor, **remove the Requirements Repository from
the figures and narrative** and replace it with the closest concrete FHIR
mechanism the BSeR workflow already relies on. Specifically:

- Replace each figure reference to "Requirements Repository" with either
  - a `PlanDefinition` / `ActivityDefinition` published by the service
    provider that conveys the referral requirements, or
  - a `Questionnaire` (and supporting value sets) when the requirements are
    purely intake-form driven,
  whichever matches the actual exchange depicted.
- Update the narrative to describe how a referring provider discovers and
  retrieves these resources (e.g., from the service provider's FHIR server,
  via a published Implementation Guide, or via a registry).
- If a registry-style component is still warranted, scope it explicitly to
  "Knowledge Artifact Registry" and reuse FHIR's existing artifact-registry
  vocabulary instead of inventing a BSeR-specific term.

#### Justification

The reporter's secondary point is that a "knowledge repository" is
optimistic if no well-agreed requirements exist yet. Disposition B addresses
that head-on by binding the figures to mechanisms FHIR already provides
(`PlanDefinition`, `ActivityDefinition`, `Questionnaire`) instead of
asserting an actor whose behavior the IG cannot yet specify. It avoids
introducing a new term that implementers might interpret as a normative
obligation, and it keeps the IG honest about what is actually exchanged
on the wire today versus what is aspirational.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline the request and leave the figures and narrative as-is, on the basis
that the Requirements Repository is intentionally illustrative — a
placeholder shown to convey the conceptual flow rather than a normative
actor — and that adding a formal definition would over-commit the IG to
infrastructure that the BSeR community has not yet agreed on.

#### Justification

If the WG's position is that the figures are conceptual diagrams meant to
orient readers to the *kinds* of systems involved (rather than a normative
component model), then forcing a definition could lock in a design choice
prematurely. A short editorial note added near the first figure ("the
Requirements Repository depicted here is illustrative; concrete
implementations may realize this capability in different ways") would
arguably address the confusion without committing the IG to a specific
mechanism. However, this is the weakest option because it leaves the
narrative gap that prompted the ticket and risks the same confusion
recurring in future ballots.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

The ticket is rooted in two distinct concerns: (1) an unexplained term in
the figures, and (2) skepticism that a "knowledge repository" is a
realistic assumption for these referrals. Disposition A only resolves (1)
and could entrench (2) by promoting an under-specified actor into the
normative narrative. Disposition C resolves neither concern substantively.
Disposition B resolves both: it removes the unexplained term *and* anchors
the workflow in FHIR mechanisms (`PlanDefinition`, `ActivityDefinition`,
`Questionnaire`) that the BSeR community already understands and uses for
intake/eligibility. The editing burden is moderate — primarily figure
relabeling and a narrative section explaining how referral requirements
are discovered — and the resulting IG will more accurately reflect what
implementers can actually build today. If the WG ultimately wants to
preserve the "Requirements Repository" name for continuity with prior
versions, it can be re-introduced in a later cycle as a thin wrapper over
those concrete artifacts, with the maturity caveat the reporter is asking
for.
