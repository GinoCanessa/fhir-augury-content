## Summary

Craig Newman is asking the Public Health work group to clarify the
semantics of an Observation profile in the US Public Health Profiles
Library (USPHPL) whose description currently reads: *"This Observation
profile represents whether or not the patient has asserted that they
have verifiable vaccine credentials."* The wording is ambiguous on two
fronts: (1) whether a `yes` value means only that the patient claims to
*possess* a verifiable credential (independent of any actual
immunization status), or that they are asserting *expected immunity*
against a specific disease; and (2) if the latter is intended, how the
target disease/vaccine is to be carried in the Observation (e.g., via
`Observation.code`, `Observation.focus`, a component, or an extension).
The reporter is asking for an unambiguous statement of what `yes` and
`no` mean in this profile so that implementers can populate and consume
the resource consistently.

No related Jira tickets, Zulip threads, or GitHub items were surfaced
by `cross-referenced` or by Zulip search on the ticket key or topic
keywords, so the request stands on its own. The ticket was previously
resolved as *Considered for Future Use* (vote 30-0-6, 2025-12-04) and
then re-opened post STU2 publication on 2026-02-17 by Lynn Laakso to
be revisited for the next release.

## Details

**Description:**

The description of "This Observation profile represents whether or not
the patient has asserted that they have verifiable vaccine credentials."
is ambiguous. Is the patient simply asserting that credentials exist
(without any statement of whether or not they have been immunized
against a certain disease)? Or are they asserting expected immunity
against a certain disease? If the latter, how is that specified using
FHIR? Please clarify what values of "yes" and "no" mean in the context
of this profile.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Rewrite the narrative `description` of the USPHPL vaccine-assertion
Observation profile so that the semantics of `valueCodeableConcept`
(`yes` / `no`) are stated unambiguously, and align the profile's
formal binding to match. Specifically:

- State explicitly that the Observation captures **only the patient's
  self-assertion that they hold a verifiable vaccine credential** for
  the target disease â€” *not* a clinical determination of immunity and
  *not* a verification of the credential itself.
- Define `yes` as: "Patient asserts that they possess a verifiable
  vaccine credential for the target disease." Define `no` as: "Patient
  asserts that they do *not* possess such a credential." Add a third
  value (e.g., `unknown` from `DataAbsentReason` or the standard
  yes/no/unknown value set) for the case where the patient declines or
  cannot answer.
- Make the **target disease/vaccine** an explicit element of the
  resource rather than leaving it implicit. Recommended encoding:
  - `Observation.code` fixed to a single LOINC concept meaning
    "Verifiable vaccine credential asserted by patient"; **and**
  - `Observation.focus` `1..1` referencing a `Condition` whose code is
    the target disease (or an `Immunization` if the patient is
    asserting against a specific vaccine event), **or** a required
    `Observation.component` with `code` = "Target disease" bound to the
    appropriate vaccine-preventable disease value set.
- Update the profile's `purpose`, the intro page, and any IG narrative
  to repeat this scope statement, and add a usage example showing a
  patient asserting a credential for measles.

#### Justification

This is exactly what the reporter is asking for and is a low-risk,
high-value clarification. Patient-asserted credential possession and
clinical immunity are distinct concepts; conflating them in a single
boolean Observation has well-documented downstream risks (CDS misfires,
inappropriate exemptions, public-health reporting errors). Making the
target disease an explicit, queryable element (rather than buried in
narrative) is consistent with how other USPHPL and US Core
Observation profiles are structured (e.g., result Observations always
carry a coded `code` plus, where needed, a `focus` or `component`).
Adding an `unknown` answer aligns with the broader FHIR pattern for
patient-reported yes/no questions and avoids forcing implementers to
choose between an incorrect `no` and an absent Observation.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Rather than patching the description, **retire the single
"vaccine-assertion" Observation profile** and replace it with a small,
purpose-built pair of artifacts that separate the two concepts the
current profile conflates:

1. A **patient-asserted Immunization** profile (or guidance on using
   `Immunization` with `reportOrigin = patient` /
   `informationSource = Patient`) for the case where the patient
   asserts they were immunized against a specific disease/vaccine.
2. A **credential-possession** artifact â€” either an Observation profile
   whose `code` is "Possession of verifiable vaccine credential" with a
   required `focus` on the target `Condition`, or a
   `DocumentReference` profile pointing at the credential itself
   (SMART Health Card / VC) when one is available.

Deprecate the existing combined profile in the next USPHPL release with
a migration note that maps prior `yes`/`no` instances to the new
artifacts.

#### Justification

The ambiguity the reporter identifies is structural, not editorial:
"asserts a credential exists" and "asserts immunity" are different
clinical statements with different downstream consumers (registries,
schools, employers, CDS). Splitting them aligns the data model with
existing FHIR resources designed for each purpose (`Immunization` for
self-reported shots, `DocumentReference` for the credential payload),
removes the need to invent profile-specific semantics for `yes`/`no`,
and keeps the credential-possession Observation purely about the
*assertion of the artifact*. The cost is a heavier migration burden
for early implementers, which the *Considered for Future Use*
disposition the WG already voted on (2025-12-04) implicitly
acknowledged.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline the change request on the grounds that the originally voted
disposition (*Considered for Future Use*, 30-0-6 on 2025-12-04) already
captured the WG's intent: the profile's current wording is acceptable
for STU2, and any structural rework should wait for a later release
under a dedicated change request rather than be folded into a
clarification ticket. Close FHIR-51790 as *Persuasive with Mod* only to
the extent of a one-sentence narrative tweak, and defer the
target-disease modeling question to a new ticket scoped to the next
ballot cycle.

#### Justification

The profile shipped in STU2 with this language and has at least one
balloted vote of confidence behind keeping it as-is. Re-opening it
post-publication risks destabilizing the few implementers who have
already built to the STU2 contract. A *Decline* (or minimal-edit) here
preserves STU2 stability while routing the deeper modeling discussion
to a properly-scoped change request where the WG can evaluate the
Disposition B-style restructuring without time pressure.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** A

The reporter is not asking the WG to redesign the profile â€” he is
asking what `yes` and `no` mean. That is a defect in the profile's
specification, not a feature request, and it is appropriate to fix it
in place. Disposition A delivers the clarification the reporter asked
for, makes the target disease explicit (which is the *only* way the
`yes`/`no` answer becomes machine-meaningful), and leaves the door
open to the more ambitious restructuring in Disposition B as a
separate, follow-on change request once implementer feedback on the
clarified semantics is available. Disposition C under-serves the
reporter and leaves a known ambiguity in a High-priority,
re-opened ticket that the WG has already decided (by reverting the
prior resolution) to revisit.
