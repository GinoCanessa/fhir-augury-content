## Summary

The reporter (Mitra Rocca) observes that, in the US Central Cancer Registry
Reporting Content IG (2026Jan ballot, Use Cases page), Figures 2.2 and 2.3 do
not depict the **Triggering Events** ("Triggers") that initiate the reporting
process. The narrative earlier in section 2 distinguishes between
**Encounter-Based Triggers** and **Non-Encounter-Based Triggers**, but neither
trigger category appears in the diagrams; instead the figures read as if the
**Data Submitter** spontaneously starts the workflow. The ticket asks the
authors to (a) make the trigger types and the entity that monitors/acts on
them visible in Figures 2.2 and 2.3, (b) clarify the relationship between
triggers and Data Submitters in the surrounding narrative, and (c) consider
associating the actors to the timeline in Figure 2.1 (Subsection 2.3.5,
"Patient Journey") so that each actor's role and action sequence is
unambiguous. No cross-referenced Jira tickets, GitHub items, or Zulip threads
were found for this ticket key, so the request stands on its own.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Update the Use Cases page of the US Central Cancer Registry Reporting Content
IG (`usecases.html`, Subsection 2.3.5 "Patient Journey") as follows:

1. **Figures 2.2 and 2.3** — re-draw to add an explicit "Triggering Event"
   element upstream of the Data Submitter, branching into the two trigger
   categories already defined in the narrative ("Encounter-Based Trigger"
   and "Non-Encounter-Based Trigger"). Show the monitoring/acting entity
   (e.g., the EHR / clinical system / submitter system that detects the
   trigger) as a distinct actor whose output feeds the Data Submitter step.
2. **Surrounding narrative** — add a short paragraph immediately before
   Figures 2.2/2.3 that states explicitly: (a) what kinds of events qualify
   as Encounter-Based vs. Non-Encounter-Based triggers, (b) which actor is
   responsible for monitoring each trigger type, and (c) how the trigger
   results in the Data Submitter initiating the reporting flow shown in the
   figure.
3. **Figure 2.1 ("Patient Journey")** — overlay or annotate the existing
   timeline with the actors (e.g., Patient, Provider, EHR, Data Submitter,
   Receiver/Registry) so that each step on the timeline is attributed to a
   specific actor and the action sequence is unambiguous (e.g., a swimlane
   layout, or per-step actor labels).

These changes are documentation-only (figures + prose); they do not affect
profiles, value sets, capability statements, or any conformance artifacts.

#### Justification

The reporter's observation is concrete and verifiable on the cited page: the
narrative defines the two trigger categories and clearly assumes a
monitoring actor, but the figures hide both. Bringing the diagrams in line
with the prose is a standard editorial fix and is fully consistent with HL7
IG authoring practice (figures are expected to faithfully represent the
narrative). Annotating Figure 2.1 with actors is a low-risk improvement that
addresses the same kind of ambiguity reviewers and implementers routinely
flag in workflow diagrams. Because the request is scoped to use-case
documentation and does not touch StructureDefinitions, ValueSets,
CapabilityStatements, or example instances, the change has no
backward-compatibility risk.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Rather than embedding triggers and the monitoring actor into the existing
Figures 2.2 / 2.3, **introduce a new "Triggering Events" subsection** (e.g.,
2.3.x) immediately preceding the current 2.3.5 "Patient Journey", containing:

- A dedicated **trigger-flow diagram** showing: clinical event →
  Encounter-Based vs. Non-Encounter-Based classification → monitoring
  system → Data Submitter hand-off.
- A **trigger inventory table** enumerating the in-scope event types for
  each category (Condition codes, Encounter types, lab results, registry
  notifications, etc.) and the actor expected to detect each.
- A short note in Figures 2.2 and 2.3 captions ("Workflow begins after a
  Triggering Event; see §2.3.x") with no change to the figures themselves.

For Figure 2.1, instead of overlaying actors on the timeline, add a separate
**actor / responsibility table** keyed to each timeline phase, leaving the
existing graphic intact.

#### Justification

This alternative addresses the same underlying gap (triggers and monitoring
actors are invisible) but isolates the new content in dedicated, easily
maintained artifacts (one new diagram, one or two tables) rather than
re-cutting three existing figures. Tables are easier than figures to keep
synchronized with downstream profile/value-set changes, and a dedicated
trigger subsection is easier for implementers to find via the table of
contents than information buried in figure captions. The trade-off is that
implementers still have to mentally connect the new subsection to Figures
2.2/2.3, which is exactly what Disposition A makes unnecessary; conversely,
A requires re-drawing diagrams that may be referenced from other IGs or
slide decks and could be more invasive than it appears.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline the request as **already addressed by existing narrative**: the
section preceding Figures 2.2/2.3 already defines Encounter-Based and
Non-Encounter-Based triggers and identifies the Data Submitter as the actor
that initiates reporting. The figures are intentionally workflow-level and
abstract over upstream trigger detection; readers seeking trigger detail are
expected to consult the surrounding prose. Close as "Persuasive with Mod" or
"Not Persuasive" depending on workgroup judgement, with a comment pointing
to the existing trigger discussion.

#### Justification

This position is defensible only if the workgroup believes the current text
is sufficient and that adding actors to figures would clutter them without
benefit. However, given that (a) the reporter is an experienced ballot
commenter, (b) the priority was confirmed as "Highest" after an accidental
change was reverted, and (c) the narrative itself implies a monitoring actor
that the figures omit, the case for declining is weak. This disposition is
included for completeness rather than as a credible recommendation.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** A

The ticket identifies a concrete inconsistency between the IG's narrative
(which defines two trigger categories and presumes a monitoring actor) and
its figures (which begin the workflow at the Data Submitter, with no
triggers and no monitoring actor visible). The fix is documentation-only,
carries no conformance or backward-compatibility risk, and directly
improves implementer comprehension of when and why a Central Cancer Registry
report is generated — exactly the question Figures 2.2/2.3 are intended to
answer. Disposition B's table-and-caption approach is reasonable but leaves
the central diagrams misaligned with the prose, which is the actual
complaint; Disposition C would close a real ambiguity flagged at "Highest"
priority by the IG's own author community. Accepting as requested (with the
authoring team free to choose between in-figure annotation and a small
companion diagram for the trigger-classification step) is the cleanest
response and keeps the change confined to the Use Cases page.
