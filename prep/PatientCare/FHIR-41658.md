## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Apply the editorial correction in the IBCM IG narrative: replace every
occurrence of the phrase **"new live form"** with **"new life form"**
(the reporter's parenthetical preferred wording). Track each instance
during the fix — the reporter says it appears in "a couple of places"
— so all occurrences are caught in one pass. The change is restricted
to the IG's narrative pages (e.g., the relevant `pages/*.md(.html)`
files in the IBCM publication source); no FHIR resources, profiles,
extensions, value sets, or examples are touched, and no behaviour or
conformance changes.

#### Justification

The ticket is filed as a **Technical Correction** with no semantic
content. "New live form" is not a term of art in the IBCM IG; it is
clearly a typing error for "new life form" / "new life", which is the
ordinary phrasing in birth-and-child narratives. Accepting verbatim
keeps the change minimal, preserves the reporter's intent, and follows
the routine pattern Patient Care (and other WGs) already apply to
narrative typo tickets. Risk is effectively zero: there are no
downstream conformance artefacts, no published examples to migrate,
and no implementer-visible behaviour change.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Accept the correction, but also do a small targeted editorial sweep of
the surrounding paragraphs to (a) confirm "new life form" is the
right substitution in each location (vs. "newborn", "neonate", or
"live birth", which may be more precise depending on context), and
(b) catch any adjacent typos or grammar issues in the same passages.
Where the surrounding text discusses the live-birth event itself,
prefer **"newborn"** or **"live birth"** over "new life form", which
reads colloquially. The fix still ships in the next IBCM publication
as a Technical Correction; only the wording chosen per occurrence
differs from Disposition A.

#### Justification

The reporter offers two candidate fixes ("new life" vs. "new life
form") and explicitly hedges with "or", which signals that the right
substitution is context-dependent. A single mechanical search-and-
replace risks producing awkward sentences (e.g., "the new life form
was registered"). A brief contextual review costs the IG editor a few
minutes, raises the editorial quality of the surrounding narrative,
and avoids spawning a follow-up ticket if a reader objects to the
chosen wording. This is still inside the scope of a Technical
Correction.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Close as **Not Persuasive** on the grounds that the typo, as
described, cannot be reproduced (no specific page anchor, file, or
quoted surrounding sentence is provided), and ask the reporter to
re-file with a precise location if the issue still exists in the
current build of the IBCM IG.

#### Justification

This is the weakest option but is defensible if the editor cannot
locate the offending phrase in the current IBCM source. Without a
GitHub repo cached in FhirAugury, no one on this prep pass was able
to confirm the typo still exists in the published narrative; if it
has already been silently corrected in a later build, declining is
honest. Declining purely on cost-benefit grounds is **not**
defensible — the fix is trivial — so this disposition only applies
if the phrase is genuinely no longer present.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** **A** (with the optional contextual check
from B if the editor's bandwidth allows).

The ticket is a textbook Technical Correction: a clearly-identified
typo in IG narrative, no semantic implications, no conformance
impact, and no community disagreement on Zulip or in linked Jira
tickets. The cost of accepting is a few minutes of editor time; the
cost of declining is a small but real loss of credibility for the
IBCM IG narrative. Disposition A is the path of least surprise and
matches how Patient Care already handles its typo backlog. If the
editor has the bandwidth, fold in Disposition B's two-paragraph
contextual sweep so the substitution reads naturally in each
occurrence — but do not let that sweep block the fix. Disposition C
should only be selected if the editor verifies that the phrase no
longer appears in the current IBCM source.
