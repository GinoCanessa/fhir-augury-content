## Summary

The reporter notes that the Ophthalmology Retinal IG uses several specialty-specific terms and acronyms — explicitly calling out *visual field*, *OCT* (optical coherence tomography), and *CDR* (cup-to-disc ratio) — without defining them in the IG narrative. They request that the IG either define the acronyms and key terms it uses inline, or provide a pointer to a high-quality external glossary/overview that covers the common terminology. The goal is to make the IG approachable for FHIR implementers who are not ophthalmology subject-matter experts so that they can correctly interpret and use the IG's profiles. There are no cross-referenced Jira tickets, Zulip threads, or GitHub items associated with this ticket in the indexed sources.

## Details

**Description:**
Because Ophthalmology is a specialized area, it would be helpful to readers to define some of the terms and acronyms used in the IG including visual field, OCT and CDR. This will help readers understand the use of the various profiles. Or if there is a quality external reference that has an overview of some of the common terms, that would be good as well.

## Proposed Dispositions

> Note: The Ophthalmology Retinal IG has no GitHub repository surfaced in cross-references and no cached `repo-analysis` briefing in this workspace. The dispositions below therefore identify the *kind* of authoring change required (IG narrative content) but cannot name a specific authoring root or file path. The work group should map the proposal to the IG's actual source repository before implementation.

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Add a "Glossary" (or "Terminology and Acronyms") narrative page to the Ophthalmology Retinal IG and link it from the IG's table of contents / left navigation. The page lists, at minimum, the acronyms called out in the ticket (*visual field*, *OCT* — optical coherence tomography, *CDR* — cup-to-disc ratio), plus other domain terms and acronyms used elsewhere in the IG narrative or in profile/element definitions. Each entry consists of: term/acronym, expanded form, plain-language definition (1–3 sentences), and, where applicable, a link to the profile(s) or value sets that use the term. On first use of each defined term in any other narrative page or profile description, add an inline link back to the glossary entry.

#### Justification

This is exactly what the reporter asked for and matches the standard IG-authoring pattern HL7 already uses for other specialty IGs (e.g., dedicated glossary or terminology pages). Defining acronyms in-spec — rather than relying on an external reference — keeps the IG self-contained, removes a dependency on third-party content that could move or change, and is friendlier to non-clinical FHIR implementers who form a meaningful share of the readership. The change is purely narrative (no profile, value set, or conformance impact) so cost and risk are low.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Instead of authoring a full HL7-maintained glossary, add a short "Terminology" subsection to the IG's existing introduction/overview page that (a) defines the small set of acronyms that actually appear in the IG (*visual field*, *OCT*, *CDR*, and any others discovered during a sweep of the narrative and profile descriptions), and (b) directs readers to one or two authoritative external references for broader ophthalmology terminology — for example, an American Academy of Ophthalmology glossary or the SNOMED CT ophthalmology hierarchy — for terms not defined in the IG itself. Where the IG already binds value sets backed by SNOMED CT or LOINC, prefer linking the term to its code-system definition rather than restating it.

#### Justification

This option preserves the reporter's underlying goal — readers can decode the IG's terminology — without committing the work group to maintaining a parallel ophthalmology glossary that duplicates content already curated by clinical bodies and standard terminologies. It is materially less ongoing maintenance burden than Disposition A and avoids the risk that an HL7-authored definition drifts from clinical consensus over time. The trade-off is that readers must occasionally follow an external link for less common terms, which is a minor inconvenience compared with the maintenance cost.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline the request as out of scope for the IG itself, on the grounds that defining specialty clinical terminology is the role of clinical-society publications and standard terminologies (SNOMED CT, LOINC), not a FHIR Implementation Guide. Close as "Not Persuasive" with a note recommending readers consult external clinical references for ophthalmology terminology.

#### Justification

A defensible-but-weak rationale: FHIR IGs typically focus on the data exchange contract (resources, profiles, bindings, conformance) rather than re-publishing clinical concept definitions, and the underlying terms are already defined authoritatively elsewhere. Declining keeps the IG narrowly scoped to FHIR mechanics. However, this rationale is weak in practice because (a) the cost of adding a short glossary is very small, (b) the reporter is explicitly speaking on behalf of non-specialist readers who are the IG's intended implementer audience, and (c) other HL7 specialty IGs routinely include glossaries. Declining would also leave the ticket's *Highest* priority unaddressed without a substantive reason.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

Disposition B captures everything the reporter actually needs (the three specifically-named acronyms and any others used in the IG get defined in-spec, and readers are pointed at authoritative references for the long tail) while avoiding the open-ended maintenance commitment of a full HL7-curated ophthalmology glossary. Disposition A is also a reasonable choice and the work group should escalate to A if the IG's narrative turns out to use a substantial number of specialty terms — in which case a dedicated glossary page is more navigable than a paragraph in the introduction. Disposition C is not recommended: the request is small, the reporter is the IG's target audience speaking for readers who lack ophthalmology background, and the priority assigned to the ticket (Highest) is incompatible with a "not persuasive" close. A targeted Terminology subsection plus inline links to SNOMED/LOINC where the IG already binds them is the lowest-cost change that fully addresses the concern.
