## Proposed Dispositions

> Note: All three dispositions are constrained by the fact that the
> ticket does not identify the page or sentence containing the broken
> "paragraph A" reference, and no GitHub repo backing the IBCM IG is
> registered in FhirAugury. Any code-level proposal therefore has to
> name the IG narrative source generically ("the IBCM IG narrative
> page that currently mentions 'paragraph A'") rather than a specific

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Treat this as an editorial / technical correction in the International
Birth and Child Model (FHIR) IG. As a first step, the assigned editor
greps the IG sources (the IBCM IG narrative pages, typically the
`input/pagecontent/*.md` directory of the IG repository) for the
literal string "paragraph A". For each occurrence, identify the page
the reporter is talking about by inspecting the surrounding sentence,
then replace "paragraph A" with a reference to a paragraph that
actually exists on that page (either by paragraph number, by section
heading, or by an inline anchor). Re-publish the IG and close the
ticket as a technical correction with a short note in the IG history /
release notes describing the fix.

#### Justification

The ticket type is **Technical Correction** at **Highest** priority,
the description is unambiguous about the nature of the defect (a
dangling intra-page reference), and the fix is purely editorial: it
does not change conformance, cardinalities, value sets, or any
artifact. Editorial corrections to dangling references are exactly the
class of change Technical Correction tickets are designed for, and
leaving a published IG with a reference to a non-existent paragraph
degrades reader trust in the rest of the document. The work is bounded
(`grep "paragraph A"` over the IG narrative gives a small, finite list
of candidates), so accepting the request is low risk.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Accept the spirit of the request but generalize the fix: rather than
patching only the one sentence the reporter noticed, the editor performs
a one-time audit pass across the IBCM IG narrative for **all** alphabetic
paragraph references ("paragraph A", "paragraph B", …, "section X",
"item (i)", etc.) and replaces them with concrete, resolvable
cross-references — preferably named anchors / heading links rather than
positional labels. Where a label such as "paragraph A" was in fact
intended to refer to a paragraph that exists but is no longer labelled
that way, restore the label by adding a Markdown anchor at the target
paragraph and link to it. Document the convention in the IG's
contributor notes so future authors do not reintroduce positional
labels.

#### Justification

Positional labels like "paragraph A" are a well-known maintenance
hazard in long-lived narrative IGs: subsequent edits insert,
reorder, or split paragraphs and silently invalidate the label, which
is exactly what produced this defect. Fixing only the one sentence the
reporter happened to read leaves the underlying fragility in place and
invites future Highest-priority technical corrections of the same
shape. The cost of the broader audit is bounded by the IG's narrative
size, and switching to anchor-based links makes the references
machine-checkable by the IG publisher's link checker, so regressions
will be caught at build time rather than by readers.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline / close as **Cannot Reproduce — insufficient information**.
Ask the reporter to update the ticket with the page name (or URL) and
the exact sentence containing the "paragraph A" reference. If the
information is not provided within the next ballot cycle, close the
ticket without action.

#### Justification

The ticket as filed does not name the affected page, the affected
section, or the surrounding sentence; it only says "the page". The
IBCM IG contains many narrative pages, and there is no guarantee that
a `grep "paragraph A"` over the current sources will find anything —
the offending text may have been edited since 2023-08-21, or the
phrasing may have been "Paragraph A", "para. A", "(A)", etc. Without
that anchor, an editor cannot be confident they are fixing the same
defect the reporter saw, and may either miss it or "fix" an unrelated
mention. Declining-with-request-for-info is defensible for any
technical correction whose target location is ambiguous.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** A

This is a Technical Correction at Highest priority filed against an IG
narrative; the reporter has clearly described the class of defect
(intra-page reference to a paragraph label that does not exist on the
page) even though they did not name the page. The cost of accepting is
low — a `grep "paragraph A"` over the IG narrative sources will surface
a small, finite list of candidate sentences, and each one can be
inspected and corrected by the IG editor without further input from
the reporter. Disposition C (declining for lack of information) is
unattractive because the ticket has been triaged at Highest and a
published IG that visibly references a non-existent paragraph is a
reader-facing defect that should not survive another publication.
Disposition B (broader audit) is appealing as a follow-up but should
not block the immediate fix; the work group can capture it as a
separate maintenance task once the specific sentence the reporter
flagged is corrected.
