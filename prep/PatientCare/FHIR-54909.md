## Summary

The reporter (Celine Lefebvre) raises a ballot-comment question against the US
SDOH Clinical Care IG: are there risks when a provider documents a patient's
enrollment in a social-care program (e.g., SNAP) without official verification
from the benefit provider, particularly when that information will be shared
with other providers via FHIR? The submitter is asking whether the IG should
require — or at least guide implementers toward — verifying enrollment with
the benefits issuer before recording and exchanging it. No cross-referenced
Jira tickets, Zulip threads, or GitHub items were surfaced for this ticket,
so the question stands on its own as a clarification request against the
ballot draft.

## Details

**Description:**

> Are there any risks in a provider documenting enrollment in a program for
> which they don't have official verification – particularly if that info
> will be shared with other providers? Should this be verified with the
> benefit provider first – SNAP, etc?

**Comments:**

- **JIRAUSER24103 — 2026-03-06:** Acknowledges to Celine Lefebvre that yes,
  there are risks to communicating enrollment in a social-care program
  without verification. However, analogous to a patient self-asserting a
  medication on their medication list, the IG is not requiring verification:
  the usefulness of communicating social-care enrollment status is judged to
  outweigh the risk of unverified data. This effectively states the
  workgroup's leaning toward declining a verification mandate.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Update the US SDOH Clinical Care IG narrative (the page that describes how
enrollment in a social-care program is represented and exchanged) to add an
explicit guidance section titled something like "Verification of program
enrollment." That guidance would (a) recommend that providers verify
enrollment with the benefit issuer (e.g., SNAP, WIC, housing program) prior
to recording it in a way that will be shared, and (b) require that whenever
enrollment is recorded without such verification, the source of the
information (patient-reported, caregiver-reported, third-party) be captured
on the resource — typically via `Provenance`, an `informationSource` element
where supported, or an equivalent IG-defined extension on the
enrollment-bearing resource (e.g., `Coverage` or the SDOH program-enrollment
profile).

#### Justification

This directly addresses the reporter's safety concern: downstream providers
who receive enrollment data should be able to tell whether it has been
verified with the issuing program. Capturing source/provenance is consistent
with established FHIR patterns for self-asserted clinical data (allergies,
medication statements) and aligns with the IG's broader interest in data
quality for SDOH information that increasingly drives clinical decisions and
referrals.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Rather than recommending verification, add narrative-only guidance to the
US SDOH Clinical Care IG explaining that program-enrollment information may
be self-reported, provider-asserted, or verified with the benefit issuer,
and that implementers SHOULD populate existing FHIR provenance / source
mechanisms (`Resource.meta`, `Provenance`, or the relevant
`*.informationSource` element) so receivers can judge reliability. Do not
introduce a new requirement, extension, or constraint; instead point
implementers at the standard FHIR mechanisms already available, with one or
two examples (e.g., a `Coverage` instance with `Provenance` indicating
patient-reported vs. issuer-verified).

#### Justification

This preserves the workgroup's stated position (per the 2026-03-06 comment)
that the value of exchanging enrollment information outweighs the risk of
it being unverified, while still giving implementers a concrete answer to
the reporter's question. It avoids adding new conformance burden late in
the ballot cycle and avoids profile churn, while making the implicit
"self-asserted is okay if labeled as such" pattern explicit in the IG.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline the request to add a verification requirement or new guidance.
Respond on the ticket that the IG intentionally treats program-enrollment
information analogously to other patient-asserted clinical data (e.g.,
medication lists), where the value of exchange is judged to exceed the
risk of unverified entries, and that existing FHIR provenance mechanisms
already let receivers reason about reliability when they need to.

#### Justification

This is essentially the position already articulated in the 2026-03-06
comment. The submission is a Question (not a Change Request), the
workgroup has already provided a substantive answer, and adding normative
verification language could introduce conformance friction with social-care
workflows where authoritative verification with benefit issuers is often
not available at the point of care.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

Disposition B is the best balance of the three. The 2026-03-06 comment
already commits the workgroup to *not* mandating verification, which rules
out Disposition A as a literal accept. Pure decline (Disposition C) is
defensible but leaves the underlying safety concern unanswered in the
spec itself — future implementers will hit the same question. Adding a
short, narrative-only paragraph that names the existing FHIR provenance
and source-of-information mechanisms (and shows one example) costs the
workgroup little, requires no new conformance, and turns the ballot
comment into a durable improvement in the IG that prevents the question
from being raised again in the next cycle.
