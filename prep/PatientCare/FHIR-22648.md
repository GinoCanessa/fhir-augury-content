## Summary

Josh Mandel reports that `CommunicationRequest.payload` is conceptually
ambiguous: the element definition says the payload is the content "to be
communicated to the recipient," but the published example carries text
("Please provide the accident report and any associated pictures to support
your Claim# DEF5647.") that reads as a message *to the sender/performer of the
request*, not as content to be forwarded to the ultimate recipient. Mandel
argues this is more than an example bug — there is "blurry conceptualization"
in `CommunicationRequest.payload` itself, which seems to be doing
double-duty as both (a) the actual content to be transmitted and (b) a
description/instruction telling the requestee what to communicate. He
suggests the element may need to be refactored rather than continuing to
serve both roles.

The cross-referenced artifacts (`CommunicationRequest.payload`,
`CommunicationRequest.payload.content`, and the parallel
`Communication.payload.content`) confirm the scope: any disposition has to
reconcile how `payload.content[x]` is meant to behave on the request
resource versus the event resource. No related Jira tickets surfaced via
cross-references, but the related Zulip threads (see below) make clear that
the same confusion has surfaced repeatedly in implementer questions.

## Details

**Description:**

The documentation for
http://build.fhir.org/communicationrequest-definitions.html#CommunicationRequest.payload
says:

> Text, attachment(s), or resource(s) to be communicated to the recipient.

... but the example shows a "payload" with a CodeableConcept whose text says:

> "Please provide the accident report and any associated pictures to support your Claim# DEF5647."

i.e. the example payload seems to be a message for the "sender", not for the
"recipient".

It's not just an issue with the example, I think — there's some blurry
conceptualization here. It's possible that "payload" needs to be refactored
instead of asking it to do double-duty.

**Comments:**

- **michelle.m.miller (2019-09-05)** — Notes from the Patient Care FHIR
  Conference Call on Sept 5, 2019. The WG framed three options:
  - **Option 1:** `CommunicationRequest.payload.content` is defined
    *differently* from `Communication.payload.content`. Rationale:
    `CommunicationRequest` is from requester → informationProvider, whereas
    `Communication` is from sender → recipient, so the element semantics
    differ and the resource may need cleanup, possibly with renamed
    elements.
  - **Option 2:** Define `CommunicationRequest.payload.content` *similarly*
    to `Communication.payload.content` (and treat the example as wrong —
    later struck through in the notes).
  - **Option 3:** Acknowledge that `CommunicationRequest.payload.content`
    serves two masters (both the informationProvider and the recipient).
  - Lloyd McKenzie's framing was added: a `CommunicationRequest` is created
    by A for B to communicate with C; `CommunicationRequest.payload` is
    better thought of as a *payload description* (describing what should be
    communicated once communication happens). Sending the literal content
    is less common — sometimes the requester lacks authority, or the
    communication has to happen in person. Likewise, a `Communication` may
    just record "I communicated the smoking-cessation pamphlet" without
    actually transporting the pamphlet; the resource documents that
    communication occurred, not necessarily the bytes exchanged.
  - Discussion was deferred to Tuesday Q3 at the Sept 2019 WGM.

## Related Zulip Discussions

### [implementers > CommunicationRequest](https://chat.fhir.org/#narrow/stream/implementers/topic/CommunicationRequest/near/167026796)

Originating thread (Jan 2018 → Mar 2020) where Josh Mandel ultimately filed
FHIR-22648. Brian Postlethwaite asked how `requester` and `sender` differ;
Lloyd McKenzie explained the canonical "A requests B to send C to D"
pattern and noted that `requester` should never equal `sender`. Implementers
Tim Berezny and Luan Garrido struggled to map their email/question-and-answer
use cases onto Communication vs CommunicationRequest vs Task. Lloyd and
others (Eric Haas, Elliot Silver, Paul Knapp) repeatedly drew the line:
`Communication` is a clinical record that a communication occurred (like
an email), `CommunicationRequest` is "please send X to Y," and `Task`
is the right tool when an action and tracking is needed. Mandel's specific
objection — that the published `payload` example reads as instructions to
the requestee rather than content to forward — went unresolved in-thread,
with Lloyd offering only that "C can either be the explicit content to send
or a description of the type of content to send" and inviting Mandel to file
an issue, which became this ticket. No consensus on whether the spec text
or the example is the source of truth.

Participants: Josh Mandel, Lloyd McKenzie, Brian Postlethwaite, Richard
Townley-O'Neill, Tim Berezny, Eric Haas, Elliot Silver, Paul Knapp, Luan
Garrido.

### [implementers > Use case Communication vs Composition](https://chat.fhir.org/#narrow/stream/implementers/topic/Use%20case%20Communication%20vs%20Composition/near/175905631)

Sept 2019 thread in which Michelle Miller posted a "FYI" pointing back at
GF#22648 to flag that Patient Care still owed a resolution on payload
semantics. The wider thread (Kenneth Myhra's national citizen-portal use
case) circles the same ambiguity from a different angle: implementers want
to send a titled message body with optional structured "kind of content"
metadata. Lloyd suggested mapping title→`Communication.subject.text`
(later corrected to `topic.text`) and body→`payload.contentAttachment`
(base64-encoded); Jose Costa Teixeira pushed back that
`contentCodeableConcept` would be more natural for short text, and asked
for a dedicated "subject of message" field distinct from "subject of care."
Michelle clarified the GF#20770 history of `contentCodeableConcept`: it
is the actual codified content being communicated, not a categorization
("when contentCodeableConcept is used, the CodeableConcept *is* what is
being communicated and is not a categorization of the content"). Kenneth
then filed GF#24661 asking for a separate code element on
`Communication.payload` analogous to `Composition.section.code`. Jose
floated `CodeableReference` (then under proposal) as a possible cleaner
modelling primitive. No consensus.

Participants: Michelle (Moseman) Miller, Kenneth Myhra, Lloyd McKenzie,
Jose Costa Teixeira, Grahame Grieve.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Treat the conflation in `CommunicationRequest.payload` as a real modelling
problem and refactor the element. Concretely, in the FHIR Core
`CommunicationRequest` resource:

1. Rename / reframe the existing `payload` to make its requester-side
   semantics explicit — e.g., add a backbone element such as
   `CommunicationRequest.contentToSend[x]` (CodeableConcept | Attachment |
   Reference(Any)) carrying the actual content the requester wants the
   sender to forward to the recipient — i.e. the analogue of
   `Communication.payload.content[x]`.
2. Separately retain (or introduce) a description-style element such as
   `CommunicationRequest.payload.instruction` (string / markdown) for
   free-text instructions to the sender about *what* to communicate
   (matching what the current published example actually shows).
3. Replace the existing example with two examples: one that uses the new
   "content to forward" element correctly, and one that uses the
   instruction element to mirror the "Please provide the accident report
   …" wording.
4. Update the element definitions on both `CommunicationRequest.payload`
   and `Communication.payload` so each cross-references the other and
   states unambiguously which side of the request/event split it lives on.

#### Justification

This addresses Mandel's underlying claim directly: the current single
element is being asked to carry two distinct concepts (content-to-forward
vs. instructions-to-the-sender), and the published example actually
exercises the second meaning while the definition text describes only the
first. Lloyd McKenzie's own framing on the Patient Care call ("payload is
better thought of as 'payload description'") and his Zulip statement that
"C can either be the explicit content to send or a description of the type
of content to send" both confirm the dual semantics in practice. Splitting
the concepts is consistent with how FHIR generally separates request
intent from request content (e.g., `ServiceRequest.code` vs
`ServiceRequest.orderDetail`), and it removes the need for downstream
implementers to guess which interpretation a sender intended.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Keep the element shape unchanged, but resolve the ambiguity through
documentation and example fixes only:

1. Rewrite the `CommunicationRequest.payload` definition to state
   explicitly that the element *may* carry either (a) the literal content
   to be communicated to the recipient, or (b) a description of the
   content to be communicated (e.g. via `contentCodeableConcept` or
   free-text in `contentString` / `contentAttachment`), per Lloyd's
   "payload description" framing — and that the choice is at the
   requester's discretion.
2. Replace the misleading "Please provide the accident report …" example
   with a clear "description of content to be sent" example (e.g.
   `contentCodeableConcept` carrying a document type code such as
   "Accident report"), and add a second example that demonstrates literal
   content using `contentAttachment` or `contentReference`.
3. Add an explicit comparison block on both `CommunicationRequest.payload`
   and `Communication.payload` clarifying that on the *request* the
   element describes what is to be sent, and on the *event* the element
   represents what was sent (or a record that it was sent).
4. Cross-link to GF#20770 (origin of `contentCodeableConcept`) and to the
   guidance discussed in GF#24661 about coded content vs. categorization.

#### Justification

This preserves backward compatibility — no element renames, no new
backbone, no impact on existing CommunicationRequest instances or
profiles (US Core, IPS, etc.). Most of the implementer confusion in the
two Zulip threads is plausibly attributable to documentation gaps rather
than to a structural defect: Lloyd repeatedly resolved confusion in chat
without invoking any new modelling primitives. Patient Care has not, in
the years since the ticket was filed, surfaced any concrete implementer
report of being *unable* to express their intent with the current
structure — only of being unsure how to express it. A definitions-and-
examples fix is proportionate and low-risk for an STU/normative resource
that already has wide deployment.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline the change request as **already addressed by existing mechanisms /
no actionable defect**. Position: the element's choice type
(`CodeableConcept | Attachment | Reference(Any)`) is by design — it lets
the requester send either the literal content, a code identifying the
content, or a reference to it, exactly as Lloyd McKenzie has explained
both on the Patient Care call and in chat. The published example is a
legitimate use of `contentCodeableConcept` to describe the content the
sender should forward. No spec change.

#### Justification

Defensible if Patient Care concludes the existing semantics, while
nuanced, are correct and the ticket is best characterised as a request
for clarification that has already been answered by Lloyd's framing.
Argues that refactoring (Disposition A) would impose a breaking change on
a long-established resource for arguably modest gain, and that even the
documentation-only fix (Disposition B) risks endorsing a binary
interpretation that the design has deliberately avoided.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

The Zulip evidence and the Sept 2019 Patient Care call notes both show
that the ambiguity is *real* (multiple senior implementers — Mandel,
Berezny, Myhra, Costa Teixeira — independently asked the same question
over more than a year) but also that it is consistently resolvable
through clearer documentation rather than a structural change. The
existing choice type already supports both Lloyd's "literal content" and
"description of content" interpretations; what is missing is an explicit
statement in the element definition that *both* are valid, plus an
example for each. A documentation-and-examples fix (Disposition B)
delivers the clarity Mandel asked for without the breaking-change cost
of Disposition A and without dismissing a confusion that has demonstrably
recurred across years and implementers (which Disposition C effectively
does). If, during the rewrite, Patient Care discovers that even the
clarified text cannot describe a coherent single element — for example
because `contentCodeableConcept`-as-instruction and
`contentReference`-as-literal-content require different security or
validation rules — that would be the trigger to escalate to Disposition A
in a future cycle.
