## Summary

The reporter raises an editorial concern about how design-option trade-offs are
expressed in the International Birth And Child Model (FHIR) specification.
Specifically, in the discussion of Options 1 and 2, one of the listed "Cons"
is phrased as a rhetorical question — *"What will be the effect if you cannot
digest the extension?"* — rather than as a declarative statement of the
disadvantage. The reporter suggests rewriting that Con as a statement so that
it actually articulates a downside rather than asking the reader to imagine
one. No cross-referenced Jira tickets, Zulip discussions, or GitHub items
were located for this ticket, so the request stands on its own as a
narrowly-scoped editorial fix.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

In the International Birth And Child Model (FHIR) specification, locate the
narrative section that compares Options 1 and 2 and contains the Con phrased
as *"What will be the effect if you cannot digest the extension?"*. Rewrite
that bullet as a declarative statement of the actual disadvantage —
for example: *"Implementations that do not understand the extension will lose
the associated information, since the data is carried only in the extension
and not in a core element."* Apply the same editorial pass to any sibling
"Con" entries in Options 1 and 2 that are also phrased as questions, so the
pros/cons table is internally consistent.

#### Justification

This is purely an editorial/Technical Correction change with no normative
impact. A "Con" should communicate a concrete downside; a rhetorical question
forces the reader to infer it, which weakens the comparison the section is
trying to support. Rewriting the bullet as a statement aligns with normal
HL7 narrative style, improves clarity for implementers evaluating the
options, and matches the way pros/cons are written elsewhere in FHIR design
discussions.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Rather than only rewriting the single bullet, restructure the Options 1 and 2
comparison so that each option exposes a small, parallel table with explicit
*Pro* and *Con* rows, each constrained to be a declarative statement. Where a
question is genuinely the right framing (e.g., an open design question the
work group has not yet resolved), move it out of the Con column into a
separate "Open questions" subsection beneath the comparison. Convert the
question highlighted by the ticket into the equivalent declarative Con, as
in Disposition A.

#### Justification

This addresses the underlying issue — that the Pros/Cons section mixes
genuine cons with unresolved design questions — rather than only patching the
specific bullet the reporter flagged. A consistent, parallel structure makes
the option comparison easier to scan, and pulling unresolved questions into
their own subsection preserves the work group's open issues without
mislabelling them as disadvantages. The trade-off vs. Disposition A is
slightly more editorial work and a minor restructuring of the page, but the
result is more durable as the model evolves through future ballot cycles.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Mark the ticket as *Not Persuasive* on the grounds that the existing wording,
while phrased as a question, is understood in context to imply the
corresponding Con (loss of data when the extension is not processed) and that
the editorial cost of rewriting narrative bullets does not justify a
specification change.

#### Justification

Declining is defensible only if the work group judges the current phrasing
adequately understood by readers and prefers to spend ballot cycles on
substantive technical issues. However, since the change is small, low risk,
and has no normative impact, the bar for declining a Technical Correction of
this kind is genuinely low — declining mostly signals that the work group
disagrees that the wording is unclear.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** A

This is a small, unambiguous editorial fix to the International Birth And
Child Model (FHIR) narrative. The reporter's point is correct: a Con phrased
as a rhetorical question does not actually state a disadvantage, and
implementers reading the option comparison benefit from a declarative
statement that names the concrete downside. Disposition A delivers exactly
what was asked for at minimal cost and risk; Disposition B is appealing as a
broader cleanup but expands scope beyond what the ticket requested and
should only be pursued if the work group sees a wider pattern. Declining
(Disposition C) is hard to defend for a zero-impact Technical Correction
where the reporter has already identified both the problem and the fix.
