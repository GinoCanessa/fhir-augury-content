## Summary

The reporter is reviewing a section of the International Birth And Child Model
(IPA-style birth/child model) IG that compares architectural options for
representing the relationship between a child Patient and another Patient
entity (presumably the mother / parent). Option 1 lists, among its **Pros**,
the statement *"No complex authorization required to view Patient data of
another Patient entity."* The reporter argues that this same point is in fact
a **Con** for Options 2 and 3, where an additional authorization step *is*
required to view another Patient entity's data, and asks that the bullet be
added to the Cons lists for both of those options so the comparison is
balanced and accurate. There are no cross-referenced Jira tickets, Zulip
threads, or GitHub items for this issue.

## Details

> One of the Pro's listed in Option 1: "No complex authorization required to
> view Patient data of another Patient entity", I think is actually a Con
> here, for Option 3, and as well for Option 2. I suggest adding it in both
> sections.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

In the International Birth And Child Model IG, locate the design-options
comparison page (the page presenting Options 1, 2, and 3 for representing
the link between a child Patient and another Patient entity such as the
mother). Add a new bullet to the **Cons** list of both Option 2 and Option 3
that reads (or paraphrases): *"Complex authorization is required to view
Patient data of another Patient entity."* No change is needed to Option 1's
Pros list; the asymmetry is the point being corrected.

#### Justification

This is a small, low-risk editorial fix that improves the symmetry and
honesty of the options-comparison narrative. Listing a benefit only on the
"good" side of one option without acknowledging it as a corresponding cost on
the alternative options misleads readers evaluating the trade-offs. The
reporter is restoring an accurate apples-to-apples comparison, which is
exactly what an options/Pros/Cons section is supposed to provide. There is
no normative impact — only IG narrative text changes.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Rather than mirroring the single bullet across Options 2 and 3, restructure
the comparison so that authorization complexity is presented once as an
explicit comparison axis (e.g., a small table with rows like *"Authorization
complexity"*, *"Implementation effort"*, *"Privacy posture"*, etc., and
Option 1 / Option 2 / Option 3 as columns). Each cell then states the
characteristic for that option directly (e.g., "Low — same-Patient access",
"Higher — cross-Patient authorization required"), eliminating the need to
keep parallel Pro/Con bullets in sync.

#### Justification

Pro/Con bullet lists are easy to drift out of alignment, and FHIR-41849 is
itself an example of that drift. Converting to a comparison matrix prevents
the same class of issue from recurring as future options or considerations
are added, makes the trade-offs scannable for implementers, and is a common
pattern in other HL7 IG design-discussion pages. The cost is a one-time
larger edit to the page versus the two-bullet change in Disposition A.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline on the basis that the existing wording already implies the asymmetry:
because the bullet appears only under Option 1's Pros, readers can infer that
Options 2 and 3 lack this property, and explicitly listing it as a Con on the
other options is redundant.

#### Justification

This rationale is weak — Pro/Con sections in design-comparison narratives
are conventionally read as self-contained per option, and readers should not
be expected to derive missing Cons by inversion of another option's Pros.
Declining would leave the comparison materially misleading, which is exactly
what the reporter flagged. Included for completeness only.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** A

The change requested is small, narrative-only, and unambiguously correct: a
property listed as a Pro for one option is, by definition, a Con (or at
least a missing Pro) for the alternatives that lack it. Accepting as
requested resolves the reporter's concern with minimal editorial effort and
no normative impact, and can be done in the next IG publication cycle for
the International Birth And Child Model. Disposition B (the comparison
matrix) is a worthwhile follow-up for the page's long-term maintainability
but is larger than what this ticket needs and can be filed as a separate
editorial improvement; it should not block the fix the reporter asked for.
Disposition C is not defensible — the asymmetry the reporter identified is
real and would continue to mislead readers.
