## Summary

The ticket asks the Financial Management work group to extend `ClaimResponse`
(and potentially other Financial Module resources) so that an **intermediary**
party — most commonly a clearinghouse acting between the provider and the
payer — can be identified as the entity that actually produced the response.
Today `ClaimResponse` distinguishes the entity responsible for reimbursement
(`insurer`) and the entity responsible for the claim (`requestor`), but it
provides no first-class way to attribute a response (or an error reported on a
response) to a clearinghouse that intercepted the claim before it reached the
payer. The reporter notes that clearinghouse-originated rejections are common
in real-world claim flows and are currently miscommunicated as if they came
from the insurer. No cross-referenced Jira tickets, Zulip threads, or GitHub
items were found for this ticket.

## Details

**Description:**

> This might apply to other specs in the Financial Module, as well.
>
> Today, it is common in the industry for providers to contract with a
> clearinghouse for claim submission to ease the burden of enrollment and
> maintaining individual connections to many payers. The spec defines the
> entities responsible for reimbursement and responsible for the claim, but
> it does not define an entity that produced the response.
>
> For instance, a provider might submit a claim to a clearinghouse, and that
> clearinghouse might find an error with the claim. ClaimResponse allows for
> reporting such errors, which is great! However, it does not allow a way to
> distinguish that the clearinghouse is who produced the error, rather than
> the "insurer."

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Add a new optional element `ClaimResponse.responseOriginator` (cardinality
`0..1`, type `Reference(Organization | Practitioner | PractitionerRole)`) that
identifies the party that actually produced the `ClaimResponse`, distinct
from `ClaimResponse.insurer` (the entity responsible for reimbursement) and
`ClaimResponse.requestor` (the original requestor of the claim). When an
intermediary such as a clearinghouse generates a response — most commonly a
rejection or front-end error before the claim reaches the payer — that
intermediary is named in `responseOriginator`. When the payer itself produces
the response, `responseOriginator` may be omitted or set equal to `insurer`.
For consistency, also evaluate adding the same element on `PaymentNotice`
and `PaymentReconciliation`, since the same intermediation pattern occurs in
remittance flows. No corresponding change is required on `Claim`.

#### Justification

`ClaimResponse` already carries `error.code` and structured rejection
information, so the missing piece is *who* produced that information. Adding a
single optional reference is backward-compatible (existing instances remain
valid) and aligns with how other FHIR resources distinguish "subject of the
record" from "author of the record" (e.g., `Composition.author`,
`Observation.performer`). It also reflects the real X12 277CA / clearinghouse
workflow that most U.S. providers and payers operate under, and gives
implementers a deterministic place to put the data instead of forcing
guidance into narrative or extensions.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Do not add a new core element. Instead, define a standard FHIR extension
(e.g., `http://hl7.org/fhir/StructureDefinition/claimresponse-originator`)
of type `Reference(Organization | Practitioner | PractitionerRole)` on
`ClaimResponse`, and recommend that implementers either (a) populate that
extension when an intermediary produced the response, or (b) link a
`Provenance` resource to the `ClaimResponse` whose `agent.who` identifies
the originating party. Document both patterns in the ClaimResponse narrative
and add an example that shows a clearinghouse-issued rejection.

#### Justification

This addresses the underlying need without enlarging the core model and
without forcing a new normative element on every implementer who never deals
with intermediaries. `Provenance` is the FHIR-wide, already-normative
mechanism for attributing *who created/transformed* a resource, and it
already supports the chain "submitter → clearinghouse → payer" cleanly.
A standard extension provides a low-friction alternative for implementers
who don't want to carry a separate `Provenance` resource. The trade-off vs.
Disposition A is discoverability — implementers must know to look at the
extension or the linked `Provenance`, and tooling support for extensions is
weaker than for core elements.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline the request on the grounds that the need is already met by existing
mechanisms: `Provenance` for attributing who produced the `ClaimResponse`,
and `ClaimResponse.error` (with `code` and `expression`) for describing the
error itself. Update the ClaimResponse narrative to point implementers at
`Provenance` for response attribution and close the ticket as "Persuasive
with Mod — documentation only."

#### Justification

The core resource is intentionally lean and the FM module has historically
resisted adding parties that aren't directly part of the financial
relationship (the obligation to pay, the obligation to provide). Attribution
of *who produced* a resource is a cross-cutting concern that FHIR has
deliberately delegated to `Provenance`. Adding `responseOriginator` risks
encouraging similar additions on `Claim`, `PaymentNotice`, and
`PaymentReconciliation`, growing the surface area for what is fundamentally
metadata. Declining is defensible if the workgroup is satisfied that
`Provenance` is operationally sufficient.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** A

Clearinghouse intermediation is the dominant U.S. claim-submission pattern,
and the asymmetry is real: today an implementer reading a `ClaimResponse`
with `error.code` populated has no machine-readable way to know whether the
payer or a front-end clearinghouse produced that error, which directly
affects how the provider's downstream system routes the rejection (re-submit
to clearinghouse vs. appeal to payer). `Provenance` (Disposition C) solves
this in theory but is rarely populated for financial transactions in
practice, and a non-mandatory extension (Disposition B) suffers the same
discoverability problem at scale. A single optional `0..1` reference on
`ClaimResponse` is a small, backward-compatible addition that resolves a
concrete interoperability gap, and it gives the workgroup a clean basis for
deciding (separately) whether the same element belongs on `PaymentNotice`
and `PaymentReconciliation`. The workgroup should confirm the element name
and the allowed reference targets, and decide whether `Claim.originator`
(the inverse — the intermediary that forwarded the claim) is also in scope
or should be deferred to a follow-up ticket.
