## Summary

John Moehrke proposes simplifying the `Contract.signer` BackboneElement so
that signing information on a `Contract` is captured directly with the
`Signature` datatype rather than through a parallel set of sibling
elements. As currently modeled, `Contract.signer.type` and
`Contract.signer.party` duplicate information that is already carried by
`Contract.signer.signature.type` and `Contract.signer.signature.who`.
The reporter believes those sibling elements are vestigial — left over
from an earlier, less complete `Signature` datatype — and that, now that
`Signature` is stable and self-contained, the cleanest model is to
replace the `signer` BackboneElement with a repeating `Signature`
element (suggested as `Contract.signature 0..* Signature`).

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Refactor `Contract.signer` in the R6 Contract resource so that signature
information is carried directly by the `Signature` datatype instead of
through a BackboneElement that wraps it. Concretely:

- Remove the `Contract.signer` BackboneElement (eliminating
  `Contract.signer.type`, `Contract.signer.party`, and
  `Contract.signer.signature`).
- Add a new repeating element `Contract.signature : Signature [0..*]`
  with binding/use guidance migrated from the prior `signer.type`
  binding (signature-type-codes / contract-signer-type) and from the
  `signer.party` reference targets (`Organization | Patient |
  Practitioner | PractitionerRole | RelatedPerson`) — those targets
  already align with `Signature.who`'s allowed targets.
- Update the Contract resource narrative, examples, search parameters
  (`Contract.signer-party`, `Contract.signer-type`), and any FHIRPath
  / invariant expressions that traverse `signer.*` to use the new
  `signature.*` paths.
- Document the change as a breaking-but-substantive simplification in
  the Contract resource's R6 change log; preserve the historical
  rationale for the BackboneElement in the resource notes.

#### Justification

The reporter's analysis is correct on its face: `signer.type` and
`signer.party` are exact semantic duplicates of `signature.type` and
`signature.who`. Once a stable, self-contained `Signature` datatype
exists, wrapping it in a BackboneElement that re-states the same
information violates DRY at the model level and forces implementers to
reconcile two parallel slots that may disagree. Collapsing onto the
datatype gives a single source of truth, removes a class of validation
ambiguity, and aligns Contract with how every other resource that
records signatures (e.g., `Bundle.signature`, `Provenance.signature`)
already does so. Contract is at Trial Use, so a substantive
restructuring at a major version boundary is in scope.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Keep the `Contract.signer` BackboneElement but eliminate the duplication
without losing the wrapper:

- Mark `Contract.signer.type` and `Contract.signer.party` as
  **deprecated** in R6 (retain for backward compatibility, add
  `MustSupport=false` and a "do not populate; use
  `signer.signature.type` / `signer.signature.who` instead" note).
- Add an invariant on `Contract.signer` requiring at least one
  `signature` and forbidding population of the deprecated `type` /
  `party` elements when `signature.type` / `signature.who` are
  populated with a different value.
- Optionally tighten `Contract.signer.signature` cardinality from
  `0..*` (current) to `1..*` so the BackboneElement is never empty.
- Plan a hard removal of the duplicate elements in a future release
  once implementer feedback confirms migration.

#### Justification

This avoids a hard breaking change for existing R4/R5 Contract
implementers (notably any Da Vinci or payer/legal-document profile that
reads `signer.party` directly) while still resolving the modelling
defect the ticket identifies. It treats the duplication as a
deprecation cycle rather than a rip-and-replace, which is the pattern
the FHIR core spec usually uses when the offending elements have been
normative-adjacent for several releases. The cost is a more cluttered
resource definition for one release and continued ambiguity in
validators that cannot enforce "use one or the other" without the new
invariant.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Leave `Contract.signer` as-is. Document explicitly in the Contract
resource notes that `signer.type` and `signer.party` are retained as
convenience accessors that mirror `signer.signature.type` and
`signer.signature.who`, and add a `signer-party-matches-signature-who`
invariant that requires the two to agree when both are populated.

#### Justification

Defensible primarily on stability and scope grounds: the ticket has sat
since 2019 with no comments, no Zulip discussion, no PRs, and no
implementer demand surfaced in the indexed sources. Contract has
significant existing implementer footprint (legal artefacts, consent
overlap, payer use cases), and a backwards-incompatible flattening of
`signer` would invalidate every R4/R5 instance currently in production.
The duplication is documented and benign as long as an invariant
prevents disagreement; the additional hierarchy under `signer` also
gives Contract-specific extensions a stable place to attach without
extending the shared `Signature` datatype.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B (deprecate the duplicate elements; keep
the BackboneElement for one release).

The reporter's underlying observation is correct and Disposition A is
the cleanest end state, but jumping straight there asks Financial Mgmt
to break every existing Contract instance and every profile that binds
to `signer.party` / `signer.type` for a defect that has generated no
implementer escalation in six years. Disposition B captures the
cleanup the reporter wants — implementers are formally told to stop
using the duplicate elements, and an invariant prevents the two slots
from diverging — while leaving an upgrade path that Da Vinci and
similar consumers can follow at their own pace. If/when a future
release confirms migration, the workgroup can then execute Disposition
A as a follow-up ticket and remove the deprecated elements with a
clean change-log entry. Disposition C is hard to justify: even if the
workgroup is unwilling to act now, the duplication is a real modelling
defect and should not be papered over indefinitely with a single
invariant.
