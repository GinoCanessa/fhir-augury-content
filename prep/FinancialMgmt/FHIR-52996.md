## Summary

The ticket asks the Financial Management work group to remove `Substance`
from the list of allowed target types of `Contract.term.action.performer`
and replace it with `Medication`. The reporter argues that it is not
clear how a `Substance` can act as a performer of a contractual action:
substances act on patients only when they are administered as a
medication, and the `Substance` resource is otherwise used as an
ingredient or to record incidental exposure. The change is also informed
by the R6 redirection of `Substance` to instance-only (with
`SubstanceDefinition` carrying type-level information), which makes
treating a `Substance` as the responsible "entity that will execute the
action" even less coherent. There are no cross-referenced Jira tickets;
the request originated in a thread on the Financial chat stream that is
linked from the ticket body.

## Details

**Description:**

It is not clear how `Contract.term.action.performer` can be a `Substance`.

A substance that is used to deliberately affect a patient is usually a
`Medication`.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Modify the `Contract` resource (FHIR Core, Financial Management) so that
the `Contract.term.action.performer` element no longer references
`Substance` and instead references `Medication`. Concretely: in the
StructureDefinition for `Contract`, locate the `performer` element under
`Contract.term.action`, remove `Reference(Substance)` from the allowed
target profiles, add `Reference(Medication)` in its place, and update
the element's narrative/comment to reflect that medication-as-performer
captures the "substance acting on a patient" intent. Add a release note
under FM_R5_Contract describing the swap and noting that any
implementation that was pointing `performer` at a `Substance` should now
either point at the wrapping `Medication` or move the reference to a
more appropriate term/action element.

#### Justification

The change matches the resource semantics: `performer` is described as
"entity that will execute the action", and within FHIR a substance only
"executes" patient-facing action when it is administered as a
medication. It is consistent with R6's narrowing of `Substance` to
instance-only and the corresponding promotion of `SubstanceDefinition`
for type-level information — keeping `Substance` on `performer` would
force implementers to choose between a batch-level resource and a type
resource for what is conceptually neither. The Zulip thread (Rik
Smithies, Lloyd McKenzie) shows alignment between the reporter and at
least one methodology-aware reviewer, with no recorded objection. Both
`Substance` and `Medication` already appear elsewhere in `Contract.term`
(e.g., `subject`, `action.subject`), so adding `Medication` here does
not introduce a new resource dependency.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Keep `Reference(Substance)` on `Contract.term.action.performer` and
*also* add `Reference(Medication)`, rather than replacing one with the
other. Tighten the element definition to clarify that `Substance` is
permitted only for non-patient-facing contractual obligations
(e.g., a contract concerning the supply, transport, storage, or
disposal of a regulated substance where the substance itself is the
subject of the obligation), and that for any patient-administration
context `Medication` is the expected target. Document this scoping in
the element comment and, if the work group wants to enforce it, add an
invariant on `Contract.term.action` that disallows
`performer = Reference(Substance)` when the action's `intent` or `code`
indicates patient administration.

#### Justification

`Contract` is a permissive, cross-domain resource and existing
implementations may already use `performer = Reference(Substance)` in
non-clinical contexts (regulated-substance supply chain, hazmat
contracts, custody-of-evidence agreements). Removing the target type
outright is a breaking change for any such implementer in R5, and the
R6 instance-only narrowing of `Substance` does not preclude a substance
*instance* being the subject of a contractual obligation. Adding
`Medication` addresses the reporter's clinical concern while preserving
backward compatibility; an explanatory note plus an optional invariant
gives the work group a path to discourage the patient-facing misuse
without breaking existing artifacts. This approach also avoids forcing
implementers who already use `Medication` elsewhere in `Contract.term`
to refactor.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Do not change the allowed target types of
`Contract.term.action.performer`. Instead, add a clarifying comment on
the element noting that when a contractual action concerns the
administration of a substance to a patient, `Medication` (or
`MedicationAdministration` / `MedicationRequest` referenced via
`Contract.term.action.subject`) is the expected pattern, and that
`Substance` on `performer` is intended for contracts whose obligations
attach to the substance itself rather than to its administration.
Categorize the rationale as "already addressed by existing mechanism
plus documentation gap, not a model defect."

#### Justification

`Contract` is intentionally a flexible resource, and `performer` is a
deliberately broad list of references — most of the resources currently
on the list (e.g., `Location`, `Group`, `Device`) are also things that
do not "execute actions" in a literal sense. Removing only `Substance`
on the basis that it cannot literally perform an action would surface
the same critique against several other targets and risk a cascading
narrowing of the resource. The reporter's clinical concern is fully
addressable via the existing `Medication` reference targets elsewhere
in `Contract.term`, so there is no implementer who is *forced* to
misuse `Substance`. A clarifying note avoids both a breaking removal
and the cardinality/permission churn of adding `Medication` here when
it is already reachable elsewhere.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

Disposition B keeps the door open for the legitimate non-clinical uses
of `Substance` in contractual contexts (supply, storage, regulated
handling) while adding the `Medication` reference that the reporter and
Lloyd McKenzie agree is the correct target for patient-administration
contracts. Disposition A is appealingly clean and matches the literal
ask, but a straight removal of `Reference(Substance)` is a breaking
change for any R5 implementer that has already modelled a
substance-supply or hazmat-style contract this way, and the Zulip
thread did not produce a survey of implementer impact. Disposition C
risks rejecting a real semantic improvement on the strength of a
broader "performer is permissive anyway" argument that the work group
may not actually want to defend. B is the smallest change that resolves
the reporter's concern, is consistent with the R6 `Substance` /
`SubstanceDefinition` split, and gives the work group a defensible,
non-breaking story for R5; it can be tightened to A in a future ballot
if no implementers object.
