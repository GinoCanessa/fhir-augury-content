## Summary

The reporter asks that the Scalable Consent Management (FHIR) IG, in section
7.2.3 "Consent Management", explicitly require that consent administration
service systems SHALL inform the patient — through the consent business
process — when their consent record is active, revoked, or otherwise updated.
The intent is to ensure that any state change to a consent is communicated
back to the patient as part of the managed consent lifecycle, rather than
leaving patient notification implicit or out of band.

No cross-referenced Jira tickets, Zulip threads, or GitHub items were
returned by the augury cross-reference index for this ticket, so the
request stands on its own. Note that the ticket already carries a
resolution of "Not Persuasive with Modification" (resolved 2026-01-26)
even though its status is still "Triaged"; the Security workgroup's
documented disposition is that the IG will instead define a "consenter"
actor (which may be the patient, a caregiver, or a guardian) and ensure
the consenter is informed via the use cases and operations, while
explicit patient notification remains out of scope of this guide.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

In the Scalable Consent Management (FHIR) IG, update section 7.2.3
"Consent Management" to add a normative statement of the form:

> Consent administration service systems **SHALL** inform the patient,
> through the consent business process, whenever a Consent transitions
> to an `active`, `inactive`, or `not-done` state, or is otherwise
> updated (e.g., changes to `Consent.provision`, `Consent.policy`, or
> `Consent.performer`).

Operationally this would mean:

- Defining a "patient notification" step in the consent lifecycle
  narrative in 7.2.3.
- Adding a conformance expectation on the Consent Administration actor
  (or equivalent) that, on each state transition or material update of
  a Consent resource for which the patient is the subject, the system
  emits a notification to the patient through whatever channel the
  business process defines (e.g., portal message, Communication
  resource, Subscription, out-of-band channel).
- Optionally referencing `Communication` / `Subscription` /
  `AuditEvent` as candidate technical mechanisms without mandating one.

#### Justification

This is consistent with broader patient-rights expectations around
consent (HIPAA-style notice, GDPR-style transparency obligations, and
21st Century Cures patient-access principles): if a patient's consent
state changes, the patient is the natural party with a need to know.
Embedding the requirement in the IG's normative text would make it
testable and would prevent implementers from quietly omitting patient
notification because it is "out of band". The reporter's wording
(SHALL, scoped to section 7.2.3) is already specific enough to drop in
with minor editorial cleanup.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach (workgroup's recorded resolution)

#### Proposal

Do not add a SHALL on patient notification to section 7.2.3. Instead,
formally introduce a **"consenter" actor** in the IG (the actor whose
consent decisions the system manages) and clarify that:

- The consenter MAY be the patient, a caregiver, or a guardian.
- The IG's use cases and operations are responsible for ensuring the
  consenter is informed of consent state changes (active, updated,
  revoked, etc.) as part of the consent business process.
- Notification of the patient *as a separate party from the consenter*
  is explicitly called out as **out of scope** of this guide and is
  deferred to other specifications, organizational policy, or
  jurisdictional regulation.

Concretely: add an Actors section (or extend the existing one) in 7.2.x
defining `Consenter` (with the three sub-roles), and update the
narrative in 7.2.3 to refer to "the consenter" rather than "the
patient" wherever consent-state communication is discussed. Add a
short scope note that explicit patient-only notification is not
specified by this IG.

#### Justification

This is the disposition the workgroup has already recorded on the
ticket ("Not Persuasive with Modification"). It addresses the
underlying need — making sure the right human is told — without baking
a SHALL into the IG that would be hard to satisfy in caregiver- or
guardian-mediated scenarios where the patient is *not* the consenter
(pediatrics, incapacity, legal guardianship, delegated consent). Using
an actor abstraction is a more durable design than a Patient-specific
SHALL: it covers the requested use case (patient-as-consenter)
automatically while remaining correct for the cases where Patient ≠
Consenter. It also keeps the IG's scope tractable by not mandating a
notification transport.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Mark the request as Not Persuasive without modification and close.
Rationale: patient notification is a governance / organizational-policy
concern that is better handled by jurisdictional regulation (HIPAA,
GDPR, etc.) and by adjacent specifications (Communication,
Subscription, AuditEvent) rather than by a Consent-management IG, and
adding a SHALL would create a normative obligation the IG cannot
verify or enforce.

#### Justification

A flat decline is defensible if the workgroup believes that the
existing FHIR machinery (Subscription on Consent, AuditEvent for
state changes, generic Communication) plus organizational policy is
already sufficient, and that adding IG-level SHALLs about
out-of-band notification creates conformance language that
implementers cannot meaningfully test against. However, this option
loses the opportunity to clarify the actor model, which the reporter's
question implicitly exposed as ambiguous, and so is weaker than
Disposition B.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

Disposition B aligns with the workgroup's already-recorded resolution
("Not Persuasive with Modification") and with the way the IG actually
needs to model consent: the entity that consents is not always the
patient, and a Patient-specific SHALL would be wrong in caregiver and
guardian scenarios. By formally introducing a `Consenter` actor with
patient/caregiver/guardian as recognized sub-roles, the IG captures
the reporter's underlying concern (the human whose decision this is
should be told what happened to it) without overreaching into general
patient-notification territory that belongs to policy and to other
specifications. Disposition A is too narrow (Patient-only) and
Disposition C wastes a useful clarification the reporter has surfaced.
The workgroup should approve B, draft the Actors text and the 7.2.3
narrative tweak together, and confirm the scope note that
patient-as-non-consenter notification is out of scope.
