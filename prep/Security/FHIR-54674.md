## Summary

John Moehrke (Security WG) reports that pages within the Scalable Consent
Management (FHIR) implementation guide are labelled "Informative" even though
they contain substantive requirements, and that not all such requirements
pages (notably the use-case pages) are decorated with the IG's "requirements"
markers. The ticket asks the Security WG to revisit the normative status and
requirements-decoration of these pages so that conformance expectations are
not hidden behind an Informative classification. The reporter has already
provided a resolution direction ("Persuasive"): the affected pages should not
be marked Informative because they are capturing requirements, and any actual
requirements on them must be explicitly marked as such. No cross-referenced
Jira tickets, Zulip threads, or GitHub items were surfaced for this ticket.

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Adopt the reporter's stated resolution exactly:

1. In the Scalable Consent Management (FHIR) IG, identify every page
   currently flagged as "Informative" that contains normative or
   conformance-style language (notably the use-case pages called out
   by the reporter).
2. Remove the Informative classification on those pages so they inherit
   the IG's default normative/standards-track status (i.e., drop the
   `informative` page-status / standards-status mark in the IG page
   metadata for those pages).
3. Audit the same pages for individual requirement statements and apply
   the IG's "requirements" decoration (the requirement-block / requirement
   anchor mechanism the IG already uses on its formal requirements pages)
   to every statement that expresses a SHALL/SHOULD/MAY-style obligation,
   so each requirement is individually addressable and testable.

#### Justification

This matches what the reporter requested and what the WG has already
written into the ticket's Resolution ("Persuasive"). Marking pages
"Informative" while embedding requirements in them is an
internal-consistency defect: it tells implementers and testers that
nothing on the page is binding, while the prose says otherwise. The IG
publisher's page-status mechanism and requirements decoration exist
precisely so that conformance expectations are visible, addressable, and
testable; using them here is consistent with how other HL7 IGs (e.g.,
US Core, IPS) handle requirement statements outside the resource
profiles. The change is editorial in nature — page metadata plus
markup — and does not alter the underlying clinical or security model
of Scalable Consent.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Keep the use-case pages classified as Informative, but factor every
binding requirement out of those pages into a dedicated "Requirements"
page (or section) that is normative and uses the IG's requirements
decoration. The use-case pages then contain only narrative scenarios
and link forward to the corresponding requirement ID(s); the
Requirements page becomes the single normative surface for
conformance.

#### Justification

This preserves the convention that "use-case" pages are illustrative
narrative (which many readers expect) while still resolving the defect
the reporter raised: requirements are no longer hidden inside
Informative content, because they no longer live on those pages at all.
It also makes the requirements individually citeable and testable from
one canonical location, which is friendlier to downstream test-tool
authors and to ballot reviewers. The trade-off versus Disposition A is
more editorial work (factoring text out, adding cross-links) and the
risk of drift between the narrative use case and the extracted
requirement; Disposition A is cheaper but leaves requirements
distributed across pages whose page-level status still has to be
re-evaluated case by case.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline the change. Leave the affected pages marked Informative and do
not add requirements decoration, on the rationale that the pages were
intentionally classified Informative because the WG considers their
contents to be illustrative use cases rather than conformance
requirements; any binding statements should be raised as separate
tickets per requirement.

#### Justification

This position is defensible only if the WG asserts that the pages do
not in fact contain requirements — i.e., that the reporter has
mis-read the prose. That is a hard position to hold here, because the
ticket's own Resolution explicitly agrees that the pages "are
capturing requirements." Declining would also leave a known
internal-consistency gap in the IG (Informative classification + SHALL
language on the same page), which ballot reviewers tend to flag.
Including this disposition primarily to make the trade-off explicit
to the WG.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** A

The reporter and the Security WG already agree (the ticket is marked
Persuasive and a resolution direction is stated) that the affected
pages are capturing requirements and should not be flagged Informative.
Disposition A operationalises that agreement with the smallest
necessary change: flip the page status and apply the IG's existing
requirements decoration to the binding statements already on the page.
Disposition B is a cleaner long-term structure, but it is a larger
editorial refactor and is not what the ticket asks for; if the WG
prefers B, treat it as a follow-up ticket once A has unblocked the
immediate conformance-visibility defect. Disposition C contradicts the
WG's own stated resolution and should only be selected if the WG
revisits the Persuasive resolution.
