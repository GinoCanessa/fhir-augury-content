## Summary

Hans Buitendijk requests that the Scalable Consent Management guide allow
`Consent.patient` to be optional (cardinality `0..1` rather than `1..1`) so
that the same `Consent` resource pattern can be used to express
**jurisdictional privacy rules** — policies that are not tied to an
individual patient but instead represent organizational, regional, or
national privacy obligations. The reporter proposes two paths: (a) relax
the profile so `Consent.patient` becomes `0..1`, or (b) introduce a
separate `PrivacyRule` profile on `Consent` where `Consent.patient` is
`0..0`. Either approach is intended to enable consistent, computable
sharing of jurisdictional privacy rules from central or federated policy
libraries that, combined with patient-specific consents, can be
ingested and managed by an authorization server. The only cross-reference
on file is to the FHIR artifact `Consent.patient` itself; there are no
linked Jira tickets, Zulip discussions, or GitHub items.

## Details

**Description:**

> Consent.patient is 0..1.  While the primary scope is for patient data
> sharing consents, the Consent resource should similarly be used for
> jurisdictional privacy rules as they need to be able to consistently
> defined in computable format and shared.
>
> There are two approaches:
>
> - enable this Consent profile to cover jurisdictional privacy
>   consents, thus change the Consent.patient from 1..1 to 0..1.
> - create a separate PrivacyRule profile on Consent where
>   Consent.patient is 0..0.
>
> Either way enables consistent definitions of the policies and
> computable rules, while also enabling consistent sharing of
> a jurisdictional privacy rules from central/federated libraries that
> in combination with patient specific data sharings can be ingested
> and managed by an authorization server (whether external or internal
> to a data source).

(Note: the reporter's first sentence appears to misstate the current
profile cardinality — within the Scalable Consent Management guide
`Consent.patient` is currently constrained to `1..1`; the request is to
relax it to `0..1`.)

**Resolution (already recorded by the WG):**

> The scope of this guide is explicitly Patient Consent and thus
> Consent.patient needs to be 1..1.
>
> We will be update the Scope section to indicate that jurisdictional
> privacy rules and policies are not in scope of this guide (and this
> project overall) and are considered part of the policy side of the
> existing diagram.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Relax the Scalable Consent Management profile on `Consent` so that
`Consent.patient` is `0..1` (instead of the current `1..1`), and update
the guide narrative and examples to describe two valid uses of the
profile:

1. A patient-specific consent (`Consent.patient` populated, as today).
2. A jurisdictional privacy rule (`Consent.patient` absent), with
   guidance on how `scope`, `category`, `policyRule`, `provision`, and
   the actor/data/period elements should be expressed when there is no
   single patient subject.

Add invariants/usage notes that constrain when `Consent.patient` may be
absent (e.g., when `category` indicates a jurisdictional/policy-class
consent).

#### Justification

Aligns the guide with the base `Consent` resource cardinality (`0..1`)
and lets implementers reuse the same computable structure for both
patient-specific consents and jurisdictional rules. Avoids forcing
implementers to maintain two parallel artifact families for closely
related policy content, and supports the reporter's stated use case of
federated/central libraries of computable privacy rules that an
authorization server can combine with patient-level consents.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Keep the Scalable Consent Management profile's `Consent.patient` at
`1..1`, but publish a **sibling profile** in the same guide — e.g.,
`PrivacyRuleConsent` — that derives from base `Consent` and constrains
`Consent.patient` to `0..0`. The two profiles share `policyRule`,
`provision`, terminology bindings, and example patterns, but are
clearly differentiated by `category` and by which subject elements are
allowed. The guide's Scope section is broadened to explicitly include
jurisdictional privacy rules as a secondary in-scope use, distinct from
patient consents.

#### Justification

Preserves the strong invariant that *patient consents always have a
patient*, which protects existing implementers, validators, and
downstream auth servers that rely on `Consent.patient` being present in
the patient-consent profile. A separate profile makes the difference
between a patient consent and a jurisdictional rule explicit at the
profile level (easy to validate, easy to route), instead of relying on
soft business rules to decide whether `patient` should be present. It
also avoids any risk of implementers populating `Consent.patient`
incorrectly on what is conceptually a jurisdictional policy.

---

### Disposition C: Decline (Not Persuasive with Modification)

#### Proposal

Do not change `Consent.patient` cardinality and do not introduce a
PrivacyRule profile in this guide. Instead, **update the Scope section**
of Scalable Consent Management to state that jurisdictional privacy
rules and policies are out of scope of this guide and of the project,
and that they belong to the "policy" side of the existing scope
diagram. Refer implementers who need computable jurisdictional rules
to a future, separate effort (or to base `Consent` directly, where
`Consent.patient` is already `0..1`).

#### Justification

The guide's name and stated charter are *Scalable Consent Management*
for **patient** consent; the `1..1` constraint encodes that scope. The
need raised by the reporter is real but is a different problem domain
(jurisdictional/organizational policy authoring and distribution) with
different governance, lifecycle, actors, and consumers. Solving it
inside this guide risks scope creep and conflating two audiences in a
single profile. A scope-clarification edit is a low-risk, low-cost
change that addresses the underlying ambiguity the reporter
encountered without committing the guide to a use case it was not
designed for. This matches the resolution already recorded on the
ticket ("Not Persuasive with Modification").

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** C

The Security WG has already resolved this ticket as *Not Persuasive
with Modification*, and that disposition is well grounded. The guide is
explicitly scoped to patient consent, and `Consent.patient = 1..1` is
how that scope is enforced computably; relaxing it (Disposition A)
re-opens the scope question and pushes a second, distinct use case
(jurisdictional policy authoring) into a profile that was not designed
for it. A sibling `PrivacyRuleConsent` profile (Disposition B) is
defensible and worth keeping on file for a future jurisdictional-policy
project, but introducing it inside *this* guide would expand the
project's charter, IG examples, conformance test surface, and review
burden for a use case the WG has decided not to take on here. The
modification proposed in Disposition C — explicitly calling out
jurisdictional privacy rules as out of scope and locating them on the
"policy" side of the scope diagram — directly addresses the reporter's
underlying concern (consistency and discoverability) without changing
the profile contract that current implementers depend on.
