## Summary

Joseph M. Lamy reports that the Security for Scalable Registration,
Authentication, and Authorization (FHIR) IG requires JWT consumers (in
section 7.1.6) to verify that certificates in the trust chain have not
been revoked, but the IG does not impose any requirements that make this
verification actually achievable in practice. Specifically, the IG does
not require trust communities to stand up CRL or OCSP endpoints, does
not require JWT generators to embed the corresponding certificate
extensions (CDP for CRL, AIA for OCSP), and does not require JWT
consumers to support extracting and querying those endpoints. The ticket
asks the work group to add explicit conformance requirements covering
all three actor types so that revocation checking is feasible end-to-end,
and to decide on the split of responsibility between generators (CRL
and/or OCSP) and consumers (CRL and/or OCSP). The reporter also flags
an open design question about whether trust communities should be
allowed to distribute revocation endpoints out of band rather than
through certificate extensions, and what that means for IG conformance.
Frequency-of-check / caching policy is explicitly out of scope and left
to individual trust communities.

No related Jira tickets, Zulip threads, or GitHub items were surfaced
by cross-reference or search, so this ticket appears to be a standalone
gap report against sections 1.2 and 7.1.6 of the IG.

## Details

**Description:**

In section 7.1.6, JWT consumers are required to verify that the
certificates in the chain have not been revoked. However, there are
not requirements to ensure this is possible. We need:

- Trust communities to be required to establish endpoints for CRL
  retrieval, OCSP query, or both, and to maintain current revocation
  status for all issued certificates. (section 1.2)
- JWT generators to be required to supply certificates that include
  the extensions for CDP (for CRL), AIA (for OCSP), or both, as
  neither are required as is. (section 7.1.6)
- JWT consumers to be required to support extracting CRL and OCSP
  endpoints from encoded certs, and querying revocation status from
  these endpoints. (section 7.1.6)

We could either give generators the flexibility (CRL or OCSP) and
consumers the full responsibility (CRL and OCSP), or give generators
full responsibility (CRL and OCSP) and consumers the flexibility (CRL
or OCSP).

We may want to allow for trust communities to take care of providing
endpoints for CRL and OCSP out of band, instead of requiring them to
be identified in the encoded certs. But if so, we should make clear
whether systems can conform to this IG without having the capability
to do this via encoded certs.

Note that this ticket doesn't have to cover the frequency of checking
(i.e. use of caching), as that can be managed by specific trust
communities. This is just to require the capability.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Add normative conformance language to the Security for Scalable
Registration, Authentication, and Authorization IG covering all three
actor types so that the existing 7.1.6 revocation-verification
requirement is achievable end-to-end:

- **Section 1.2 (Trust Communities):** Add a SHALL requirement that
  every trust community establish at least one revocation distribution
  mechanism (CRL endpoint, OCSP responder, or both) for all
  certificates it issues, and maintain current revocation status for
  the lifetime of those certificates.
- **Section 7.1.6 (JWT Generators):** Add a SHALL requirement that
  certificates supplied by JWT generators include the corresponding
  X.509 extensions — `CRL Distribution Points` (CDP) when CRL is
  offered and/or `Authority Information Access` (AIA) with the OCSP
  access method when OCSP is offered — and require at least one of the
  two be present.
- **Section 7.1.6 (JWT Consumers):** Add a SHALL requirement that JWT
  consumers be able to parse CDP and AIA extensions from the encoded
  certificate chain and perform revocation lookups against both CRL
  and OCSP endpoints.

Adopt the "generators flexible / consumers full responsibility" split
that the reporter raised first: the generator picks CRL or OCSP (or
both) per its trust community's policy, and consumers MUST implement
both lookup mechanisms so they can verify any conforming generator's
chain. Leave caching/refresh frequency out of the IG, as the ticket
requests.

#### Justification

This is the smallest set of changes that makes the existing 7.1.6
"verify revocation" obligation testable. Putting the burden of
supporting both lookup methods on consumers mirrors how other PKI
profiles (e.g., the IETF RFC 5280 stack and most browser PKI policies)
distribute responsibility: relying parties are expected to handle the
algorithms a CA chose to publish, not the other way around. The
reporter has already implemented a partial check ("warn if no CDP is
included") in testing, which is direct evidence that the absence of
these requirements is causing real conformance ambiguity today.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Adopt the same three-actor structure as Disposition A, but invert the
flexibility split and explicitly carve out an "out-of-band" trust
community mode:

- **Generators MUST** include both CDP and AIA extensions in issued
  certificates (full responsibility on generators).
- **Consumers MUST** support at least one of CRL or OCSP lookup, and
  SHOULD support both. Consumers MUST be able to parse both extensions
  even if they only query one mechanism.
- **Trust communities MAY** distribute CRL/OCSP endpoint information
  out of band (e.g., via a community policy document or a directory
  service) instead of via certificate extensions, but if they do, they
  MUST publish a machine-readable endpoint descriptor and the IG MUST
  state that systems relying solely on encoded-cert extensions are
  still conformant — i.e., out-of-band distribution is additive, never
  a substitute that breaks extension-based clients.

#### Justification

This addresses the open design question the reporter explicitly raised
about out-of-band endpoint distribution. Making generators carry both
extensions removes a class of "consumer can't find the endpoint"
interop failures, which is the more common real-world failure mode in
federated PKI. Allowing consumers to pick one mechanism keeps the
implementation cost down for resource-constrained JWT consumers (e.g.,
embedded systems) while still guaranteeing that some revocation path
exists. The trade-off vs. Disposition A is that generator complexity
goes up — every issuer must operate or contract for both a CRL and an
OCSP service — but in practice modern CA software emits both by
default, so the marginal cost is low.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline to add new normative conformance requirements in this ballot
cycle. Instead, file a tracker note that the existing 7.1.6 revocation
text is "trust-community dependent" and rely on individual trust
communities (and their certificate policies / CPs) to specify the
revocation mechanisms their participants must support. Optionally add
a non-normative implementer note pointing readers at RFC 5280 §4.2.1.13
(CDP) and §4.2.2.1 (AIA/OCSP) for guidance.

#### Justification

X.509 revocation mechanics are already exhaustively specified by RFC
5280 and operationalized by every mainstream trust community
(DirectTrust, UDAP-aligned communities, eIDAS, FPKI, etc.). Re-stating
those requirements inside this IG risks divergence from those
underlying profiles and creates a maintenance burden as the PKI
community evolves (e.g., short-lived certificates that intentionally
omit CDP/AIA, or future post-quantum revocation schemes). The
declination would be defensible if the work group judges that any
trust community that fails to publish revocation endpoints is already
out of conformance with industry-standard CP/CPS practice and
therefore self-disqualifying.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

The ticket is well-scoped and clearly identifies a real conformance
gap: section 7.1.6 imposes a verification obligation that the rest of
the IG provides no machinery to satisfy, and the reporter has already
hit this in test tooling. Disposition C is hard to defend — pointing
at RFC 5280 doesn't tell an implementer which extensions are mandatory
in *this* IG, which is exactly the question that gets asked at
connectathons. Between A and B, Disposition B is the better starting
point because (a) it removes the most common real-world failure mode
(consumer can't find an endpoint because the generator omitted the
extension), (b) it directly answers the reporter's open question about
out-of-band distribution by allowing it as additive rather than
substitutive, and (c) it lowers the floor for consumer
implementations, which matters for the heterogeneous JWT-consuming
landscape this IG targets. The work group should expect pushback from
issuers on the "MUST include both CDP and AIA" line and should be
ready to relax it to "MUST include at least one, SHOULD include both"
if implementer feedback warrants — but starting from the stricter
position is the right anchor for the discussion.
