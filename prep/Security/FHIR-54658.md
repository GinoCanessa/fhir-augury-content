## Summary

In the Scalable Consent Management (FHIR) IG's **Definitions** section, the
current text explains "Consent" without qualifying which type of consent is in
scope. The reporter (John Moehrke) asks the authors to clarify that the IG
constrains Consent to **Privacy Consent**, arguing that explicitly using the
word "Privacy" in the definition will be immediately understood by readers and
will emphasize the narrow scope of the IG. The Jira cross-reference service
returned no linked tickets, so this is a standalone editorial request
specific to the Scalable Consent Management IG.

## Details

**Description:**

> In the Definitions section you explain Consent. I think it would be good to
> clarify that you are constraining Consent to "Privacy Consent". Use of the
> word Privacy will be understood, and will emphasize the point.

**Comments:**

No comments on the ticket.

**Resolution Description (Persuasive):**

> We will update the Consent definition in the Definitions section to
> explicitly clarify that this IG is focused on **Privacy Consent**,
> distinguishing it from research consent, treatment consent, or other
> consent types defined in the FHIR Consent resource. Adding the word
> "Privacy" in the definition sets appropriate expectations from the outset.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Edit the **Definitions** section of the Scalable Consent Management (FHIR) IG
so that the Consent entry explicitly identifies the scope as *Privacy
Consent*. Concretely, replace the current "Consent" term heading / lead
sentence with something along the lines of:

> **Consent (Privacy Consent):** Within this implementation guide, "Consent"
> refers specifically to *Privacy Consent* — a patient's (or authorised
> representative's) decision about the collection, use, and disclosure of
> their health information, as represented by the FHIR `Consent` resource
> with `category` indicating a privacy/IPA-style consent. Other FHIR Consent
> categories (e.g., research consent, treatment consent, advance directives)
> are out of scope for this IG.

No structural/profile changes, no new extensions, no cardinality changes —
this is a pure narrative edit confined to the Definitions page (and any
glossary entries or cross-page links that currently say "Consent" without
qualification where the distinction matters).

#### Justification

This is exactly what the Security WG already voted as **Persuasive**, and the
reporter's argument is solid: FHIR's `Consent` resource deliberately covers
multiple categories (privacy, research, treatment, advance directive), and
readers familiar with the base spec will otherwise assume the broader
meaning. Naming the scope "Privacy Consent" in the Definitions section
anchors the rest of the IG and prevents implementers from wiring up, say,
research-consent workflows against profiles that were never intended for
that purpose. The change is editorial, backwards compatible, low risk, and
does not require rebuilding profiles, examples, or terminology.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Accept the clarification but go slightly further than the literal request:
in addition to updating the Definitions entry, (1) add a short
**Scope** callout at the top of the IG home page stating that the guide
addresses Privacy Consent only, and (2) where the IG's Consent profile(s)
constrain `Consent.category`, make the narrative point back to the
Definitions entry so the term "Privacy Consent" is reinforced at the profile
level, not only in the glossary. If the IG publishes a `ValueSet` or fixed
coding for `category`, also cross-reference that binding from the
Definitions entry.

#### Justification

The reporter's concern is really about reader expectations, and those
expectations are formed on the landing page and in the profile narrative,
not only in Definitions. Making the clarification in three mutually
reinforcing places (home/Scope, Definitions, profile narrative) costs very
little and produces a much stronger signal for downstream implementers
and reviewers — especially reviewers coming from adjacent WGs (CBCC,
Research, EHR) who may otherwise assume the IG applies to their consent
type. The trade-off versus Disposition A is a slightly larger editorial
footprint, but still no normative change.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Close as *Not Persuasive*: keep the current Definitions wording on the
grounds that the IG's title ("Scalable Consent Management"), its scope
statement, and its Consent profile(s) already communicate the Privacy
Consent focus, and that adding the qualifier "Privacy" to the definition
is redundant.

#### Justification

A decline is defensible only if the authors believe the current title and
scope text already eliminate ambiguity, and that adding "Privacy" risks
under-specifying the IG later (for example, if the WG plans to expand into
adjacent consent categories). In that case, leaving the definition general
preserves future flexibility. This rationale is weak given the reporter is
a recognised Security WG expert who has explicitly stated the text is
unclear; the WG has already voted Persuasive, so this disposition is listed
for completeness rather than as a realistic option.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** A

The change is editorial, the WG has already resolved the ticket as
Persuasive, and the reporter is arguably the most experienced privacy/consent
voice in the Security WG — when John Moehrke says the Consent definition
needs the word "Privacy" to set reader expectations, that signal should be
taken at face value. Disposition A implements exactly the resolution already
agreed and closes the ticket with the minimum possible disruption. Disposition
B is a reasonable stretch goal but adds editing work beyond what the ticket
asks for and is better tracked as a follow-up editorial pass; Disposition C
is not credible given the existing Persuasive vote. Proceed with A and, if
the IG team has spare editorial bandwidth, fold in the home-page Scope
callout from B as a stretch.
