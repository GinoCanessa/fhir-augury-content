## Summary

John Moehrke reports that the *Definitions* section of the Scalable Consent
Management (FHIR) IG contains an awkward statement that the IG's
"Authorizations" are "not in scope". He argues the framing conflates two
distinct things: the *authorization decision* (correctly out of scope for
this IG) and the *consent information that informs that decision* (clearly
in scope). The request is to rewrite the scope statement so it explicitly
says the IG provides the consent details an authorization component would
consume, while the act of making the authorization decision itself remains
out of scope.

No related Jira tickets, Zulip threads, or GitHub items were surfaced by
cross-reference or text search for this ticket key.

## Details

**Description:**

> in the Definitions section the Authorizations statements of not in scope is very awkward. I think you mean to say that the Authorization decision is out-of-scope, but that what is in scope is informing that Authorization decision through the IG. I think it is important to explain this IG provides the Consent details that inform an authorization.

**Resolution Description (already recorded on the ticket):**

> We will revise the authorization scope statement in the Definitions section to replace the awkward "not in scope" framing with language that explains the IG's role more accurately: this IG standardizes the representation and communication of consent information so that authorization enforcement components — which are out of scope — can use it to make access control decisions. The distinction between "recording consent" (in scope) and "acting on consent" (out of scope) will be made explicit.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Edit the *Definitions* section of the Scalable Consent Management (FHIR)
IG to remove the wording that says "Authorizations … not in scope" and
replace it with a clarified scope statement that:

1. Defines **Authorization decision** as the act of granting or denying
   access to a resource based on policy and context, and explicitly
   marks that decision (and any policy enforcement / decision points
   such as a PDP/PEP) as **out of scope** for this IG.
2. States that what **is in scope** is the standardized representation
   and exchange of **Consent** information — patient/grantor directives,
   permitted purposes, actors, data scope, time bounds, etc. — in a form
   that an authorization decision component can consume.
3. Adds a one-line summary contrasting "recording consent" (in scope)
   with "acting on consent" (out of scope), as already captured in the
   ticket's resolution description.

This is a narrative / editorial change to the Definitions page; no
StructureDefinitions, value sets, profiles, or examples need to be
modified.

#### Justification

- The reporter is the Security WG co-chair / domain SME; the wording
  problem is unambiguous and the suggested fix matches how Security WG
  has historically scoped consent-related IGs (consent representation
  in scope, PDP/PEP behavior out of scope).
- The ticket is already marked **Persuasive** with a resolution
  description that mirrors this proposal, so accepting as requested is
  the path of least surprise for ballot reviewers.
- Editorial-only change: no risk to conformance, examples, or
  downstream implementers; trivially reversible if the WG dislikes the
  new phrasing.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Adopt the same intent as Disposition A, but instead of editing the
Definitions section in isolation, restructure the IG's scope material
so it lives in **two coordinated places**:

1. A short, normative **Scope** statement on the IG home page that
   names "in scope: representation and exchange of consent" and "out of
   scope: authorization decision / enforcement".
2. A **Definitions** section that defines the terms (Consent,
   Authorization decision, PDP, PEP, Grantor, Grantee, etc.) without
   re-stating scope, only cross-linking to the Scope section.

Optionally include a small diagram showing where Consent (in scope)
sits relative to a PDP/PEP (out of scope) in a typical access-control
flow.

#### Justification

- Definitions sections that mix glossary entries with scope assertions
  are exactly what produced the awkward wording the reporter flagged;
  separating the two reduces the chance of the same confusion
  re-emerging.
- A diagram + canonical Scope statement is consistent with how other
  HL7 IGs (e.g., SMART App Launch, Bulk Data) frame
  authorization-adjacent boundaries.
- Trade-off vs. A: more editorial work and more pages touched, and it
  goes beyond what the ticket literally requests, so it warrants WG
  sign-off rather than being a quiet editorial fix.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Close as **Not Persuasive — editorial only / no normative impact**,
leaving the existing Definitions wording in place and noting that
readers familiar with the consent-vs-authorization distinction can
already infer the intent.

#### Justification

- The change is purely narrative; no conformance behavior is affected.
- Declining keeps the ballot stable and avoids re-litigating wording
  that has presumably already been reviewed.
- This rationale is weak: the reporter is a Security WG SME and the
  current wording is genuinely confusing, so declining is hard to
  defend on its merits — included here for completeness.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** A

The ticket has already been triaged as **Persuasive** with a resolution
description that essentially restates Disposition A, and the change is
a low-risk editorial clarification in a single Definitions section.
Accepting as requested resolves a real source of reader confusion
(conflating "authorization decision" with "consent information") without
touching any conformance artefacts, examples, or terminology bindings.
Disposition B is attractive structurally but expands the scope of the
fix beyond what the reporter asked for and would need its own WG
discussion; if the WG wants to adopt B's restructuring, it should do so
as a follow-up ticket so this one can close cleanly. Disposition C is
listed only for completeness — declining a Security WG SME's editorial
clarification on the WG's own IG is not defensible here.
