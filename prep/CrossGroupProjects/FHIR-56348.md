## Summary

The reporter (Eric Haas) is requesting a small editorial rewrite of a sentence
in the US Core specification that describes the two mechanisms a client may
use to correct previously-submitted records. The current wording — "Clients
have two distinct mechanisms for corrections: entered-in-error for full
retractions (see 4.12) and replacement for supersessions (below)…" — is
considered awkward; the ticket proposes replacing it with plainer prose:
"Clients can correct records in two ways: by retracting entries using the
'entered-in-error' status (see 4.12) or by replacing them with updated
content (see below)…". No cross-referenced Jira tickets, Zulip threads, or
GitHub items were found for this issue, and there are no comments yet. The
change is purely narrative; it does not alter behavior, conformance, or any
artifact definitions.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Apply the wording change exactly as proposed in the US Core narrative
section that introduces the two correction mechanisms. Replace:

> Clients have two distinct mechanisms for corrections: entered-in-error
> for full retractions (see 4.12) and replacement for supersessions
> (below).

with:

> Clients can correct records in two ways: by retracting entries using
> the "entered-in-error" status (see 4.12) or by replacing them with
> updated content (see below).

This is a narrative-only edit in the US Core IG's authoring sources (the
relevant `.md`/`.xml` page that introduces the corrections section). No
conformance resources, profiles, value sets, or examples need to change,
and the cross-reference to section 4.12 is preserved verbatim.

#### Justification

The proposed sentence is shorter, uses plain verbs ("retract", "replace")
in place of nominalisations ("retractions", "supersessions"), and quotes
the literal status code (`entered-in-error`) so the link between prose
and the underlying value is unambiguous. It keeps the existing internal
references ("see 4.12", "see below") so cross-page anchors continue to
resolve. Since the change is editorial, it can ship at any point in the
publication cycle without affecting implementer conformance or downstream
test artifacts.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Accept the spirit of the request but tighten the wording further and
make the two mechanisms parallel in structure, e.g.:

> Clients correct records in one of two ways: retract an entry by setting
> its status to `entered-in-error` (see § 4.12), or replace it with an
> updated version (see below).

Additionally, while the editor is in this paragraph, verify that the
cross-references ("4.12", "below") still resolve to the intended
anchors after any recent section renumbering, and convert them to named
anchor links (rather than bare numbers) so that future renumbering
doesn't silently break them.

#### Justification

Eric's proposal is already a clear improvement; the alternative just
goes one step further by giving the two mechanisms strictly parallel
syntax ("retract … by setting", "replace … with") and by formatting
`entered-in-error` as code, which is the convention used elsewhere in
US Core when referring to specific status codes. Auditing the section
references at the same time avoids leaving a known-fragile link
("see 4.12") in place for the next editor to discover. The trade-off vs.
Disposition A is slightly more reviewer effort and a marginally larger
diff.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Close the ticket as "Not Persuasive" / "No Change". The current
sentence, while not maximally elegant, is technically accurate and
unambiguous: it names the two mechanisms, ties each to the relevant
status or section, and matches the surrounding prose style.

#### Justification

US Core has a substantial backlog of substantive change requests with
real implementer impact. Spending review time and a publication cycle
on a non-functional rewrite of a single sentence — especially one with
no community discussion attached (no Zulip thread, no related tickets,
no comments) — is hard to justify when the existing wording is not
incorrect or misleading. Declining preserves reviewer bandwidth for
changes that affect conformance.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** A

This is a low-risk, low-cost editorial improvement from a long-standing
US Core contributor. The proposed sentence is objectively easier to
read, preserves the existing anchor references, and touches only IG
narrative — no profiles, value sets, or examples are affected, so there
is no implementer impact and no balloting risk. Disposition B's extra
polish is reasonable but probably more than the workgroup needs to
debate for a one-sentence change; if an editor naturally adopts the
parallel-structure / code-formatting touches while applying A, that's a
fine outcome. Disposition C is defensible on triage-bandwidth grounds
but feels disproportionate for a clearly-improving edit that the
reporter has already drafted in full.
