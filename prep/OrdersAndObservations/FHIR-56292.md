## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Accept the report as a publication defect against FHIR Core. Triage
owner contacts MarcoWedekind to obtain the URL of the page where the
diagram fails to render and, if possible, a screenshot or the raw
filename being requested. Once identified, fix the source authoring
file under the HL7/fhir authoring tree so that the image renders in
the published build — typical fixes are: correcting a misspelled or
case-mismatched filename, restoring a missing source asset (PNG/SVG)
into the per-page image directory, or repairing the markup (e.g., an
`<img>`/`{% include img.html %}`/`![](…)` directive whose path no
longer resolves after a content reorganisation). Verify in a local
publish that the diagram renders for both the canonical and the
versioned publication paths, and add the correction to the next
technical-correction publication.

#### Justification

The ticket is filed as a Change Request at "Highest" priority against
a published spec page; the reporter is asserting that a normative or
explanatory diagram is simply not visible. Honouring the report
literally — finding and repairing the broken reference — is the
lowest-risk action: it does not change normative content, it restores
information the spec already intends to convey, and it removes a
visible defect that erodes implementer trust. O&O has historically
treated "missing diagram" tickets as publication-track fixes rather
than ballot-affecting changes.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Accept the underlying intent (the page should communicate the
information the diagram was meant to convey) but, in addition to (or
instead of) restoring the original image, replace the diagram with an
accessible alternative: a structured table or a short narrative
description of the relationships shown in the figure, with the
restored image provided as a supplementary, captioned figure. Apply
this in the HL7/fhir source for the affected O&O page and add `alt`
text to the image markup so future broken references degrade
gracefully.

#### Justification

A broken-image bug on a "Highest"-priority page is also evidence that
the page relies on an image as the sole carrier of information, which
is an accessibility and durability problem (screen readers, PDF
exports, low-bandwidth viewers, and future asset-path refactors all
silently lose the content). Replacing or supplementing the diagram
with a textual/tabular form means the page remains informative even
when the image pipeline fails again, and aligns with HL7's general
move toward more accessible spec content. The trade-off versus
Disposition A is more authoring effort and a slightly larger diff,
but the change is still scoped to a single page and carries no
normative impact.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline the ticket as filed, on the grounds that it is not actionable
in its current form: it does not identify the page, the diagram, the
image filename, or the publication build in which the failure was
observed. Close the ticket as "Not Reproducible / Insufficient
Information" with a request that the reporter re-file with (a) the
exact URL where the image is missing, (b) the build (current CI,
specific publication, or a named version), and (c) a screenshot or
the browser console's failed asset path.

#### Justification

The triage team cannot reliably locate "a diagram" on the FHIR Core
spec from a one-sentence description; guessing risks either no fix
(the wrong page is investigated) or a speculative change that does
not address the user's actual complaint. Declining-with-request is
defensible because the ticket has no comments, no cross-references,
no linked artifact, and no Zulip/GitHub corroboration to triangulate
which page is affected. This disposition costs the reporter a
follow-up but ensures the eventual fix targets the real defect.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** A

The ticket is plainly a publication defect against a "Highest"-priority
FHIR Core page in O&O's remit, and FhirAugury surfaces no
disagreement, alternative interpretation, or design discussion that
would justify a more invasive change. Disposition A keeps the fix
narrow, reversible, and on the publication track — exactly what a
broken-image report should produce — while still requiring triage to
contact MarcoWedekind to pin down the exact page (which is the only
gap Disposition C is genuinely worried about). Disposition B is
attractive for accessibility reasons and worth folding in
opportunistically when the affected page is identified, but it should
not block the immediate fix. Disposition C is too aggressive given
the reporter's clear intent and the "Highest" priority; the
information gap is better closed by a triage email than by a closed
ticket.
