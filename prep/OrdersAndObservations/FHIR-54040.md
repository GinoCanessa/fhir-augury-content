## Summary

The reporter notes that the "Grouping" section of the FHIR R6 (6.0.0-ballot4)
Observation resource page mentions the core extensions
[`observation-sequelTo`](http://hl7.org/fhir/StructureDefinition/observation-sequelTo)
and
[`observation-replaces`](http://hl7.org/fhir/StructureDefinition/observation-replaces)
in its opening sentence, but provides no further explanation of when those
extensions should be used. From the reporter's perspective, these two
extensions don't fit the "grouping" framing of the section at all — they
express *relationships between Observations* (much like the in-resource
element `Observation.triggeredBy`) rather than aggregation/grouping.

The ticket asks Orders & Observations to either (a) remove the references to
`observation-sequelTo` and `observation-replaces` from the grouping section,
or (b) keep them and add prose / examples that explain when each extension
applies and how it relates to `Observation.triggeredBy`.

There are no cross-referenced Jira tickets, Zulip threads, or GitHub items
for this ticket in the augury index, so the request stands on its own.

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested — Add explanatory prose / examples

#### Proposal

Keep the references to `observation-sequelTo` and `observation-replaces`
in the Observation resource's notes/grouping section, but expand the
surrounding prose so each extension is actually explained:

- Define `observation-sequelTo` as "a relationship asserting that *this*
  Observation is a continuation of (a sequel to) a prior Observation",
  and give a short example (e.g., a follow-up vital-sign reading whose
  result supersedes context but does not replace the prior reading).
- Define `observation-replaces` as "a relationship asserting that *this*
  Observation supersedes a prior Observation that should no longer be
  used", with a short example (e.g., a corrected lab result issued
  after a transcription error).
- Add a brief contrast paragraph that distinguishes both extensions from
  `Observation.triggeredBy` (which captures *why* this Observation was
  produced, not what it supersedes or follows), and clarifies that none
  of the three are "grouping" in the aggregation sense — they are
  inter-Observation relationships that often appear alongside grouping
  use cases.
- Optionally split the section header so the grouping discussion (panels,
  hasMember, derivedFrom) is visibly distinct from the
  "relationships to other Observations" discussion.

#### Justification

This addresses the reporter's actual complaint — the extensions are
named without context, leaving implementers to guess. Keeping the
references and explaining them preserves discoverability of the core
extensions (which is one of the reasons they are mentioned in the base
resource notes at all) while removing the confusion that motivated the
ticket. It is also consistent with the pattern used elsewhere in the
spec where notable core extensions are called out from the base
resource's narrative with at least a one-line definition.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach — Move the references to a "Relationships to other Observations" subsection

#### Proposal

Rather than adding prose inside the grouping section, restructure the
Observation notes page so that:

1. The current "Grouping" section is narrowed to its true scope —
   `Observation.hasMember`, `Observation.derivedFrom`, and panel/battery
   patterns — and no longer mentions `observation-sequelTo` or
   `observation-replaces`.
2. A new sibling subsection (e.g., "Relationships to other
   Observations") is added that explicitly covers
   `Observation.triggeredBy` (in-resource), and the
   `observation-sequelTo` and `observation-replaces` core extensions,
   with the same definitions and examples described in Disposition A.

This is a documentation-only, non-normative restructure of the
Observation notes page; the resource definition, extensions, and
examples themselves are unchanged.

#### Justification

The reporter's underlying observation is structural: sequelTo and
replaces are not grouping mechanisms, and forcing their explanation into
the grouping section perpetuates that mismatch. A dedicated
"relationships" subsection lines up `triggeredBy` (which is already in
the resource) with the two core extensions in one place, which is where
implementers would naturally look. It avoids the awkwardness of
explaining "these are mentioned in the grouping section but aren't
really grouping" inside the grouping section itself. The cost is a
slightly larger documentation diff than Disposition A and the need to
adjust any anchors that currently link into the grouping section.

---

### Disposition C: Decline — Remove the references entirely

#### Proposal

Persuasive: remove the mentions of `observation-sequelTo` and
`observation-replaces` from the Observation notes/grouping section and
do not add a replacement subsection. Implementers who need these
extensions can find them via the registered core extension catalogue,
and `Observation.triggeredBy` already covers the in-resource
relationship case.

#### Justification

If the working group judges that the Observation notes are already
overgrown and that the two extensions are sufficiently low-traffic to
not warrant prose in the base resource page, the simplest fix is to
delete the offending sentence. This matches one of the literal options
in the ticket title ("remove reference to the core-extension in this
section"). The argument against is loss of discoverability: the core
extension catalogue is large, and many implementers learn about
notable extensions specifically through call-outs in the base resource
notes.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

Disposition B addresses the reporter's structural complaint cleanly:
sequelTo and replaces stop being mis-filed under "grouping", and they
get a real home alongside `Observation.triggeredBy`, which is the only
place an implementer can compare the in-resource and extension-based
mechanisms side by side. It preserves discoverability of the core
extensions (the main argument against Disposition C) while also
providing the explanatory prose that Disposition A would supply (the
main argument for Disposition A) — with the additional benefit that the
grouping section itself becomes more focused. The cost is a modestly
larger documentation diff than Disposition A, which is acceptable for a
non-normative narrative change.

Disposition A is a reasonable fallback if the work group prefers a
minimal-diff solution for this ballot cycle. Disposition C should only
be chosen if there is an explicit decision that these two core
extensions do not warrant any narrative call-out from the Observation
resource page.
