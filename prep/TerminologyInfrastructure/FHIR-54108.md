## Summary

FHIR-54108 is a Terminology Infrastructure change request, filed by Carmela
A. Couderc, that asks the workgroup to align and disambiguate the parameter
descriptions used across the terminology operations defined in FHIR Core.
The request is anchored on an attached spreadsheet (not retrievable through
the augury CLI snapshot) that catalogues current parameter descriptions,
proposes revised wording, and raises open questions about inconsistent use
of the terms "concept code", "concept", and "code" across operations.
Because the scope and number of parameters span multiple operations, the
TI co-chairs explicitly suggest forming a task force to work through the
spreadsheet and to extend the review to related operations such as
`$lookup` that share similar parameter shapes and ambiguities. No Jira
links, GitHub PRs/issues, or other Zulip threads are cross-referenced; the
only related discussion is the TI Tracker agenda message that schedules

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Adopt the spreadsheet's proposed parameter descriptions as a coordinated
edit to the terminology `OperationDefinition` resources in FHIR Core —
specifically `CodeSystem-validate-code`, `CodeSystem-lookup`,
`CodeSystem-subsumes`, `ValueSet-expand`, `ValueSet-validate-code`,
`ConceptMap-translate`, and `ConceptMap-closure` — replacing each
parameter's `documentation` element with the harmonised wording from the
spreadsheet. As part of the same edit, normalise the use of the terms
"code", "concept", and "concept code" across these OperationDefinitions
so that each term has a single, consistently-applied meaning, and apply
the same harmonisation to `$lookup` and any other terminology operation
the task force identifies as sharing the affected parameters. Stand up
the task force the co-chairs propose to drive the spreadsheet to closure
and to triage the open questions it raises before the edits land.

#### Justification

The request is editorial-and-clarifying rather than a behavioural change:
it tightens prose on existing parameters without altering their
cardinality, type, or runtime semantics, which keeps the change
backward-compatible for implementers while measurably improving
specification clarity. Inconsistent use of "code" vs "concept" vs
"concept code" across `$validate-code`, `$lookup`, `$expand`, and
`$translate` is a long-standing source of implementer confusion, so a
coordinated pass — exactly what the spreadsheet enables — is materially
more valuable than fixing one operation at a time. Standing up the task
force aligns with the TI co-chairs' own recommendation in the ticket and
matches how the Tracker agenda already frames the work (a "targeted
group review and resolution proposal").

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Split the ticket into two work items: (1) a narrowly-scoped editorial
ticket (or set of child tickets, one per operation) that adopts only
those rows of the spreadsheet where the proposed wording is already
uncontested, and (2) a separate Terminology-Infrastructure task-force
deliverable that resolves the spreadsheet's open questions and the
cross-operation terminology choices ("code" vs "concept" vs "concept
code") before any wording that depends on those choices is merged. The
task force would publish a short style-guide note in the Terminology
module documentation defining each of those three terms, and that note
would then drive a second wave of OperationDefinition edits.

#### Justification

The ticket bundles two qualitatively different changes — uncontroversial
prose cleanups and unresolved terminology-policy questions — and
batching them blocks the easy wins behind the harder debate. Splitting
lets TI ship the low-risk descriptions in the next normative window
while giving the task force room to converge on terminology semantics
without ballot pressure. Publishing a small style-guide note before the
second wave also gives reviewers and implementers a single anchor to
cite, which reduces the chance that the same inconsistency reappears in
future operations or in downstream IGs.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline the ticket on the grounds that the substantive change content
lives in an external spreadsheet rather than in the Jira ticket itself,
and ask the reporter to refile once concrete, ticket-resolvable
proposals (specific OperationDefinitions, specific parameter names,
specific replacement text) have been distilled out of the spreadsheet
into Jira — preferably as one ticket per operation or one ticket per
terminology choice.

#### Justification

As filed, the ticket is effectively a request to convene a task force
rather than a reviewable change proposal: there is no in-ticket
description of which parameters change, no proposed text in the Jira
record, and the spreadsheet is not part of the ticket snapshot
available through tooling. That makes it impossible for the workgroup
to vote a clear disposition or for downstream tooling and reviewers to
trace what actually changed. Declining (with a constructive ask to
refile) protects the integrity of the change-tracking process without
losing the underlying work, which can resume immediately under properly
scoped tickets.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

Disposition B preserves the ticket's intent and the co-chairs'
task-force recommendation while acknowledging the operational reality
that the spreadsheet mixes settled editorial fixes with unresolved
terminology-policy questions. Accepting the whole package as one change
(Disposition A) risks stalling the easy improvements behind the harder
"code vs concept vs concept code" debate, and a single bulk edit makes
it harder for reviewers and implementers to trace what changed and why.
Declining (Disposition C) throws away momentum the co-chairs and
reporter have already built and is hard to justify when the work is
clearly in scope for TI. Splitting the work — ship the uncontested
wording now, let the task force land a short terminology style note,
then do a second wave of OperationDefinition edits driven by that note
— is the path most likely to produce durable, internally consistent
specification text without holding up R6 normative readiness on
operations whose descriptions are already obviously broken.
