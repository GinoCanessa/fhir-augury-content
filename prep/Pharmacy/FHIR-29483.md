## Summary

Josh Mandel suggests broadening the scope language in the US Prescription Drug
Monitoring Program (PDMP) FHIR Implementation Guide. The IG currently scopes
itself to accessing a patient's controlled-substance prescription history from
"the State PDMP systems." The reporter proposes rewording so the scope
describes accessing data from *any* PDMP system, rather than tying the IG's
applicability to the current State-operated infrastructure. The intent is to
keep the IG durable as PDMP infrastructure evolves (e.g., regional, federal,
or commercial aggregators), without requiring a scope change every time the
underlying operational model shifts.

No cross-referenced Jira tickets, Zulip threads, or GitHub items were
returned for this ticket by the augury index, so the request stands on its
own narrative scope rewording.

## Details

**Description:**

> The US Meds PDMP FHIR IG defines how an EHR or an App or other Clinical
> Systems can access a Patient's controlled substance prescription history
> from the State PDMP systems

It might be better to describe the scope here as accessing data from any
pdmp system, rather than tying it to State specific infrastructure. Over
time perhaps different infrastructure will evolve but this implementation
guide may still apply.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Reword the IG's Scope narrative on the PDMP IG home/scope page so the
opening sentence reads, in substance:

> The US PDMP FHIR IG defines how an EHR, App, or other clinical system
> can access a patient's controlled substance prescription history from a
> PDMP system. Today, PDMP systems are predominantly operated by states,
> but this IG is intended to apply to any PDMP system, regardless of
> whether it is operated at the state, regional, federal, or private-sector
> level.

Apply the same wording shift consistently anywhere the narrative says
"the State PDMP systems" / "State PDMP" in a defining (rather than
descriptive) sense — e.g., the abstract, scope, and "How to Read this
Guide" pages. Leave references intact where the text is genuinely
describing today's State-operated reality (e.g., regulatory background).

#### Justification

This is a narrative-only change with no impact on conformance resources,
profiles, value sets, or capability statements. It directly addresses the
reporter's concern: PDMP infrastructure is evolving (PMP InterConnect,
RxCheck, and emerging federated models already span beyond a single
state), and the technical artifacts in the IG (FHIR queries against a
PDMP endpoint) are not state-specific. Decoupling the scope from a
particular operational model is consistent with FHIR's general practice
of describing capabilities independently of who operates the endpoint.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Keep the existing "State PDMP" wording in the headline scope sentence as
the *primary* described use case, but add an explicit "Applicability"
subsection (or a paragraph in Scope) that clarifies the IG also applies
to any non-State PDMP system that exposes the same conformance surface.
Add a glossary entry defining "PDMP system" generically and cross-link to
it from the abstract. Optionally introduce an "Out of Scope" bullet that
lists what is *not* in scope (e.g., the PDMP–PDMP back-end exchange,
state-specific authorization workflows) so the broader applicability
doesn't dilute the IG's testable boundaries.

#### Justification

This preserves the historical framing that grounded the project (the
sponsoring use case really is State PDMPs) while still answering the
reporter's durability concern. It is lower-risk for stakeholders who
care that the IG is recognizable as the "State PDMP" guide they
chartered, and it reduces the chance that a wider scope statement gets
read as a commitment the workgroup hasn't validated against non-State
systems. It also gives the workgroup a natural place to enumerate
assumptions that today implicitly come from the State-operated model
(authorization, audit, data retention, etc.).

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Close as *Not Persuasive*. Retain the current "State PDMP systems"
scope wording on the basis that the IG's project charter (PSS-2225) and
sponsoring stakeholders are explicitly the State-operated PDMP
community, and the IG's normative content (security, consent, audit
expectations) is calibrated to that operational model. Non-State PDMP
systems can reuse the IG informally, but expanding the scope statement
without re-validating those assumptions across other operators would
overpromise.

#### Justification

The technical conformance surface may look infrastructure-neutral, but
PDMP IGs typically embed assumptions about authority to query, audit
obligations, and disclosure restrictions that derive from State law and
inter-state compacts. Broadening the scope statement risks implying the
IG has been validated against operational models it hasn't been tested
against. A targeted scope change should follow, not precede, that
validation work — and in the meantime the existing wording is accurate.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

The reporter's concern is legitimate: the technical content of the IG
really is independent of who runs the PDMP, and the IG was already
withdrawn once and reopened, so language that ages well is valuable.
However, the workgroup chartered this IG against the State PDMP
ecosystem (per the PSS-2225 reopening referenced in the comments), and
there are real assumptions (authority to query, audit, consent, inter-
state data sharing) that today derive from the State-operated model. A
clean rewrite to "any PDMP system" (Disposition A) under-acknowledges
that. A flat decline (Disposition C) ignores a low-cost narrative fix
that the reporter is correctly identifying.

Disposition B threads the needle: keep the State PDMP framing as the
primary, validated use case; add an explicit Applicability paragraph and
glossary entry that names the generic "PDMP system" concept; and
optionally add an Out-of-Scope bullet so the broader applicability is
bounded. This addresses the reporter's durability concern without
committing the workgroup to conformance claims it hasn't tested.
