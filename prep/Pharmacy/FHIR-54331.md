## Summary

The reporter notes that the FHIR R6 ballot (6.0.0-ballot4) Dosage Examples page
mixes two distinct `Timing.repeat` mechanisms — `Timing.repeat.count` and
`Timing.repeat.boundsDuration` — to express the *intended overall duration* of a
`MedicationRequest.dosageInstruction.step.component`. In Example 3 (section
14.7.1.4), phrases such as "for 5 days", "for 10 days", and "for 3 months"
are sometimes encoded with `Timing.repeat.count` and other times with
`Timing.repeat.boundsDuration`. Per the `Timing` element definition,
`repeat.count` denotes a *number of repetitions* ("repeat 5 times"), not a
*duration*, so its use to mean "for 5 days" is semantically incorrect and
inconsistent within the same example. The request is to rewrite the affected
dosage examples so that the intended duration is consistently expressed via
`Timing.repeat.boundsDuration`, removing the misuse of `Timing.repeat.count`.
The reporter has supplied a full XML rewrite of Example 3 that uses
`boundsDuration` throughout. There are no cross-referenced Jira tickets, Zulip
threads, or GitHub items associated with this ticket in the indexed sources.

## Details

**Description:**

The given Dosage Regime examples in
<https://hl7.org/fhir/6.0.0-ballot4/dosage-examples.html> use both
`Timing.repeat.count` and `Timing.repeat.boundsDuration` to express the
intended time duration of a `DosageInstruction.step.component`.

In example 3 (14.7.1.4) the elements `Timing.repeat.count` and
`Timing.repeat.boundsDuration` are used interchangeably to express
"for 5 days", "for 10 days" and "for 3 months". In `Timing`, the
element `Timing.repeat.count` is described as the number of times to repeat,
which expresses "repeat 5 times" or "repeat 10 times" and not "for 5 days"
or "for 10 days".

Suggestion is to only use `Timing.repeat.boundsDuration` to express the
intended time duration and remove `Timing.repeat.count` from the examples and
only use `Timing.repeat.boundsDuration` instead.

The reporter provides a corrected XML for 14.7.1.4 Example 3 (Prednisone
taper, 7 steps plus a `safety.doseLimit`) in which every step's
`Timing.repeat` block uses `boundsDuration` (e.g., `value=5 unit=days code=d`,
`value=10 unit=days code=d`, `value=3 unit=months code=mo`) together with
`period`/`periodUnit`/`frequency`/`timeOfDay` to describe the intra-day
schedule, and `Timing.repeat.count` no longer appears.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Persuasive. Edit the FHIR Core narrative page `dosage-examples.html`
(rendered at <https://hl7.org/fhir/6.0.0-ballot4/dosage-examples.html>,
section 14.7.1.4 — Example 3, the Prednisone taper) so that every
`MedicationRequest.dosageInstruction.step.component.timing.repeat` block
expresses the intended *overall* duration of that step via
`Timing.repeat.boundsDuration` and removes any use of
`Timing.repeat.count` that is currently being used to mean "for N
days/months". Adopt the corrected XML supplied by the reporter as the
replacement for Example 3, where each step uses `boundsDuration` (with
UCUM `d`/`mo`) plus `frequency`/`period`/`periodUnit`/`timeOfDay` to
describe the intra-day cadence. While editing, sweep the rest of the
page (and the matching JSON/Turtle renderings of the same example, plus
any other examples on the page that exhibit the same pattern) to apply
the same fix so the page is internally consistent.

#### Justification

`Timing.repeat.count` is defined in the `Timing` element as the number
of times the event is to occur, not as a duration. Using it to encode
"for 5 days" therefore mis-states the data: a downstream system that
honours the definition would interpret `count=5` together with
`period=1, periodUnit=d, frequency=2, timeOfDay=08:00,20:00` as "5 doses
total" (i.e., 2.5 days), not "twice daily for 5 days". `boundsDuration`
is the element specifically designed to bound a repeating schedule by
elapsed time, which is exactly what the narrative ("for 5 days", "for
3 months") requires. Normative published examples are heavily copied by
implementers; leaving an internally inconsistent and semantically wrong
example in the spec is a high-impact authoring defect, not a stylistic
preference. The reporter has done the work of producing a corrected
example, lowering the cost of acceptance to a narrative edit on a single
ballot page with no schema, value-set, or invariant change required.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Persuasive with modification. Accept the underlying defect (the example
must not use `Timing.repeat.count` to mean "for N days") but, instead of
collapsing every step onto `boundsDuration` alone, rewrite the example
so that each step explicitly demonstrates *both* a "bounded by elapsed
time" pattern (using `boundsDuration`) **and** a "bounded by number of
administrations" pattern (using `count` together with `frequency` /
`period` / `periodUnit`). Add a short prose paragraph at the top of
section 14.7.1.4 that contrasts the two patterns and explains when each
is appropriate. The Prednisone taper itself would still adopt the
reporter's `boundsDuration` rewrite (because the clinical intent is
duration-based), but a parallel sub-example would show, e.g., "30
tablets, take twice daily" expressed correctly with `count=30,
frequency=2, period=1, periodUnit=d`.

#### Justification

The root cause of this ticket is not just one bad example — it is
reader confusion about the distinction between `count` (repetitions)
and `boundsDuration` (elapsed time). Fixing only the symptom (Disposition
A) leaves the spec without an authoritative worked example of the
*correct* use of `Timing.repeat.count`, which makes the same misuse
likely to recur in IGs and downstream examples. Adding a short
contrasting sub-example with a paragraph of guidance turns the
correction into a teaching moment and reduces the chance of a parallel
ticket landing against `MedicationDispense`, `MedicationStatement`,
`ServiceRequest`, or `ActivityDefinition` examples that share the same
`Timing` datatype. The trade-off is a slightly larger narrative edit and
an editorial review of the new prose, but no schema or invariant impact
beyond Disposition A.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Not Persuasive. Close the ticket without changing the published
example, on the grounds that `dosage-examples.html` is illustrative
narrative rather than normative content and that the meaning the
reporter expects ("for 5 days") can in practice be reverse-engineered
from the surrounding `period`/`periodUnit`/`timeOfDay` context.
Optionally add an editorial note acknowledging the ambiguity but defer
any rewrite to a future release.

#### Justification

This disposition would only be defensible if (a) the example were
genuinely normative-free *and* unlikely to be copied, and (b) there were
no authoring capacity to make the fix in the current ballot cycle.
Neither is true here: dosage examples are among the most heavily copied
artefacts in the spec, the reporter has already supplied the corrected
XML, and the change is confined to a single narrative page. The defect
is also a clear semantic error against the published `Timing` element
definition, not a matter of taste. Declining would leave a known wrong
example in a normative resource's documentation, which is a poor signal
to implementers and to other work groups maintaining derived examples.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

Disposition A is the minimum viable fix and is unambiguously correct.
However, the cost delta from A to B is small — a paragraph of prose and
one extra worked sub-example — while the educational value is large,
because the underlying confusion (`count` vs. `boundsDuration`) is a
recurring source of bad data in the wild and is not adequately
illustrated anywhere else in the dosage examples. Disposition B keeps
the reporter's proposed Prednisone-taper rewrite intact (so the Pharmacy
work group is not relitigating that example) and additionally gives the
spec a positive demonstration of the *correct* use of
`Timing.repeat.count`, which Disposition A does not. Disposition C
should be rejected: there is no defensible reason to leave a known
semantically wrong, internally inconsistent example in the published
ballot when a corrected version has already been drafted by the reporter.
