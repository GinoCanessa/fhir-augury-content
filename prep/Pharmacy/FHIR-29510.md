## Summary

The ticket asks the Pharmacy work group to amend the PDMP Requestor actor
definition in the US Prescription Drug Monitoring Program (PDMP) FHIR
Implementation Guide so that it explicitly states that non-clinical
consumers — specifically law enforcement and employers — are out of scope
of the guide. The current wording lists only clinical-care examples
(EHRs, SMART on FHIR Apps, other Clinical Systems used for care
delivery) but does not affirmatively exclude non-care use cases, leaving
the scope ambiguous. The reporter proposes appending a single sentence
to the existing PDMP Requestor description that names law enforcement
and employers as out-of-scope examples.

No related Jira tickets, Zulip threads, or GitHub items were surfaced
through the cross-reference index for this ticket.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Adopt the reporter's wording verbatim. Update the PDMP Requestor actor
definition page in the US PDMP FHIR Implementation Guide so it reads:

> PDMP Requestor: PDMP Requestor is a health IT system that is
> requesting a Patient's controlled substance history. Real-world
> examples of such health IT systems include EHRs, SMART on FHIR Apps
> and other Clinical Systems used for care delivery. **Other possible
> users, such as law enforcement and employers, are out of scope of
> this guide.**

The change is narrative-only: a single appended sentence in the actor
description; no conformance resources, profiles, value sets, or
operations are affected.

#### Justification

The proposed wording closes a real ambiguity in the existing
description. The current list of "EHRs, SMART on FHIR Apps and other
Clinical Systems used for care delivery" is only illustrative and could
be read as non-exhaustive, leaving room for non-care actors (law
enforcement, employers, insurers acting outside care coordination) to
claim conformance. PDMP data is among the most sensitive controlled
information exchanged in US healthcare, and explicit out-of-scope
language is consistent with how other US Realm IGs (e.g., the security
sections of US Core and Da Vinci IGs) handle sensitive-data scope
boundaries. Because the change is purely textual and matches what the
reporter requested, it is the lowest-risk, highest-clarity option.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Accept the intent but broaden and relocate the scope statement so it is
not limited to two examples and is harder to miss. Concretely:

1. Keep the PDMP Requestor actor description focused on positive scope
   (who the IG *does* support) and add a short cross-reference such as
   "See *Scope and Out-of-Scope Uses* for restrictions on non-care
   requestors."
2. Add (or extend) a top-level **Scope** / **Out of Scope** section in
   the IG narrative that states:
   > This guide supports requests for a patient's controlled substance
   > history made by health IT systems used for clinical care delivery.
   > Requests by non-care actors — including but not limited to law
   > enforcement, employers, insurers acting outside of treatment, and
   > legal discovery — are out of scope. Conformance to this guide does
   > not authorize, define, or constrain such uses.
3. Optionally add a Security and Privacy considerations bullet pointing
   back to the same statement.

#### Justification

A single embedded sentence in one actor description is easy for
implementers and reviewers to overlook, and the reporter's two examples
("law enforcement and employers") are not exhaustive — insurers
operating outside treatment, legal discovery, and research uses raise
the same scoping question. Centralising the out-of-scope language in a
dedicated Scope section, and linking the actor description to it,
matches the editorial pattern used by most HL7 IGs and produces a
single canonical place to update if the workgroup later wants to add
qualifications. The trade-off versus Disposition A is slightly more
editorial work and a small structural change to the IG narrative, in
exchange for a clearer and more durable scope boundary.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Mark the ticket as "Not Persuasive" on the basis that the existing
wording, combined with the IG's general purpose statement and US
regulatory context for PDMP data, already implies that non-care actors
are out of scope, and that listing specific excluded actors risks being
both incomplete and misleading.

#### Justification

PDMP access in the United States is gated by state law and program
policy, not by FHIR conformance, so what the IG enumerates as "out of
scope" has no normative effect on whether law enforcement or employers
can obtain PDMP data through other channels. Naming only two excluded
actor classes could be read by implementers as implicitly permitting
others (e.g., insurers, researchers, legal discovery) that the
workgroup also did not intend to support. A decline is defensible if
the workgroup judges that the current "used for care delivery" phrase
is already sufficient, or if it prefers to handle scope qualifications
through a future, more comprehensive Security and Privacy section
rather than a one-line patch.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

Disposition A solves the immediate ambiguity but bakes a non-exhaustive
example list into a single actor definition, which is exactly the kind
of placement that makes scope statements easy to miss and hard to
extend. Disposition C understates a legitimate concern — the current
wording really is ambiguous, and PDMP is sensitive enough that the IG
should be unambiguous about who it supports. Disposition B keeps the
spirit of the reporter's request, places the out-of-scope language
where reviewers and implementers actually look for scope, and gives
the Pharmacy work group a single canonical hook to refine later (for
insurers, research, etc.) without re-editing the actor definition.
Given that the IG is being revived under PSS-2225, this is also a good
moment to invest in the slightly larger structural fix rather than
patch the actor description in isolation.
