## Summary

FHIR-45272 ("Linking Parent and Child Identifiers") is a Patient
Administration change request filed against the *Interoperable Digital
Identity and Patient Matching (FHIR)* (IDPM) specification. The ticket
title indicates a request for guidance or a normative mechanism for
expressing the link between a parent's identifier and a child's
identifier — a recurring need in patient-matching, registry, birth /
maternity, and pediatric scenarios. The Augury-cached snapshot of the
ticket carries no description body and no comments, and there are no
cross-referenced Jira tickets, GitHub items, or Zulip threads recorded
against the key. The ticket has already been triaged with a resolution
of **Considered for Future Use**, meaning the workgroup acknowledged
the underlying need but did not adopt a specific change against the
current IDPM publication. This review treats the disposition as
provisional and proposes options for a future revisit, anchored by the
historical Zulip discussion that explored the same problem space.

## Related Zulip Discussions

### [implementers > Parent/child patient links](https://chat.fhir.org/#narrow/stream/implementers/topic/Parent%2Fchild%20patient%20links/near/154016707)

This 2018–2019 implementers thread is the closest historical
discussion of the problem this ticket targets, even though it predates
the ticket and is not formally cross-referenced. James Agnew opened
the topic asking how to link two `Patient` resources in a
parent/child relationship, observing that `RelatedPerson` can point to
only one Patient. Several mechanisms were floated and weighed:
`FamilyMemberHistory` (clinical history, not administrative linkage),
`RelatedPerson` (treated as the canonical actor mechanism, but
single-patient-owned), the `patient-relatedperson` extension,
`Person.link` (cross-record linking with explicit privacy caveats),
and the HL7 AU child-health IG's `mother-relatedperson` /
`mother-patient` profiles. Lloyd McKenzie consistently positioned
`Person.link` (with its security/privacy implications managed) and a
`RelatedPerson`-based actor model as the existing answers, while
Thomas Tveit Rosenlund argued — based on the Norwegian central
citizen registry — that a more general `Person`-to-`Person`
relationship would better fit MPI use cases and pushed back on
requiring extensions for "the most basic use cases of a Person master
index." Brian Postlethwaite pointed at logical linking via shared
identifiers in `Person`/`Patient` as a privacy-preserving
alternative. The thread did not reach a formal consensus and ended
without a normative spec change.

*Participants:* James Agnew, Joel Schneider, Yunwei Wang, Lloyd
McKenzie, Kevin Power, René Spronk, Brett Esler, Thomas Tveit
Rosenlund, John Moehrke, Brian Postlethwaite, Jens Villadsen.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Add normative guidance to the *Interoperable Digital Identity and
Patient Matching* IG describing how to express a parent-to-child (or
more general kin) linkage at the **identifier** level, as the title of
the ticket asks. Concretely, introduce a small extension (e.g.,
`patient-linkedKin` or similarly named) carried on `Patient` that
takes:

- a `relationship` `CodeableConcept` bound to (or extending)
  `v3-RoleCode` family-relationship codes (`MTH`, `FTH`, `CHILD`, …),
  and
- a typed `identifier` value (for the related party's identifier, so
  the link can be expressed *administratively* without requiring a
  resolvable `Patient` reference — which is the IDPM use case).

Pair this with a short narrative section in the IDPM IG that:

1. shows how to populate the extension on a child `Patient` to point
   at the parent's identifier (and vice-versa), and
2. distinguishes this *identifier-level* administrative linkage from
   `RelatedPerson` (actor) and `Person.link` (cross-role identity
   linkage).

#### Justification

Identifier-level linkage is what patient-matching pipelines actually
consume in birth, immigration, and registry workflows: the parent's
`Patient` resource may not be retrievable by the receiver, but a
strongly-typed kin identifier supports deterministic matching. Having
this in IDPM (rather than in core `Patient`) keeps the broadly
normative `Patient` resource untouched while serving the specific
matching audience the IG is written for. The Zulip thread above shows
this is exactly the gap implementers complain about — `RelatedPerson`
forces an actor model that they do not need, and `Person.link` is too
heavy / too privacy-loaded for a national-registry-style use case.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Do not add a new mechanism. Instead, publish IDPM guidance that
directs implementers to use the **existing** machinery, layered as
follows:

- For the *administrative* identifier-only case the ticket targets:
  use `Patient.identifier` with a kin-typed identifier slice (e.g.,
  `type` of `MTHID` / `FTHID` from a designated codesystem), profiled
  by the IDPM IG. No extension is required.
- For the *actor* case (the parent acts on behalf of the child): use
  `RelatedPerson` with the existing `patient-relatedperson` linkage
  back to the parent's `Patient`.
- For the *cross-record identity* case (one human is both Patient and
  RelatedPerson, or the same human in two Patient records): use
  `Person.link`, with the privacy controls Lloyd McKenzie called out
  in the implementers thread.

Provide a small decision matrix in the IDPM IG that maps
parent/child use cases to one of those three options, plus
worked examples.

#### Justification

`Patient.identifier`, `RelatedPerson`, and `Person.link` already
cover, between them, the three distinct shapes of "parent/child
linkage" that surface in the FHIR community. Most of the friction is
that implementers cannot tell from the IG which one is appropriate
for their use case — not that the underlying primitives are missing.
A guidance-only disposition avoids new normative surface area, avoids
introducing a competing mechanism that downstream profiles would have
to choose between, and is much cheaper to ballot. It also aligns with
the FHIR design principle of preferring guidance over new elements
when the problem is "users picked the wrong tool."

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline the request as out of scope for the IDPM specification at
this time, and close confirming the existing **Considered for Future
Use** resolution. Document the rationale on the ticket: parent/child
identifier linkage cuts across base `Patient`, `RelatedPerson`, and
`Person`, and any normative guidance must be authored against base
FHIR by Patient Administration before IDPM can layer matching
guidance on top.

#### Justification

The ticket is unspecified (no description, no comments, no linked
work items) and the closest community discussion is six years old
without convergence. Adopting a mechanism inside IDPM in isolation
risks creating a second, IDPM-specific way to express family linkage
that conflicts with whatever Patient Administration eventually
chooses for base FHIR. Declining here — while leaving the underlying
need flagged for future consideration — preserves consistency. The
Jira resolution is already *Considered for Future Use*, so this
disposition simply formalizes that.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

Disposition B (guidance using existing mechanisms) is the best
starting point for the workgroup. The historical Zulip thread shows
the implementer pain is real but consistently centers on *not knowing
which existing primitive to use*, not on a missing primitive. Adding a
new extension (Disposition A) creates an additional authoring path
that downstream profiles will have to reconcile, and it does so
without strong evidence — the ticket itself contains no description,
no use cases, and no cross-references that would justify net-new
normative surface. Declining outright (Disposition C) leaves the same
implementers in the same fog and does nothing with the IDPM IG, which
is precisely the venue best placed to publish a parent/child
identifier-linkage decision matrix grounded in matching use cases.
Disposition B can be drafted as IG narrative plus one or two example
`Patient` / `RelatedPerson` / `Person` instances — low ballot risk,
high implementer value — and it leaves the door open to revisit
Disposition A later if profiling experience shows that
`Patient.identifier` slicing is consistently insufficient.
