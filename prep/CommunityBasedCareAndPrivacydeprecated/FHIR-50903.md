## Summary

The reporter (Nictiz / HL7 NL) is asking FHIR for guidance on which core
resource should represent the Dutch zib **TreatmentDirective2** — a joint,
pre-recorded decision between a patient (or representative) and a clinician
about whether specific treatments (e.g., ICU admission, CPR, artificial
respiration) should be executed, not executed, or executed only under
stated conditions. The submitter has experimented with both `Consent`
(emphasises the patient/representative agreement) and `ServiceRequest`
(actionable, supports `intent = directive` and `doNotPerform`, used by the
PACIO Advance Directive Interoperability IG), and finds neither perfectly
fits because both effectively constrain the answer to a binary
permit/deny or do/do-not-perform, while the zib also requires a third
"conditional / other" option (e.g., "do not execute artificial respiration
*in a hospital*").

Following the May 2025 Patient Care WGM in Madrid, the submitter notes a
broader inconsistency in the FHIR ecosystem: **PACIO uses ServiceRequest
while IPS uses Consent for advance directives**, and the WGM further
distinguished four buckets (Goal for informal wishes, ServiceRequest for
practitioner-authored portable medical orders / DNR, Consent for legal
directives, and Observation for patient preferences). The reporter is
asking the workgroup(s) to **harmonise** the recommended resource for
treatment directives and to clarify how the third "conditional" option
should be expressed (extension vs. native modelling). For R4 Nictiz has
provisionally settled on `Consent`, but expects a better answer for R6.

The related historical ticket [FHIR-10028](https://jira.hl7.org/browse/FHIR-10028)
("CarePlan: Provide ability to specify patient and/or provider
preferences") is referenced by Michelle Miller; it captures the parallel,
unresolved discussion about how to model patient/provider *preferences*
(as distinct from binding directives) and confirms the pattern of using
`Observation` for preferences and `ServiceRequest` for practitioner-
authored portable medical orders that the September 2024 ADI report-out
agreed on.

## Details

**Description:**

Currently, we are researching how to implement [TreatmentDirective](https://zibs.nl/wiki/TreatmentDirective2-v1.0(2020EN))
(which contains a joint decision between a health professional and a
patient or his representative(s) about the desirability of performing a
certain treatment) in the Netherlands.

For example, some topics that a Patient/representative could agree and
register with the health professional:

- Admission to the intensive care unit
- Cardiopulmonary resuscitation
- Artificial respiration

With the options:

1. Execute
2. Do not execute
3. Other *(Example: do not execute artificial respiration in a hospital)*

Now we are considering which HL7 FHIR R4 resource is compatible with this
kind of information.

First, we thought the [Consent](https://hl7.org/fhir/R4/consent.html)
resource, but now we are doubting whether to use [ServiceRequest](https://www.hl7.org/fhir/R4/servicerequest.html)
resource like [PACIO](https://build.fhir.org/ig/HL7/fhir-pacio-adi/index.html)
([example STU2](https://build.fhir.org/ig/HL7/fhir-pacio-adi/ServiceRequest-Example-Smith-Johnson-CPR-ServiceRequest1.html)).
Both resources have their pros and cons. Consent describes more of an
agreement between both parties, and ServiceRequest is more used for
planned/requested actions. So, does PACIO choose the right resource for
this TreatmentDirective purpose?

The only main issue both resources have only two options (`ProvisionType`:
Deny/Permit or `doNotPerform`: boolean). So now we're thinking of using an
extension to support this other (condition) option.

[Zulip thread](https://chat.fhir.org/#narrow/channel/179166-implementers/topic/TreatmentDirective.3A.20which.20resource.20is.20for.20this.20purpose.3F)

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Treat this as a harmonisation / clarification request and update FHIR
core (R6) to make `Consent` the single recommended resource for
**person-authored advance treatment directives**. Concretely:

- In `Consent.scope` documentation and the Consent narrative, promote
  the `adr` (Advance Care Directive) scope from "intended use, not
  modeled yet" to a fully described use case, with worked examples for
  CPR, ICU admission and ventilation directives that mirror the zib
  TreatmentDirective2 structure.
- Add a standard FHIR core extension (e.g.,
  `http://hl7.org/fhir/StructureDefinition/consent-conditionalProvision`)
  on `Consent.provision` that lets implementers carry the third
  "conditional / other" option the zib requires (a coded condition plus
  free-text qualifier) without overloading `Consent.provision.type`.
- Cross-reference IPS, PACIO ADI, and US Core Treatment Preferences from
  the Consent page so implementers see one canonical guidance hub.
- Coordinate with PACIO so ADI publishes an alternative (or successor)
  Consent-based profile alongside its existing ServiceRequest profile,
  retiring the latter for *person-authored* directives but retaining
  ServiceRequest for *practitioner-authored* portable medical orders
  (POLST/MOLST/CPR orders).

#### Justification

This is what the reporter literally asks for: pick one resource and
harmonise the ecosystem. `Consent.scope` already names this exact use
case ("Consent to instructions for potentially needed medical treatment
(e.g. DNR)"), IPS already uses Consent for advance directives, and
Nictiz has empirically validated that Consent works for the zib. A
standard conditional-provision extension addresses the only concrete
modelling gap the reporter identified (the third "Other" option). It
also keeps the social/legal framing — a *jointly-recorded agreement*
between patient and clinician — aligned with what Consent semantically
represents, rather than rebranding it as an actionable order.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Reject the premise that one resource fits all "treatment directive" use
cases and instead **publish formal guidance that splits the use cases by
authorship and actionability**, codifying the September 2024 ADI
Report-Out consensus directly in FHIR core narrative pages:

- **Goal** — informal patient wishes ("die at home").
- **Observation** (with PACIO/US-Core-style profiles) — person-authored
  preferences and treatment intervention preferences, *including* zib
  TreatmentDirective entries that are essentially preference statements.
- **ServiceRequest** with `intent = directive` and `doNotPerform` —
  practitioner-authored portable medical orders (CPR, intubation,
  artificial nutrition/hydration, etc.) that are intended to be acted
  upon by other clinicians.
- **Consent** — legal directives that grant or withhold permission
  (HIPAA-style, organ donation, research participation).

Add a new "Choosing the right resource for advance / treatment
directives" topic page under the Clinical module that maps zib
TreatmentDirective sub-fields onto the appropriate target resources, and
define a small core extension (e.g.,
`request-conditionalQualifier`) usable on any Request resource so the
"do not execute artificial respiration *in a hospital*" condition can be
expressed regardless of the chosen carrier.

#### Justification

The cross-workgroup consensus already documented in FHIR-10028 (PACIO,
PC, OO, CBCC, IPS) explicitly distinguishes person-authored from
practitioner-authored content and chose Observation/ServiceRequest
accordingly; forcing everything into Consent would actively contradict
that decision and would require IPS, PACIO and US Core to retire
profiles that are already in production. Lloyd McKenzie's chat reply
also points out that `Request.intent = "directive"` exists precisely for
this purpose, so semantically a Request resource is a better fit for
actionable items than Consent. The trade-off versus Disposition A is
ecosystem disruption: Disposition B preserves existing PACIO and US
Core investments and gives implementers a decision tree rather than a
single (potentially wrong) answer, at the cost of leaving
implementers — including Nictiz — to choose between two or three
resources for borderline zib elements.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline as **out of scope for FHIR core**: FHIR already provides four
viable carriers (Consent, ServiceRequest, Observation, Goal), each with
appropriate semantics, and the `Request.intent = "directive"` value plus
`Consent.scope = adr` already signal advance-directive intent. The
remaining decision — which resource to use for a given national
artefact such as the Dutch zib TreatmentDirective2 — is an
implementation-guide concern that belongs to PACIO ADI, IPS, US Core
and HL7 NL, not the FHIR core specification. The "third option"
modelling gap can be solved with a local IG extension without changing
core. Recommend the workgroup close this ticket as **Persuasive with
Mod**: no normative change, but add a short disambiguation paragraph to
the Consent and ServiceRequest "Boundaries and Relationships" sections
pointing readers at the relevant IGs.

#### Justification

FHIR core has historically resisted prescribing one resource per
real-world artefact when several plausible carriers exist; that decision
is delegated to IGs by design. Two of the three "options" the reporter
lists (Execute / Do not execute) are already directly representable in
both Consent and ServiceRequest, and the third (conditional) is exactly
the kind of locally-meaningful constraint that extensions are intended
for. Forcing harmonisation across PACIO, IPS, US Core, and zib through
a core change would be a breaking change for at least PACIO ADI's STU2
ServiceRequest profile, with limited upside given that the underlying
data is already round-trippable today. Declining keeps core stable while
acknowledging the reporter's point with non-normative guidance.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

Disposition B is the most defensible because it aligns with the
*already-achieved* September 2024 ADI cross-workgroup consensus
(documented in FHIR-10028) and with Lloyd McKenzie's Zulip guidance,
without forcing PACIO, US Core or IPS to retract published profiles. The
core problem the reporter is surfacing is not "Consent is wrong" or
"ServiceRequest is wrong" — it is that **the FHIR specification
currently gives implementers no authoritative decision tree** for the
overlapping space of preferences, directives and portable medical
orders, so different IGs have landed in different places. Publishing
that decision tree in FHIR core (Clinical module narrative + a small
shared `conditionalQualifier` extension on Request resources) directly
answers the harmonisation ask without locking the world into one
resource that doesn't actually fit every sub-case of the zib model.
Disposition A is tempting because it is simple, but it ignores
practitioner-authored portable medical orders (where ServiceRequest's
actionability genuinely matters) and would invalidate PACIO ADI.
Disposition C is the cheapest path but leaves the reporter with the
same "PACIO says one thing, IPS says another" problem they came in
with — which is exactly what they are asking the workgroup to fix.
