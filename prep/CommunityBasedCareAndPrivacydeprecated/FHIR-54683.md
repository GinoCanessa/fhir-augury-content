## Summary

The ticket asks the Scalable Consent Management IG authors to clarify
whether mandating the `File Consent` and `Revoke Consent` operations
(per §OP1 of the technical page) is intended to *prohibit* the standard
RESTful `create` and `update` interactions on the `Consent` resource by
a consent administration service. In other words: if the IG SHALLs the
two custom operations, does that implicitly forbid clients from also
POST/PUTing `Consent` resources directly? The reporter (John Moehrke)
is flagging an ambiguity in conformance language, not requesting a
behavioral change per se.

No related Jira tickets, Zulip threads, or GitHub items were surfaced
by the cross-reference index for this ticket, so the ticket stands on
its own. The recorded resolution on the ticket reads: *"While we are
not going to explicitly restrict RESTful CREATE and UPDATE from
happening, we are going to include a section indicating why allowing
those alongside the operations is dangerous and not recommended by
this guide."* — i.e., the workgroup has marked it Persuasive with a
non-prohibitive, narrative-warning approach.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Treat the question literally and amend the IG to explicitly prohibit
direct RESTful `create` / `update` of `Consent` resources by a
conformant *consent administration service*. Concretely, in the
Scalable Consent Management IG (`hl7.org/fhir/us/consent-management`,
technical page `technical.html`, §OP1 area), add a normative SHALL NOT
clause stating that a conformant consent administration service SHALL
NOT expose `create` or `update` interactions on the `Consent`
endpoint, and reflect this in the server CapabilityStatement
(`interaction.code` for `Consent` limited to `read`, `search-type`,
plus the two `OperationDefinition` references for `$file-consent` and
`$revoke-consent`).

#### Justification

This reading is the strongest interpretation of "two operations are
defined that a consent administration service SHALL support" combined
with the IG's stated business intent: the operations exist precisely
because filing/revoking consent has side effects (provenance,
versioning, audit, downstream notification) that a bare REST
write cannot guarantee. Allowing parallel `create`/`update` paths
opens a gap where a client can bypass the operation's required
semantics, defeating the conformance goal. Forbidding them outright
gives implementers a single, testable conformance target and matches
how other IGs (e.g., where `$submit`-style operations carry mandatory
side effects) discourage parallel REST writes.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach (matches the recorded resolution)

#### Proposal

Do not add a normative prohibition. Instead, in the Scalable Consent
Management IG add a new explanatory subsection (e.g., under
`technical.html#ci-c-OP1`, titled something like *"Relationship to
RESTful create/update"*) that:

1. Explicitly states the IG does **not** forbid a consent
   administration service from also supporting RESTful
   `create`/`update` on `Consent`.
2. Lays out the risks of doing so — bypass of the side effects bound
   to `$file-consent` / `$revoke-consent` (provenance, status
   transitions, notification, audit), divergence between filed
   consents and resources written via REST, and conformance ambiguity
   for clients trying to choose a path.
3. Recommends that implementers either (a) disable `create`/`update`
   on `Consent` at the consent administration endpoint, or (b)
   internally route any direct write through the same logic invoked
   by the operations.
4. Optionally adds a SHOULD that the server's CapabilityStatement
   reflect whichever choice is made so clients can discover it.

#### Justification

This is the disposition the workgroup actually recorded ("Persuasive"
with the resolution text quoted above). It preserves implementer
flexibility (some deployments legitimately need REST writes for
migration, bulk load, or admin tooling) while removing the ambiguity
that prompted the ticket. It is also the lower-risk change to make
late in the IG's cycle: it is purely additive narrative, requires no
CapabilityStatement constraint changes, and avoids breaking
implementations that are already exposing REST write on `Consent`
behind an admin role. The ticket's question is fundamentally a
*documentation* gap, and a documentation fix is the proportional
response.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Close the ticket as "no change required" on the grounds that §OP1
already says what it needs to say: it mandates support for the two
operations and is silent on other interactions, which by the normal
rules of FHIR conformance language means other interactions are
neither required nor forbidden. The reporter should be pointed at the
existing FHIR conformance prose that says SHALL on a feature does not
imply prohibition of unmentioned features.

#### Justification

Strict reading of FHIR conformance: a SHALL on operation support does
not, on its own, prohibit RESTful interactions; CapabilityStatement
declarations are the canonical place where allowed interactions are
enumerated. If the IG wanted to forbid `create`/`update` it would
have to do so explicitly, and it does not. Therefore the answer to
the reporter's question is "no, §OP1 does not prohibit
`create`/`update`," and that answer is already derivable from the
spec without a textual change.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

The ticket has already been resolved as Persuasive with a resolution
that matches Disposition B almost word-for-word, so the recommendation
is to formalize that resolution as the IG edit. Disposition C is
technically defensible but leaves the exact ambiguity that prompted a
senior reviewer (John Moehrke) to file the ticket; if one experienced
reader had to ask, more implementers will. Disposition A overcommits:
forbidding `create`/`update` outright would break legitimate
deployment patterns (administrative loading, migration, server-side
tooling that already routes writes through the same internal logic
as the operations) and would invalidate any existing CapabilityStatements
that allow them, with no clear safety win once the cautionary
narrative from B is in place. B is the right balance: it removes the
ambiguity, gives implementers a concrete recommendation against the
risky pattern, and preserves the flexibility the IG has so far chosen
to allow.
