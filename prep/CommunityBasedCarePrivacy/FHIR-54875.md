## Summary

FHIR-54875 ("recognition of dependency processing") asks the Scalable
Consent Management (FHIR) IG to make explicit, for readers of all kinds,
that turning a `Consent` resource into runtime authorization behavior is
not free: the IG's referenced provisions and identifiers must be
*imported* into an access-control decision point (PDP) and then
maintained as the underlying `Consent` evolves. The reporter
(John Moehrke) observes that while the IG already covers the
"consent has changed" trigger, it does not explain *why* that trigger
matters — namely, that PDPs typically cache an interpreted ruleset and
must add new provisions and remove stale ones when the source `Consent`
changes, because re-interpreting the `Consent` on every authorization
decision would be prohibitively expensive.

The ticket has already been **Triaged** with resolution **Persuasive**.
The recorded resolution is to add a section on the IG's index page
(largely reusing the reporter's wording) that explicitly discusses this
import/cache lifecycle and uses it to motivate why the IG mandates
**Subscriptions** to be notified of consent changes, instead of
requiring systems to fetch the consent on every authorization decision.

There are **no recorded cross-references** (Jira / Zulip / GitHub /
Confluence) for this ticket in FHIR Augury, and a Zulip search for
`FHIR-54875` returned no hits. The Confluence source is not configured

## Details

**Description:**

> This specification, given the scope, should make clear to the reader
> of all kinds, that there is an expensive process of interpreting a
> Consent into an access control decision point. The Consent may have
> well formed rules using well formed identifiers and references; but
> these provisions do need to be imported into the access control
> decision point rules. It might be possible to do this dynamically,
> but that task will be expensive. Thus one would tend to take on that
> expense once, and use internal cached knowledge unless it is
> determined that the Consent has changed. This "consent has changed"
> is covered, but the reason why this is needed is not clear, and that
> clarity needs to recognize the expense of importing new provisions,
> and removal of old provisions.

**Resolution Description (already recorded on the ticket):**

> We will add a section on the index page explicitly discussing this
> (using most of the wording you included) and will indicate that this
> is why the IG requires Subscriptions for being notified of consent
> changes as opposed to requiring systems to go fetch a consent every
> time a decision is required.

## Proposed Dispositions

> The ticket already carries resolution **Persuasive** with a recorded
> plan that closely matches Disposition A. The dispositions below are
> framed as workgroup-review options against that recorded plan.

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Adopt the recorded resolution as written: add a new sub-section to the
**index page** of the Scalable Consent Management (FHIR) IG, titled
something like *"Why consent change notifications matter — provisions
must be imported into the decision point"*. The section reuses the
reporter's wording and makes three explicit points:

1. Interpreting a `Consent` (its `provision` tree, referenced
   `valueSet`s, identifiers, and party references) into the rules a
   policy decision point (PDP) actually evaluates is **expensive**, and
   is therefore typically performed once and then cached as an
   interpreted ruleset.
2. When the underlying `Consent` changes, the PDP must **import the new
   provisions and remove the old ones** from its cached ruleset; doing
   the full interpretation on every authorization decision is not
   viable.
3. **This is why the IG requires Subscriptions** to be notified of
   consent changes, rather than requiring consumers to fetch the
   `Consent` on each decision.

Cross-link the new section from wherever the IG currently introduces
the "consent has changed" trigger and from the Subscriptions section,
so a reader arriving from either direction lands on the rationale.

#### Justification

The reporter has already supplied substantive, well-scoped wording, the
work group has agreed it is persuasive, and the change is purely
narrative (no resource, profile, value-set, or operation impact). The
addition closes a documented "why" gap for the existing Subscription
requirement, which strengthens implementer understanding without
imposing any new conformance burden. It also keeps the IG aligned with
how real-world PDPs (XACML-style or otherwise) are operated, where
ruleset compilation/import is a well-known discrete step.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Accept the *intent* of FHIR-54875 but place the new content in a
dedicated **"Implementation Considerations"** (or
"Architecture & Performance") page, not on the IG's index page, and
have the index page link to it under a single short paragraph. While
doing so, fold in the closely related conceptual tickets:

- FHIR-54874 (management narrative) as the operational lifecycle
  framing,
- FHIR-54630 (provision processing order) as a forward-link from the
  "interpretation" paragraph,
- FHIR-46021 (Permission / Consent.provision mapping) as the canonical
  example of *what* gets imported into the PDP.

Specifically: add a sub-page under the IG's narrative TOC that walks
through (a) parsing/validating a `Consent`, (b) compiling its
provisions into PDP-evaluable rules (e.g., `Permission` instances or
PDP-internal structures), (c) cache invalidation on
`Consent`-change subscriptions, and (d) removal of stale provisions.
The index page then carries only a 2–3 sentence pointer.

#### Justification

The recorded resolution risks making the index page itself a catch-all
for conceptual clarifications (54874, 54630, 54702 all want similar
narrative additions), which dilutes the index page's role as a
high-level orientation. A purpose-built page lets the IG cover the same
"why subscriptions" rationale with room to also address the sibling
conceptual tickets coherently, without forcing every reader of the
index to wade through it. Trade-off vs. Disposition A: slightly higher
authoring cost (a new page plus index pointer) and one extra click for
the casual reader, in exchange for a more navigable IG.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline as **out of scope for narrative-only change** at this stage,
on the basis that the IG already (per the ticket itself) covers the
"consent has changed" mechanism normatively. Close FHIR-54875 as
*Not Persuasive — already addressed*, optionally pointing readers to
external profiles such as `Permission` or downstream PDP-vendor
documentation for the import/cache semantics.

#### Justification

Defensible only if the work group judges that *why* a normative
mechanism exists is implementer education, not specification scope —
i.e., the IG describes *what* to do (subscribe to consent changes) and
*how* (Subscription topic), and the rationale belongs in companion
material. This is the weakest position because the reporter's text
explicitly identifies a comprehension gap that current readers hit, and
the work group has already judged the request persuasive; declining
would also re-litigate a closed disposition without new information.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** A

The ticket is already triaged Persuasive, the reporter has supplied
usable wording, and the change is narrative-only with no normative
ripple. Disposition A delivers the requested clarification with the
least friction and lands the rationale exactly where readers of the
"consent has changed" / Subscription requirement will encounter it.
Disposition B is a reasonable structural improvement and worth
revisiting if the work group decides to consolidate the conceptual
backlog (54874, 54630, 54702, 46021) into a single new
"Implementation Considerations" page — but that consolidation is a
larger editorial project that should not block this ticket. Disposition
C is not recommended: the gap the reporter identified is real, the
fix is cheap, and the work group has already accepted it.
