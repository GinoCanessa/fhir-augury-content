## Summary

The ticket questions an internal inconsistency in the Scalable Consent
Management profile of the `Consent` resource. The `Consent.provision`
element is flagged as Must Support, and its definition characterizes a
provision as a constraint or exception to `Consent.policyRule`. However,
`Consent.policyRule` is `0..1` in the base resource and is not flagged
as Must Support in the profile. The reporter (Hans Buitendijk) asks how
a provision can meaningfully be defined when no `policyRule` is
guaranteed to be present, and therefore whether `policyRule` should
itself be Must Support (or otherwise have a stronger cardinality) in
the profile. The ticket has been triaged with a `Persuasive` resolution
that proposes (a) raising `Consent.policy` to `1..*` and (b) rewording
the `provision` definition so it refers to constraints on the base
`Consent.policy` (as identified by its URI element) rather than to
`Consent.policyRule`. The cross-reference index links the ticket to the
two FHIR elements at issue, `Consent.policyRule` and `Consent.policy`,
but surfaces no related Jira tickets, Zulip threads, or GitHub items.

## Details

**Description:**

> The .provision is Must Support and the definition indicates that this
> is a constraint (or exception) to the Consent.policyRule.  Yet the
> .policyRule is 0..1 in the base, but not stated as a Must Support in
> the profile.  How can a provision be defined without a policyRule in
> place (if the definition is accurate), thus why is policyRule not
> Must Support?

**Resolution Description (recorded on the ticket):**

> a) we will make Consent.policy 1..*
> b) We will change the description of provision to say that it is
> constraints on the base Consent.policy as defined by the URI element.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Adopt the resolution recorded on the ticket as-is in the Scalable
Consent Management profile / `Consent` resource definition:

1. Tighten the cardinality of `Consent.policy` from `0..*` to `1..*`
   so that at least one base policy (identified by its URI element) is
   always present whenever a provision is asserted.
2. Rewrite the `Consent.provision` element definition to state that a
   provision expresses constraints on (or exceptions to) the base
   `Consent.policy` identified by the URI element, rather than to
   `Consent.policyRule`.

These two changes together address the inconsistency the reporter
flagged: provisions become anchored to a guaranteed-present base policy
rather than to an optional `policyRule`.

#### Justification

The reporter's observation is correct: a Must Support `provision`
defined as a constraint on `policyRule` cannot be coherently
interpreted when `policyRule` is `0..1` and not Must Support. Anchoring
provisions to `Consent.policy` (which carries the policy URI) is more
faithful to the way modern Consent profiles identify the governing
policy, and raising `Consent.policy` to `1..*` enforces the prerequisite
the provision definition implicitly relies on. This matches the
`Persuasive` resolution already recorded by the work group and avoids
re-litigating the underlying design judgment.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Instead of changing cardinality on the base `Consent` resource, address
the inconsistency entirely within the Scalable Consent Management
profile:

1. Leave base `Consent.policy` at `0..*` and base
   `Consent.policyRule` at `0..1`.
2. In the profile, flag **either** `Consent.policy` **or**
   `Consent.policyRule` as Must Support with a profile-level invariant
   requiring at least one of them to be populated whenever
   `Consent.provision` is present.
3. Update the profile's narrative for `Consent.provision` to clarify
   that it constrains "the governing policy expressed via
   `Consent.policy` (URI) and/or `Consent.policyRule` (CodeableConcept)",
   without mandating one form over the other.

#### Justification

This keeps base-resource cardinality stable (avoiding a breaking change
for implementers and other profiles that rely on `Consent.policy` being
optional) while still resolving the logical gap the ticket identifies.
It also preserves backward compatibility for systems that currently
express their governing policy via `policyRule` rather than the URI in
`policy`, which can be material for jurisdictions whose policies are
identified by a coded value rather than a URI. The trade-off versus
Disposition A is more profile complexity (an invariant plus alternative
Must Support) in exchange for less disruption to the base resource and
to other Consent profiles.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline to change either the base `Consent` cardinalities or the
`Consent.provision` definition. Instead, add an editorial note to the
profile explaining that `Consent.provision` is interpreted relative to
whichever policy expression is supplied (`policy` and/or `policyRule`),
and that profiles or implementation guides may further constrain
either element if they require a guaranteed base policy.

#### Justification

The base `Consent` resource has long allowed both `policy` and
`policyRule` to be optional precisely because some Consent instruments
are self-contained (the consent itself *is* the policy) and do not
need to reference an external policy artifact. Forcing `policy` to
`1..*` at the base or in this profile may exclude legitimate use cases
and create a breaking change for downstream implementers without a
strong, documented harm. An editorial clarification preserves
flexibility while answering the reporter's question.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** A

The ticket's resolution is already recorded as `Persuasive`, which
indicates the work group has tentatively accepted the reporter's
analysis. Disposition A is the cleanest expression of that conclusion:
it removes the logical contradiction between a Must Support
`provision` and an optional `policyRule`/`policy`, and it aligns the
provision definition with the way the Scalable Consent Management
profile actually identifies the governing policy (via the URI in
`Consent.policy`). Disposition B is a reasonable fallback if the work
group is uncomfortable with raising base-resource cardinality, but it
introduces an "either/or" Must Support pattern that is harder for
implementers to validate consistently. Disposition C does not address
the underlying inconsistency and effectively re-asks the question the
reporter raised. Recommend the work group ratify Disposition A and
proceed with the two specific edits (cardinality on `Consent.policy`
and re-wording of `Consent.provision`).
