## Summary

The ticket reports that the textual definitions of the two codes `permit`
and `deny` in the `http://hl7.org/fhir/consent-provision-type` CodeSystem
are weak. Specifically:

- The `deny` definition ("Consent is denied for actions meeting these
  rules.") is circular — it uses the word being defined.
- The `permit` definition ("Consent is provided for actions meeting these
  rules.") uses the ambiguous verb "provided".
- Both codes are now also reused outside `Consent` (notably by the
  `Permission` resource), so the definitions should not be phrased
  exclusively in terms of Consent; they should describe the behavior of a
  matching provision/rule generically.

The reporter proposes the following replacement definitions:

- **deny** — *"The provision or rule, when matching the activity request,
  forbids the activity."*
- **permit** — *"The provision or rule, when matching the activity
  request, allows the activity."*

A follow-up comment from the reporter raises two adjacent questions for
the workgroup: (1) whether the CodeSystem itself should be renamed to
something less Consent-specific now that Permission also uses it, and
(2) whether the CodeSystem should be migrated to THO (Terminology.HL7).
The most recent comment notes that the Security WG has reviewed and
would approve the change.

There were no Jira or GitHub cross-references and no Zulip threads
directly cross-referenced to this ticket. Topic-level Zulip search
surfaced several historical discussions where the ambiguity of `permit`
/ `deny` (and its interaction with `Consent.policyRule`) was actively
debated — those are listed under *Related Zulip Discussions* for
context.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Update the two existing concept definitions in the
`http://hl7.org/fhir/consent-provision-type` CodeSystem (located in the
core spec source as `source/consent/codesystem-consent-provision-type.xml`
in the HL7/fhir authoring tree) to the reporter-supplied text:

- `deny.definition` → "The provision or rule, when matching the
  activity request, forbids the activity."
- `permit.definition` → "The provision or rule, when matching the
  activity request, allows the activity."

Leave the CodeSystem URL, codes, displays, version, and bindings (in
`Consent.provision.type` and `Permission.rule.type` / equivalent)
unchanged. Defer the reporter's secondary questions (rename of the
CodeSystem; migration to THO) to follow-up tickets so they don't block
this purely-editorial fix.

#### Justification

The change is editorial — definition text only — and removes two
clearly-acknowledged defects: a circular definition (`deny` defined
using "denied") and a vague one (`permit` defined using "provided").
The replacement wording also matches how the codes are actually used in
the `Permission` resource (per-rule outcome on a matching activity),
which the Security WG has already reviewed and indicated it would
approve. Because URL, codes, and displays are unchanged, there is no
breaking impact on instances, profiles, or terminology services. The
rename / THO migration questions are governance/structural decisions
that deserve their own ticket and impact analysis; bundling them here
would convert a low-risk editorial fix into a higher-risk change.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Adopt the reporter's revised definitions (as in Disposition A) **and**
add a short CodeSystem-level `description` clarifying the generic
"per-provision/per-rule outcome" semantics and listing the resources
that bind the value set (`Consent`, `Permission`, and any current
binders). At the same time, open a companion ticket — and reference it
from this one — covering (a) whether to rename the CodeSystem to
something Consent-neutral (e.g., `provision-rule-outcome` or similar)
and (b) whether to move it to THO. Do not change the URL or codes in
this ticket; only the prose is touched here.

#### Justification

The reporter's own follow-up comment explicitly asks both questions
("rename the codeSystem to something less consent specific?", "Should
this codeSystem go to THO?"). Capturing them as tracked, linked
follow-ups — instead of dropping them — preserves the workgroup's
intent without forcing a controversial rename/move into an editorial
ticket. The added CodeSystem-level `description` text is the lowest-cost
way to communicate to readers (today, before any rename) that the codes
are now general-purpose provision/rule outcomes, which is the actual
underlying confusion the ticket is responding to.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline the change on the grounds that the existing definitions, while
imperfect, are unambiguous in context (the codes appear on
`Consent.provision.type`, where "Consent is denied/provided for actions
meeting these rules" is self-consistent), and that any rephrasing risks
subtle semantic drift in implementations that have already coded
against the current text. Encourage the requester to instead address
the cross-resource concern via a CodeSystem-level `description` update
or via implementation guidance, without touching the per-code
definitions.

#### Justification

This option is included for completeness. Its strongest argument is
risk-aversion: per-code `definition` text is normative-grade prose for
terminology consumers, and changing it (even editorially) can ripple
through downstream materials, translations, and implementation guides.
The argument is weak here, however, because (i) the current `deny`
definition is genuinely circular and (ii) the codes are demonstrably
reused outside Consent (Permission), so the Consent-only phrasing is
factually misleading rather than merely stylistically awkward.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

Disposition B preserves everything good about Disposition A — a
low-risk, editorial fix to two genuinely defective definitions, already
blessed by the Security WG — while honoring the reporter's explicit
follow-up questions about renaming the CodeSystem and moving it to THO.
Those two questions are real and will recur as `Permission` adoption
grows; spawning linked follow-up tickets now is cheap and avoids
losing them. Adding a CodeSystem-level `description` that names the
binders (`Consent`, `Permission`) is the most direct response to the
underlying complaint that the codes "should not be about Consent, but
about provision/rule" without taking on a rename in this ticket.
Disposition C is not defensible given the circular `deny` definition
and the cross-resource reuse the reporter flags.
