## Summary

John Moehrke (CBCP) is asking the workgroup to revisit `Consent.provision.expression`.
He argues the element is unclear in two ways: it is not obvious *how* the
expression is intended to be evaluated against a request for data, and it is
not obvious *why* an implementer would choose this mechanism over the other
modelled provision conditions (actor, action, purpose, class, code, securityLabel,
dataPeriod, data, etc.). He further notes that implementing a general expression
evaluator is *very expensive* for a Consent processor, and proposes that the
capability would be better expressed as an extension rather than a first-class
provision element.

The relevant historical context is captured in the cross-referenced (by
keyword/topic, not by formal Jira link) ticket
[FHIR-31550](https://jira.hl7.org/browse/FHIR-31550) "Add Expression to
Consent.provision" (CBCP, Persuasive/Published, 2021), which is the change that
introduced the element in the first place. That ticket was driven by a use
case for FHIRPath-based, machine-readable provision targets (e.g.,
`Patient.gender` instead of LOINC `76691-5`). FHIR-54637 is effectively a
re-litigation of that 2021 decision now that real-world implementation
experience has accumulated.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Remove the `expression` choice from the `Consent.provision.expression`
element in the next FHIR Core release. Replace it with a standard core
extension (e.g., `http://hl7.org/fhir/StructureDefinition/consent-provision-expression`)
typed as `Expression`, with context `Consent.provision` (and recursively
`Consent.provision.provision`). Update `Consent`'s narrative to describe the
extension and to direct implementers wanting computable rules toward either
the extension or the `Permission` resource. Mark the move as a backwards-
incompatible change in the change-log and provide migration guidance for
the small number of implementations known to use the element.

#### Justification

The 2021 thread that motivated FHIR-31550 explicitly traded off "reasonable
choices in core" against "extension-only". With four years of implementation
experience and the arrival of the `Permission` resource (which is the
purpose-built home for computable access-control rules per the Security and
Privacy thread above), the balance has shifted: most Consent implementers
do *not* implement an Expression evaluator, and the rare ones that do are
already wiring in CQL/XACML engines that are happy to be referenced via an
extension. The reporter (John Moehrke) is a primary CBCP contributor and is
flagging real implementation cost. Moving to an extension preserves the
capability for those who need it without imposing a "very expensive"
evaluator on every Consent processor.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Keep `Consent.provision.expression` in core (no breaking change), but:

1. Substantially expand the element definition, comments, and the
   `Consent` narrative to describe (a) *when* an author should choose
   `expression` over the structured `actor`/`action`/`purpose`/`class`/
   `code`/`securityLabel`/`dataPeriod`/`data` provision conditions, and
   (b) *how* a Consent processor is expected to combine an `expression`
   result with the structured conditions. Re-use the language captured by
   René Spronk in the 2025 implementers thread (computable rules in an
   expression language of the author's choice, granting *or* denying
   access).
2. Add an explicit "implementer note" stating that supporting
   `Consent.provision.expression` is optional and that processors that do
   not implement an evaluator should reject Consent resources whose
   `expression` they cannot evaluate (rather than silently ignoring them).
3. Add a cross-reference to `Permission` for the access-control use case,
   so implementers can choose the right resource.

#### Justification

The reporter's primary complaint is *clarity* ("unclear how this is used,
and very unclear why"). The "expensive to implement" complaint follows from
the lack of documented expectations: implementers do not know whether
support is mandatory, what languages they must accept, or how the result
combines with the rest of the provision. Disposition B fixes the actual
defect (under-specification) without a breaking change to a published
element, and without forcing every Consent IG and existing implementation
to migrate to an extension. It also avoids re-opening the 2021 design
decision that was reached with explicit Lloyd McKenzie support for putting
the choice in core.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Mark FHIR-54637 as Not Persuasive. Leave `Consent.provision.expression`
unchanged in the core spec. Defer any further work on computable consent
rules to the `Permission` resource and to future ballot cycles when CBCP is
no longer marked deprecated and a successor work group is in place.

#### Justification

The element was added in FHIR-31550 with explicit implementer consensus on
the original Zulip thread, including Lloyd McKenzie's design guidance to
keep "reasonable" expression choices in core. It is published. The reporter
identifies clarity and cost concerns but does not present new use-case
evidence that the original decision was wrong, and there is no recorded
implementer push from the broader community to remove or relocate it. The
work group is also flagged as `[deprecated]`, so a breaking change driven
through it is governance-fragile. Declining preserves the status quo and
the prior cross-implementation contract.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

The reporter has named two distinct problems — *unclear semantics* and
*high implementation cost*. The cost problem is largely a *consequence* of
the clarity problem: when a normative element has no documented evaluation
contract and no documented optionality, every implementer has to assume
worst-case mandatory support for an arbitrary expression language. The 2021
design intent (Lloyd McKenzie, Zulip) was deliberately *open* — FHIRPath,
CQL, even SQL — and the 2025 René Spronk explanation confirms that
language-pluggability is the feature, not a bug. Disposition B captures
that intent in the spec text, makes optionality explicit, and points
implementers at `Permission` for the pure access-control case. Disposition
A solves the cost problem more aggressively but at the price of a
breaking change to a published element, driven through a deprecated work
group, against the prior Persuasive resolution of FHIR-31550 — that is a
high bar to clear without new implementer evidence. Disposition C ignores
the legitimate documentation gap the reporter has identified. Recommend B,
with a note that a future ticket against the successor work group can
revisit Disposition A once Permission adoption is clearer.
