## Summary

Craig Newman observes that the Scalable Consent Management (FastConsent)
profile lists `Consent.provision.actor` under the "Must Have" requirements
section, but the underlying element has cardinality `0..*`. He asks whether
the element should instead be moved to the "Must Support" list, since
"Must Have" implies presence is mandatory and is incompatible with an
optional cardinality. The ticket has already been triaged with a
**Persuasive** resolution: yes, `actor` belongs in Must Support and not
in Must Have. There are no cross-referenced Jira tickets, Zulip threads,
Confluence pages, or GitHub items in the FHIR Augury index that elaborate
on this point, so the disposition rests on the FastConsent profile text
itself and on standard FHIR conformance semantics.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Move `Consent.provision.actor` out of the FastConsent profile's
**Must Have** requirements list and into the **Must Support**
requirements list. Concretely, in the FastConsent (Scalable Consent
Management) profile narrative — wherever the requirements lists are
authored (intro page or the Consent profile's `differential`/narrative
fragment) — delete the `actor` row/bullet from the Must Have section
and add it to the Must Support section. Leave the underlying element
cardinality at `0..*` and ensure the StructureDefinition carries
`mustSupport = true` on `Consent.provision.actor` (and on its
sub-elements that already participate in must-support, e.g.
`actor.role` and `actor.reference`, if the profile already constrains
them).

#### Justification

This is exactly the change the Persuasive resolution authorises and is
internally consistent with FHIR conformance semantics: "Must Have" is
conventionally used for elements that are required to be present
(min ≥ 1), whereas "Must Support" describes elements that may be absent
but, when present, MUST be handled by conformant implementations. With
`provision.actor` constrained to `0..*` the element can legitimately be
absent (consent applies to all actors), so listing it as Must Have is a
documentation defect. Moving it to Must Support preserves the original
intent — actor handling is required of implementers when it appears —
without forcing senders to populate an element that is semantically
optional.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Rather than only re-classifying the element, take the opportunity to
clarify the FastConsent profile's requirements taxonomy. Add a short
prose definition at the top of the requirements section explaining
that "Must Have" means `min` cardinality ≥ 1 in the profile, while
"Must Support" means `mustSupport = true` regardless of cardinality.
Then audit every entry in both lists against the profile's
StructureDefinition and reclassify any other mismatches found
(`provision.actor` being the trigger, but likely not the only one).
For `provision.actor` specifically, also consider tightening the
profile slice — e.g., if FastConsent expects at least one actor in
practice, slice `provision.actor` by `role` and require a minimum
cardinality on the meaningful slice — before deciding the final
classification.

#### Justification

The reporter's observation suggests the requirements lists were
hand-authored and may have drifted from the StructureDefinition. A
one-line fix to `actor` resolves the immediate inconsistency but
leaves the same class of bug latent for every other element in the
lists. A definitional gloss plus a one-pass audit is cheap, prevents
repeat tickets, and gives implementers a precise reading of the two
labels. The optional slice tightening is offered because FastConsent
may have a stronger real-world expectation about actors than `0..*`
suggests; resolving that intent now avoids a later breaking change.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline to move the element and instead leave the requirements lists
as-is, on the basis that the profile's "Must Have" label is being
used as an authoring/intent marker for "the workgroup considers this
element important", independent of cardinality, and changing it would
mis-state authorial intent.

#### Justification

This disposition is included for completeness but is weak. It would
require explicitly redefining "Must Have" in the FastConsent profile
to mean something other than its conventional FHIR usage, which is a
larger documentation change than the ticket originally requests, and
would conflict with implementer expectations across other HL7 IGs
that use the same labels. It should only be chosen if the workgroup
genuinely intends "Must Have" to be a non-conformance editorial label
— which the existing Persuasive resolution suggests they do not.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** A

The workgroup has already triaged the ticket as Persuasive with a
direct, unambiguous answer ("actor should be in the Must Support and
not in the Must Have section"), so Disposition A simply executes that
decision. It is a low-risk, narrative-only change to the FastConsent
profile that aligns the requirements list with the underlying
`0..*` cardinality and with the standard FHIR meaning of Must Support.
Disposition B is attractive as a follow-up — a one-pass audit of the
rest of the requirements lists is a sensible hygiene step — but it
should be tracked as a separate ticket so this one can be closed
quickly against its existing resolution. Disposition C is not
defensible given the resolution already on file.
