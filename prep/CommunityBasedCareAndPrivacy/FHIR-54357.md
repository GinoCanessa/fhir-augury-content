## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Update Section 3.8 of the Scalable Consent Management (FHIR)
specification to add the missing Post-condition. Per the ticket's
resolution text, the new post-condition reads (substantively): *"The
lifecycle events were returned to the Consenter."* Place this as a new
"Post-condition" subsection in Section 3.8 alongside the existing
Pre-condition / Trigger / Main Flow content, mirroring the structure
used by the other use cases in the specification so the section
becomes structurally consistent with its peers.

#### Justification

This is exactly what the reporter asked for and matches the agreed
resolution wording already captured on the ticket
([FHIR-54357](https://jira.hl7.org/browse/FHIR-54357), resolved
*Persuasive*). Use-case sections in the Scalable Consent Management
specification follow a regular Pre-condition / Trigger / Flow /
Post-condition shape, and a missing Post-condition in 3.8 is a clear
editorial gap rather than a substantive design issue. Returning
lifecycle events to the Consenter is also consistent with the broader
consent-lifecycle posture of the spec, so the change is purely
clarifying and carries no implementation impact.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Accept the post-condition as proposed, but expand it to make the
returned lifecycle information explicit and verifiable. Specifically,
the Post-condition for Section 3.8 would state that, on completion of
the use case: (1) the Consent resource is in the expected lifecycle
state (e.g., `active` / `inactive` / `entered-in-error` as
appropriate); (2) the lifecycle events that occurred are made
available to the Consenter via a defined channel (e.g.,
AuditEvent/Provenance entries scoped to the Consent, or a notification
returned in the response payload); and (3) the Consenter can correlate
those events back to the originating Consent. Optionally cross-link
the Post-condition to whichever section of the spec already defines
how lifecycle events are surfaced.

#### Justification

The minimal wording in Disposition A closes the gap but leaves
"returned to the Consenter" undefined — readers will reasonably
disagree on whether that means an AuditEvent trail, a Provenance
chain, an in-band response, or an out-of-band notification. Spelling
out the observable post-state and the carrier mechanism makes the
post-condition testable, which is the usual purpose of a use-case
post-condition, and avoids a likely follow-up ticket asking the same
question more pointedly. The trade-off is modest extra editorial work
and the need to align the wording with whatever lifecycle-event
mechanism the spec already commits to.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Close the ticket without changing Section 3.8, on the basis that the
use-case Post-condition is implicitly covered by the surrounding
narrative and by the consent-lifecycle behavior described elsewhere in
the specification.

#### Justification

This is defensible only if the workgroup believes the Post-condition
is genuinely redundant with existing text and that adding it would
duplicate normative content. Given that the ticket has already been
resolved *Persuasive* and the reporter identified a concrete
structural inconsistency (other use-case sections do carry
Post-conditions), declining is hard to justify on the merits and
would leave the section visibly out of pattern with its peers.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

Disposition A faithfully implements the agreed resolution and is the
safe minimum. Disposition B is recommended because the reporter's
underlying concern is that Section 3.8 is structurally incomplete, and
a one-line "lifecycle events were returned to the Consenter"
post-condition, while accurate, is not operationally testable. The
small additional editorial investment in B — naming the expected
Consent state, the carrier for lifecycle events (AuditEvent /
Provenance / response payload), and the Consenter's ability to
correlate them — turns the post-condition into something an
implementer can verify against, which is the normal job of a use-case
post-condition. Disposition C should be rejected: the ticket has
already been triaged *Persuasive*, and the spec's other use-case
sections do carry Post-conditions, so leaving 3.8 without one is an
acknowledged gap.
