## Summary

Hans Buitendijk reports that the FHIR Consent resource does not make the
relationship between a patient's consent directives and the underlying
authorizing policy clear. Specifically, it is not clear how the
`Consent.policyBasis`, `Consent.regulatoryBasis`, and `policyText` (as
referenced via `Consent.policyBasis` / overriding policy) elements are
intended to be used together to express that a consent exists *because*
of, and is shaped *by*, a regulation (e.g., HIPAA) and/or a more
specific policy (e.g., a state-law privacy rule). The reporter asks for
worked examples that show how a consent can be expressed under HIPAA
and under a state law where the consent is either a *constraint* on
what the privacy rule otherwise permits (the typical HIPAA case) or an
*exception* that authorizes sharing not otherwise permitted (typical of
several state laws). No cross-referenced Jira tickets were returned by
the cross-reference index for this ticket.

## Details

**Description:**

> The relationship between a patient consent directives, the policy that authorizes such a consent to exist (policyBasis? regulatoryBasis? and/or policyText?) where that is relevant is not clear.  A couple of examples would help, e.g.,
>
> - Under HIPAA (regulatoryBasis further expressed using policBases that is computable and policyText that explains it in English) the provisions of this consent are defined.
> - Under State X law (regulatoryBasis further expressed using policBases that is computable and policyText that explains it in English) the provisions of this consent are defined.
>   Such provisions may be constraints (sharing less than permitted under the privacy rule, typical under HIPAA) or an exception (sharing what is otherwise not permitted to be shared, typical under various state laws).

## Related Zulip Discussions

### [Security and Privacy > Consent.policyRule and provision](https://chat.fhir.org/#narrow/stream/Security%20and%20Privacy/topic/Consent.policyRule%20and%20provision/near/405860423)

Sherry Yuan opened a thread asking how `Consent.provision` should
relate to `Consent.policyRule` (R4) / `Consent.regulatoryBasis` (R5),
including how the OPTIN/OPTOUT codes interact with `provision.type` of
PERMIT/DENY. John Moehrke clarified that in R4 the root-level provision
is a statement about the patient's relationship to the policy and
sub-provisions are the exceptions (pointing to IHE PCF Appendix P), and
he separately argued that OPTIN/OPTOUT are not useful codes at all and
should be retired. He also explained that `policyBasis` was originally
a *non-actionable* listing of the regulations a Consent is addressing,
and suggested that this is more logically expressed at the overriding
policy level — leaving open whether the value set should be refined or
the element removed in favor of a core extension. The discussion led
Sherry to file [FHIR-43331](https://jira.hl7.org/browse/FHIR-43331) and
[FHIR-43326](https://jira.hl7.org/browse/FHIR-43326); it does not reach
a final consensus, and the same conceptual confusion is what
FHIR-54155 is asking to be resolved with worked examples.

Participants: Sherry Yuan, John Moehrke.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Add at least two new narrative-plus-instance worked examples to the
Consent resource page (`Consent` resource, "Notes" / examples area)
that explicitly walk through the `regulatoryBasis` → `policyBasis` →
`policyText` chain:

1. A HIPAA example where `Consent.regulatoryBasis` cites HIPAA, one or
   more `Consent.policyBasis` entries point at a computable policy
   instance (e.g., a referenced `DocumentReference` or `Basic`
   carrying the policy), and `Consent.policyText` (or the policy
   document referenced from `policyBasis`) carries the human-readable
   English version. Show how the `Consent.provision` set models the
   *constraints* (sharing less than HIPAA otherwise permits).
2. A "State X" example where `regulatoryBasis` cites the state law,
   `policyBasis` again points at the computable policy, and the
   `provision` set models *exceptions* — i.e., authorizing sharing the
   privacy rule does not otherwise permit. Use the same instance shape
   as example 1 so the contrast is pure.

In addition, expand the element-level definitions of
`Consent.regulatoryBasis`, `Consent.policyBasis`, and any associated
`policyText` reference to state plainly: (a) `regulatoryBasis` is the
*authorizing regulation*; (b) `policyBasis` is a *computable* expression
of the policy that the regulation requires/permits; (c) policy text is
the *human-readable* expression of the same policy; and (d)
`provision` carries the *constraints or exceptions* that this specific
consent applies on top of that policy.

#### Justification

The reporter is not asking for a model change — they are asking for
disambiguation of three closely related elements that already exist on
the Consent resource. Worked examples plus tightened element
definitions are the lowest-risk way to address this: they do not
change the wire format, do not break existing implementations, and
align directly with what John Moehrke described in the Security and
Privacy Zulip thread (regulation → overriding policy → consent
exceptions). Adding paired HIPAA/State examples also addresses the
recurring confusion Sherry Yuan surfaced about how `provision.type`
relates to the regulatory framing.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Treat the unclear semantics — not just the missing examples — as the
real problem, and refactor the policy-related elements:

- Deprecate `Consent.policyBasis` in its current form and re-anchor the
  computable-policy linkage on a referenced "overriding policy"
  artifact (as Moehrke argued in Zulip), so a Consent points at a
  policy resource which in turn cites its `regulatoryBasis`.
- Constrain `Consent.regulatoryBasis` value set to actual regulations
  (removing OPTIN/OPTOUT-style codes from any inherited binding) and
  document `policyText` strictly as the human-readable rendering of
  the policy referenced from `policyBasis` / overriding policy.
- Then add the HIPAA and State X worked examples from Disposition A on
  top of the refactored model.

#### Justification

The Zulip thread shows that the same confusion has surfaced
repeatedly, and that at least one long-standing Security and Privacy
contributor believes the current `policyBasis` / OPTIN-OPTOUT framing
is structurally wrong, not just under-documented. If the workgroup
shares that view, fixing the model now (in the next normative window)
will prevent the same ticket being filed again under different names.
The trade-off is clear: this is a breaking change for implementers who
already populate `Consent.policyBasis` directly and a coordination
cost for related IGs (e.g., IHE PCF) — those impacts must be weighed
against the long-term clarity gain.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline the change as "Not Persuasive — already addressed by existing
guidance." Point the reporter at the existing element definitions of
`Consent.regulatoryBasis`, `Consent.policyBasis`, and the IHE PCF
Appendix P explainer (referenced in the Zulip thread) as the canonical
description of how the regulation, the overriding policy, and the
consent's provisions interrelate, and close the ticket without
spec-text changes.

#### Justification

The relationship the reporter is asking about *is* documented today
across the element definitions and an IHE companion guide; the gap is
arguably one of discoverability rather than specification. Declining
is defensible if the workgroup judges that adding new examples risks
implying semantics that the normative text does not support, or if it
prefers to wait for a more comprehensive Consent revision rather than
spot-patch examples.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** A

Disposition A directly answers what the reporter actually asked for —
worked examples — at the lowest cost and with the lowest risk to
existing implementations. The Zulip discussion makes clear that even
experts have to reach for an external IHE appendix to explain the
intended layering, which is a strong signal the spec itself should
carry the explanation. Disposition B may well be the right *next*
step, but it is a much larger change and should be triaged as its own
ticket (or rolled together with [FHIR-43326](https://jira.hl7.org/browse/FHIR-43326)
and [FHIR-43331](https://jira.hl7.org/browse/FHIR-43331)) rather than
attached to a clarification request. Disposition C is hard to defend
given that the same confusion has now been raised more than once on
chat and in Jira; declining without adding clarifying material is
likely to lead to the ticket being re-filed.
