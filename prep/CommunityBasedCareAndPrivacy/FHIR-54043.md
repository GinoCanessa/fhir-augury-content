## Summary

The reporter (AndrewFischer) requests that `Consent.subject` be expanded to
allow `RelatedPerson` as an additional permitted reference target. Today,
`Consent.subject` accepts `Patient`, `Practitioner`, `ResearchSubject`, and
`Group`, which leaves out common real-world cases in which a person other
than the patient is the actual subject of a consent — for example, a parent
of a pediatric patient who consents to billing-related obligations because
they are financially responsible for the patient's care. In that scenario
the patient is not the subject of the consent; the parent (modeled in FHIR
as a `RelatedPerson`) is. The single cross-reference Augury surfaces is to
the FHIR core element `Consent.subject` itself, confirming that this is
purely an element-level change request against the Consent resource. No
linked Jira tickets, Zulip threads, or GitHub items were found.

## Details

**Description:**

Consent.subject has reference types: Patient, Practitioner, ResearchSubject,
and Group. Often there are additional consent subjects that do not fit
these categories. For example, a pediatric patient may have a parent that
consents to billing related requirements directly as they are covering the
cost of the patients care. The patient themselves is not the subject but
the parent in that case. I'd suggest adding RelatedPerson as a subject
reference type.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Add `RelatedPerson` to the list of permitted reference targets on
`Consent.subject` in the FHIR Core Consent resource. The element would
move from
`Reference(Patient | Practitioner | ResearchSubject | Group)` to
`Reference(Patient | Practitioner | RelatedPerson | ResearchSubject | Group)`.
No cardinality change; the element remains 0..1. Update the element
definition / short description to clarify that `RelatedPerson` is
appropriate when the subject of the consent is someone other than the
patient (e.g., a parent or guardian acting in a non-patient capacity such
as financial responsibility), and add a usage note distinguishing
`Consent.subject` (the person whose preferences/permissions are being
expressed) from `Consent.grantor` / `Consent.grantee` participants.

#### Justification

The reporter's pediatric-billing example is a legitimate use case that
the current target list cannot model cleanly: a parent acting as the
financially-responsible party is not a `Patient`, not a `Practitioner`,
not a `ResearchSubject`, and not a `Group`. `RelatedPerson` is the
canonical FHIR resource for "a person related to the patient who is not
themselves the patient", so adding it is consistent with how
`RelatedPerson` is already used as a reference target on consent-adjacent
resources (e.g., `Consent.performer` and various provenance/authorship
slots). The change is additive — existing instances using `Patient`,
`Practitioner`, `ResearchSubject`, or `Group` remain valid — so the
backward-compatibility cost is minimal.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Instead of widening `Consent.subject`'s base target list, keep
`Consent.subject` focused on the patient/research subject and instead
recommend that non-patient consent subjects be modeled via the existing
participant/actor structure: record the `RelatedPerson` as a
`Consent.performer` (or, where available, an actor entry on
`Consent.provision.actor`) with an appropriate role code, and use
`Consent.subject` to reference the underlying `Patient` whose data the
consent is governing. Document this pattern explicitly in the Consent
resource notes, with the pediatric-billing example called out as the
canonical illustration.

#### Justification

`Consent.subject` has historically meant "the patient (or research
subject) whose information / care this consent pertains to", and
downstream implementers (registries, EHRs, HIE consent engines) often
key on it for record linkage and access-control evaluation. Repointing
that element at a `RelatedPerson` for billing-style consents could
silently change the semantics for those consumers, even though the
schema change is technically additive. Modeling the parent as a
performer/actor and keeping `subject` pointed at the `Patient` preserves
that "whose record is governed" invariant while still capturing that the
parent is the consenting party. The cost is documentation and
implementer education rather than a wire-format change.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline the request on the grounds that the use case is already
addressable with the current model and that broadening `Consent.subject`
risks diluting its semantics. The pediatric-billing scenario can be
expressed today by referencing the `Patient` from `Consent.subject` and
recording the parent as a participant (e.g., `Consent.performer`,
`Consent.grantor`, or via `Consent.provision.actor`) with a role code
indicating they are acting as the financially responsible party.

#### Justification

`Consent.subject` is widely treated as "the patient this consent is
about", and changing its target list is the kind of small change that
tends to surface in unexpected places — search parameters, profiles,
implementation guides (US Core, IPS, IPA, etc.), and access-control
logic that assumes a `Patient` reference. Without evidence that the
existing performer/actor pattern is insufficient, the additional
flexibility may not justify the downstream churn.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** A

The reporter's example is exactly the kind of scenario the FHIR
information model is supposed to support directly, and the existing
target list already mixes "the person" (`Patient`, `Practitioner`) with
"a research subject" (`ResearchSubject`) and "a population" (`Group`),
so adding `RelatedPerson` does not break a clean conceptual axis — it
rounds out an axis that is already heterogeneous. The change is purely
additive at the schema level, so existing instances and profiles remain
valid; implementations that assume `Consent.subject` always points at a
`Patient` are already wrong against the current spec, since
`Practitioner`, `ResearchSubject`, and `Group` are also allowed today.
The semantic concern raised in Disposition B is real and worth
addressing, but the right mitigation is a usage note on the element
(distinguishing "subject of the consent" from "subject of the underlying
record") rather than refusing to model a legitimate real-world case.
Disposition C effectively asks implementers to encode the parent as a
performer while pointing `subject` at the patient, which inverts the
reporter's actual semantics — the parent really is the subject of *this*
consent, even though the patient is the subject of the care. Accept as
requested, with the documentation tightening from B folded in.
