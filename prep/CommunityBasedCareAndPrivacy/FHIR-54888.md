## Summary

This ticket asks the Scalable Consent Management (FHIR) IG to make sure
that a caregiver or guardian — i.e., somebody acting on behalf of the
patient — has access to the consent, and to explain where that situation
applies in the guide. The reporter raises the question of whether the
caregiver/guardian should be modeled as a "subscriber" in the consent
flow. No related Jira tickets, Zulip discussions, or GitHub items were
surfaced by cross-reference or full-text search, so the request stands on
its own. A resolution was already recorded by the work group ("Not
Persuasive with Modification") indicating the IG will clarify that the
consenter actor may be a patient, caregiver, or guardian, but that
explicitly informing a caregiver/guardian is out of scope of this guide.

## Details

**Description:**

> Ensure that a caregiver or guardian has access to the consent. Explain where this applies–as a subscriber?

**Resolution Description (already recorded on the ticket):**

> The consenter can be the patient or a caregiver or guardian. The use
> cases and operations will support the consenter as part of the
> process. But the explicit informing of a guardian or caregiver is out
> of scope of this guide. We will be defining our actors and will make
> it clear what the "consenter" actor entails.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Update the Scalable Consent Management (FHIR) IG to:

1. Add explicit narrative in the use-case / actors section stating that
   the **consenter** may be the patient, a **caregiver**, or a
   **guardian**, and that all three are first-class subjects of the
   IG's consent flows.
2. Add a sub-section titled "Caregiver / Guardian access to the
   consent" that walks through how a caregiver or guardian sees,
   reviews, and acts on a Consent (e.g., as the
   `Consent.performer` / `Consent.grantor`, with the patient referenced
   via `Consent.subject` / `Consent.patient`, and any
   `RelatedPerson`-style linkage that the guide chooses to require).
3. Address the reporter's "as a subscriber?" question directly by
   defining whether a caregiver/guardian is a **Subscriber** actor for
   the IG's notification / subscription mechanism, and document the
   matching Subscription / SubscriptionTopic configuration when so.
4. Provide at least one use-case example (e.g., parent of a minor,
   guardian of an incapacitated adult) showing the caregiver/guardian
   exercising consent on behalf of the patient.

#### Justification

The reporter raises a real gap: it is not currently obvious from the
guide whether caregiver/guardian access is a supported scenario or how
to wire it up. Treating the consenter as a single, well-defined actor
who may be the patient or a representative is consistent with FHIR
Consent's existing performer/grantor model and with how PCP/CARIN-style
patient-representative work has been done elsewhere. Calling out
Subscription wiring closes the specific "as a subscriber?" question
rather than leaving it implicit.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach (matches the recorded resolution)

#### Proposal

Adopt the path the work group has already recorded as "Not Persuasive
with Modification":

1. Define the IG's actors explicitly, including a **Consenter** actor
   whose definition makes clear it may be filled by the patient, a
   caregiver, or a guardian. Use that single actor wherever the guide
   today says "patient" in a consent-authoring context.
2. State explicitly, in the actor definition and/or scope section,
   that **explicitly informing** a caregiver or guardian (e.g.,
   notification workflows, attestation that the representative was
   reached) is **out of scope** for this guide and is left to
   implementer policy or other IGs.
3. Do **not** introduce a new "Subscriber" mechanism for caregivers /
   guardians in this version, beyond what the actor definition already
   implies.

#### Justification

This keeps the guide focused on the consent lifecycle (capture,
representation, exchange) without taking on the much larger problem
space of representative-notification, which has policy, jurisdictional,
and identity-proofing concerns that the IG is not currently scoped to
solve. It also still resolves the reporter's underlying concern —
caregiver/guardian access to consent is acknowledged and modeled —
while drawing a clean boundary that future ballot cycles can revisit.
The trade-off vs. Disposition A is that the "as a subscriber?" question
is answered with "no, not in this version" rather than with a worked
Subscription example.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Mark the ticket **Not Persuasive** with no spec change, on the grounds
that the existing FHIR Consent resource already supports caregivers
and guardians via `Consent.performer`, `Consent.grantor`, and
`RelatedPerson`, and that the IG inherits those capabilities without
further narrative.

#### Justification

A pure decline is defensible only if one believes the base resource
documentation is sufficient and that no IG-level clarification is
needed. This is the weakest position because the reporter is explicitly
saying the current guide does not make this clear, and "the base
resource supports it" does not answer the IG-specific question of
whether and how caregiver/guardian access is intended to be used here.
Declining would likely cause the same question to be raised again in a
later ballot cycle.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

Disposition B is the right balance and matches the resolution the work
group has already recorded on the ticket ("Not Persuasive with
Modification"). It commits the guide to an explicit Consenter actor
that covers patient/caregiver/guardian — which is what the reporter
actually needs in order to know that the scenario is supported and
where it applies — while drawing a clear, defensible scope line around
"explicitly informing the caregiver/guardian," which is a separate
problem space (notification, identity proofing, policy) that this IG is
not currently chartered to solve. Disposition A is attractive but risks
expanding the IG's scope mid-cycle by pulling Subscription wiring into
a guide that has not previously committed to it; that is better
deferred to a follow-up ticket once the Consenter actor is in place.
Disposition C should be avoided because it leaves the reporter's
clarity gap unaddressed.
