## Summary

The ticket reports an internal inconsistency in the definition of
`Consent.provision`: the short and long descriptions in FHIR Core use the
two terms "constraint" and "exception" as if they were interchangeable,
even though they are not synonymous. The reporter (Hans Buitendijk) asks
that the wording be reconciled across the short and long descriptions —
either by combining the terms (e.g., "Constraint or exception to the
`Consent.policyRule`") or by selecting one term and using it consistently
in both places. The cross-references confirm the issue is scoped to the
two `Consent` element definitions touched by this wording: `Consent.provision`
itself and the `Consent.policyRule` it acts upon. There are no other
linked Jira tickets, Zulip discussions, or GitHub items associated with
this ticket at this time.

## Details

**Description:**

> The definition of Consent.provision is ambiguous as two different terms
> are used in the short and long description: "constraint" and "exception"
> as if they are interchangeable. They are not synonymous, thus either
> need to both be used together, e.g., "Constraint or exception to the
> Consent.policyRule", or one must be chosen over the other for both.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Reconcile the wording of `Consent.provision` so that the short and long
descriptions use the same terminology. Specifically, change both the
`short` and `definition` of the `Consent.provision` element in the FHIR
Core `Consent` resource to read along the lines of:

- **short:** "Constraint or exception to the base Consent policy"
- **definition:** "An exception or additional constraint to the base
  `Consent.policyRule` (or other policy referenced by the Consent),
  describing what is being permitted or denied."

Apply the same combined "constraint or exception" phrasing in any
narrative/guidance text on the Consent resource page that paraphrases
this definition (e.g., the introduction's discussion of provisions).
No structural change to the resource (cardinality, type, bindings) is
proposed — this is a purely editorial / definitional alignment.

#### Justification

The reporter's underlying concern is real: the two words carry slightly
different meanings ("constraint" implies a narrowing of an existing
permission; "exception" implies a deviation that may either narrow or
broaden it), and FHIR element definitions are normative artefacts where
that distinction matters. Using both terms together makes the intent
explicit — that a provision can act either way relative to the base
`Consent.policyRule` — which matches how implementers actually use the
`type` field (`deny` / `permit`) and the resource's overall opt-in /
opt-out modelling. Editorial alignment of `short` and `definition`
without any structural change is the lowest-risk way to resolve the
ambiguity, and it is consistent with how prior wording-only Consent
tickets have been handled by the work group.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Pick one term and use it consistently in both `short` and `definition`,
rather than combining them. The recommended single term is
**"exception"**, because it covers both narrowing and broadening
deviations from the base policy and is already the term used in the
existing long definition's surrounding prose. The change would be:

- **short:** "Exception to the base Consent policy"
- **definition:** "An exception to the base `Consent.policyRule` (or
  other referenced policy) describing what is being additionally
  permitted or denied."

Add a single sentence to the Consent resource narrative clarifying that
"exception" here encompasses both additional constraints and additional
permissions, controlled by `Consent.provision.type`.

#### Justification

Combining "constraint or exception" (Disposition A) preserves the
ambiguity in a softer form — implementers still have to decide which
applies in their context. Choosing one term forces the spec to commit
to a single conceptual model and lets the narrative explain the nuance
once, rather than repeating a compound noun phrase throughout the
element documentation. "Exception" is the broader, more neutral term
and matches common usage in privacy policy frameworks (XACML, etc.)
where a `provision` is conceptually an exception clause to a base rule.
This keeps the definition shorter and more declarative, which is the
preferred style for FHIR element `short` strings.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline as **editorial / no normative impact**. The two words
"constraint" and "exception" are used in everyday English nearly
synonymously when discussing exceptions to a base rule, and the
existing long definition gives implementers enough context to
understand what `Consent.provision` represents. No interoperability
behaviour depends on this distinction.

#### Justification

The Consent resource has a long history of substantive open issues
(policy modelling, base/provision interaction, mapping to legal frame-
works); spending an editorial cycle on the choice of "constraint" vs.
"exception" without an associated implementer report of actual mis-
implementation may not be the best use of work-group time. If the
distinction were causing systems to behave differently, the ticket
would normally include such an example, and it does not.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** A

The reporter has correctly identified that two non-synonymous terms are
being used interchangeably in a normative element definition, and the
fix is cheap, low-risk, and editorial only. Disposition A keeps both
concepts visible — which matches how `Consent.provision` is actually
used (via the `permit` / `deny` `type` and the `actor`, `action`,
`purpose` filters, a provision may either narrow *or* broaden the base
policy) — while removing the impression that the spec is using the two
words as synonyms. Disposition B is also defensible, but committing to
a single term ("exception" or "constraint") risks over-narrowing how
implementers read the element. Disposition C is not recommended: even
though the issue is editorial, fixing self-inconsistent normative text
is exactly the kind of low-cost cleanup that improves the spec's
authority without any backwards-compatibility cost. The work group
should adopt the combined "constraint or exception" phrasing and apply
it to both `short` and `definition` of `Consent.provision`.
