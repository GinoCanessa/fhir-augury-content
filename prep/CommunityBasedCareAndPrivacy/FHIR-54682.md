## Summary

The reporter (John Moehrke) observes that the Scalable Consent Management
IG (2026Jan) currently mandates two requirements that presuppose the
Consent Administration / Decision System actually performs access-control
decisions and writes the corresponding disclosure audit events. Specifically:

- **§OP12** declares that the Record Disclosure Operation `SHALL` be used
  when a system accesses a Consent instance to determine whether information
  can be accessed.
- **§OP8** declares that consent administration services `SHALL` be able
  to record and retrieve disclosures of when a consent was accessed to
  determine whether patient information could be accessed.

The reporter asserts these `SHALL` requirements are not defensible because
the access control decision and enforcement are explicitly out of scope
for this IG — the system being profiled does not necessarily make the
decision or generate the disclosure audit, so it cannot be obliged to
"record" them. The reporter proposes that, at most, the Consent
Administration System can be required to respond to AuditEvent searches
for CRUD actions performed against the Consent resource itself, and that
those returned events be required to conform to the IHE-BALP CRUD
AuditEvent profiles.

No cross-referenced Jira tickets, Zulip threads, or GitHub items were
returned by the FHIR-Augury cross-reference and search APIs for this
ticket key, so the request is being assessed in isolation against the
text of the linked IG sections and the accompanying resolution note.

## Details

**Description:**

- [§OP12](https://hl7.org/fhir/us/consent-management/2026Jan/technical.html#ci-c-OP12)
  : The [Record Disclosure Operation](https://hl7.org/fhir/us/consent-management/2026Jan/OperationDefinition-record-disclosure.html)
  SHALL be used when a system accesses a Consent instance for determining
  whether information can be accessed.

This can't be a requirement as the access control decision and enforcement
is not in-scope.

At best, the Consent Administration System could be expected to respond to
Searches against AuditEvent for CRUD actions against the Consent resource.
One could require that the results returned are compliant with the CRUD
profiles of AuditEvent defined in IHE-BALP.

**Resolution Description (Persuasive, 2026-02-20):**

We will be splitting the Consent Decision System into a part that is
out-of-scope — the decision making and the process of recording audit
events — and a part that is in-scope — the ability to query a system to
find out about its audit events.

**Comments:**

- **john_moehrke (2026-01-18):** Notes that the same critique applies to
  [§OP8](https://hl7.org/fhir/us/consent-management/2026Jan/technical.html#ci-c-OP8)
  — "consent administration services SHALL be able to record and retrieve
  disclosures of when a consent was accessed to determine whether patient
  information could be accessed." Recording a disclosure is a side-effect
  of an access-control decision that is also out of scope, so the `SHALL`
  to *record* cannot stand on the same footing as the `SHALL` to *retrieve*.
- **david.pyke (2026-02-18):** Asks for clarification and suggests adding
  text that explains what is in-scope vs. out-of-scope, indicating the
  workgroup considered an editorial / scoping fix rather than wholesale
  removal.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Remove the `SHALL` requirements that oblige the Consent Administration
System to *invoke* or *record* disclosure audit events, on the basis that
the underlying access-control decision and enforcement are out of scope.
Concretely, in the 2026Jan Scalable Consent Management IG technical page:

- **§OP12:** Remove the conformance statement that the Record Disclosure
  Operation `SHALL` be used when a system accesses a Consent instance to
  determine access. Either delete the bullet entirely or downgrade it to
  a non-normative note ("when an access-control system accesses a Consent
  to make a decision, it MAY use the Record Disclosure Operation to record
  that disclosure"). The `OperationDefinition/record-disclosure` artifact
  can remain as an optional capability for systems that *do* perform the
  decision.
- **§OP8:** Drop the "record … disclosures" half of the `SHALL` and keep
  only the "retrieve" half — i.e., consent administration services `SHALL`
  be able to *retrieve* disclosures (via AuditEvent search) but are not
  required to *record* them.
- Add a scoping paragraph (per david.pyke's comment) that explicitly
  states the access-control decision/enforcement and the generation of
  disclosure AuditEvents are out of scope for this IG, and that
  conforming systems are only required to expose disclosure audits that
  they happen to hold.

#### Justification

This matches the reporter's substantive concern and the workgroup's own
"Persuasive" resolution note, which already commits to splitting the
Consent Decision System into out-of-scope (decision + audit recording)
and in-scope (audit retrieval) halves. Conformance verbs in an IG should
constrain only behaviour the IG actually scopes; mandating an operation
that a conformant actor may have no role in performing creates
untestable conformance and discourages adoption. The Record Disclosure
OperationDefinition itself is harmless and useful, so leaving it as an
optional capability preserves the design work without overreaching.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Keep both §OP8 and §OP12 in the IG but split each into two distinct
conformance statements scoped to two different actors / capability
profiles:

- A new **"Consent Decision System"** capability (or actor) where the
  `SHALL` to invoke the Record Disclosure Operation and the `SHALL` to
  record disclosures live — clearly labelled as applicable only to
  systems that actually make access-control decisions against Consent.
- The existing **"Consent Administration System"** capability where only
  a `SHALL` to *respond to AuditEvent search for CRUD on Consent* lives,
  with the additional constraint that returned `AuditEvent` resources
  conform to the IHE-BALP CRUD AuditEvent profiles (as the reporter
  suggested).

In effect, the Record Disclosure Operation requirement is preserved for
systems that opt into the decision-making role, while the administration
profile is narrowed to what an administration system can actually be held
to.

#### Justification

This preserves the design intent that disclosure auditing is an
important interoperability surface for consent, while honouring the
reporter's point that the *administration* system cannot be held
responsible for behaviours it does not perform. Splitting by capability
is more aligned with how other privacy IGs (and IHE-BALP itself) layer
actor responsibilities, and it lets implementers that *do* combine
decision + administration in one server make a single conformance claim.
The trade-off versus Disposition A is added IG complexity (two
capabilities, more conformance text) and a longer change window — but
the conformance burden is correctly placed.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Reject the change. Retain §OP8 and §OP12 as-is on the basis that the IG
is intentionally aspirational about disclosure auditing and that any
conformant Consent Administration System is expected to either implement
the decision/enforcement role itself or coordinate with a paired system
that does, such that the `SHALL` to record and to use the Record
Disclosure Operation is satisfiable in deployment.

#### Justification

Defensible only if the workgroup intends the Consent Administration
System to always be co-deployed with a Consent Decision System (or to
always perform that role itself). In that reading, the `SHALL` is a
deployment-architecture constraint rather than a per-actor capability
constraint, and weakening it would lose an important conformance hook
that ensures disclosures are auditable downstream.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** A

The workgroup's own resolution note has already committed to splitting
"decision + recording" from "retrieval" and treating only the latter as
in-scope; that is exactly what Disposition A operationalises with the
minimum amount of structural churn to the 2026Jan IG. Disposition B is
defensible and is arguably the *cleanest* long-term shape, but it
introduces a second capability/actor that the IG does not currently
define and would require a more invasive edit pass — better tracked as
a follow-up if the workgroup later wants to explicitly profile a
Consent Decision System. Disposition C is hard to defend given the
reporter's correct observation (echoed by the resolution) that the
underlying behaviour is genuinely out of scope; keeping `SHALL`
statements that conformant actors cannot necessarily satisfy
undermines the IG's testability. Pair the §OP12 / §OP8 edits with a
short scoping paragraph (per david.pyke's comment) so future readers
understand why the requirement was relaxed.
