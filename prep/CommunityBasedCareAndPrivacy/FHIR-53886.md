## Summary

The reporter (EssienGe) requests that the Consent resource specification be
clarified with respect to the relationship and intended interplay between
several elements that appear to have overlapping functionality:
`Consent.provision`, `Consent.policyBasis`, `Consent.policyText`,
`Consent.sourceAttachment`, `Consent.sourceReference`, and the
`RegulatoryBasis` concept. The narrative in §6.2.1.1 frames `provision` and
`policyBasis` as two alternative mechanisms for recording computable rules,
suggesting they are mutually exclusive, but §6.2.6 describes a usage pattern
in which `Consent.provision[0].type` is used together with `policyBasis`
("opt-out recorded with an opt-in policy where `provision[0].type = deny`"),
which contradicts the alternative-mechanism reading. The ticket asks (1) which
elements are independent vs. dependent, (2) whether they may co-exist on the
same Consent instance and how to interpret them when they do, (3) what
distinguishes `policyText` from `sourceAttachment`/`sourceReference`, and
(4) which element should be used to attach a privacy notice that serves as
the basis of consent. There are no cross-referenced Jira tickets and no
related Zulip threads or GitHub items; the only cross-reference recorded is a
mention of the FHIR artifact `Consent.provision`. The request is editorial /
clarification in nature and may also lead to a tightening of the Consent
narrative or new invariants.

## Details

**Description:**

This artifact includes several elements with overlapping functionality, such
as provision, policyBasis, policyText, sourceAttachment, sourceReference, and
RegulatoryBasis. Further explanation may be necessary to clearly define the
distinctions and relationships among these components, including whether any
element is depend on another and if all can coexist simultaneously — and if
so, clarification on their respective roles.

**For instance:**

6.2.1.1 specifies that the Consent resource provides two mechanisms for
recording computable rules:

- the provision structure
- the policyBasis

This appears to suggest that they are separate schemes and should not be used
together. If this interpretation is correct, it would be beneficial to
establish constraints to reinforce this separation.

However, section 6.2.6 states: "Policies attached to PolicyBasis should be
written using positive language. For an example, a patient wanting to opt-out
will be recorded with an opt-in policy where the Consent.provision[0].type
indicates 'deny'." This seems to imply that provision may depend on
policyBasis.

**Another point of clarification:**

What distinguishes policyText from sourceAttachment or sourceReference?

When including a privacy notice as a basis for consent, which element should
be utilized?

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Treat the ticket as **Persuasive – With Modifier (editorial + narrative)**
against the Consent resource. Specifically:

1. In Consent §6.2.1.1 ("Computable Consent Rules"), rewrite the introduction
   to state explicitly that `Consent.provision` and `Consent.policyBasis` are
   **complementary**, not mutually exclusive: `policyBasis` carries the
   human-/machine-authored policy artifact (Resource or URL) that the consent
   is **based on**, while `provision` carries the runtime, decidable rule
   tree that an access-decision engine evaluates. State that an instance MAY
   carry either, both, or neither, and describe the intent of each
   combination.
2. Reconcile §6.2.6 with §6.2.1.1 by clarifying that the "positive language"
   guidance applies to the policy referenced by `policyBasis`, and that when
   both are present `provision` is the authoritative computable form derived
   from (or layered over) the `policyBasis` policy. Provide a worked
   opt-out-as-opt-in example matching the §6.2.6 wording.
3. Add a definitional table to the Consent narrative that contrasts
   `policyText` (the raw, human-readable text of the consent the patient
   agreed to — typically inline `Attachment`), `sourceAttachment` /
   `sourceReference` (the *signed/captured* source artifact that **is** the
   consent record, e.g., the scanned PDF or DocumentReference), and
   `policyBasis` (the *policy* the consent operationalizes). Recommend
   `policyText` for an attached/inline privacy notice that the patient
   acknowledged, and `policyBasis` (Reference or URL) when the privacy notice
   is an externally maintained policy.
4. Add a brief paragraph clarifying `RegulatoryBasis` (the regulation under
   which the consent is collected, e.g., HIPAA, GDPR) versus `policyBasis`
   (the specific organisational policy implementing that regulation).
5. Do **not** add a hard invariant forbidding co-occurrence of `provision`
   and `policyBasis`; instead add non-normative guidance on intended usage
   patterns and an optional best-practice note.

#### Justification

The narrative inconsistency between §6.2.1.1 and §6.2.6 is real and is
exactly what the reporter identified, so a clarification is warranted. The
elements have distinct, well-established semantics in the Consent design
(policy vs. computable rules vs. captured artifact vs. regulatory frame) and
implementations in the wild already use them in combination — a hard
"either/or" invariant would break valid existing usage. The change is
editorial, low risk, and confined to the Consent resource intro / usage
sections, so it is a good fit for an FMM-mature normative resource where
breaking-change tolerance is low.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Treat the ticket as **Persuasive – With Modifier (narrative + invariant)**:
do everything in Disposition A, **and additionally**:

1. Add a normative `Consent` invariant warning (severity = `warning`, not
   `error`) that flags instances which set `policyBasis` but also populate
   `provision.type` with a value that contradicts the referenced policy's
   stated default (e.g., `policyBasis` of an opt-in policy together with a
   top-level `provision.type = deny` and no nested rules). Because this
   cannot be fully checked statically, scope the invariant to the
   self-consistent subset (e.g., "if `provision` is present and
   `policyBasis` is present, `provision` SHALL be expressed in positive
   language consistent with the policy").
2. Mark `Consent.policyText` as deprecated-in-favour-of either
   `sourceAttachment` (for inline/scanned text) or `policyBasis` (for the
   policy URL), to remove one of the overlapping elements over time. Keep it
   present for backward compatibility but add a "Use instead" note.
3. Add an example bundle to the Consent page demonstrating each of the four
   intended patterns: provision-only, policyBasis-only, both-together
   (opt-out-as-opt-in), and source-captured-only.

#### Justification

This goes beyond a pure documentation fix and reduces the *structural*
overlap that motivated the ticket. The warning-level invariant catches the
class of mistake the §6.2.6 example was trying to prevent without breaking
existing instances. Soft-deprecating `policyText` aligns with FHIR's general
preference for `Attachment`/`Reference` over free-text fields and removes one
of the elements the reporter found ambiguous. The trade-off vs. Disposition
A is increased implementer burden (new invariant, new deprecation note) and
the risk of community pushback on touching a normative element, which is why
the deprecation is *soft* (no removal) and the invariant is *warning-level*.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Resolve as **Not Persuasive**. Close the ticket with a comment pointing the
reporter to the existing element definitions on the Consent page, the
`policyBasis` element comments, and the existing examples, asserting that
the elements are sufficiently distinct (`policyBasis` = referenced policy
artifact; `provision` = computable rules; `policyText`/`sourceAttachment`/
`sourceReference` = captured consent artifact; `RegulatoryBasis` =
regulatory frame), and that §6.2.6 is illustrative of one valid combined
usage pattern rather than evidence of a true contradiction.

#### Justification

The element definitions do, individually, already describe each role, and
the Consent resource is a mature, normative artifact where editorial churn
has a real cost (re-publication, downstream IG re-balloting, implementer
re-validation). If the reporter has not produced a concrete confusion in an
implementation, the request can be deferred. This disposition is defensible
only if the workgroup judges the §6.2.1.1 / §6.2.6 wording to already be
internally consistent on careful reading.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** A

The reporter has identified a genuine narrative inconsistency between
§6.2.1.1 (which presents `provision` and `policyBasis` as alternative
mechanisms) and §6.2.6 (which uses them together). That is an editorial
defect, not a misreading, so Disposition C understates the issue. Disposition
B's structural changes (a new invariant and soft-deprecation of `policyText`)
go beyond what the ticket asks for, touch a normative resource, and are
likely to attract substantive comments of their own — they would be better
raised as separate change requests once the narrative is first cleaned up.
Disposition A delivers exactly what the reporter asked for (clarity on
distinctions and on co-occurrence) with minimal risk to implementers, and it
sets up Disposition B's structural changes as a follow-on if the workgroup
later decides the overlap is also a structural problem.
