## Summary

Craig Newman is questioning the design of `Consent.scope` within the
**Scalable Consent Management** IG. In that IG, `Consent.scope` is
fixed to the value `patient-privacy`, yet `Consent.scope` is
simultaneously exposed as both a **subscription topic filter element**
and a **search element** in the same profile. If every conformant
instance is required to carry the same fixed value, scope cannot
meaningfully partition either the search space or a subscription topic
— filtering on it is a no-op for IG-conformant resources. The reporter
asks two related questions:

1. Is the IG implicitly assuming that a consent administration system
   will also be handling Consent resources for purposes outside of
   patient privacy (in which case scope filtering would matter, but
   only against non-conformant instances)?
2. Would `Consent.category` make more sense as the filter / search
   element, given that category is the dimension along which conformant
   instances actually vary?

The ticket has already been triaged with resolution **Persuasive**, and
the resolution narrative records the workgroup's intent to drop scope
as a subscription topic filter and replace it with category, which can
plausibly carry differentiating values in a patient-privacy-scoped IG.
There are no explicitly-linked Jira tickets and no useful GitHub
cross-references (the one surfaced commit is a typo against
`FHIR-53574`, not this ticket). Historical Zulip discussion in
**Security and Privacy > Consent.scope** anticipated exactly this kind
of redundancy concern between `scope` and `category`.

## Details

**Description:**

> If Consent.scope is fixed to "patient-privacy" why is scope a
> subscription filter element and search element? Does the IG assume
> that while the scope of the IG is patient privacy, that a consent
> administration system will also be working with consent resources for
> other purposes? Would .category make more sense for filtering and
> searching?

**Resolution Description:**

> We should not have scope as a subscription topic filter. We will
> change that to category as that could conceivably be a valid filter.

## Related Zulip Discussions

### [Security and Privacy > Consent.scope](https://chat.fhir.org/#narrow/stream/Security%20and%20Privacy/topic/Consent.scope)

This 2021 thread (18 messages, kicked off by Mohammad Jafari and
referencing FHIR-31903) discussed the CBCP proposal to phase out
`Consent.scope` and capture its semantics through multiplicity in
`Consent.category`. **David Pyke** argued that `scope` and `category`
are duplicative and that any required differentiation should be done at
the profile level. **John Moehrke** initially objected, framing
`Consent.scope` as analogous to `Observation.category` (high-level
partitioning) and `Consent.category` as analogous to `Observation.code`
(specific kind), and noted that with two distinct elements you can
write rules without slicing. **Lloyd McKenzie** argued the opposite —
that there is no equivalent of `Observation.code` in Consent, that
category is `0..*` (which makes no sense for a code-style element), and
that consent categorization is for discovery and does not drive
provision behavior, so a single `category` element with a preferred
value set is sufficient. The thread did not reach explicit consensus
but established that the redundancy concern is well-known and that
`category` is widely viewed as the more appropriate axis for
filtering / discovery.

Participants: Mohammad Jafari, John Moehrke, David Pyke, Lloyd McKenzie.

### [terminology > Consent Scope Codes](https://chat.fhir.org/#narrow/stream/terminology/topic/Consent%20Scope%20Codes)

A separate, terminology-focused thread that surfaces concerns that the
codes bound to `Consent.scope` are not orthogonal or complete (notably
the `adr` code being included in the value-set expansion when its
status is debatable). It is referenced here because it reinforces the
broader observation that `Consent.scope` itself is a contested element
even before the IG-level fixed-value concern raised by FHIR-53754.

Participants: David Pyke and others on the Terminology stream.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Adopt the workgroup's stated resolution verbatim against the
**Scalable Consent Management** IG:

1. **SubscriptionTopic** — Remove the filter on `Consent.scope` from
   the IG's Consent-related `SubscriptionTopic` definition and replace
   it with a filter on `Consent.category`. Because `Consent.scope` is
   fixed in the profile, the existing filter is non-discriminating;
   `Consent.category` is `0..*` and is the dimension along which
   conformant instances actually vary, making it a meaningful filter.
2. **SearchParameter / profile search element** — Remove
   `Consent.scope` from the IG's exposed search elements (or, at
   minimum, demote it from a profile-promoted search element) and
   ensure `Consent.category` is exposed as a search element with
   appropriate documentation. Retain the base-spec `_scope` search
   parameter only as inherited.
3. **Profile narrative** — Add a brief note in the Consent profile's
   description explaining that because `scope` is fixed to
   `patient-privacy`, discovery and subscription filtering for
   conformant resources is performed against `category`.

#### Justification

This is exactly what the resolution description commits to and what
the reporter is asking for. It is internally consistent: when a
profile fixes an element to a single value, exposing that element as
a filter or search axis on profile-conformant data is by definition
a no-op and is misleading to implementers, who may reasonably assume
the element is variable. The 2021 Zulip discussion in
**Security and Privacy > Consent.scope** establishes broad community
agreement (Pyke, McKenzie, and ultimately the workgroup itself in
FHIR-31903) that `category` is the appropriate axis for consent
discovery and filtering. The change is low-risk for this IG because
no conformant instance carries a varying `scope` value.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Resolve the inconsistency by **relaxing the profile constraint on
`Consent.scope`** instead of rewiring the filters. Concretely:

1. Change `Consent.scope` in the IG's Consent profile from `fixed =
   patient-privacy` to `pattern = patient-privacy` (or a `required`
   binding to a value set that includes `patient-privacy` plus any
   adjacent codes the consent administration system handles), thereby
   permitting conformant resources to carry related but non-identical
   scope codes.
2. Keep the existing `SubscriptionTopic` filter and search element on
   `Consent.scope`, since the element would once again carry meaningful
   variation.
3. Optionally also expose `Consent.category` as a secondary filter and
   search element so implementers can choose either axis.

#### Justification

This addresses the reporter's second hypothesis directly: if the IG
genuinely expects a consent administration system to handle Consent
resources for purposes beyond patient privacy, then constraining
`scope` to a single value is itself the wrong design choice, and the
filter / search element on `scope` is correct. Relaxing the
constraint is a single small profile change rather than coordinated
edits to the profile, the SubscriptionTopic, and the SearchParameter.
The trade-off versus Disposition A is significant: it broadens the
IG's surface area, weakens the IG's "scalable patient-privacy
consent" framing, and requires a vocabulary decision (which other
scope codes are in scope), so it should only be chosen if the
workgroup affirmatively wants the IG to handle non-patient-privacy
consents. This is contrary to the resolution already recorded on the
ticket.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline to make any change. Document in the IG that the
`Consent.scope` filter and search element are intentionally retained
to support **mixed-tenancy consent administration servers** that hold
Consent resources both inside and outside the IG's profile, and that
the filter/search exists to let such servers partition by scope when
the responding endpoint is shared across multiple consent IGs.

#### Justification

This is the most defensible "do nothing" reading: a real-world
Consent service is rarely IG-pure, so a search/subscription axis on
`scope` is useful at the **server** level even when every
IG-conformant instance carries the same fixed value. However, this
position contradicts the resolution already recorded by the workgroup
("We should not have scope as a subscription topic filter. We will
change that to category…") and leaves a documented inconsistency in
the IG that future implementers will repeatedly raise. It is a weak
disposition unless the workgroup explicitly reverses course on the
recorded resolution.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** A

The ticket is already marked **Persuasive** and the workgroup has
recorded a clear remediation in the resolution description: drop
`scope` as a subscription topic filter and switch to `category`.
Disposition A simply executes that decision and rounds it out by also
addressing the symmetric inconsistency on the search element (which
the reporter explicitly called out and which the resolution narrative
does not). It is internally consistent — fixing an element and then
filtering on it is a contradiction — and aligns with the long-running
community position visible in the 2021 Zulip discussion that
`Consent.category` is the appropriate axis for consent discovery and
filtering. Disposition B is only preferable if CBCP wants to broaden
the IG's scope beyond patient privacy, which is a much larger
architectural decision that this ticket does not raise. Disposition C
contradicts the workgroup's already-recorded resolution and leaves a
known inconsistency in the published IG. Recommend the reviewers
endorse Disposition A and confirm whether the search-element change
(beyond the subscription filter change already promised in the
resolution) is in scope for the same patch.
