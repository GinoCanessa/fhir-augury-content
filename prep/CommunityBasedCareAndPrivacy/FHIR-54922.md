## Summary

The reporter, Virginia Lorenzi, observes that the *Scalable Consent
Management (FHIR)* specification uses an inconsistent and unclear
collection of terms for the actors and systems in play — including
"consenter," "patient," "client system" (which could be an EHR),
"consent management services," "server system," "consent system," and
"consent administration system." She notes that formal definitions do
not appear until §6.1 and that even those definitions are confusing
(e.g., "consenter" is not unpacked into patient/guardian/proxy; the
client side is described as an "EHR" even though an EHR can serve as
both client and server). She recommends that the spec (1) define a
small, fixed set of actors and actor systems up front, with clear
language and concrete examples (e.g., "patient portal module of an
EHR" or "PHR" rather than just "EHR"), and (2) then use those names
consistently throughout the specification.

The ticket has been triaged with resolution **Persuasive**. The
recorded resolution direction is to enumerate the actors and systems,
provide explicit definitions for each, and then audit the entire
specification to use these terms consistently. No related Jira tickets,
Zulip threads, or GitHub items were surfaced by the FHIR Augury
cross-reference / search indexes for this ticket key, so the request
stands on its own.

## Details

**Description:**

The specification is inconsistent and unclear as to the actor systems
in play. Consenter, patient, client system (which could be EHR),
consent management services, server system, consent system, consent
administration system are all used. Recommend that you pick a few
terms and use them consistently. They are formally defined I think but
not until 6.1. However, the definitions are very confusing. What is a
consenter? If that could be a patient, guardian, proxy be more
explicit and explain that before using the term everywhere. If there
is a system used by the patient/guardian/proxy to request/create/revoke
consents, please give that a clear name and define it. I am concerned
with the definition is 6.1 describing it as an EHR, since an EHR has
many functions and could be both the client and server. If instead you
said the patient portal module of an EHR or a personal health record
(since it is a FHIR client seems that PHR might be a more common use
case anyway) because most EHRs would not need FHIR to connect their
internal modules. Recommend that right in the beginning you define the
actors and actor systems with clear language and examples that allows
people to see what human roles are interacting with what systems. And
then you consistently use those actors everywhere in the spec.

**Resolution Description (workgroup-recorded):**

We are going to list the set of actors and systems and provide
definitions of them. We will then scour the specification to make sure
that we consistently use these names.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Adopt the resolution direction already recorded on the ticket: produce
a normative **Actors and Systems** section in the *Scalable Consent
Management (FHIR)* IG, placed at the front of the specification (well
before today's §6.1), that:

1. Enumerates the human/organizational actors — minimally:
   *Consenter* (with explicit sub-roles **Patient**, **Guardian**,
   **Personal Representative / Proxy**), *Custodian / Information
   Holder*, *Requester / Relying Party*, and *Consent Administrator*.
2. Enumerates the actor **systems** — minimally: *Consenter-facing
   System* (with examples: **patient portal module of an EHR**, **PHR
   application**, dedicated consent app), *Consent Management Service*
   (the FHIR server hosting `Consent` resources), *Custodian System*
   (the system holding the data the consent governs, which may or may
   not be co-resident with the Consent Management Service), and
   *Requester System*.
3. For each actor and system, gives a one-paragraph definition plus a
   concrete example, and explicitly notes when an EHR can play more
   than one role (e.g., an EHR may simultaneously be a Consenter-facing
   System, a Custodian System, and host a Consent Management Service).
4. Replaces every other ad-hoc term used elsewhere in the IG
   ("consent system," "consent administration system," "client
   system," "server system," etc.) with one of the canonical names
   defined above, and removes or rewrites the partial definitions
   currently in §6.1 so they redirect to the new front-of-spec section.

A reviewer's checklist (search the rendered IG for each retired term
and confirm zero remaining occurrences) should be attached to the
tracker for the editorial pass.

#### Justification

This is exactly what the reporter asked for and what the work group has
already agreed to ("Persuasive"). Defining a fixed actor/system
vocabulary up front and applying it consistently is a standard FHIR
authoring pattern (e.g., the Subscriptions framework's *Subscriber /
Notification Receiver* actors, the SMART app launch *EHR / Authorization
Server / App* actors, IHE profiles' explicit actor diagrams). It
removes a class of implementer confusion that is especially damaging in
a privacy/consent specification, where actor identity drives the
permission model. The work is purely editorial — no normative element
or constraint changes — so the cost is bounded, and it sets the IG up
for cleaner trial use feedback.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Rather than introducing only a glossary, ground the actor model in a
**reusable artifact** that downstream IGs can cite by URL:

1. Stand up a small, named **Actor Model** page in the IG (e.g.,
   `actors.html`) that not only defines each actor and system in prose
   but also publishes them as **`ActorDefinition` resources** (and the
   pairings as `Requirements` / `ObligationsAndCapabilities` where
   applicable). Use canonical URLs under the IG's namespace so other
   IGs and profiles can `actor` -reference them.
2. Add an actor diagram (sequence + context) showing the typical flows
   (Consenter creates consent via Consenter-facing System → Consent
   Management Service; Requester System asks Consent Management
   Service for an authorization decision; Custodian System enforces
   the decision and writes `AuditEvent`).
3. Tag every existing capability statement, profile, and example in
   the IG with the actor(s) that play it, replacing the ad-hoc terms
   the reporter listed.
4. Keep a short prose glossary at the top for readers who do not need
   the machine-readable form, but treat the `ActorDefinition` set as
   the source of truth.

#### Justification

The reporter's underlying concern is that implementers cannot tell
which human role is interacting with which system. A
prose-only glossary fixes that for careful readers but is easy to
drift from in future ballots. Publishing first-class
`ActorDefinition` resources makes the actor set discoverable to
tooling, allows other consent-adjacent IGs (e.g., privacy-focused
US-Core derivatives, IHE-bridged profiles) to reference exactly the
same actors instead of inventing parallel terms, and gives the work
group a concrete artifact to review at every subsequent ballot rather
than re-litigating prose. The cost is moderately higher than
Disposition A — authoring and rendering the resources, validating
URLs, and updating profiles to cite them — but the durability is
substantially higher and aligns with where R5/R6 actor modelling has
been heading.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline the request on the grounds that the existing §6.1 definitions
already cover the actor set, and that the editorial cleanup the
reporter is asking for can be deferred to a future ballot cycle or
handled implicitly via reader feedback on individual sections.
Document on the ticket that no further action will be taken in the
current ballot.

#### Justification

The only honest defense for declining is "scope" — the work group is
already past the deadline for substantive editorial change in this
ballot, and a global terminology audit can be expensive if the IG is
large. However, this rationale is weak in practice: the resolution
direction has already been accepted as Persuasive, and consent
specifications carry an outsized risk of harm (mis-routed PHI,
unenforceable revocations) when actor identity is ambiguous. Decline
is included for completeness but is not credibly defensible against
the reporter's evidence.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** A, with the option to graduate toward B in
a follow-up cycle.

The work group has already accepted the request as Persuasive, the
reporter's complaint is concrete and well-evidenced, and the change is
editorial rather than normative — so Disposition A is the lowest-risk
way to deliver the agreed resolution inside the current ballot. The
key practical difference between A and B is *durability*: A fixes the
text today, while B turns the actor set into a referenceable artifact
that resists drift over future ballots. Because the IG is still young
and likely to be cited by adjacent privacy/consent IGs, the work group
should plan to graduate the prose definitions produced under A into
`ActorDefinition` resources (Disposition B) at the next opportunity,
treating the front-of-spec section produced now as the source text for
those resources. Disposition C should be rejected — declining a
"Persuasive" terminology fix in a privacy specification would actively
harm implementer trust.
