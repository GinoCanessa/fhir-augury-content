## Summary

Hans Buitendijk reports that the Scalable Consent Management implementation
guide is silent on how a subscriber establishes the trust or authority needed
to subscribe to consent changes for a particular patient. Managing such
subscriptions one connection and one patient at a time will not scale. He
recommends that, at minimum, the IG acknowledge this gap with an
out-of-scope statement and plan to address it in a future version. The IG
should consider how a patient can share their consent administrator's
"address" as part of the authorization process when establishing a
relationship with a data source, and should also address how secondary data
sources are authorized and how the patient is informed about and consents to
that downstream sharing so the patient can remain in the loop where
appropriate. The ticket has been triaged as Persuasive; no related Jira
tickets, Zulip threads, or GitHub items were surfaced by cross-reference
lookups.

## Details

**Description:**

> The guide is silent on how one establishes trust/authority to be able to
> subscribe to consent changes for a particular patient. Having to manage
> that one connection and patient at a time will not scale.
>
> Recommend that it at least is acknowledged through an out-of-scope
> statement, while planning it for a next version. It should consider how a
> patient can share their consent administrator's "address" as part of an
> authorization process as they establish a relationship with a data source.
> That still leaves open how a secondary data source would be authorized and
> how the patient is then informed about that and can authorize access are
> all questions to be addressed to enable scaled access with the patient in
> the loop where appropriate.

**Resolution Description (Persuasive):**

> We will add a section on security and privacy that talks about:
> - the authorization to exchange consents between systems
> - the fact that authorization to exchange between systems implies an
>   authorization for those systems to subscribe to receive changes to the
>   consent
> - the patient needs to be involved in the conversation around the
>   authorizations between systems, whether within network or outside the
>   network
> - that some times the patient might revoke authorization to share which
>   would revoke the subscription capability
>
> We will also beef up our use case on exchange consents to make it clear
> that the patient has authorized the exchange.
>
> We will include the conformance statement: "When a patient revokes a
> consent, when the Revoke Operation is sent, any outstanding subscriptions
> against that consent SHALL be terminated."

**Comments:**

- **david.pyke (2026-02-18):** Add a statement that Subscription
  authorization is a policy problem. Potentially add a Privacy and Security
  section.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Adopt the resolution already drafted on the ticket. Add a new "Security and
Privacy" (or "Privacy and Security") section to the Scalable Consent
Management IG that covers:

1. Authorization required for two systems to exchange consent resources at
   all (out-of-band trust establishment between source and subscriber).
2. The principle that a system authorized to exchange consent for a patient
   is, by that same authorization, permitted to subscribe to changes for
   that patient's consent — i.e., subscription authority is derived from
   exchange authority rather than negotiated per-subscription.
3. Patient involvement in establishing those inter-system authorizations,
   both within a trust network and across networks, including how a patient
   shares their consent administrator's endpoint as part of onboarding with
   a data source.
4. Revocation semantics: when a patient revokes the underlying authorization
   to share, the corresponding subscription capability is also revoked.

Strengthen the existing "exchange consents" use case so that the narrative
explicitly shows the patient authorizing the exchange (not just the systems
agreeing to it).

Add a normative conformance statement to the IG: **"When a patient revokes a
consent, when the Revoke Operation is sent, any outstanding subscriptions
against that consent SHALL be terminated."**

Frame remaining questions (secondary/downstream data sources, notifying the
patient about subsequent re-authorizations) as explicitly out-of-scope for
this version with a forward-looking note for a next version.

#### Justification

This matches the "Persuasive" resolution already on the ticket and the
consensus reflected in david.pyke's comment that subscription authorization
is fundamentally a policy problem rather than a wire-protocol problem. It
closes the documentation gap Hans raised without inventing a new mechanism
the IG isn't yet ready to normatively define. Tying subscription rights to
the existing exchange authorization keeps the IG aligned with how
SMART-on-FHIR and similar trust frameworks already scope authorization, and
the SHALL on revoke→terminate-subscription gives implementers a single,
testable rule that prevents the obvious privacy failure (a subscription
outliving the consent that justified it).

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Address the same gap, but instead of (or in addition to) a prose Security
and Privacy section, define a lightweight conformance-level mechanism:

- Require Consent-administrator servers to publish their subscription
  endpoint and supported authorization profiles (e.g., SMART backend
  services, UDAP, or a named trust framework) in a discovery artifact —
  either the server's `CapabilityStatement` (via an extension on
  `rest.security`) or a well-known JSON document referenced from the IG.
- Define a conformance expectation that a subscribing system MUST present
  credentials traceable to a patient-granted authorization (e.g., an access
  token whose scopes include the patient and the consent resource), and
  that the consent administrator MUST validate that linkage before
  accepting the subscription.
- Keep the patient-revocation rule from Disposition A: revocation of the
  underlying authorization SHALL terminate any subscription it justified,
  and the IG SHOULD describe how the consent administrator detects that
  revocation (e.g., token revocation, a paired authorization record).
- Defer the secondary-data-source / downstream-notification problem to a
  future version, but scope it explicitly so a future ticket can pick it
  up without re-litigating the framing.

#### Justification

Disposition A leaves "how do you actually know a subscriber is authorized"
as a policy exercise outside the IG. That is honest — it is a policy
problem — but it also leaves implementers with no interoperable hook to
test against, which is exactly the scaling problem Hans flagged. A
discovery + token-scope expectation gives implementers a concrete pattern
without picking a single trust framework, and it makes the
revoke→terminate-subscription SHALL enforceable in practice (the
administrator has a defined way to detect that the authorization that
justified the subscription is gone). The trade-off is a larger surface
area to ballot and the risk of constraining a space the work group may
prefer to leave to deployment-specific policy in this version.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Mark the ticket "Not Persuasive" on the grounds that subscription
authorization is fundamentally a deployment/policy concern outside the
scope of an exchange-mechanics IG, and that existing FHIR Subscription
security guidance plus the host trust framework (SMART, UDAP, IHE, etc.)
already cover it. Take no normative or narrative action in this version of
the Scalable Consent Management IG.

#### Justification

The IG is about how consent is exchanged, not about how trust is
established between participating systems. FHIR's general Subscription
security guidance and the surrounding trust-framework specifications
already say that subscribers must be authorized; restating that in this IG
risks duplicating (and potentially conflicting with) those normative
sources. The ticket itself acknowledges the issue is conceptual and
forward-looking, which argues for handling it in a future version once
real deployments surface concrete requirements.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** A

The work group has already converged on a Persuasive resolution with a
specific edit plan, and that plan directly answers Hans's concern: it adds
the missing Security and Privacy narrative, ties subscription authority to
exchange authority, keeps the patient in the loop, and adds one
testable SHALL (revoke → terminate subscription) that closes the most
important privacy hole without overreaching. Disposition B is attractive
on the merits — it would give implementers a concrete hook — but it
materially expands scope for what is currently a Medium-priority,
Conceptual-labeled ticket and would benefit from its own ballot cycle;
the work group can capture it as a forward-looking item when scoping the
next version. Disposition C is hard to defend given that the reporter and
commenter agree there is at least a documentation gap worth closing now.
Recommend proceeding with A and opening a follow-up ticket to track the
discovery/conformance work outlined in B for a future version.
