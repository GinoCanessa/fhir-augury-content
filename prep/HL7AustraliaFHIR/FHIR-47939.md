## Summary

The reporter (Heath Frankel) has identified confusion around the use of
`referencePolicy = resolves` in the AU Core Requester and AU Core Responder
CapabilityStatement resources. After reviewing the FHIR core guidance on
[logical references](https://www.hl7.org/fhir/references.html#logical) and
testing against significant reference implementations (Smile CDR / HAPI JPA
Server, and reportedly Google Cloud FHIR Server), he found that those
servers do not actually support resolving `Reference.identifier` to a
literal reference — making `resolves` an unrealistically strong expectation
even though the underlying conformance verb is only SHOULD. He further
argues that `resolves` is, by its core-spec definition, only meaningful on
the responder (server) side and has no clear meaning on the requester
(client) side. The ticket therefore proposes either removing
`referencePolicy` from each resource entry in both AU Core CapabilityStatements,
or — for the Responder — switching the value from `resolves` to `logical`.
A comment by Grahame Grieve notes for context that `resolves` is required
by US Core and IPA, while IPS does not declare a referencePolicy at all,
so AU Core is choosing a different position from US Core if it adopts this
change. No cross-referenced AU Core or core-FHIR Jira tickets surfaced
beyond the two Zulip threads the reporter raised on `#conformance` (which
attracted no community response).

## Details

**Description:**

There has been some confusion about the intent of the Reference Policy of
Resolves in the AU Core Responder and AU Core Requester CapabilityStatement
resources.

Through investigation https://www.hl7.org/fhir/references.html#logical,
suggest that this is related to the capability of the server to support the
`Reference.identifier` element and hence only relevant to the AU Core
Responder CapabilityStatement.

Having tested this with Smile CDR (HAPI JPA Server) and significant
investigation looking for configuration or usage documentation, it appears
that it does not support the resolving of the `Reference.identifier`.

From documentation, it also appears that Google Cloud FHIR Server doesn't
support it either.

Therefore, given that this is not supported by at least one of the most
significant reference implementations I would suggest that resolves is to
strong expectation even though it is only a SHOULD. I don't think this is
something AU Core needs to specify at this time.

Recommendation:

1. remove `referencePolicy` from each resource in AU Core Requester
   CapabilityStatement.
2. remove `referencePolicy` from each resource in AU Core Responder
   CapabilityStatement, otherwise change value to `logical`.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Edit both AU Core CapabilityStatements (`CapabilityStatement-au-core-requester`
and `CapabilityStatement-au-core-responder` in the AU Core IG) and remove
the `referencePolicy` element from every `rest.resource` entry. This
removes the implicit "client/server SHALL/SHOULD attempt to resolve
logical references" expectation from the AU Core conformance surface and
leaves resolution behaviour out of scope for AU Core, the same position
IPS currently takes.

#### Justification

The reporter's analysis is well grounded in the core spec: per
https://www.hl7.org/fhir/references.html#logical, `resolves` is about a
server resolving a logical `Reference.identifier` into a literal
reference, which is meaningful for create/update but has no clear test
target for read/search and no clear meaning for a client capability. With
two of the most widely deployed FHIR server stacks (HAPI/Smile CDR and
Google Cloud FHIR Server) not implementing identifier resolution, asserting
`resolves` in AU Core sets a SHOULD-level expectation that AU Core
implementers cannot reasonably meet using the dominant server platforms in
their market. The community reference point provided in the comments
(US Core and IPA require `resolves`; IPS does not declare a policy)
confirms there is no single "FHIR house style" — declining to constrain
this is internally consistent with IPS and avoids over-claiming AU Core
conformance behaviour. Removing the element on both sides is also the
cleanest resolution of the requester-vs-responder semantic asymmetry the
reporter raised on Zulip and which received no countering guidance.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Keep `referencePolicy` in the AU Core **Responder** CapabilityStatement
but change its value from `resolves` to `logical` on the resource entries
where AU Core actually expects servers to support search-by-identifier
(typically the resources whose AU Core profiles include `identifier` as a
SHALL-supported `SearchParameter`, e.g. Patient, Practitioner,
PractitionerRole, Organization, Encounter, etc.). Remove `referencePolicy`
entirely from the AU Core **Requester** CapabilityStatement. Add a short
narrative note in each CapabilityStatement explaining what AU Core does
and does not require regarding logical references and identifier-based
resolution.

#### Justification

This preserves a deliberate AU Core statement about server behaviour while
correcting the two specific defects the reporter identified: (i) `resolves`
overstates what real-world AU implementations can do, but `logical`
("the server supports references where the target is identified by a
business identifier rather than a literal URL") is exactly what AU Core
servers already need to support to honour the AU Core identifier search
parameters and the AU Core profile guidance on `Reference.identifier`;
and (ii) `referencePolicy` on the Requester side does not have a defined
testable meaning, so removing it there avoids implying client conformance
the spec cannot describe. This option is more conservative than Disposition
A — it keeps a published AU Core position rather than going silent — but
it diverges from US Core and IPA, which use `resolves`. The Zulip threads
attracted no defence of `resolves`, so shifting AU Core to `logical` is
defensible without waiting on a core-FHIR clarification.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Reject the change and retain `referencePolicy = resolves` in both AU Core
CapabilityStatements as currently published, on the grounds that AU Core
intentionally aligns with US Core and IPA on conformance posture, and that
`resolves` is a SHOULD-level expectation that implementers are free to not
meet without breaking conformance. Reasonable category: "alignment with
existing aligned IGs; no change to declared conformance bar".

#### Justification

The element involved is a SHOULD-level capability declaration, not a
SHALL; non-supporting servers (HAPI, Smile CDR, Google) are not
non-conformant to AU Core just because they do not implement
identifier-to-literal resolution. The reporter's two `#conformance` Zulip
topics did not produce a community consensus to change the meaning of
`referencePolicy`, so removing it from AU Core would be a unilateral
divergence from US Core and IPA based on a single-implementation analysis.
Any clarification of `referencePolicy` semantics is more appropriately
resolved in the FHIR core specification — once core clarifies it, AU Core
can revisit. Until then, leaving the existing values in place avoids
churn in AU Core conformance materials and keeps cross-jurisdiction
alignment with US Core/IPA, which simplifies life for global implementers
mapping between the IGs.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

Disposition B addresses every concrete concern Heath raised — the
client-side semantic gap, the unrealistic `resolves` expectation, and the
lack of community guidance — without going fully silent on a capability
that AU Core implementers actually do need to know about. The reporter
himself offered "change value to `logical`" as the fallback in his own
recommendation for the Responder, and `logical` is the precise core-spec
verb that matches what AU Core profiles already imply through their
identifier-based search parameters and `Reference.identifier` usage.
Disposition A is the cleanest edit and is a perfectly defensible vote if
the work group prefers minimalism, but it leaves AU Core silent on a
behaviour AU Core servers are in fact expected to support, and that may
re-surface as a future ticket. Disposition C is the safest political
option vs. US Core/IPA but it does not resolve the substantive complaint
that `resolves` overstates what AU implementations deliver, and the
absence of any Zulip pushback means there is little community cost to
moving. Recommend B, with the work group choosing the precise resource
list where `logical` is asserted.
