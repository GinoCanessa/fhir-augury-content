## Summary

This ballot-style comment, submitted by Kassenärztliche Bundesvereinigung
(KBV) via Alexander Zautke, raises two distinct legal/policy concerns
about the narrative of the **EU Laboratory Report (FHIR)** Implementation
Guide. First, on the **Purpose** section (page 6), the commenter notes
that the IG mentions "another entity" as a potential recipient of
laboratory data — which could be read to include employers, life and
health insurers, the pharmaceutical industry, market-research
organisations, universities, or other research/statistical institutions —
and asks how patients are informed about, and how they consent to, such
secondary data transfers when these flows are not already governed by an
electronic patient record framework. Second, on **page 9, item 8** of
the Use Cases, the commenter observes that the described cross-border
flows do not clearly distinguish whether the laboratory data reaching a
health professional in country B originates from the patient's
electronic health record or directly from a health professional in
country A; in the latter case, the country-A clinician's medical
confidentiality has to be **waived in writing by the patient**, and the
associated administrative burden may, in many cases, exceed the savings
from avoiding a repeat laboratory analysis. No cross-referenced Jira
tickets, Zulip threads, or GitHub items were found for this ticket via
FhirAugury, so the request stands alone as KBV's narrative-level
critique of the IG's privacy/consent and confidentiality framing.

## Proposed Dispositions

The two concerns in this ticket are about the **narrative text** of the
EU Laboratory Report IG (Purpose, Use Cases) rather than about FHIR
resources, profiles, or constraints. The dispositions therefore focus on
narrative clarifications, scope statements, and (optionally) consent
modelling guidance, rather than on conformance changes. Because no
GitHub repository is cross-referenced in FhirAugury for this ticket, no
authoring root or file path can be cited from a `repo-analysis`
briefing; the workgroup will need to apply any agreed change to the
HL7 Europe Laboratory Report IG sources directly.

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Update the EU Laboratory Report (FHIR) IG narrative to address both
points raised by KBV:

1. **Purpose section (page 6).** Tighten the wording of "another entity"
   so that the IG's intended scope is explicit. Concretely:
   - Limit the enumerated recipients to those that are in scope for the
     IG (e.g., treating clinicians, laboratories, patients, and
     authorised public-health / cross-border eHealth services under
     EHDS), and explicitly list out-of-scope recipients (employers, life
     and private health insurers, pharmaceutical companies, commercial
     market research, etc.) as **not addressed** by this IG.
   - Add a paragraph stating that patient information and consent for
     any such secondary use are governed by national law and the
     European Health Data Space (EHDS) regulation, not by this IG, and
     reference those instruments rather than re-defining them.

2. **Use Cases, page 9 item 8.** Rewrite the use case to make the data
   flow explicit. Distinguish at minimum:
   - **Flow 1 (EHR-to-clinician):** country-B clinician retrieves the
     report from the patient's electronic health record / EHDS
     MyHealth\@EU service, where consent and confidentiality are managed
     by the EHR framework.
   - **Flow 2 (clinician-to-clinician):** country-A clinician
     transmits the report directly to a country-B clinician, in which
     case national rules on **waiver of medical confidentiality**
     apply, and a written patient authorisation is typically required
     before the country-A clinician may release the report.
   Add a short note on the administrative-cost trade-off raised by KBV
   so implementers understand when each flow is appropriate.

#### Justification

Both concerns are legitimate readings of the current narrative and are
straightforward to address with text changes only — there is no impact
on the FHIR profiles, value sets, or examples. KBV is a major German
implementer constituency; declining narrative clarifications of this
kind would leave national medical associations uncertain about the IG's
legal posture. The change keeps the IG firmly inside its remit
(structuring laboratory reports for cross-border exchange) while
deferring consent and confidentiality regulation to the instruments
that actually govern them (national law, GDPR, EHDS). This is also
consistent with how other HL7 Europe IGs handle the consent/legal-basis
question — by signposting rather than restating.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Rather than expanding the narrative inline, add a single new
**"Legal context, consent, and confidentiality"** section to the IG
that:

- States that the IG is a **technical specification** for representing
  laboratory results and does **not** authorise any particular data
  flow or recipient.
- Points to GDPR, the EHDS regulation, and national law (with named
  examples such as Germany's §203 StGB on professional secrecy and the
  KBV/§291a SGB V framework for the German EHR) as the binding sources
  for who may receive data and under what consent regime.
- For cross-border flows, references the **MyHealth\@EU** /
  eHDSI architecture and notes that consent capture and
  confidentiality waivers occur in those layers, not in the FHIR
  payload.
- Optionally, recommends — but does not require — that implementers
  carry consent or legal-basis information using existing FHIR
  mechanisms (e.g., `Consent`, `Provenance.reason`, or
  `Composition.author` plus a security label such as
  `http://terminology.hl7.org/CodeSystem/v3-Confidentiality`) when a
  use case demands it.

The Purpose and Use Cases pages are then updated only with short
forward-references to this new section, leaving their existing
structure intact.

#### Justification

Centralising the legal/consent discussion in one place is easier to
maintain and to translate, and it avoids partial answers scattered
through the narrative. It also lets the IG remain neutral about
specific national regimes (the IG is European, not German) while still
acknowledging KBV's underlying point. The optional pointers to
`Consent`, `Provenance`, and confidentiality security labels give
implementers a concrete FHIR-level handle without making any of those
elements normative for this IG, which preserves backward compatibility
with already-published profiles. The trade-off versus Disposition A is
that the changes touch one extra page (the new section) rather than
just the two pages KBV cited, but the resulting IG is more coherent.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Mark the comment as **Not Persuasive** with the rationale that the
issues raised — patient information about secondary data use, consent
mechanics, and waiver of medical confidentiality — are **out of scope**
for the EU Laboratory Report (FHIR) IG. Reply that:

- The IG specifies the *structure* of laboratory reports for exchange,
  not the *legal basis* for any particular exchange.
- Consent and confidentiality are governed by GDPR, EHDS, and national
  law, and by the surrounding eHealth infrastructure (e.g.,
  MyHealth\@EU), all of which sit outside the IG's scope.
- The use-case narrative is illustrative; concrete data-flow rules are
  defined by the deploying jurisdiction, not by this IG.

No textual change is made.

#### Justification

The IG is explicitly a content/structure specification. FHIR IGs
routinely defer consent, authorisation, and professional-secrecy rules
to the surrounding policy and infrastructure layers, because these are
jurisdiction-specific and change independently of the FHIR artefacts.
Embedding national-law guidance risks the IG going stale or being read
as legal advice. Declining is defensible on scope grounds and is
consistent with how comparable HL7 IGs (e.g., IPS, IPA) handle similar
comments.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

KBV's comment identifies a real ambiguity in the current narrative —
the open-ended list of "another entity" recipients on page 6, and the
unclear sender on page 9 item 8 — and simply declining (Disposition C)
will leave that ambiguity in the published IG, where it is likely to
resurface from other national bodies. Disposition A fixes both
passages but spreads the legal/consent framing across multiple pages
and risks the IG becoming a partial restatement of GDPR/EHDS.
Disposition B captures the same clarifications as A but localises the
legal/consent discussion in a single, maintainable section, makes the
IG's scope boundary explicit, and gives implementers an optional FHIR
handle (`Consent`, `Provenance`, confidentiality labels) without
imposing new conformance requirements. It is the most respectful
response to KBV's concerns while keeping the IG's scope tight, and it
sets up a stable place to address future comments of the same kind
from other Member State bodies.
