## Summary

The reporter (Bryn Rhodes) has identified a documentation/specification error
in the Clinical Quality Language (CQL) specification regarding the
`properly includes` operator. Specifically, the additional overload that
mirrors the `contains` operator behavior on a `List<T>` value has its
argument signature documented in the wrong order, and the accompanying
narrative refers to the variant by an inverted name. The signature is
currently shown as `(T, List<T>)` but should be `(List<T>, T)`, and the
narrative's reference to the "singleton-list overload" should read
"list-singleton overload" to accurately describe a list on the left-hand
side and a singleton on the right-hand side. No cross-referenced Jira
tickets, Zulip threads, or GitHub items were surfaced for this ticket;
the request stands on its own as a focused technical correction to the
CQL specification.

## Details

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Apply the textual corrections described in the ticket to the CQL (FHIR)
specification page that documents the `properly includes` operator:

1. In the operator-signature listing for the additional `properly includes`
   overload that mirrors `contains` semantics, swap the argument order from
   `(T, List<T>)` to `(List<T>, T)` so the list-valued argument appears on
   the left and the singleton appears on the right — matching the actual
   semantics of "the list properly includes the singleton."
2. In the surrounding narrative paragraph that introduces this overload,
   replace every occurrence of "singleton-list overload" with
   "list-singleton overload" to reflect the corrected left-to-right
   argument order.

The change is purely a specification text/signature correction; no
behavior, conformance, or implementation contract changes.

#### Justification

The reporter is the author/maintainer of the CQL specification and is
flagging an internal inconsistency: the operator's stated signature does
not match its semantics or the corresponding `contains` overload it is
intended to mirror. CQL consistently names overloads by argument order
(left-to-right), so "list-singleton" is the correct label when the list
is the left operand. Accepting the change preserves consistency with the
`contains` operator family and with CQL's documented conventions for
naming overloads, and avoids reader confusion or misimplementation by
engines that lean on the narrative for disambiguation.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Adopt the corrections in Disposition A, and additionally:

1. Add a short worked example beside the corrected overload showing both
   `properly includes(List<T>, T)` and the parallel
   `contains(List<T>, T)` invocation with the same operands, to make the
   parallelism between the two operator families explicit.
2. Audit the `properly includes`, `includes`, `properly included in`,
   `included in`, and `contains` operator pages for the same kind of
   left/right ordering mismatch in their additional overloads, and fix
   any analogous issues in a single editorial pass referenced by this
   ticket.

#### Justification

The reported defect is plausibly not isolated: the `includes` /
`included in` / `contains` operator family share a common documentation
pattern, and a copy-paste of the original (incorrect) signature could
have propagated to sibling pages. Bundling a targeted audit with the
fix avoids reopening the same class of defect in a future ticket and
gives implementers a clean, internally consistent operator reference.
The trade-off versus Disposition A is a slightly larger editorial
diff and the associated review cost.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Close the ticket as **Not Persuasive – editorial only**, leaving the
current text and signature in place on the grounds that the operator's
behavior is unambiguously defined elsewhere (by analogy to `contains`
and by the symmetry with `included in`), and that engines do not key
off the narrative wording.

#### Justification

Declining could be defended on the basis that the request is a pure
documentation polish with no impact on conformance, normative
behavior, or test outcomes — and that the cost of a normative spec
revision (republish, ballot bookkeeping) outweighs the benefit of a
clearer narrative. This rationale is weak in this specific case
because the defect is in the *signature* (not just prose), but it is
the most honest framing of a "do nothing" outcome.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** A

This is a small, well-scoped technical correction raised by the
specification's principal author against his own text, with a clear
right answer: the list-valued operand belongs on the left, and the
overload must be named accordingly. There is no community
controversy (no Zulip threads, no linked tickets, no opposing
viewpoints), no conformance impact, and no implementer burden — only
a risk of confusion if the inconsistency is left in place. Disposition
B's broader audit is attractive and could be folded in by the editor
if convenient, but it is not required to resolve this ticket and
should not delay the fix. Disposition C is not defensible: the
incorrect signature, not just the narrative wording, is the primary
defect, and leaving an incorrect signature in a normative
specification harms downstream readers and tooling.
