## Summary

The reporter argues that Section 1.5 of the Clinical Guidelines (FHIR)
Implementation Guide — which states that the CPG effort is a "concerted
attempt to build on, learn from, contribute to, and work with existing
approaches, initiatives, and efforts in guideline development, translation,
and implementation" — fails to acknowledge or reference the "knowledge
coupling" approach to clinical decision support originally developed by
Dr. Lawrence L. Weed (LLW), the originator of the problem list, the SOAP
note, and the problem-oriented health record (POHR). The reporter
asserts that knowledge-coupling tools differ in crucial ways from the
guideline-translation paradigm CPG is built around, and that those
differences are directly relevant to CPG's stated goals. The ticket
points readers to the HL7 EHR WG's POHR Project Confluence page
(`pageId=120752354`) for source material, including specific chapters of
*Ending Medicine's Chronic Dysfunction: Tools and Standards for Medical
Decision Making* (Springer, 2021) and a 2023 talk script on knowledge
coupling and POHRs. The only cross-reference in Augury is to that same
Confluence page; no related Jira tickets, Zulip threads, or GitHub items
were surfaced.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Update **Section 1.5 ("Approach")** of the Clinical Guidelines (FHIR)
Implementation Guide to add a paragraph (and a corresponding entry under
any "References" / "Related Work" subsection) that:

1. Names Dr. Lawrence L. Weed and his "knowledge coupling" approach to
   CDS as a recognized prior body of work in problem-oriented care and
   computable decision support.
2. Briefly characterizes how knowledge coupling differs from the
   guideline-translation paradigm CPG is built around (i.e., it couples
   patient-specific data to a structured knowledge base at the point of
   care, rather than executing a pre-authored guideline pathway).
3. Cites the materials the reporter pointed to: the HL7 EHR WG POHR
   Project Confluence page (`pageId=120752354`), the relevant chapters
   of *Ending Medicine's Chronic Dysfunction* (Springer, 2021), and the
   March 2023 POHR talk script.

No normative content (resources, profiles, operations, value sets) is
changed; this is a non-normative narrative-only edit.

#### Justification

The change is editorial and limited to a non-normative section that
already promises to acknowledge prior and parallel work. Adding a
reference to a recognized historical CDS paradigm is consistent with
that stated intent and costs nothing in terms of conformance, tooling,
or implementer burden. It also has the secondary benefit of bringing
CPG's "Approach" section into closer alignment with the EHR WG's POHR
project artifacts already hosted on HL7 Confluence.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Rather than embedding a paragraph on knowledge coupling directly into
Section 1.5, add a short **"Related Approaches and Prior Art"**
appendix (or sub-page) to the Clinical Guidelines IG that catalogues
non-CPG paradigms relevant to computable CDS — knowledge coupling /
POHR being one entry, alongside other recognized approaches (e.g.,
Arden Syntax, GLIF, PROforma, SAGE, EON). Section 1.5 then gains a
single sentence pointing to that appendix. Coordinate the knowledge
coupling entry with the HL7 EHR WG so the CPG appendix links to the
EHR WG's POHR Project Confluence page as the system of record rather
than restating its content.

#### Justification

A standalone appendix is more durable than ad-hoc paragraphs in
Section 1.5: future "we should also mention X" tickets can be
addressed by adding an appendix entry rather than re-litigating the
"Approach" narrative each time. It also avoids singling out one
historical paradigm in the body of the IG without context, which can
read as advocacy rather than scholarship. Linking out to the EHR WG's
existing POHR page keeps a single source of truth and avoids CPG
having to maintain a substantive description of an approach owned by
another work group. The trade-off vs. Disposition A is more editorial
work up front (drafting the appendix, deciding what else belongs in
it) in exchange for a cleaner pattern going forward.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline the change on the basis that **Section 1.5 is a non-normative
overview, not an exhaustive related-work bibliography**, and that the
materials cited (a Springer monograph and an HL7 Confluence page
already accessible to the community) are not artifacts CPG needs to
recognize in its narrative to fulfill its scope. The reporter is
encouraged to coordinate with the EHR WG to keep the POHR Project
Confluence page current; CPG can hyperlink to that page from its
existing cross-IG references if and when an EHR-WG-owned reference
becomes appropriate.

#### Justification

CPG's Section 1.5 deliberately speaks at a paradigm level (guideline
development, translation, and implementation) rather than enumerating
every CDS approach in the literature. Accepting this request as a
narrative-only edit invites a long tail of similar "please also
mention X" requests that the work group then has to triage and
maintain. There is also no concrete normative or implementer-facing
gap identified — the ticket does not point to a missing resource,
operation, profile, or value set; it asks for additional prose
acknowledging a third-party paradigm. Declining preserves the IG's
current editorial scope without preventing the reporter from raising
a more targeted change request later if a specific CPG mechanism
needs to interoperate with a knowledge-coupling system.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** A

The request is small, the cost is low, and the change lives entirely
in non-normative narrative that Section 1.5 already invites by
explicitly framing CPG as building on and learning from existing
approaches. Dr. Weed is a historically significant figure in CDS and
problem-oriented care, the cited materials are HL7-hosted (POHR
Project Confluence) rather than third-party-only, and the EHR WG
already maintains the source page — so CPG can cite without taking on
maintenance. Disposition B is attractive but is more work than this
single ticket warrants and would be a better response to a *pattern*
of "related approaches" requests rather than a one-off; it can be
adopted later if other similar tickets arrive. Disposition C is
defensible procedurally but trades a few sentences of editorial work
for a community signal that CPG's Section 1.5 is closed to the kind
of acknowledgement it explicitly promises, which seems disproportionate.
