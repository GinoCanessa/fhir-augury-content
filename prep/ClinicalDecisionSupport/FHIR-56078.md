## Summary

The ticket challenges a normative statement in the US Da Vinci DTR IG that
reads: *"Even after an application has been successfully registered, payers
and EHRs **SHOULD** monitor the application behavior and **MAY** suspend an
application's access if it is suspected of malicious behavior."* The reporter
(Cooper Thompson) argues that post-registration monitoring and suspension are
operational/governance guidance rather than interoperability conformance
requirements, and asks whether the normative verbs `SHOULD` and `MAY` should
be relaxed to non-conformance wording such as "could" and "might". No related
Jira tickets, Zulip threads, or GitHub items were surfaced by
`cross-referenced` or Zulip search.

The ticket has been Triaged with resolution *Persuasive with Modification*
and is labeled `Ready-For-Vote`. The recorded resolution keeps `SHOULD` on
the monitoring clause but downgrades the suspension clause from `MAY` to
"might", reworks "EHRs" to "DTR client organizations", and broadens
"malicious behavior" to "malicious or inappropriate behavior".

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

In the US Da Vinci DTR IG's app-registration / security guidance page,
replace both normative verbs in the sentence so it reads:

> *"Even after an application has been successfully registered, payers and
> EHRs **could** monitor the application behavior and **might** suspend an
> application's access if it is suspected of malicious behavior."*

Retain the sentence's placement and surrounding context, but remove it from
any conformance-testable list (if one exists) since "could"/"might" carry no
RFC 2119 force.

#### Justification

The sentence describes actions a payer or EHR takes *after* a successful
registration has completed — i.e., ongoing operational monitoring and
unilateral access revocation. Those behaviors are not testable against the
wire, not required for a DTR exchange to interoperate, and have no tooling
or artifact in the IG that would validate them. RFC 2119 `SHOULD`/`MAY`
imply conformance obligations; using them here has historically confused
implementers who read the IG as "must demonstrate a monitoring capability
to claim conformance". Downgrading to non-normative "could"/"might" matches
the reporter's reading and keeps the IG's conformance surface limited to
what can actually be validated.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach (recorded resolution)

#### Proposal

Adopt the resolution already captured on the ticket: keep `SHOULD` on the
monitoring clause, downgrade only the suspension clause, generalize the
actor, and broaden the behavior category. The sentence becomes:

> *"Even after an application has been successfully registered, payers and
> DTR client organizations **SHOULD** monitor the application behavior and
> might suspend an application's access if it is suspected of malicious or
> inappropriate behavior."*

Concretely, in the DTR app-registration / security narrative:

1. Replace "EHRs" with "DTR client organizations" so the guidance covers
   any organization hosting a DTR client (EHRs, provider portals, etc.).
2. Keep `SHOULD monitor` as a normative expectation of good security
   hygiene for credential-issuing organizations.
3. Replace `MAY suspend` with the non-normative "might suspend".
4. Broaden "malicious behavior" to "malicious or inappropriate behavior".

#### Justification

This is the position the BR call on 2026-03-25 already approved and is the
path that balances the reporter's concern with the IG's security posture.
Monitoring of credentials issued by an organization *is* a reasonable
normative expectation even without a wire-level test — it aligns with
standard credential-issuer obligations (cf. SMART App Launch security
considerations) and gives auditors something to point at. Suspension, by
contrast, is a unilateral operational action with no DTR-level conformance
surface, so dropping it to "might" resolves the reporter's objection
without weakening the monitoring posture. Generalizing to "DTR client
organizations" also corrects a narrower drafting error (not every DTR
client is an EHR). The trade-off vs. Disposition A is that `SHOULD monitor`
is still technically non-testable in the DTR exchange, but the BR group has
judged the reputational/security value of keeping it normative to outweigh
that purity concern.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Keep the sentence as currently published — `SHOULD monitor` and `MAY
suspend` — on the grounds that RFC 2119 verbs are appropriate signals of
expected implementer behavior even when the behavior isn't validated by a
wire-level test.

#### Justification

RFC 2119 keywords are routinely applied to security and governance
expectations in HL7 IGs (see SMART App Launch, US Core privacy/security
narrative, and the base FHIR `security` page) without those expectations
being mechanically testable. Stripping the verbs here could be read as
"HL7 does not expect DTR credential issuers to monitor or suspend
apps", which is the opposite of the IG's intent. Declining keeps the IG
consistent with peer specifications and avoids re-opening a settled
drafting choice.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

Disposition B is the resolution the Business Requirements call has already
approved on 2026-03-25 and the ticket is labeled `Ready-For-Vote` against
that wording, so the workgroup's default path is to confirm it. On the
merits, B is also the strongest of the three: it concedes the reporter's
real complaint — that `MAY suspend` has no conformance surface — while
preserving `SHOULD monitor` as a defensible normative expectation of
credential-issuing organizations, and opportunistically fixes the
"EHRs"-vs-"DTR client organizations" scope bug and the
"malicious"-vs-"malicious or inappropriate" gap. Disposition A is cleaner
in conformance theory but weakens the IG's security narrative in a way the
workgroup has already signaled it does not want; Disposition C ignores a
legitimate reading of RFC 2119 that has clearly tripped up at least one
implementer. The workgroup should vote B as written.
