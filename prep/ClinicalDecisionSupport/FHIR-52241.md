## Summary

This ticket is an editorial / consistency request against the Clinical
Quality Language (CQL) FHIR specification. The reporter, Lynn Perrine,
has observed that the chapter descriptions inconsistently refer to the
language by either the acronym ("CQL") or the fully spelled-out form
("Clinical Quality Language"). Specifically, the descriptions of
Chapters 2 and 3 spell out "Clinical Quality Language", while Chapter 6
and Appendices A, B, C, and L use the "CQL" acronym. The reporter asks
the Clinical Decision Support work group to choose one convention —
acronym throughout or spelled-out throughout — and apply it
consistently across the chapter and appendix descriptions. There are
no linked Jira tickets, no Zulip discussions, and no GitHub items
cross-referenced to this issue, so the request stands on its own as a
small editorial cleanup.

## Details

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Accept the request as a documentation/editorial fix in the Clinical
Quality Language (FHIR) specification. Select a single convention for
referring to the language in **chapter and appendix description text**
and apply it uniformly. Recommended choice: spell out "Clinical
Quality Language (CQL)" on first reference within each chapter or
appendix description, then use the acronym "CQL" in subsequent
sentences within that same description block. Update the affected
descriptions: Chapter 2, Chapter 3, Chapter 6, Appendix A, Appendix B,
Appendix C, and Appendix L so that they all follow the
"first-use-spelled-out, acronym thereafter" pattern. No normative
content changes; affects only narrative description prose in the spec
build.

#### Justification

This is a low-risk, non-normative editorial change. Adopting the
"spell-out-on-first-use, acronym after" convention is the most common
technical-writing style and is consistent with how most HL7 / FHIR
specs introduce abbreviations. Applying it per-description block
(rather than once for the whole spec) means a reader who lands
mid-document still sees the expansion. It directly resolves the
inconsistency Lynn reported without forcing reviewers to re-read every
chapter to verify a global-only expansion was unambiguous.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Standardize on the bare acronym "CQL" in **all** chapter and appendix
description headers/intros, on the rationale that the title page and
spec name already establish "Clinical Quality Language" as the
expansion of CQL, and that the descriptions are short scan-targets
rather than standalone prose. Update Chapters 2 and 3 to use "CQL"
where they currently spell the name out, leaving Chapter 6 and
Appendices A, B, C, and L unchanged. Optionally add a one-line
glossary entry near the top of the spec ("CQL — Clinical Quality
Language") so the expansion is discoverable from any landing page.

#### Justification

This alternative minimizes diff noise — only Chapters 2 and 3 need to
change, versus rewriting every affected description under Disposition
A. It also matches the way most readers actually refer to the language
(everyone in the CDS / CQI community says "CQL"). The trade-off vs.
Disposition A is that a brand-new reader who deep-links into a
specific chapter description page may not see the expansion in
context; the optional glossary line mitigates that. Choose this if the
work group values terseness in description blocks and wants the
smallest possible editorial change.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline the request as a not-persistent-impact editorial nit.
Document that the spec uses "CQL" and "Clinical Quality Language"
interchangeably and that both forms are acceptable in narrative text.
Close as "Not Persuasive" or equivalent without changing the spec.

#### Justification

The inconsistency is purely stylistic, has no impact on
implementability, conformance, or testability, and is unlikely to
cause confusion because both forms are well-known to the CQL
community. Spending review/publication cycles on a wording-only change
in description blocks may not be a good use of work-group bandwidth,
and there is some risk (small, but non-zero) of regenerating
downstream tooling, mirrors, or translations purely for cosmetic prose
changes. If the work group has a higher-priority backlog, this is a
defensible pass.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** A

The request is correct on its face — the spec really is inconsistent
in the places Lynn called out, and consistency in normative spec
documents is a real (if small) quality signal. Disposition A is
recommended over B because the "spell out on first use, acronym
thereafter" convention is the standard HL7/FHIR house style and
serves first-time readers landing on any single chapter or appendix.
Disposition A is recommended over C because the change is genuinely
trivial (text-only, in a small enumerated set of description blocks,
with no normative or schema impact), so the cost of fixing it is much
lower than the cost of re-litigating the same nit at a future ballot
cycle. The work group should ask the editor to apply the convention
across the seven enumerated description blocks (Chapters 2, 3, 6 and
Appendices A, B, C, L) and confirm during balloting that no other
description blocks were missed.
