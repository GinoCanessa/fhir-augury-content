## Summary

The reporter raises an editorial / authoring question about how acronyms are
introduced in the Clinical Quality Language (FHIR) specification. They observe
that the same acronym is being defined repeatedly within close proximity —
specifically, "CQL" is expanded in the section 1.1 header, again in the first
paragraph of that section, and again in section 1.1.1 — and asks whether an
acronym needs to be defined in *each* section, or whether the more conventional
practice of defining the acronym once on first use and then using the short
form thereafter should apply. No cross-referenced Jira tickets, Zulip
discussions, or GitHub items were surfaced for this ticket, so the request
stands on its own as a request for editorial guidance / cleanup in the CQL
(FHIR) specification.

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Treat this as an editorial defect against the Clinical Quality Language
(FHIR) specification. Audit sections 1.1 and 1.1.1 (and, by extension, all
narrative pages of the CQL IG) and apply a "define-on-first-use" rule:

- Define each acronym (starting with **CQL** — *Clinical Quality Language*)
  exactly once, on its first occurrence in running prose.
- Remove the parenthetical expansion on every subsequent occurrence within
  the same document, including in subsection headers and in the opening
  paragraphs of subsections that fall under a parent section that already
  defined the term.
- Keep a glossary / acronyms list in a single canonical location for readers
  who land on a deep-link page out of context, rather than re-expanding the
  acronym inline.
- Optionally add an editorial note to the IG's contributor / style guidance
  recording the rule so future authors do not reintroduce the redundancy.

#### Justification

This matches the dominant editorial convention used across the FHIR Core
specification and the majority of HL7 IGs, and it is also standard
technical-writing practice (define on first use, then use the short form).
Repeated expansion within a few hundred words adds noise without aiding the
reader, and the reporter has identified a concrete, easily-fixed instance.
A glossary entry safely covers the deep-link / out-of-context concern that
sometimes motivates re-expansion.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Adopt a **page-scoped** (rather than spec-scoped) define-on-first-use rule,
combined with an explicit acronyms / glossary page:

- Allow each top-level published page in the CQL IG to expand each acronym
  once on its first use *within that page*, but not again within the same
  page (so subsection headers and follow-up paragraphs use the short form).
- Add or reuse a dedicated "Acronyms" / "Glossary" page that lists every
  acronym used by the IG with its expansion and a short definition.
- Document this rule in the IG's authoring guidance so it is enforceable in
  review.

This differs from Disposition A in that it explicitly preserves a single
expansion at the top of each independently-navigable page, which acknowledges
that IG pages are routinely linked to directly and read out of order.

#### Justification

Pure once-per-spec expansion (Disposition A) can be hostile to readers who
arrive via deep links, search results, or table-of-contents jumps and have
not seen the earlier section where the acronym was introduced. A page-scoped
rule is a small, principled relaxation that preserves readability for
out-of-order readers while still removing the immediate within-page
redundancy the reporter flagged (header + first paragraph + subsection all
re-expanding "CQL"). It also produces a glossary artifact that has standalone
value, and it is enforceable mechanically in review.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Close as **Not Persuasive / Editorial — No Change**: state that the current
practice of expanding acronyms in section headers and at the top of major
subsections is intentional, and decline to make a sweeping editorial pass.
Optionally note that targeted PRs against any specific instances the
community finds distracting will still be considered on a case-by-case
basis.

#### Justification

The defensible reason to decline is that re-expanding common acronyms in
headers and at the start of major subsections can be an intentional
accessibility / navigability choice for a specification whose pages are
frequently read out of order and indexed by search engines. If the workgroup
believes that aiding direct-link readers outweighs the prose redundancy, the
status quo is consistent with that goal. Declining also avoids spending
editorial cycles and risking inconsistent partial cleanup across the IG.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

The reporter's underlying concern — that "CQL" is expanded three times in
very close proximity — is real and worth fixing, so a flat decline
(Disposition C) understates the issue. At the same time, a strict
once-per-spec rule (Disposition A) ignores the legitimate reason editors
sometimes re-expand acronyms: IG pages are routinely entered via deep links
and search, not read top-to-bottom. Disposition B captures the best of both:
it removes the within-page redundancy the reporter actually flagged
(eliminating the duplicate expansions inside section 1.1 and section 1.1.1
once the page-level expansion has occurred), it produces a reusable glossary
that helps readers regardless of entry point, and it codifies the rule in
authoring guidance so the cleanup does not regress. The workgroup should
confirm whether a glossary page already exists in the CQL (FHIR) IG and, if
not, treat creating one as part of the work.
