## Summary

This ticket is a ballot comment on the Clinical Quality Language (CQL)
specification responding to an explicit ballot question: *"NOTE: We seek
ballot feedback on whether directives should be allowed throughout a
library, rather than only at the beginning as proposed."* The reporter,
Mitra Rocca, argues that the new `directive` declaration should follow
the same convention as other CQL declaration statements (e.g.,
`library`, `using`, `include`, `codesystem`, `valueset`, `parameter`,
`context`, `define`), which are **sequence-independent** within the
library. Under that model, authors would be free to place directives
anywhere in the library, and dependency / ordering concerns between
directives and the statements they affect would be handled by the CQL
engine rather than imposed on the human author. No related FHIR Jira
tickets, Zulip threads, or GitHub items were surfaced by the
cross-reference index for this key.

## Details

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Change the CQL grammar so the `directive` declaration is treated as a
**sequence-independent declaration**, on par with the other library-level
declaration statements (`library`, `using`, `include`, `codesystem`,
`valueset`, `parameter`, `context`, `define`). Concretely:

- Update the CQL grammar production for `library` to allow `directive`
  declarations to appear anywhere a top-level declaration is allowed,
  rather than constraining them to a leading "directives block".
- Update the normative prose in the CQL spec section that introduces
  `directive` to remove the "must appear at the beginning" wording and
  replace it with language stating that directives are
  sequence-independent and may appear anywhere at the library level.
- Add a non-normative note clarifying that the engine is responsible
  for collecting all `directive` declarations during library load and
  applying them as a set, before evaluating any expression that depends
  on them.
- Add or update conformance language so that translators MUST resolve
  directive effects independent of textual position, matching how other
  declarations are resolved.

#### Justification

This treatment is internally consistent with every other CQL
declaration statement, which the author rightly notes are
sequence-independent. CQL is a declarative language in spirit: the
order of `define` statements does not affect semantics, and forward
references between defines are routinely resolved by the translator.
Forcing `directive` to be the lone position-sensitive declaration
introduces an inconsistency that will be a frequent footgun for
authors and authoring tools (especially generators that splice content
into a library). Pushing dependency management onto the engine — which
already performs library-wide symbol resolution — is the lower-cost
side of the trade.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Keep the **proposal-as-balloted** position-sensitive rule for
directives (must appear at the top of the library), but soften it in
two ways:

1. Make the rule a **SHOULD** rather than a **MUST**: translators MUST
   accept directives anywhere in the library, but authoring guidance
   recommends placing them at the top for human readability.
2. Define a small set of directives that are **explicitly allowed
   anywhere** because they are scoped (e.g., a hypothetical
   `@suppress`-style directive that applies to a single following
   declaration), and require the rest to appear at the library header.

This gives implementers a clear parsing model (directives are
recognised wherever they appear) while preserving the readability
intent of the original proposal.

#### Justification

The balloted "top of library only" rule was likely chosen to make
directives easy for a human reader to find and to give the translator
a single, well-defined "configuration phase" before the rest of the
library is interpreted. Disposition B preserves that authoring
convention as guidance while removing the hard parser-level
constraint that the reporter is pushing back on. It also opens a path
for genuinely *local* directives (which would be awkward under
strict Disposition A, since "anywhere" then collides with "applies to
the whole library"). The cost is a slightly more nuanced rule than
either extreme, and the ballot-resolution write-up has to clearly
distinguish library-scope directives from declaration-scope ones.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Retain the proposed rule that directives must appear at the beginning
of the library, with no change. Resolve the ballot question by
documenting the WG's rationale: directives configure the
interpretation of the entire library and therefore belong at the
header, analogous to a `library` or `using` statement, both of which
also have de facto positional conventions in published CQL.

#### Justification

Restricting directives to the top of the library gives readers and
tooling a single, predictable place to find the configuration that
governs how the rest of the file is interpreted. It also avoids a
class of subtle bugs where two directives placed in different parts
of a long library quietly conflict, or where a directive's effect on
*earlier* lines surprises a linear reader. A "MUST appear at the top"
rule is easy to state, easy to lint for, and easy to teach.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** A

The reporter's argument is the strongest one in the file: every other
CQL declaration is sequence-independent, and CQL translators already
do whole-library symbol resolution, so making `directive` the one
position-sensitive declaration creates a special case that authors,
tool builders, and code generators all have to learn and work around.
A "MUST appear at the top" rule is also fragile in practice — it
breaks naturally with templating, code generation, and machine-edited
libraries, all of which are growing use cases (e.g., SMART guideline
authoring, CDS Hooks tooling). Disposition B is a reasonable fallback
if the WG wants to preserve the *style* of leading directives while
loosening the *grammar*; the ballot-resolution wording for B should
be precise about what "SHOULD" means here. Disposition C should only
be chosen if the WG has a concrete semantic reason — not just a
readability preference — that directives cannot be resolved
sequence-independently, and that reason should be documented in the
spec rather than left implicit.
