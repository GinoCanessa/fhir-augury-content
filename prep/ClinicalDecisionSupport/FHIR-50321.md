## Summary

The ticket requests that the CDS Hooks Library generalize references to the
actor invoking or interacting with hooks, replacing the term "clinician"
with "practitioner" throughout hook definitions where appropriate. The
reporter, Lloyd McKenzie, observes that several existing hook definitions
describe a "clinician" ordering items, entering problems, and so on, but
that CDS Hooks are increasingly used outside traditional clinical settings —
notably in social-services and SDOH workflows where the actor may be a
social worker, food-bank employee, personal trainer, or similar
non-clinician practitioner. The request is to audit each hook and replace
"clinician" with "practitioner" unless the hook is unambiguously clinical
in scope. There are no cross-referenced Jira tickets or related GitHub
items associated with this issue in the Augury index.

## Details

**Description:**

A number of the hooks have definitions that refer to a 'clinician'
ordering, entering problems, etc. However, that term is narrower than the
hook applies to. CDS Hooks can be used in social services environments and
other spaces that address SDOH concerns. Orders and problems might be
created by individuals who are not 'clinicians' - e.g. personal trainers,
foodbank employees, social workers, etc. Suggest looking at all of the
hooks and generalizing the language to say 'practitioner' rather than
'clinician' unless it's clear that for all possible types of
orders/encounters/appointments/whatever, the only individuals that could
invoke the hook are truly clinicians.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Audit every hook definition published in the CDS Hooks Library
specification (the in-spec catalog: e.g., `patient-view`,
`order-sign`, `order-select`, `order-dispatch`,
`appointment-book`, `encounter-start`, `encounter-discharge`,
`medication-prescribe`, `problem-list-item-create`, etc.) and replace
the word "clinician" with "practitioner" in each hook's prose
description (Workflow / Context narrative) wherever the hook can
plausibly be invoked outside a traditional clinical setting. Retain
"clinician" only for hooks where every conceivable trigger is clinical
in nature. Apply the same wording change to any hook-catalog summary
text and to the introductory pages that cross-reference the hook actor.
The `context.userId` / `context.user` field semantics (which already
allow `Practitioner`, `PractitionerRole`, `Patient`, `RelatedPerson`,
etc.) need not change; only the human-readable narrative is being
generalized.

#### Justification

This is a no-cost editorial change that aligns the published hook
narratives with the actual `context.user` typing in the spec, which
already permits non-clinician `Practitioner` resources and other actors.
Generalizing the prose removes an inconsistency that could otherwise be
read as restrictive guidance, supports the SDOH and social-services use
cases that the workgroup has been encouraging, and matches the FHIR
preference for "practitioner" as the umbrella term for any individual
acting in a professional capacity. There is no breaking change because
no field names, value sets, or context structures are altered.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Instead of a blanket find-and-replace, introduce a single normative
paragraph in the CDS Hooks Library overview (and/or the "Hook Context"
section) that defines the term used throughout the catalog — e.g.,
"Throughout this specification, 'practitioner' refers to any
individual acting in a professional capacity (clinical or otherwise)
who may invoke a hook; it is not limited to licensed clinicians." Then
update only the hook narratives where the current "clinician" wording
is actively misleading (e.g., `order-sign`, `appointment-book`,
`problem-list-item-*`), and leave wording untouched on hooks that are
unambiguously clinical (e.g., `medication-prescribe`,
`encounter-discharge` from inpatient settings). Optionally add a
non-normative note on each generalized hook pointing back to the
overview definition.

#### Justification

A definitional approach is more durable than per-hook word swaps: it
prevents the same ambiguity from recurring in future hooks and avoids
the risk of an over-broad replacement reading awkwardly on hooks that
really are clinician-specific. It also distinguishes "we changed a word"
from "we expanded who can invoke this hook," which is closer to the
truth — the actor was already broad, but the prose under-specified it.
This carries the same low implementation burden as Disposition A while
giving implementers and downstream IGs a single anchor to cite.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline the request as an editorial-only change with no
interoperability impact, on the basis that the hook context already
permits non-clinician actors via the typed `context.user` field, and
that individual hooks remain free to scope their actor narrative as
their authors intend. Document the workgroup's interpretation in the
ticket resolution and close as "Not Persuasive" / "Won't Fix."

#### Justification

The technical surface (context fields, value sets, hook IDs) is
unchanged regardless of which word the prose uses, so implementers
gain nothing concrete from the rewrite. Some hooks are also genuinely
clinical, and a sweeping prose change risks softening guidance that
hook authors deliberately wrote as restrictive. If a downstream
implementer wants to use a hook in an SDOH workflow, nothing in the
current spec prevents them.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

Disposition B captures the legitimate concern Lloyd raises — that the
current narratives under-describe who can invoke a hook — while avoiding
the two failure modes of Disposition A (collateral edits to hooks that
really are clinician-only) and Disposition C (leaving a real
inconsistency in place). Anchoring the term in a single definitional
paragraph also future-proofs the catalog: new hooks added by the
workgroup or by external IGs can simply cite the overview rather than
relitigate the wording each time. The targeted prose updates that
follow are then a small, low-risk follow-on edit. The workgroup should
expect the editor cycle to take one ballot iteration; no implementer
behaviour or conformance posture changes.
