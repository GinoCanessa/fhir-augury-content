## Summary

The reporter flags an internal-consistency problem in the Personal Health
Record (PHR) FHIR specification: the introductory narrative uses both
"EMR" and "EHR" to refer to the systems that PHR apps exchange data with.
Specifically, an early paragraph uses **EMR**, while a later sentence —
*"What it does provide, is guidance on how to successfully exchange data
with other PHR and **EHR** apps."* — switches to **EHR**, with no
definition or cross-reference between the two terms. The reporter asks
that the spec either pick a single term and use it consistently, or
explicitly define both terms so that the distinction is intentional and
clear to readers. The Patient Empowerment WG triaged the ticket as a
Technical Correction and committed to harmonising the wording (resolution
recorded as *Persuasive*; the resolution text says "Typo fixed."). There
are no cross-referenced Jira tickets, Zulip threads, GitHub items, or
Confluence pages for this ticket in Augury's index.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Edit the PHR IG narrative so that the term used for non-PHR clinical
record systems is consistent throughout. Concretely:

- In the introductory section that currently reads "…with other PHR and
  **EHR** apps.", change `EHR` to `EMR` so it matches the term used in
  the first paragraph; **or**, conversely, change the first-paragraph
  occurrence of `EMR` to `EHR`. Pick whichever the WG considers the more
  accurate description of the systems PHR apps actually exchange data
  with (the WG comment from awatson1978 confirms the original choice was
  not deliberate).
- Apply the chosen term globally across the IG narrative pages so that
  only one of `EMR`/`EHR` appears, except where a glossary or
  definitions block intentionally introduces both.

This is the change the resolution ("Typo fixed.") already commits to.

#### Justification

The reporter's request is a pure editorial-consistency fix in narrative
text — no resource, profile, extension, terminology, search parameter,
or operation is affected. The WG has already accepted the change in
principle (resolution = *Persuasive*) and a maintainer comment commits
to harmonising the wording. The change is non-normative, non-breaking,
and low-risk; it improves implementer comprehension at zero cost.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach — Keep Both Terms, Add a Glossary Entry

#### Proposal

Rather than collapsing to one term, retain both `EMR` and `EHR` in the
narrative where each is genuinely the right word, and add a short
**Definitions / Glossary** block (or a sentence in the introduction) that:

1. Spells out both acronyms ("Electronic Medical Record" and "Electronic
   Health Record").
2. Briefly notes the distinction the IG intends (e.g., EMR = a single
   provider/practice clinical record system; EHR = a longitudinal,
   cross-organisation health record).
3. States that, for the purposes of PHR data exchange in this IG, the
   guidance applies to both classes of system.

This is the second option the reporter explicitly raised: "Or at least
provide a clear definition of them."

#### Justification

The two terms are not perfect synonyms in industry usage, and silently
collapsing them risks losing a real semantic distinction (e.g., scope of
record, organisational boundary, regulatory framing). A short glossary
entry preserves precision, addresses the reporter's underlying concern
(reader confusion), and is still cheap — narrative-only, no normative
change. The trade-off vs. Disposition A is slightly more text to maintain,
but a one-time documentation cost in exchange for fewer downstream
"what's the difference?" questions.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Close the ticket as **Not Persuasive** on the grounds that mixed
EMR/EHR usage in narrative prose is a stylistic preference rather than
a technical defect, and that readers in the PHR audience are expected
to treat the two terms as effectively interchangeable for the purposes
of data-exchange guidance.

#### Justification

This disposition is hard to defend here. The reporter raises a
legitimate consistency concern, the fix is editorial and low-cost, and
the WG itself has already conceded the point ("No particular reason.
Will harmonize and make consistent."). Declining would contradict the
WG's own triage decision and leave a known inconsistency in the
specification's introduction — exactly the kind of friction that
discourages new readers. Declining is included only for completeness.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** A (with a small lean toward folding in
Disposition B's glossary sentence).

The ticket is a textbook *Persuasive* technical correction: the WG has
already agreed to the change, the reporter's complaint is well-founded,
and the fix is narrative-only with no impact on conformance, profiles,
or implementations. Disposition A matches the resolution already
recorded on the ticket and should be applied as the minimum change.

If the WG has even a few minutes for editorial polish, it is worth
combining A with the small glossary addition from B: the reporter
explicitly offered "or at least provide a clear definition of them" as
an acceptable outcome, and a one-line definition of EMR vs. EHR removes
the ambiguity for future readers without expanding scope. Disposition C
should not be selected — it would reverse a triage decision the WG has
already made and leave a known inconsistency in the IG's introduction.
