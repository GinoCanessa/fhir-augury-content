## Summary

The reporter (Rebecca Baker) observes that the opening paragraph of Section 10
of the Personal Health Record (FHIR) IG — the Physiology Domain section —
conflates two distinct concerns. As written, the paragraph begins by noting the
challenge of deciding *which data to include* in a PHR, then pivots within the
same paragraph to the practical problem of *finding suitable test data* and
the use of the Synthea synthetic data generator for various medical
conditions. The reporter argues that these are separate issues and that
mixing them in a single paragraph reduces clarity for implementers reading
the IG.

The ticket recommends splitting the discussion into two distinct points:

1. **What data should be in the PHR (which physiology domains?)** — argued
   to be driven by the person's state of health, with examples such as the
   Endocrine domain being relevant for a person with diabetes and the
   Cardiovascular domain for a person with heart failure.
2. **How to test an implementation using synthetic data and the Synthea
   generator.**

There are no Jira cross-references, no related Zulip threads, and no related
GitHub items associated with this ticket, so the request stands on its own as
an editorial / structural improvement to a single section of the PHR IG.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Restructure the opening paragraph of Section 10 (Physiology Domain) of the
Personal Health Record (FHIR) IG into two clearly separated sub-sections (or
two distinct paragraphs with sub-headings):

1. **"Choosing which physiology domains to include"** — A paragraph that
   discusses how the set of physiology domains relevant to a given PHR is
   typically driven by the person's clinical state. Use the reporter's
   examples (Endocrine domain for diabetes; Cardiovascular domain for heart
   failure) as illustrations, and frame domain selection as a function of
   active problems / conditions rather than a one-size-fits-all list.
2. **"Testing a PHR implementation with synthetic data"** — A separate
   paragraph that introduces the use of the Synthea synthetic data generator
   for producing test data across various medical conditions, with a
   pointer/link to the Synthea project for implementers who want to generate
   their own datasets.

No normative content changes; this is an editorial / structural change to
narrative text in the PHR IG only.

#### Justification

The reporter's analysis is correct on its face: the two ideas serve
different audiences and answer different questions ("what should I model?"
vs. "how do I test what I built?"). Splitting them improves
scan-readability of the IG, which is consistent with general FHIR IG
authoring guidance to keep narrative paragraphs focused on a single concept.
Because the change is purely editorial — no resources, profiles,
extensions, value sets, or constraints are touched — it is low-risk,
imposes no implementation burden, and does not introduce any
backward-compatibility concerns.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Adopt the spirit of the request but go further: promote each of the two
concerns to its own dedicated subsection within Section 10 with stable
anchors, e.g.:

- **§10.x "Selecting Physiology Domains for a PHR"** — narrative + a small
  table mapping representative conditions (diabetes, heart failure,
  asthma, CKD, etc.) to the physiology domains they typically engage. Where
  the IG already defines or references domain-level
  artifacts/profiles/value sets, link to them from the table.
- **§10.y "Generating Test Data with Synthea"** — a dedicated subsection
  covering recommended Synthea modules per condition, links to the Synthea
  project, and any PHR-specific guidance on shaping the generated data
  (e.g., filtering to patient-relevant resources, time-window selection,
  de-identification considerations).

This converts the opening paragraph from prose into navigable, durable
sections that implementers can deep-link to.

#### Justification

Disposition A fixes the immediate clarity problem but leaves both topics
buried in narrative. Both topics are routinely-asked questions for PHR
implementers ("which domains do I model?" and "how do I get realistic
test data?"), so promoting them to first-class subsections with stable
anchors makes them discoverable from the IG table of contents and
linkable from external materials. The trade-off is greater editorial
effort and a small risk that the condition→domain table becomes a
maintenance burden if it drifts out of sync with the rest of the IG; that
risk can be mitigated by keeping the table illustrative rather than
exhaustive and labeling it as such.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline the change as a low-value editorial tweak and leave the existing
paragraph as written. Rationale: the paragraph already contains both pieces
of information, and a careful reader can extract them; the workgroup's
limited editorial bandwidth is better spent on normative or structural
issues.

#### Justification

Defensible only if the workgroup is constrained on editor cycles and
prioritizes normative work over narrative polish. In that case, this
ticket can be closed as "won't fix — narrative readable as-is" without
any harm to implementers or downstream tooling, since no normative
content is at stake.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** A

The reporter has correctly identified that the paragraph mixes two
distinct concerns ("what to include" vs. "how to test"), and a simple
split into two paragraphs (or short subsections) directly addresses the
clarity problem with negligible cost and zero implementation impact. It
is a pure narrative change in the PHR IG, with no profiles, extensions,
or value sets in scope, and no related Zulip or GitHub discussion suggests
controversy. Disposition B is attractive and the workgroup may want to
escalate to it if an editor is willing to author the
condition-to-domain table and a fuller Synthea subsection, but B should
not block A — A can land first as the minimum acceptable fix, with B
filed as a follow-up enhancement if desired. Disposition C is hard to
defend because the editorial cost of A is genuinely small and the
clarity benefit for first-time readers of the PHR IG is real.
