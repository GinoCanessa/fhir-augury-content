## Summary

Virginia Lorenzi is requesting that the "Relevant Laws" section of the
Personal Health Record (PHR) FHIR Implementation Guide either be
clarified with sufficient detail to make its contents actionable, or be
removed entirely. The reporter notes that the entries currently in the
section are US-specific and that the IG does not explain how the cited
laws are relevant to implementers, leaving the section ambiguous and of
limited practical value. Their preference, as worded in the ticket, is
to "keep it simple and remove" the section unless the work group is
prepared to invest in proper clarification.

No other Jira tickets, Zulip threads, or GitHub items were surfaced as
cross-references for FHIR-50632, so the request stands as a focused
editorial change scoped to the PHR IG narrative.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Remove the "Relevant Laws" section from the Personal Health Record
(FHIR) Implementation Guide narrative in its entirety. This includes
deleting the section heading, any introductory paragraph, and the list
of US-specific statutes/regulations currently enumerated there. Update
the IG's table of contents and any in-page anchors that reference the
section so no broken navigation remains. No structural changes to
profiles, extensions, value sets, or examples are required.

#### Justification

The reporter's primary concern is that the section provides
jurisdiction-specific names without explaining how those laws bear on
implementing the PHR IG. FHIR base specifications and most cross-
jurisdictional IGs intentionally avoid embedding US-only legal
references in the normative narrative because they are not generally
applicable to the international implementer audience and risk going
stale when statutes are amended or superseded. Removing the section
eliminates that maintenance debt and aligns the PHR IG with the FHIR
project convention of keeping jurisdiction-specific guidance in
realm-scoped IGs (e.g., US Core, Da Vinci IGs) rather than in
multi-realm guidance. It is also a low-risk change: deleting narrative
prose has no impact on conformance, generated artifacts, or downstream
validators.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Retain the section but rewrite it so that each cited law is paired
with: (1) an explicit statement of which PHR-IG behavior it
constrains (e.g., access, disclosure, audit, breach notification),
(2) a citation to the authoritative source, and (3) an explicit
"US realm only" callout. Move the rewritten section under a
clearly-scoped heading such as "Jurisdictional Considerations
(United States)" so non-US implementers can skip it. Leave space for
parallel subsections (e.g., EU/GDPR) to be added later by other
realms.

#### Justification

If the work group believes the legal context is genuinely useful for
US implementers, the underlying defect is editorial vagueness rather
than the section's existence. Rewriting it with concrete linkages
between each law and a specific IG behavior addresses Virginia's
"clarify or remove" framing in the "clarify" direction and preserves
information that may help US implementers reason about consent,
disclosure, and audit obligations. Scoping the heading to a named
jurisdiction also signals to non-US readers that the content is
optional, which mitigates the "US-specific without warning" concern
the ticket raises. The trade-off versus Disposition A is the
ongoing editorial cost of keeping the citations current as US law
evolves.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Leave the "Relevant Laws" section as it currently stands, on the
basis that it provides historical/contextual framing for US
implementers and that any deficiency is a matter of editorial
preference rather than a defect in the IG. Resolve the ticket as
"Persuasive with Modification — no change" or "Not Persuasive".

#### Justification

A Decline is defensible only if the work group concludes that (a)
the section as written is already serving its intended audience,
and (b) the cost of either rewriting or removing it outweighs the
benefit. Given that the reporter is an experienced PHR/Patient
Empowerment contributor and explicitly identifies the section as
unclear, this is a weak position: the ticket does not allege that
the laws cited are wrong, only that their relevance is opaque, and
that is a real implementer-experience problem. This disposition is
included for completeness so the work group can weigh it against
A and B, but it should be selected only if there is concrete
evidence (e.g., implementer feedback) that the section in its
current form is being used as-is.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** A

The ticket is small, the requested change is editorial, and the
reporter's framing ("clarify or remove") explicitly authorizes the
removal path. Removing the section is a one-time, low-risk edit that
permanently eliminates a US-specific paragraph from a multi-realm IG
and ends the maintenance burden of keeping statutory references
current. Disposition B is reasonable but commits the Patient
Empowerment work group to ongoing editorial upkeep of legal content
that is only marginally within its remit — and the IG can always
re-introduce a properly-scoped "Jurisdictional Considerations"
section in a future ballot cycle if implementers request it.
Disposition C is the weakest option because it preserves the very
ambiguity the ticket flags. Recommend the work group accept the
removal at triage and ask the editor to verify there are no inbound
links from other PHR-IG pages before applying the change.
