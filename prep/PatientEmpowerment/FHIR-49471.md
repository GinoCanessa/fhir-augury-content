## Summary

The reporter is reviewing a table of clinical "domains" in the Personal
Health Record (PHR) FHIR specification — a table the spec itself flags
as having been generated with the assistance of Claude LLM and in need
of human review. Rather than carrying forward an LLM-generated list of
domains, the reporter recommends adopting an existing, authoritative
domain taxonomy — specifically the NIH NCI Thesaurus "Medical Specialty"
hierarchy (NCIt C12913), the CDISC SDTM Implementation Guide v3.4
domains (Section 6, page 92), or the relevant ICD-11 chapter. They
additionally call out three concrete domain-grouping issues in the
current PHR table:

1. *Obstetrics* and *Pregnancy* are typically combined, and usually
   combined with *Gynecology* (e.g., OB/GYN).
2. *Andrology* is typically combined with *Urology*.
3. *Immunology* and *Virology* are typically combined.

No cross-referenced Jira tickets, Zulip threads, or GitHub items were
returned by FhirAugury for this ticket, so the request stands on its
own.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Replace the LLM-generated domain table in the Personal Health Record
(FHIR) specification with a curated value set that is bound to (or
extracted from) an established external domain taxonomy. Specifically:

- Define a new value set, e.g., `phr-clinical-domain`, sourced from
  NCI Thesaurus C12913 ("Medical Specialty"), with optional supplemental
  concepts drawn from CDISC SDTM Section 6 where SDTM offers domain
  granularity not present in NCIt.
- Bind the relevant PHR element (the field that today selects from the
  hand-authored table) to that new value set with strength `extensible`.
- Apply the three explicit consolidations the reporter calls out — i.e.,
  collapse the separate "Obstetrics", "Pregnancy", and "Gynecology"
  rows into a single Obstetrics/Gynecology entry; merge "Andrology"
  into "Urology"; merge "Immunology" and "Virology" — so the published
  list matches established clinical practice rather than the
  LLM-suggested split.
- Remove the editorial note that the table was generated by Claude LLM
  and was pending review, since the table will no longer be a hand-rolled
  artifact.

#### Justification

The reporter's underlying concern — that an LLM-generated list of
clinical domains is being shipped in a normative-track FHIR
specification without grounding in an established taxonomy — is a
legitimate quality and provenance issue for a HL7 work product. FHIR's
own conventions strongly prefer terminology bindings to recognised code
systems (UMLS-family, SNOMED, LOINC, NCIt, ICD, etc.) over ad hoc
in-spec enumerations, and NCIt C12913 is a stable, freely-available,
maintained source for medical-specialty concepts that other HL7 IGs and
US Core artifacts already reference. Using NCIt also positions the PHR
spec to interoperate with downstream consumers (clinical research,
oncology, patient-facing apps) that already use NCIt. The three
specific consolidations are clinically uncontroversial and align with
how care is organised in practice, so accepting them does not introduce
ambiguity.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Keep an in-spec domain list for the Personal Health Record's UX-facing
classification, but (a) treat it as `example`-strength terminology
rather than a normative enumeration, (b) explicitly map each entry to
its corresponding NCIt C12913 concept (and, where useful, CDISC SDTM
domain code) in a side table, and (c) apply the reporter's three
consolidations (OB/GYN, Andrology→Urology, Immunology+Virology) to the
in-spec list. Also strike the "generated by Claude LLM, please review"
editorial note now that human review has been performed.

This keeps the PHR spec self-contained and patient-friendly while
restoring the provenance and interoperability that the reporter is
asking for.

#### Justification

The PHR specification is patient-facing and intentionally pitched at a
different vocabulary level than clinical research instruments. NCIt
C12913 and CDISC SDTM both contain very granular specialty concepts
that are useful for indexing professional records but can overwhelm a
patient-facing browse list. A hybrid — short, plain-language in-spec
list with a documented mapping to NCIt — preserves usability while
addressing the reporter's concern about ungrounded, LLM-generated
content. An `example` binding also avoids constraining implementers
who may already have their own specialty lists (e.g., from EHR or payer
directories), which would otherwise have to be reconciled against an
extensible binding under Disposition A.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline the request as written, on the grounds that "review the
LLM-generated table for accuracy" is itself an editorial task the work
group can perform without a spec change, and the three specific
consolidations the reporter suggests can be handled as inline editorial
fixes to the existing table without introducing a new value-set
dependency. Track the editorial fixes as a follow-up and close this
ticket as "Persuasive with Modification" once those are made.

#### Justification

This is defensible only if the work group judges that (a) NCIt / CDISC /
ICD-11 are out of scope for the current PHR ballot cycle, and (b) the
table is small and stable enough that a one-time human review eliminates
the LLM-provenance concern. A pure decline is hard to justify on the
substance of the reporter's argument — the request is reasonable — so
the rationale here is essentially scope/timing, not disagreement.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

The reporter is right that a HL7 specification should not ship an
LLM-generated, ungrounded enumeration of clinical domains, and the
three consolidations they call out are clinically standard and should
be adopted regardless of which disposition wins. However, the Personal
Health Record specification is patient-facing, and the full NCIt
"Medical Specialty" hierarchy is too granular for a PHR browse/
classification UI. Option B captures the substance of the reporter's
request — provenance, grounding in an established taxonomy, and the
three consolidations — while keeping the in-spec list short and
patient-appropriate, and avoiding the implementer churn that an
extensible binding to NCIt would introduce. If the Patient Empowerment
work group later decides the PHR domain list should be normative and
machine-comparable across systems, escalating from B to A is a
straightforward follow-on change.
