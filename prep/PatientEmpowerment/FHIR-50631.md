## Summary

The ticket asks the Personal Health Record (PHR) FHIR Implementation Guide
to add an explicit definition of what a "Personal Health Record" is. The
reporter notes that the IG currently lacks such a definition and proposes
adopting the wording from the Patient Care Device (PCD) white paper, which
in turn relies on the Markle Foundation Connecting for Health work group's
2003 definition: "an electronic application through which individuals can
access, manage and share their health information, and that of others for
whom they are authorized, in a private, secure, and confidential
environment." The ticket also asks the IG to clarify how the PHR is
distinct from (but overlaps with) the provider-managed EHR and the
payer-managed insurance record, and suggests including a diagram showing
the three record types and their shared content. No related Jira tickets
were surfaced via cross-references.

## Details

**Description:**

A definition of PHR is not provided and is needed. We could reference the
PCD white paper which provides a detailed definition and the following
summary:

> Nearly two decades ago, the Markle Foundation's Personal Health
> workgroup convened to discuss the state-of-the-art in managing personal
> health information. The workgroup defined the PHR as "an electronic
> application through which individuals can access, manage and share
> their health information, and that of others for whom they are
> authorized, in a private, secure, and confidential environment." Their
> early vision was that PHRs would enable individuals to access and
> coordinate comprehensive, lifelong health information and exchange
> necessary parts of it.
>
> (The Personal Health Working Group. A public-private collaborative:
> final report. Markle Foundation's Connecting for Health 2003:1-58.
> https://markle.org/publications/1429-personal-health-working-group-finalreport/)

The PHR is distinct from the EHR for the patient — which is managed by
the healthcare provider — and the insurance record for the patient,
which is managed by the payer. However all three types of records have
information in common, as shown by the following diagram.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Add an explicit "What is a PHR?" subsection to the PHR (FHIR) IG's
introduction / scope narrative (in `HL7/personal-health-record-format-ig`).
The new content would:

1. Quote the Markle Foundation 2003 definition verbatim: *"an electronic
   application through which individuals can access, manage and share
   their health information, and that of others for whom they are
   authorized, in a private, secure, and confidential environment."*
2. Cite the source (PCD white paper / Markle "Connecting for Health"
   final report, with the markle.org URL).
3. Add a short paragraph distinguishing the PHR from the EHR
   (provider-managed) and the insurance record (payer-managed), and
   note the substantial information overlap among the three.
4. Include the three-circle Venn-style diagram referenced in the ticket
   description, showing PHR ∩ EHR ∩ insurance-record overlap.

This matches what was actually merged via PR #76 according to the
resolution note.

#### Justification

The IG of a *Personal Health Record* format that does not define
"Personal Health Record" is missing a foundational scoping element;
implementers have no authoritative anchor for what is and is not in
scope. The Markle Foundation definition is widely cited, has stood for
two decades, and is already used by the PCD white paper, so adopting it
keeps the PHR IG aligned with prior HL7 / community work. The
EHR/insurance-record distinction is necessary because all three record
classes share data (Observations, MedicationStatements, Conditions,
etc.) and implementers routinely conflate them; without explicit
boundaries, conformance testing and downstream profiling are harder.
This disposition matches the action the work group already took
(Motion Rachel / 2nd Hisashi, 4-0-0, Resolution: Persuasive).

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Adopt the Markle definition but place it in a dedicated **Glossary /
Definitions** page rather than only inside the introduction, and link
to it from the introduction, the scope page, and any conformance page
that uses the term "PHR." Pair the definition with an explicit
*non*-definition section that lists what the PHR (FHIR) IG does **not**
cover (e.g., provider-side EHR workflows, payer adjudication data,
device-only telemetry pipelines). Keep the three-record Venn diagram,
but render it with FHIR resource examples in each region (e.g.,
"Observation" in the overlap, "Coverage / EOB" in the payer-only
region) so it doubles as informative guidance.

#### Justification

A glossary entry is reusable and can be referenced from many narrative
pages without duplicating prose, which keeps wording consistent if the
work group later wants to refine the definition. The explicit
out-of-scope list addresses the most common implementer confusion — "is
this an EHR replacement?" — which a definition alone tends not to
prevent. Annotating the diagram with concrete FHIR resources upgrades
it from a conceptual picture to actionable scoping guidance, which is
where the IG actually adds value over the original Markle / PCD prose.
Trade-off vs. Disposition A: more authoring work and a slightly larger
set of pages to maintain, but better long-term coherence.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline to embed a formal PHR definition in the IG, on the grounds
that: (a) the Patient Empowerment work group's published charter
already defines the PHR / PHR-S scope, and (b) the term "Personal
Health Record" is well-established in the broader health-IT literature.
Instead, add a single sentence in the introduction pointing readers to
the Patient Empowerment charter and to the Markle / ONC literature, and
let those external sources carry the definitional weight.

#### Justification

A defensible decline rests on the principle that an IG should focus on
*format and exchange* rather than on restating definitions that already
exist elsewhere. If the work group's charter already says what a PHR
is, a second definition inside the IG creates a maintenance burden:
the two can drift out of sync, and implementers have to decide which
one is authoritative.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition: A (with optional uplift toward B).**

The work group has already voted Persuasive (4-0-0) and merged PR #76,
so Disposition A is, in effect, what shipped. It is the right call:
the IG was missing a foundational definition, the Markle wording is
well-established, and the EHR/insurance-record distinction directly
addresses a known source of implementer confusion. Disposition C is
weak because the IG audience (PHR app builders) cannot reasonably be
expected to read the Patient Empowerment charter to learn what the IG
is for. Disposition B's glossary-and-non-scope structure is a strict
improvement over A and is worth doing as a follow-up enhancement
(separate ticket) once the basic definition added by PR #76 has had
review time; it does not justify reopening the current ticket.
