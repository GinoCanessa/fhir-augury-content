## Summary

The ticket questions the provenance and validation of the "Data Viewing"
section of the AI Transparency on FHIR IG, which enumerates a set of actors
together with the questions each actor would purportedly ask when reviewing
AI-influenced data. The reporter (Virginia Lorenzi) asks whether the IG
authors actually engaged representatives of those actor roles to confirm the
questions, or whether the lists were assembled without that validation. She
specifically calls out the "lawyer" actor as ambiguous — a lawyer's
information needs depend heavily on whom they represent (provider vs.
patient) — and points out that patients and caregivers themselves are
likely an additional viewer category whose questions are not represented.
The ticket is one of 12 items the AI Transparency on FHIR work group has
classified under "Use Cases & Examples" for the January 2026 ballot
triage (see the related Zulip thread). There are no linked or
cross-referenced Jira tickets and no associated GitHub items.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Treat the ticket as a content-validation gap in the IG's Data Viewing
section. Concretely:

1. The work group asks the spec authors to identify, for each actor
   currently listed in Data Viewing (including "Lawyer"), the
   stakeholder representative(s) who confirmed the listed questions, and
   record that attribution either in a non-normative "Acknowledgements /
   Stakeholder Validation" subsection of the Data Viewing page or in the
   IG's overall acknowledgements page.
2. Split the "Lawyer" actor into context-qualified variants (e.g.,
   "Lawyer representing provider/health system" and "Lawyer
   representing patient"), each with its own question set, since the
   reporter correctly notes that the information needs differ.
3. Add a new "Patient / Caregiver" actor entry with its own questions,
   developed with input from a patient/caregiver representative
   (e.g., HL7 Patient Empowerment WG or a PFAC-equivalent reviewer).
4. If an actor's questions cannot be sourced to a representative
   reviewer, mark them in the IG as "illustrative — not stakeholder
   validated" so implementers understand their status.

#### Justification

The ticket is filed at "Highest" priority by an EHR-WG reviewer and
points at a real methodology gap: the IG presents actor questions as if
they were elicited from those actors, without documenting that
elicitation. Adding attribution and explicitly adding the
patient/caregiver viewer is consistent with FHIR's general practice of
crediting stakeholder input in IG acknowledgements, and the lawyer
split addresses a concrete ambiguity the reporter raised. The change is
non-normative, has no impact on conformance resources, and can be made
within the existing Data Viewing page.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Rather than expanding the actor list and chasing per-actor stakeholder
attribution, restructure the Data Viewing section so the questions are
presented as **illustrative information needs**, organized by viewing
*purpose* (e.g., clinical decision-making, audit/quality review,
legal/forensic review, patient understanding) rather than by named
actor role. Add a short methodology note stating that the questions
were derived by the authoring group as illustrative examples and invite
implementers and reviewers to extend them. Retain the existing actor
labels only as informal tags on each question.

#### Justification

The underlying complaint — that "lawyer" is too coarse and that
patients/caregivers are missing — is really a symptom of trying to map
information needs to a fixed set of human role labels. Re-anchoring on
purpose-of-viewing avoids the recurring "did you talk to a real X"
challenge for every role label, eliminates the lawyer-perspective
ambiguity automatically, and naturally encompasses
patient/caregiver viewing without the IG having to claim formal
patient-stakeholder validation it may not have. The cost is a larger
editorial change to the Data Viewing page than Disposition A, but it is
still non-normative and avoids re-litigating the same question on
future ballots.

---

### Disposition C: Decline (Persuasive with Modification)

#### Proposal

Decline the request to formally validate the actor questions with each
named role group, on the grounds that the Data Viewing section is
explicitly illustrative and not normative. Instead, address the
reporter's concern with a single editorial change: add a clearly
worded disclaimer at the top of the Data Viewing section stating that
the listed actors and questions are illustrative examples produced by
the IG authors to motivate the use cases, are not the result of formal
stakeholder elicitation, and may be extended by implementers. Note in
the disposition response that patient/caregiver viewing and the
multiple lawyer perspectives are intentionally left to implementer
discretion.

#### Justification

A January-ballot IG cannot realistically run a stakeholder-validation
exercise across every named actor before publication, and demanding
that bar would block ballot resolution on a non-normative section.
Marking the section as illustrative is the standard FHIR mechanism for
this situation and matches how comparable IGs (e.g., other transparency
and provenance IGs) handle role enumerations. The reporter's specific
concerns (lawyer ambiguity, missing patient/caregiver) are
acknowledged but treated as editorial improvements that do not require
restructuring the section.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** A

The ticket comes from an experienced EHR-WG reviewer at Highest
priority and identifies two concrete, fixable defects: an under-specified
"lawyer" actor and a missing patient/caregiver viewer. Disposition C is
the path of least resistance but leaves both concrete defects unaddressed
and would likely draw a negative-vote follow-up from the reporter.
Disposition B is structurally cleaner but is a larger editorial rewrite
than the ballot resolution window comfortably allows for a single
comment, and it risks de-emphasising the actor framing that the rest of
the IG (and other AI-Transparency stakeholders) are already using.
Disposition A keeps the existing actor structure, fixes the two specific
defects the reporter named, and adds a small attribution / "illustrative"
hedge for any actor where stakeholder validation cannot be sourced — which
gives the work group a clean, in-scope response without committing to a
broader restructure.
