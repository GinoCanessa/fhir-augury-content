## Summary

Erik Moll requests that the AI Transparency IG not *require* a device that
incidentally has an AI capability — for example, a consumer smart watch that
detects heart-rate-variability anomalies, or a smart blood-pressure meter
that runs an on-device classifier — to declare its `Device.type` (or
equivalent type slot) as "AI Device Type". The position is that such a
device should be **able to** use the AI Device profile (so the AI-specific
data points are still expressible) without being **forced** into an
"AI Device" type categorisation that may misrepresent what is, in
clinical/inventory terms, primarily a medical device. The single comment
on the ticket, from John Moehrke (2026-01-26), asks the reporter for
concrete examples and whether an existing Device profile for medical
devices is in scope.

The only cross-reference is a single Zulip post — the AI Transparency WG's
"IG - Jan ballot" triage table, which simply files this ticket under the
"Profile & Model Structure" category alongside ~18 other ballot comments.
No substantive Zulip discussion specific to FHIR-54757 has occurred yet,
and no related GitHub PRs/issues/commits were found.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

In the AI Transparency IG's `AIDevice` profile (and any companion type
value set), relax the constraint that pins `Device.type` to an "AI Device
Type" code. Concretely:

- Change the `Device.type` slicing/binding on the AI Device profile from
  *required* to **extensible** (or **preferred**), so that a device whose
  primary `Device.type` is, e.g., a SNOMED CT "wrist-worn heart rate
  monitor" code can still conform.
- Move the "this device performs AI" signal off `Device.type` and onto a
  dedicated, additive slot — for example a sliced `Device.property`
  (code = `ai-capability`, value = AI category code) or a sliced
  `Device.specialization`. Keep `Device.type` describing **what the
  device is**; let the AI-capability signal be orthogonal.
- Document in the profile narrative that conformance to AI Device does
  **not** require the device to be primarily an "AI device", only that
  it expose at least one AI capability via the new property/specialization
  slot.

#### Justification

This is what the reporter asked for and is consistent with how FHIR
typically separates "what something is" (`Device.type`) from "what
features/capabilities it has" (`Device.property`,
`Device.specialization`). It removes a category error — a smart watch is
a wrist-worn heart-rate monitor that *also* runs AI, not an "AI device"
in the inventory-management sense — and makes the profile usable by the
much larger population of consumer wearables and home medical devices
that ship AI features as one capability among many. It also addresses
John Moehrke's implicit question: a device may already conform to a
medical-device Device profile; AI Device should compose with that, not
displace it.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Keep `Device.type` as it is on the AI Device profile, but **split the
profile into two**:

- `AIDevice` — for devices whose *primary* purpose is to deliver an AI
  service (e.g., a standalone clinical decision support box). This
  profile keeps the existing required "AI Device Type" constraint.
- `AICapableDevice` (new, derived sibling) — for devices whose primary
  purpose is something else (clinical measurement, monitoring, etc.) but
  which include one or more AI capabilities. This profile does **not**
  constrain `Device.type` and instead requires at least one
  `Device.property` (or `Device.specialization`) slice declaring an AI
  capability code.

Update the IG navigation and any referencing profiles (e.g., the AI
Transparency Provenance / Observation profiles) to accept either
profile as the `subject`/`device` reference.

#### Justification

Preserves the current strict semantics of `AIDevice` (useful for
implementers who *do* want to flag a device as primarily an AI device,
e.g. for procurement or FDA-registration metadata) while still solving
the reporter's case. Trade-off vs. Disposition A: more profile surface
area to maintain, and downstream profiles need to reference a choice of
two profiles instead of one. Backward-compatible for anyone already
conforming to today's `AIDevice`; nothing they have published becomes
non-conformant.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline as duplicative of existing FHIR mechanisms: a device that is
"primarily a medical device with an AI capability" should simply
conform to a medical-device Device profile *and* (separately) be the
subject of AI-related Provenance / Observation resources from the AI
Transparency IG, without claiming conformance to `AIDevice` at all. The
AI Device profile is intentionally for devices whose marketed identity
is "AI device", and the required type is a feature, not a defect.
Recommend the reporter use the per-inference Provenance/Observation path
instead.

#### Justification

Defensible if the WG's design intent is that `AIDevice` is a narrow
classification used for regulatory/discovery purposes and that
AI-as-a-feature on otherwise-conventional devices is already covered by
attaching AI-Transparency Provenance/Observation resources to those
devices. Avoids profile churn during ballot. The cost is real, however:
implementers will lose a single profile they can validate consumer-
wearable AI features against, and it leaves the reporter's concern
substantively unaddressed.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

Disposition B (split the profile) is the best balance of the three. It
directly addresses the reporter's concern — a smart watch / smart BP
meter is no longer forced to mis-declare its `Device.type` — while
preserving the current `AIDevice` semantics for implementers who want
the strict "this is an AI device" classification (which has real
regulatory value and is likely why the original constraint exists).
Disposition A is cleaner but more disruptive to anyone who has already
implemented against the current `AIDevice`, and it weakens a binding
that some balloters likely consider load-bearing. Disposition C
plausibly matches the WG's original intent but leaves a real gap for
the consumer-wearable / home-medical-device population that the IG will
increasingly need to cover. Before locking B in, the WG should confirm
with the reporter that "AICapableDevice" (or an equivalent name)
satisfies the use case, and should ask John Moehrke's question
explicitly: which medical-device Device profile is expected to compose
with the new sibling profile.
