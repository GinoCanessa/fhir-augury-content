## Summary

Mitra Rocca submits that the AI Transparency IG's current treatment of "AI
algorithms" and the corresponding Scope discussion is over-simplified. The
ticket observes that AI algorithms span a wide spectrum — from classic,
deterministic AI (e.g., rule-based / symbolic systems) to modern
machine-learning and generative models — and that the IG's Background
section only addresses problems characteristic of ML-style algorithms
(notably non-determinism). Classic AI raises a different set of concerns
(e.g., brittleness of rules, knowledge-base maintenance, explainability of
chained inferences) that the current Scope text does not acknowledge. The
reporter requests a deeper, more nuanced Scope discussion that distinguishes
between these algorithm classes and what each implies for transparency.

There are no directly linked Jira tickets and no GitHub cross-references.
The only cross-reference is a Zulip triage message for the January ballot
that places this ticket in the "Documentation & Clarity" bucket alongside a
large family of similar Scope/Background editorial requests
(e.g., FHIR-54275, FHIR-54276, FHIR-54262, FHIR-54260), suggesting it
should be addressed as part of a coordinated Scope/Background rewrite
rather than in isolation.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Expand the IG's **Scope** page (and the supporting **Background** section)
to introduce an explicit taxonomy of AI algorithms covered by the IG —
minimally distinguishing (a) classic / symbolic / rule-based AI from
(b) statistical / machine-learning approaches, and noting (c) generative
and foundation-model variants. For each class, add one short paragraph
describing the transparency concerns that motivate IG coverage:
non-determinism, training-data provenance, drift, and update cadence for
ML; rule-base provenance, knowledge-source attribution, and inference
chain explainability for classic AI. Make explicit that the Background
section's current emphasis on non-determinism is one motivating concern
among several, not the only one. Tie each concern back to the
specific IG artifacts (model-card profiles, Provenance usage) that
address it, so readers see the through-line from algorithm class →
transparency concern → IG mechanism.

#### Justification

The reporter's observation is factually correct: the current Background
text frames the problem space primarily through ML-shaped concerns
(notably non-determinism), which leaves classic AI implementers without a
clear answer to "does this IG apply to me, and if so, what should I
disclose?" An explicit taxonomy in Scope is a low-risk, editorial-only
change that improves implementer understanding without altering any
profiles, value sets, or examples, and it aligns with how other HL7 IGs
scope themselves when the underlying technology family is heterogeneous.
It also positions the IG to age gracefully as new algorithm classes
emerge.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Keep the Scope statement intentionally narrow — declaring that the IG
covers transparency disclosures expressible via the model-card community
standard the IG profiles — and instead add a short **"Applicability"**
or **"What this IG does and does not address"** subsection that
acknowledges the spectrum of AI algorithms (classic vs. ML vs.
generative) and explicitly states that the IG's mechanisms are tuned to
the disclosures captured by model cards. Where a classic-AI deployment
has analogous disclosures, the IG can be reused; where it does not, the
IG defers to other guidance. Cite the model-card community standard as
the authoritative source for how transparency expectations evolve as the
underlying algorithm landscape changes — directly mirroring the
position John Moehrke articulated in the comment.

#### Justification

This addresses the reporter's underlying concern (classic AI is not
adequately spoken to) without over-committing the IG to maintaining its
own algorithm taxonomy that will inevitably drift from how the broader
AI/ML community describes itself. It also preserves the IG's stated
strategy of riding the model-card standard, which is a defensible
architectural choice: the IG profiles a community artifact rather than
inventing one. Trade-off vs. Disposition A: less prescriptive guidance
for classic-AI implementers, but lower long-term maintenance burden and
no risk of the IG's taxonomy contradicting future model-card revisions.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline as **out of scope for this ballot cycle / addressed by reliance
on the model-card community standard**. Respond that the IG deliberately
delegates the evolving definition of "AI algorithm" to the upstream
model-card standard it profiles, and that the IG's Background section is
intended to motivate why model-card-style disclosures matter — not to
serve as a comprehensive taxonomy of AI techniques. No spec change.

#### Justification

This is the position John Moehrke staked in his comment on the ticket.
It is defensible if the workgroup is comfortable that "AI algorithm" is
a definitional question owned by the model-card community and not by
HL7. The risk is that this leaves the reporter's concern unaddressed —
classic-AI implementers continue to read the Background section and
conclude that the IG is ML-only — and the same comment is likely to
re-appear on the next ballot.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

The reporter's concern is real and is unlikely to disappear — the
Background section reads as ML-centric, and consensus across the
"Documentation & Clarity" bucket on the January-ballot triage shows that
Scope/Background language is the single largest source of ballot
feedback. At the same time, the workgroup commenter's instinct to defer
algorithm-taxonomy questions to the upstream model-card standard is
sound architecture: the IG profiles a community artifact and should not
fork its own definition of what counts as "AI." Disposition B threads
both needles by adding a brief Applicability / "what this IG does and
does not address" passage that explicitly acknowledges the
classic-vs.-ML spectrum and points to the model-card standard as the
authority on evolving definitions, without committing the IG to its own
taxonomy. It directly answers the reporter's complaint, costs only
editorial effort, and preserves the architectural posture from the
john_moehrke comment. Disposition A is the right answer if the
workgroup wants the IG to take a stronger normative position on
algorithm classes; Disposition C will likely re-surface as a comment in
the next cycle.
