## Summary

The reporter (IgnacioJauregui) asks the AI Transparency on FHIR IG to take a more explicit editorial position on Model Cards. The IG currently states that "the AI community is defining standards for describing an AI model" and that "two Model-Card formats" (Hugging Face and CHAI) are shown in the Guide, "although other formats may be used as well." The reporter wants the IG to:

1. State whether the IG endorses any particular model-card format (and, if so, which one and why).
2. Explain the differences between the Hugging Face (HF) and CHAI model cards.
3. Acknowledge other standard model-card formats that exist.
4. Characterise the maturity and industry adoption of each.

The request is editorial / scope-clarifying rather than a structural change to FHIR resources or profiles — it asks the IG narrative to be opinionated where it is currently neutral. The ticket has no Jira or GitHub cross-references; it has the highest priority and is currently Triaged with a previously-applied "Persuasive" resolution that was reverted on 2026-02-02 by John Moehrke pending a decision on how to resolve.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Update the AI Transparency IG narrative — both the introductory "Defining the AI" paragraph and §2.6.2 — to:

1. Replace "There are two Model-Card formats shown in this Guide…" with an explicit IG position statement that names the two formats currently illustrated (Hugging Face Model Card and CHAI Model Card / Applied Model Card) and states why they were chosen as exemplars (e.g., HF for breadth of community adoption across open-source ML, CHAI for healthcare-specific applicability and alignment with the U.S. ONC HTI-1 transparency expectations).
2. Add a new short subsection ("Model Card Format Comparison") that summarises the salient differences (scope, intended audience, level of detail, healthcare specificity, regulatory alignment, machine-readability, governance body).
3. List other notable formats — at minimum the Sendak et al. Model Facts Label, and references to ONC HTI-1 / EU AI Act mandated transparency artifacts — and characterise their maturity and adoption based on cited public sources.
4. Conclude with the IG's editorial position: the IG **does not endorse a single format** but **recommends** using a recognised standard format (HF or CHAI) for the Device.property carrying the model card, and treats Hugging Face and CHAI as the IG's reference exemplars while remaining open to other formats via the same `Device.property` extension mechanism.

#### Justification

This is what the reporter literally asks for, and it is achievable as a narrative-only change: the underlying conformance pattern (model-card content carried on `Device.property` via an extension supporting markdown/attachment) is unchanged. The Zulip "AI Working Group?" thread shows the design team has already considered HF, CHAI and Sendak's Model Facts Label, and that there is community appetite for the IG to be clearer about its exemplar choices without forcing a single format. Being explicit about why HF and CHAI were chosen, and acknowledging the Model Facts Label and HTI-1/EU AI Act artifacts, improves transparency of the IG itself and reduces implementer confusion. The previous "Persuasive" resolution that was reverted (per Moehrke's 2026-02-02 comment) suggests the workgroup was already on this trajectory; reinstating it with the additional specificity above closes the loop.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Rather than endorsing or comparing specific formats in IG normative text, restructure §2.6.2 around **requirements a model-card format must meet** to be usable with the IG, and move the format comparison to a non-normative companion page. Concretely:

1. In §2.6.2, define a small set of "model-card format requirements" the IG cares about (e.g., must cover intended use, training data provenance, performance metrics by subgroup, known limitations, version identifier, regulatory/clinical-validation status — aligning with HTI-1 and EU AI Act mandated disclosures).
2. Show HF and CHAI cards as **examples** that satisfy these requirements, with a one-line maturity note for each, but explicitly state the IG endorses **the requirements**, not a particular vendor format.
3. Move the deeper format comparison (HF vs CHAI vs Model Facts Label vs vendor cards) into a new non-normative page (e.g., `model-card-formats.html`) that can evolve with the AI community without re-balloting the IG.
4. Keep the Device.property mechanism unchanged; add a profile or extension hint that the chosen format SHOULD be identifiable (e.g., via a coded `Device.property.type` slice naming the format used).

#### Justification

The Zulip "AI Working Group?" thread makes clear that the model-card landscape is moving fast (LoRA adapters, agentic AI, regulatory churn under HTI-1 and the EU AI Act) and that picking a single endorsed format risks dating the IG. Encoding **requirements** rather than **brands** preserves the IG's authority on transparency without coupling it to any one community's release cadence, and aligns with FHIR's general preference for capability-based conformance over brand-based conformance. Mattias Seth's feedback in that thread — that fragmentation across resources/extensions is the real implementer pain — also supports a stable, requirements-based hook (`Device.property.type` slice) so that consumers can discover *which* card format is in play without parsing the markdown. This still answers the reporter's underlying concern (the IG today is too vague to be actionable) while avoiding the political cost of picking winners between HF and CHAI.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline as out of scope for the 2026 January ballot of the AI Transparency on FHIR IG, with a Not Persuasive — Out of Scope rationale. Retain the current IG language that two model-card formats are shown as examples and that other formats may be used. Optionally add a single sentence pointing readers to the Coalition for Health AI (CHAI) and Hugging Face documentation for further reading, but do not endorse, compare, or rank formats in IG text.

#### Justification

The IG's normative scope is *how* AI metadata attaches to FHIR (Device + property/extension carrying model-card content), not *which* AI-community standard is "best." Endorsing a specific format would commit HL7 to an editorial position on rapidly-evolving non-FHIR standards (HF, CHAI, Model Facts Label, plus emerging EU AI Act and HTI-1 artifacts) that HL7 does not govern and cannot keep current. Maturity/adoption claims would have to be re-balloted whenever the underlying ecosystem shifts. A neutral, format-agnostic position is also more defensible internationally, where neither HF nor CHAI is universally accepted. This rationale would be the basis of the original "Persuasive" resolution that was reverted; the revert reflects a desire to give a *better* answer, not necessarily a different outcome.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

Disposition B best balances the reporter's legitimate complaint (the IG today is too vague to act on) against the durability concerns that drove the revert of the prior resolution. Picking HF or CHAI by name in normative IG text (Disposition A) puts HL7 in the position of editorialising on standards owned by other communities and forces re-ballot whenever those standards evolve — a real risk given how fast the model-card / Model Facts Label / HTI-1 / EU AI Act landscape is moving (visible across the AI/ML Zulip thread). Declining outright (Disposition C) leaves the same gap the reporter is flagging and will simply attract more comments. Restructuring §2.6.2 around **requirements** a model-card format must meet, naming HF and CHAI as exemplars that satisfy those requirements, slicing `Device.property.type` so the format used is machine-discoverable, and moving the comparative narrative to a non-normative companion page gives implementers concrete guidance now while keeping the IG ahead of the format churn. It also matches the design team's stated philosophy in Zulip — leverage external standards where they exist — without forcing HL7 to pick a single one.
