## Summary

The reporter asks the FHIR Infrastructure work group to define the
endianness of the byte stream represented by the `base64Binary`
datatype. Today the spec describes the base64 encoding of a sequence of
bytes but does not state how multi-byte values within that stream are
ordered, and the reporter argues this ambiguity can break interoperable
use of fields such as `Attachment.hash`: if a sender on one platform
serialises a hash with a different byte order than the receiver assumes,
the receiver will recompute a different hash and (if validating)
reject the attachment. The reporter proposes mandating big-endian as
the canonical byte order for the stream encoded into `base64Binary`.

The request grew directly out of the related ticket
[FHIR-51236](https://jira.hl7.org/browse/FHIR-51236) ("Handling of
Attachment.hash"), filed at the same time by the same reporter. That
ticket asks the work group to clarify in the description of
`Attachment.hash` that the *raw* hash bytes (not a hex string of those
bytes) must be base64-encoded, and flags three core examples that
violate that rule. FHIR-51237 generalises one strand of the same
conversation: even after the raw-bytes-vs-hex question is settled,
implementers still need a defined byte order for the bytes they encode.
The supporting Zulip thread "Attachment.hash and hash representation"
in `#implementers` shows Grahame Grieve pushing back that hashing
algorithms are byte-stream operations and not endian-sensitive, while
Quentin Ligier argues the spec should still pin down the byte order
exchanged across systems.

## Details

**Description:**

> The endianness of bytes encoded in the base64Binary datatype is not
> defined. This could cause reading issues for systems that have a
> different endianness than the document creator.
>
> E.g. in Attachment.hash, a change in endianness will make the hash
> invalid, what could cause the reader to reject the data.
>
> Could the big-endianness be specified as required representation of
> the provided stream of bytes?

**Comments:**

- **JIRAUSER28937 (2025-06-25):** Notes that for `Binary.data` (and
  potentially other properties) the endianness is dictated by the
  payload content itself, so any rule should be specified per-property
  rather than on the `base64Binary` datatype.

## Related Zulip Discussions

### [implementers > Attachment.hash and hash representation](https://chat.fhir.org/#narrow/stream/implementers/topic/Attachment.hash%20and%20hash%20representation/near/525702058)

Quentin Ligier opens the thread by noting that two official FHIR
examples encode `Attachment.hash` differently — one base64-encodes the
SHA-1 raw bytes and another base64-encodes the SHA-1 hex string — and
asks which is correct. Grahame Grieve replies that the intent is
clearly to base64 the raw hash bytes and that the validator has now
started enforcing this. Quentin then pivots to a second concern: even
once raw bytes are mandated, the byte order of the hash stream itself
isn't pinned down, and asks whether `base64Binary` should mandate
big-endian. Grahame counters that the standard hashing algorithms are
endian-independent and that he sees no actual interoperability
problem; Quentin maintains that consumers still need a defined byte
sequence to compare against. Jens Villadsen flags that IHE MHD may
need tighter rules even if FHIR core does not, and John Moehrke
agrees this is an MHD problem (he has filed
[IHE/ITI.MHD#248](https://github.com/IHE/ITI.MHD/issues/248) for the
hexBinary↔base64 round-trip) and pushes back that the hash has always
been understood to be calculated on the document byte stream itself.
Grahame closes the FHIR-side discussion suggesting tickets be filed
("make a task then"); Quentin then opens FHIR-51236 and FHIR-51237.
No consensus is reached on FHIR-51237's specific request to mandate
big-endian in `base64Binary`.

Participants: Quentin Ligier, Grahame Grieve, Jens Villadsen, John
Moehrke.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Amend the `base64Binary` datatype definition to state that the
underlying byte stream is an ordered, big-endian sequence of octets,
and that the base64 alphabet from RFC 4648 §4 is applied to that
stream in document order. Add a normative sentence such as: *"The
content of a `base64Binary` element is an ordered sequence of 8-bit
octets; conforming systems SHALL serialise the octets in their
natural (big-endian, network) byte order before applying the base64
encoding, and consumers SHALL interpret the decoded octets in the
same order."* Add a small note in the `Attachment.hash` and
`Binary.data` descriptions pointing back to that rule, since both are
consumed as raw byte streams that must round-trip exactly.

#### Justification

Pinning down the byte order at the datatype level is the smallest
change that closes the ambiguity the reporter identified, and it
matches every implementation observed in the wild — base64 libraries
emit and consume octets in document order, so codifying that as
"big-endian / network byte order" is a no-op for conformant
implementations and merely documents the existing de-facto behaviour.
It also lets `Attachment.hash` validation (which Grahame confirmed the
core validator now enforces) rest on a published rule rather than on
implementer folklore.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Do not change the `base64Binary` datatype itself. Instead, add a
short clarifying paragraph to the `base64Binary` page (and a
cross-reference from `Attachment.hash`, `Attachment.data`, and
`Binary.data`) explaining that `base64Binary` carries an ordered
stream of octets, that the base64 alphabet from RFC 4648 is applied
to the octets in document order, and that the *meaning* of those
octets — including any multi-byte numeric framing within the payload
— is defined by the property's content type, not by the datatype.
Pair this with a worked example for `Attachment.hash` showing that
the bytes encoded are the raw output octets of the hash function in
the order the algorithm produces them.

#### Justification

This approach takes the comment from JIRAUSER28937 seriously: most
`base64Binary` payloads (PDFs, images, signed JWTs, raw `Binary.data`
content, etc.) are self-describing byte streams whose byte order is
fixed by their own format. Imposing a global "big-endian" rule on
`base64Binary` would be either redundant for those cases or, worse,
suggest that the spec is reordering bytes inside an opaque payload.
A clarification keyed to "octet stream in document order" gives
implementers exactly the guarantee FHIR-51237 needs (a hash sender
and receiver will agree on the byte sequence) without overloading
the datatype with a notion that does not actually apply to most of
its uses. It also aligns with Grahame's position in the Zulip thread
that hash algorithms are byte-stream operations.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Mark FHIR-51237 as "Not Persuasive" on the grounds that
`base64Binary` already carries a well-defined ordered octet stream
(every base64 implementation since RFC 4648 produces and consumes
octets in document order), and that "endianness" is a property of
multi-byte numeric encodings within a payload — a layer that
`base64Binary` deliberately does not look inside. Note in the
resolution that for `Attachment.hash` specifically the relevant
question (raw bytes vs hex string) is being addressed under
[FHIR-51236](https://jira.hl7.org/browse/FHIR-51236), and that no
concrete interoperability failure has been demonstrated for
`base64Binary` itself.

#### Justification

This mirrors Grahame's repeated position in the Zulip thread that he
"can't find any evidence that there's an actual problem here for us
to be concerned about." Standard hashing algorithms (SHA-1, SHA-256,
…) emit a defined sequence of octets, base64 encodes octets in
document order, and no committer has produced an example of a real
sender / receiver pair that disagrees on byte order at the
`base64Binary` layer. Adding a normative endianness rule to a
datatype that is already byte-oriented risks confusing implementers
about whether FHIR is making claims about the *contents* of opaque
payloads (PDFs, signed objects, etc.), which it deliberately is
not.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

Disposition B is the right balance. The reporter's concrete worry —
two FHIR systems disagreeing on the byte sequence behind a hash — is
real enough to deserve a documented answer, and FHIR-51236 (which is
the *real* fix for the surfaced examples) leaves "in what order are
those raw bytes laid out before base64?" unanswered. A pure decline
(Disposition C) is defensible against a hostile reading of the spec,
but it leaves implementers with the same paragraph that prompted the
ticket in the first place. Disposition A is too aggressive: as
JIRAUSER28937 points out, `base64Binary` carries many self-describing
formats whose internal byte order is defined elsewhere, so a
datatype-wide "big-endian" rule would either be misleading or
require many caveats. A short clarification at the datatype level
plus a focused note on `Attachment.hash` (and a similar note on
`Binary.data`) gives the reporter the interoperability guarantee
they want without overstepping into payload semantics, and lines up
with both the Zulip discussion and the parallel FHIR-51236 fix.
