## Summary

The ticket asks the FHIR Infrastructure work group to clarify or remove the
final sentence of section 6.1.2.1.2 ("Uncertainty about what is signed") on
the FHIR Core `signatures.html` page. That sentence currently suggests that
"one potential approach systems might take is to include plain text or image
representations of the signature content beside the digital signature, so
that humans can be clear about what was signed from a human user
perspective." The reporter (Eric Haas) finds the recommendation ambiguous on
two fronts: (a) it is not clear whether the suggestion is aimed at signers
or verifiers, nor whether it concerns resource-narrative generation versus
implementer UI; and (b) if the underlying intent is that the digital
signature only covers the narrative, that should instead be expressed as a
formal FHIR canonicalization scheme rather than buried in advisory prose.
The reporter's recommended outcome is to remove the sentence.

There are no formal Jira issue links and no cross-references recorded for
this ticket in the FHIR Augury index. However, this ticket sits within a
clearly coordinated cluster of `signatures.html` change requests filed by
the same reporter ([FHIR-53891](https://jira.hl7.org/browse/FHIR-53891),
[FHIR-53893](https://jira.hl7.org/browse/FHIR-53893),
[FHIR-54080](https://jira.hl7.org/browse/FHIR-54080),
[FHIR-54081](https://jira.hl7.org/browse/FHIR-54081)), all aimed at
tightening the prose in the Signatures and Canonicalization sections of the
Security & Privacy module. Disposition should be coordinated with that
cluster.

## Details

**Description:**

> 6.1.2.1.2 Uncertainty about what is signed
> Signing content and the consequential non-repudiation is often associated
> with human user actions, and human-level accountability. However, human
> users do not sign the resources themselves, they sign some representation
> of the resource, and the difference between these two forms is a
> challenge for implementers. When the goal of the digital signature
> includes human-level non-repudiation, implementers should pay careful
> attention to the resource narrative as the signed form in such cases.

> &nbsp;

> One potential approach systems might take is to include plain text or
> image representations of the signature content beside the digital
> signature, so that humans can be clear about what was signed from a human
> user perspective.

What does the last sentence mean? If it means the signature only applies to
the narrative, that should be discussed as a FHIR canonicalization scheme.

It is unclear who this "potential approach" is directed at, the verifier or
the signer, and whether this recommendation is for resource narrative
generation or implementers' UI.

I recommend removing the sentence.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Remove the trailing sentence from section 6.1.2.1.2 ("Uncertainty about
what is signed") of `signatures.html` in FHIR Core, leaving only the
problem statement about human users not signing resources directly. The
deleted sentence is:

> One potential approach systems might take is to include plain text or
> image representations of the signature content beside the digital
> signature, so that humans can be clear about what was signed from a
> human user perspective.

The remainder of the subsection (the lead paragraph about human-level
non-repudiation and attention to the resource narrative as the signed
form) is retained unchanged. No normative content is affected; the
removed text is purely an advisory "one potential approach" suggestion.

#### Justification

The sentence is genuinely ambiguous on its face: it does not specify
whether the actor is the signer or the verifier, nor whether the
"plain text or image representations" are expected to be produced as
part of the signed payload, generated by the verifier UI for display,
or held outside the signature envelope entirely. As written, it is
non-actionable advice and risks being interpreted as endorsing a
specific (unspecified) UI pattern that FHIR Core does not actually
define. Removing it leaves the subsection with a cleaner statement of
the underlying problem without offering guidance the spec cannot
support. This matches the reporter's stated recommendation and is
consistent with the pattern in the sibling tickets
(FHIR-53891 / 53893 / 54080 / 54081), which all favour trimming
speculative prose from `signatures.html`.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Keep the intent of the sentence but rewrite it so that it (a) names its
audience explicitly, (b) refers to the resource narrative rather than
"plain text or image representations", and (c) defers any
narrative-only signing semantics to a future canonicalization scheme
rather than implying one ad hoc. Suggested replacement text:

> When human-level non-repudiation is in scope, signers should be aware
> that what humans see is typically the resource narrative, not the
> serialized resource. If a deployment needs the signature to bind
> specifically to what the human reviewed, that requirement should be
> expressed via an explicit FHIR canonicalization scheme that targets
> the narrative, rather than through ad hoc side-by-side
> representations.

This rewrite is paired with a follow-up note in section 6.1.2.1.3
("Canonicalization Variants" — addressed by FHIR-53893) confirming that
no narrative-only canonicalization variant is currently defined, and
inviting implementers to bring forward proposals.

#### Justification

The original sentence is trying to acknowledge a real implementation
concern: humans review narrative, not raw JSON/XML, so a digital
signature's bytes don't necessarily match the bytes the human saw. That
concern is worth keeping in the spec; it just needs to be said
precisely. Disposition B preserves the educational value while removing
the ambiguities the reporter flagged: it names the signer as the
audience, drops the under-specified "plain text or image
representations" mechanism, and explicitly routes the
"signature-binds-narrative" idea to where it belongs (canonicalization).
The trade-off versus Disposition A is that this is a larger editorial
change requiring coordination with FHIR-53893; the upside is that the
spec retains useful guidance that purely deleting the sentence would
lose.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline the change. Leave section 6.1.2.1.2 of `signatures.html`
unchanged. Rationale: the sentence is intentionally non-normative
implementer guidance, and pruning every advisory paragraph from the
Security & Privacy module on grounds of ambiguity sets an unhelpful
precedent for prose maintenance.

#### Justification

The sentence begins "One potential approach systems might take..." —
unmistakably hedged advisory language. It does not impose a conformance
requirement, and an implementer who finds it unactionable can simply
ignore it without affecting interoperability. Declining is defensible
on three grounds: (1) the cost of leaving it in is essentially zero
(no implementer is bound by it); (2) the cost of removing it forfeits
the only place in the page that warns implementers about the
human-vs-machine signed-form gap, however imperfectly worded; and
(3) treating "the prose is ambiguous" as sufficient cause for deletion
opens a wide front of similar tickets across the Security & Privacy
module.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

Disposition A (delete) is the cheapest action and matches the reporter's
literal request, but it loses a real and legitimate teaching point:
that the bytes a signer signs are not the bytes a human reviewed. That
gap is one of the genuinely tricky aspects of applying digital
signatures to FHIR, and the spec is the right place to flag it.
Disposition C (decline) preserves the warning but accepts that the
warning is poorly worded, which is the substantive complaint the
reporter has raised.

Disposition B keeps the warning, removes the ambiguities the reporter
called out, and explicitly routes the "signature-binds-narrative" idea
to the canonicalization machinery where any normative version of it
would have to live anyway. It also dovetails with the sibling
canonicalization-cleanup tickets (FHIR-53891, FHIR-53893) that are
already being processed by the same work group, so the editorial cost
is low if all four are dispositioned together. The work group should
confirm with the reporter that the rewritten wording addresses the
underlying concern; if the reporter still prefers outright removal,
falling back to Disposition A is straightforward and loses little.
