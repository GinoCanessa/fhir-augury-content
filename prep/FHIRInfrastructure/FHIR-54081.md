## Summary

Eric Haas is requesting a small editorial / clarifying change to the
"Digital Signatures" narrative page (`source/signatures.html`) that
governs how `Provenance` is used to convey signatures. Two changes are
proposed: (1) replace the phrase "When a provenance resource is used to
carry a signature" with the simpler "When Provenance is used to carry a
signature"; and (2) rewrite the third bullet that explains why
`Provenance` may carry multiple signatures so that it more accurately
describes the intent — namely that multiple signatures on a single
`Provenance` represent the *same* attestation by the *same* signer at
the *same* time, expressed in *different* electronic forms (e.g., an
image of a wet signature plus a digital signature). The reporter also
challenges the existing requirement that the alternate representations
"should be signed by the digital signature(s)" and asks why they must
all be digital signatures, suggesting that constraint be relaxed or
removed. There are no cross-referenced Jira tickets, Zulip discussions,
or GitHub items recorded in FhirAugury for this issue.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Apply the proposed editorial / clarifying changes to
`source/signatures.html` in the FHIR Core specification:

1. In the lead-in to the "rules apply" bullet list, replace
   *"When a provenance resource is used to carry a signature, the
   following rules apply:"* with
   *"When Provenance is used to carry a signature, the following rules
   apply:"*.
2. Replace the existing third bullet with the reporter's proposed
   wording:
   > Provenance can support multiple signatures. However, all digital
   > signatures in the same Provenance share the same signer, time,
   > purpose, etc. Therefore, the primary use of multiple signatures
   > in Provenance is to include different electronic signatures, such
   > as an image and a digital signature, that represent the same
   > attestation.
3. Drop the trailing sentence "If these are present, they should be
   signed by the digital signature(s)." (the strikethrough portion of
   the proposal), thereby removing the requirement that alternate
   representations be re-signed by the accompanying digital signatures.

#### Justification

The first edit is purely editorial — using the resource name
`Provenance` rather than "the provenance resource" matches the prose
style used elsewhere in the spec and is consistent with how other
narrative pages refer to resources. The second edit reframes a long,
hard-to-parse sentence into two crisp ones that better convey the
actual design intent (one attestation, multiple representations) and
introduce the term *electronic signature* to cover both image and
digital cases. Removing the "should be signed by the digital
signature(s)" sentence resolves the open question Eric raises — there
is no normative reason within the Provenance design that an image
representation must itself be cryptographically signed; the binding
attestation is the digital signature, and the image is co-located in
the same `Provenance.signature` array as a human-readable companion.
This is a non-substantive (or at most low-impact substantive) change
that improves clarity without altering the resource definition or any
constraint on `Provenance.signature`.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Apply edits (1) and (2) from Disposition A as written, but **retain a
softer form of the dropped sentence** rather than removing it
entirely. Specifically, replace the original
> If these are present, they should be signed by the digital
> signature(s).

with something like:
> Where multiple representations are present and one of them is a
> digital signature, the digital signature SHOULD cover the same
> content as (or be cryptographically bound to) the other
> representations, so that the alternate forms cannot be substituted
> independently of the digital attestation.

Optionally, lift this guidance into a small "Implementation
Considerations" sub-section rather than leaving it inline in the
bullet list.

#### Justification

The reporter's question — "Why must they all be digital signatures?" —
is a fair one, but the original sentence is trying to defend a real
security property: if a `Provenance` carries both a human-readable
signature image and a digital signature, an implementer that trusts
only the image is vulnerable to substitution attacks unless the
digital signature is bound to the same payload. Disposition A's
deletion solves the wording problem but loses that warning. Disposition
B keeps the clarity wins of A while preserving the security guidance
in a form that's clearly advisory (`SHOULD`, not `must`) and explains
*why*, which is what the reporter actually asked for. It also
sidesteps any backwards-compatibility concern from implementers who
may have read the original sentence as normative.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline the change on the grounds that the existing wording, while
slightly awkward, is not incorrect and that the third bullet's
"should be signed by the digital signature(s)" clause is intentional
guidance about avoiding signature-substitution attacks. Recommend that
the reporter raise a separate, narrower ticket if the only real
concern is the security rule rather than the editorial polish.

#### Justification

This disposition is defensible only if the workgroup believes that
(a) the editorial differences are too minor to spend a ballot cycle
on, and (b) the dropped sentence encodes a deliberate security
property that should not be silently removed via a clarifying-text
ticket. Both are weak arguments here: the editorial changes are
genuinely an improvement in readability, and the security concern can
be addressed by Disposition B without a full decline. Decline should
be reserved for cases where the proposed change introduces ambiguity
or harm, neither of which applies.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

Disposition B captures everything that is good about the reporter's
proposal — the simpler lead-in, the clearer multi-signature
explanation, and the explicit framing of an image + digital signature
as two representations of the same attestation — while preserving the
security guidance that Disposition A would discard. The reporter's
question ("Why must they all be digital signatures?") is best answered
in the spec itself rather than by deleting the sentence; an explicit
`SHOULD` with a one-line rationale leaves implementers in a better
place than either silence (A) or status quo (C). Because the change
touches a single narrative page (`source/signatures.html`) and does
not modify the `Provenance` resource definition or any
constraints/invariants, the impact on implementers is minimal and the
risk of regression is negligible. FHIR-I should be able to
disposition this without escalation.
