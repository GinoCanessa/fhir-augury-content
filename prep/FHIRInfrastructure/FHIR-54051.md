## Summary

The reporter (DanielSchuster) observes that the FHIR Core "canonical
identifiers" section in the References page is structured awkwardly: it
opens with several paragraphs about *chaining* (beginning "Chaining
functions by resolving a resource reference…") before it ever defines what
a canonical resource actually is — that definition only appears in the
second-to-last paragraph ("Canonical resources are identified by their
url…"). The ticket asks the FHIR Infrastructure work group to consider
restructuring the section so that chaining gets its own dedicated
sub-section *within* the canonical identifiers section, with the
defining material on canonical resources presented first. This is a pure
editorial / readability technical correction — no normative behavior is
being changed, no resources or elements are being added or removed, and
no comments, related Jira tickets, Zulip discussions, or GitHub items
were found through cross-reference lookups or targeted searches.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Restructure the "Canonical Identifiers" section of the FHIR Core
References page so that:

1. The section opens with the *definition* material currently buried
   near the end ("Canonical resources are identified by their `url`…"),
   establishing what a canonical resource is and how its identity is
   formed before any traversal/lookup behavior is described.
2. A new third-level sub-heading — e.g., `#### Chaining on Canonical
   Identifiers` (or, in the source markup, an `<h4>` / appropriate
   heading-level anchor consistent with the surrounding section) — is
   introduced inside the canonical identifiers section to host the
   existing paragraphs about chaining ("Chaining functions by resolving
   a resource reference…").
3. Existing anchors used by the rest of the spec or by external
   consumers are preserved by adding a redirect/aliased anchor on the
   new sub-heading, so that any deep links into the chaining text
   continue to resolve.
4. No normative content changes: the prose, examples, and constraints
   are kept intact; only their order, headings, and (if needed)
   intra-section cross-references are adjusted.

#### Justification

This directly addresses the readability concern the reporter raised:
defining a concept before describing operations on it is a long-standing
documentation convention used elsewhere in the FHIR spec (e.g.,
`Reference` is defined before search-modifier behavior on it is
discussed). Because chaining on canonical identifiers is conceptually a
*specialization* of the general behavior, hosting it under a dedicated
sub-heading inside the canonical identifiers section keeps the
information co-located while making the table of contents more
self-describing. The change is editorial only, so it carries no
implementer cost beyond updating any deep links — and the redirect
anchor recommendation mitigates even that.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Instead of introducing a new sub-section *inside* the canonical
identifiers section, **promote chaining-on-canonicals to a sibling
section** under the same parent heading (e.g., a new top-level "Chaining
on Canonical References" section that immediately follows "Canonical
Identifiers"). At the same time, leave a one-paragraph "Chaining" lede
inside Canonical Identifiers that links forward to the new sibling
section. The Canonical Identifiers section itself is reordered to lead
with the definition paragraph, matching Disposition A on that point.

#### Justification

If the work group judges that chaining on canonical identifiers is
sufficiently distinct in scope (it interacts with `Reference`, with
search modifiers like `:resolve`, and with profile/value-set lookup
semantics) it may deserve a peer-level entry in the table of contents
rather than being nested inside another concept. Promoting it makes it
discoverable from the page-level TOC and cross-linkable from other
chapters (e.g., search) without forcing readers to navigate through
canonical identifiers first. Trade-off vs. Disposition A: this
fragments the canonical-identifiers narrative slightly and creates a
second anchor surface that needs maintaining; conversely, it is more
honest about chaining being a behavior that spans more than just
canonicals.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline the request as **out of scope for a Technical Correction**:
the existing text is technically accurate and the ordering, while not
ideal pedagogically, does not introduce ambiguity, conflict with other
parts of the spec, or risk implementer error. Close as "Not Persuasive
— editorial preference" without reordering the section, optionally
inviting the reporter to open a more concrete proposal (with a draft
diff) if they wish to pursue it.

#### Justification

The work group's Technical Correction backlog typically prioritizes
items that fix incorrect, ambiguous, or contradictory normative text.
This ticket does not allege any such defect; it proposes a structural
re-arrangement of correct prose to improve flow. With no cross-refs, no
Zulip discussion, no implementer reports of confusion, and no concrete
diff attached, the cost/benefit of opening up a frequently-referenced
spec page (and the attendant risk of breaking deep links) is hard to
justify against other Triaged work.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** A

The reporter has identified a real and easily-fixed pedagogical defect:
the section operationally describes chaining before it ever defines
what a canonical resource is, which forces readers to read the section
twice. Disposition A is the lowest-risk way to fix that — it preserves
all normative content, requires only a heading and paragraph reorder,
and (with an aliased anchor) does not break deep links. Disposition B
is defensible if the work group feels chaining deserves more visibility,
but it is a larger change to the page's structure and creates a second
anchor to maintain; the editorial benefit does not clearly outweigh
that overhead, and Disposition A's nested sub-heading already gives
chaining its own TOC entry. Disposition C understates the cost of
poorly-ordered reference material on a heavily-cited page; given how
cheap the fix is, declining a clean readability improvement on a page
this central is the least defensible option.
