## Summary

The reporter (Dan Cinnamon, Okta) proposes deprecating the SMART App Launch
`authorize-post` capability because the cross-origin form-post flow it relies
on conflicts with current browser cross-origin / cookie policies (SameSite
enforcement, third-party cookie phase-out, CSRF mitigations). When an
`authorize-post` is initiated from a SMART client to an authorization server
on a different origin, browsers may withhold cookies the AS depends on for
SSO and "remember device" experiences, breaking common practitioner-facing
flows. The ticket frames `authorize-post` as no longer best practice, points
to the OpenID/OAuth community's preferred replacement (RFC 9126 — Pushed
Authorization Requests, "PAR"), and explicitly notes that a separate ticket
will be filed to propose adopting PAR as the long-term solution. There are
no other Jira tickets cross-referenced to this one, so this ticket stands
alone, with the parallel PAR proposal expected to be tracked separately.

## Details

**Description:**

> Since the SMART launch v2 was originally launched, significant cross
> origin policy changes have been introduced by browser vendors. There are
> various reasons for introducing these new policy changes, but they're
> mostly in the spirit of protecting users against attacks such as cross
> site request forgery attacks, in addition to reducing the prevalence of
> marketing tracker cookies.
>
> The current behavior of the `authorize-post` capability is such that a
> SMART client application will perform a cross-origin form post to the
> authorization server. Depending upon the security flags on the
> authorization server cookies, this behavior would cause browsers to not
> send critical cookies that the authorization server may rely on such as
> SSO session cookies, "remember me" cookies, "known device" tracking
> cookies, and maybe others that help drive a consistent user experience.
> Without these cookies being sent, those commonly implemented authorization
> server features will no longer function when `authorize-post` is used.
>
> The impacts of this will be felt most by the practitioner community,
> where an expectation of SSO may exist between multiple applications.
>
> Regardless of the end user experience, it is in general no longer best
> practice to perform these cross-origin form posts, and therefore the
> OpenID working group has instead recommended the use of the new RFC-9126
> (pushed authorization requests) to solve the challenges that
> `authorize-post` was meant to solve. More detail on that will be provided
> in another issue.
>
> This issue is a proposal to deprecate the `authorize-post` capability in
> its current form. A separate issue will be filed to propose adoption of
> RFC-9126 to serve the long term needs of the SMART/FHIR community. Thank
> you for your consideration.

## Related Zulip Discussions

### [smart > Deprecating authorize-post?](https://chat.fhir.org/#narrow/stream/smart/topic/Deprecating%20authorize-post%3F/near/419504457)

Josh Mandel opened this thread on 2024-02-02 to socialize FHIR-44648 ahead
of SMART 2.2 ballot reconciliation, asking the community two questions:
(1) is anyone using POST-based authorization as a client, and (2) has
anyone hit URL-length limits on GET-based authz that would justify keeping
`authorize-post`. Cooper Thompson (Epic) confirmed Epic supports
`authorize-post` and has documented/recommended it in some cases, and that
they have hit URL-length issues on GET — not in browsers, but in API
gateways in front of their servers. Mikael Rinnetmäki reported similar
URL-length pain on GET that they resolved via server / load-balancer
configuration without moving to POST. Alexander Zautke said his
organization implements `authorize-post` only because of regulatory
demand. John Moehrke clarified the discussion is scoped strictly to the
authorize endpoint, not REST POST in general. Dan Cinnamon (the reporter)
reiterated that the JIRA does not deny the need for handling large
authorization requests — it just argues the mechanism should evolve, with
PAR as the OIDC-standard replacement that also delivers extra security
benefits.

Josh then ran experiments with form-post against Google and Okta authorize
endpoints in current Chrome/Firefox, including with Chrome's third-party
cookie blocking flags enabled, and observed that authz cookies were still
being sent. Dan investigated further, eventually concluding (and
documenting in the thread): SameSite enforcement *does* affect
`authorize-post` — it forces AS cookies to be set as `SameSite=None;
Secure`, which is the least secure setting and which most major AS
vendors have already done; but the separate third-party cookie deprecation
initiative does *not* break `authorize-post`, because the form submission
constitutes a "top-level navigation" of the browser and is not classified
as a third-party request. Dan's final position: the originally-feared
urgent breakage is not materializing, but `authorize-post` should still be
superseded by PAR over time for the additional security it brings. Tom
Jones added that an attempt to deprecate POST in OpenID itself has met
heavy push-back. Josh agreed there is no need to rush a deprecation marker
in 2.2 and suggested folding PAR into a broader future discussion about
client-bound keys (PAR + DPoP). Dan agreed. There is also a brief
side-thread between Sujeito de Sorte / Felipe Soriano and Jens Villadsen
about whether GET has a 2048-character limit (consensus: no formal RFC
limit, but real-world infrastructure often imposes one).

Participants: Josh Mandel, Dan Cinnamon, Cooper Thompson, John Moehrke,
Alexander Zautke, Mikael Rinnetmäki, Sujeito de Sorte (Felipe Soriano),
Jens Villadsen, Tom Jones.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Deprecate the `authorize-post` capability in the next SMART App Launch
release (2.2). Concretely:

- In the SMART App Launch IG capability list (the `capabilities` value set
  used by the `.well-known/smart-configuration` document and the
  `oauth-uris` `CapabilityStatement` extension), mark `authorize-post` as
  *deprecated*, with non-normative guidance directing implementers to
  Pushed Authorization Requests (RFC 9126) for handling oversized or
  sensitive authorization requests, once a PAR profile is added to SMART.
- Update the SMART "App Launch" / "Authorization endpoint" narrative pages
  to add a deprecation notice and a forward pointer to the (separately
  tracked) PAR ticket / future capability.
- Make no breaking change for existing servers: implementations MAY
  continue to advertise and accept `authorize-post`, but new
  implementations SHOULD NOT rely on it.

#### Justification

This matches the literal request in FHIR-44648 and aligns SMART with the
broader OAuth 2.0 / OpenID Connect direction of travel toward PAR. It
gives implementers a clear long-term signal while remaining
backward-compatible for the ballot cycle. It also acknowledges the
SameSite=None concern Dan documented in the Zulip thread: even though
top-level navigation currently keeps the flow working, requiring AS
cookies to be set with the weakest SameSite policy is a real
security/operational tax that PAR avoids.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Defer formal deprecation; instead, add a normative-language Security
Considerations note to the SMART App Launch IG describing the cookie /
cross-origin trade-offs of `authorize-post`, and treat the future PAR
capability as the preferred replacement once it is profiled. Specifically:

- Keep `authorize-post` as a non-deprecated capability in 2.2.
- Add a Security Considerations subsection on the Authorization endpoint
  page noting (a) that AS cookies used during `authorize-post` will need
  `SameSite=None; Secure`, (b) that browser cookie policies are evolving
  and `authorize-post` may be subject to future restrictions, and (c) that
  servers SHOULD plan to support PAR (RFC 9126) once SMART profiles it.
- Couple the eventual `authorize-post` deprecation to the *publication* of
  the SMART PAR profile (the companion ticket Dan referenced), so the
  deprecation never lands without a profiled replacement.

#### Justification

This reflects the conclusion the participants converged on in the Zulip
thread: Josh and Dan both agreed that the originally-feared cookie
breakage is not currently materializing in browsers (because the form-post
is a top-level navigation), and that there is no need to rush deprecation
in 2.2 ahead of a profiled replacement. It also addresses Cooper
Thompson's and Mikael Rinnetmäki's data point that real-world
implementations have hit URL-length limits — pulling `authorize-post`
without a replacement would force those implementers into operational
remediation they have already been doing on GET. Tom Jones's note that
OpenID itself has seen heavy push-back against deprecating POST is a
further reason to hold off on a formal deprecation flag until the
replacement is in hand.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline to deprecate `authorize-post` at this time. Resolve the ticket as
*Persuasive with Modification* / *Not Persuasive* on the deprecation ask,
and instead track the underlying need (modernizing how SMART handles large
or sensitive authorization requests) under the separate PAR ticket the
reporter said would be filed. Optionally add an informative note on the
Authorization endpoint page that PAR is the preferred direction once
profiled, but make no change to the capability vocabulary.

#### Justification

The Zulip thread ultimately concluded there is no urgent browser-driven
breakage: top-level navigation flow keeps cookies flowing in current
Chromium/Firefox, and `samesite=none` is already in place at the major
identity vendors implementers actually deploy against. Cooper Thompson
confirmed real-world reliance on `authorize-post`, and OpenID's own
attempt to deprecate POST has met significant push-back. Marking the
capability as deprecated without a profiled, balloted replacement risks
fragmenting implementations and creating a window in which servers feel
pressure to drop a feature their clients still need.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

Disposition B is the cleanest fit for what the discussion actually
produced. Both the reporter (Dan Cinnamon) and Josh Mandel landed on the
position that `authorize-post` should eventually be superseded by PAR for
its additional security properties, but that the urgency originally
believed to exist (browser-driven breakage of SSO via cookie blocking) is
not materializing today thanks to the top-level-navigation classification.
Implementers (Epic, others) demonstrably rely on the capability and have
real URL-length pain that PAR — not removal — solves. The right move for
ballot reconciliation is therefore to (1) document the security and
cookie-policy trade-offs prominently in the SMART App Launch IG, (2)
signal PAR as the preferred long-term direction, and (3) sequence any
formal deprecation of `authorize-post` to follow, not precede, publication
of the SMART PAR profile (the companion ticket the reporter referenced).
Disposition A is defensible if FHIR-I wants the strongest possible
forward-signal in 2.2, but it risks creating a deprecation gap before PAR
is profiled; Disposition C under-serves the reporter's well-grounded
concern about long-term direction and the operational tax of requiring
`SameSite=None`.
