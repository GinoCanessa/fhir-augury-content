## Summary

The reporter (HaydenBader) flags an inaccuracy in the "Generating
human-readable content" paragraph on the HL7 V2 → FHIR conversion
guidance page. That paragraph currently asserts that "FHIR requires that
every resource have a human readable narrative that contains all
information relevant to human decision-making." The reporter notes that
this overstates the conformance rule: the FHIR core specification does
not in general *require* a narrative on every resource — it strongly
recommends one and, where one is present, requires that it be safe — but
the requirement is conditional and varies by resource type, profile, and
context (e.g., FHIR Documents vs. plain RESTful exchange). The ticket
asks that the conversion-guidance prose be aligned with what
`narrative.html` actually says so that V2-conversion implementers do not
draw the wrong conclusion about narrative obligations.

No other Jira tickets were surfaced as cross-references. A long-running
Zulip discussion on `#conformance > Intended and allowed use of
narrative` (2021) covers exactly this nuance and is included below as
context.

## Details

**Description:**

The "Generating human-readable content" paragraph states the following:

> FHIR requires that every resource have a human readable
> [narrative](https://build.fhir.org/narrative.html) that contains all
> information relevant to human decision-making.

Last I checked, FHIR does NOT require narratives (though it does suggest
them). We should not imply it does.

## Related Zulip Discussions

### [conformance > Intended and allowed use of narrative](https://chat.fhir.org/#narrow/stream/conformance/topic/Intended%20and%20allowed%20use%20of%20narrative/near/254324973)

Morten Ernebjerg opened the thread asking whether it is ever safe for a
consumer to ignore `DomainResource.text`, given that the spec
simultaneously (a) allows resources that are essentially narrative-only
and (b) does not flag `text` as a modifier element. Grahame Grieve
confirmed that if you process the structured data, you can ignore the
narrative — but only because the narrative is constrained never to
contain anything that is not also in the data, *and* this guarantee
hinges on `Narrative.status` (`generated`, `extensions`, `additional`,
`empty`). Lloyd McKenzie added that "safe to ignore" is fuzzy in
practice: for `additional`, the narrative may carry information not
present in the structured data, and for FHIR Documents the narrative is
expected to be displayed regardless of status. Both Grahame and Lloyd
acknowledged that the spec's nuance around when narratives are required,
generated, or safely ignorable is under-explained, and Grahame agreed
that a clarifying ticket against the Clinical Safety Concerns section
would be welcome — that follow-up was filed as FHIR-34041.

Participants: Morten Ernebjerg, Grahame Grieve, Lloyd McKenzie,
Yannick Boerner.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Edit the "Generating human-readable content" paragraph in
`source/comparison-v2.html` (anchor `HL7 V2-humanReadable`) on `HL7/fhir`
to remove the unconditional "FHIR requires" assertion and replace it
with language that matches the actual conformance rules in
`narrative.html`. Concretely, replace the opening sentence with
something like:

> FHIR strongly encourages every resource to carry a human-readable
> [narrative](narrative.html) (`DomainResource.text`) that conveys the
> information relevant to human decision-making. Whether a narrative is
> *required* depends on the context: FHIR Documents and certain profiles
> mandate one, while in plain RESTful exchange `DomainResource.text` is
> optional. When a narrative is present, the rules in `narrative.html`
> (in particular the constraints tied to `Narrative.status`) govern what
> it must contain and how it may be consumed.

Keep the rest of the paragraph (about V2-conversion implementers
deciding what to render and how to generate it) intact. Preserve the
`<a name="HL7 V2-humanReadable"></a>` anchor so the inbound link from
`comparison-v3.html` continues to resolve.

#### Justification

The current sentence is factually wrong as written and is the precise
defect the reporter identified. `narrative.html` itself describes
narratives as required only in specific contexts (e.g., FHIR Documents,
certain profiles) and otherwise leaves `DomainResource.text` optional;
the Zulip thread above shows experienced implementers reading the spec
the same way the reporter does. Aligning the conversion guidance with
the canonical narrative page closes a real interoperability trap (V2
implementers concluding they must always synthesise narrative, or worse,
that doing so satisfies their conformance obligations regardless of
status). The change is local to one HTML page in `source/`, has no
schema, terminology, or downstream IG impact, and does not invalidate
existing instances.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Rather than re-deriving the rule on `comparison-v2.html`, shorten the
paragraph to a one-sentence pointer and delegate the normative statement
to `narrative.html`. For example:

> When converting from HL7 V2, developers (likely with guidance from
> clinicians) will need to decide what information from the message
> should be rendered as the resource's
> [narrative](narrative.html#Narrative) and how to generate it. The
> conformance rules for when a narrative is required, what it must
> contain, and how `Narrative.status` constrains its consumption are
> defined on the [Narrative](narrative.html) page; this page does not
> restate them.

This removes the inaccurate "FHIR requires" claim, gives the conversion
audience the V2-specific guidance they actually need, and avoids
duplicating normative prose that could drift out of sync with
`narrative.html` later.

#### Justification

The underlying defect is duplication of a normative statement on a
non-normative guidance page, which is exactly the kind of drift FHIR
historically tries to avoid. Replacing the assertion with a link to the
authoritative page is more robust than rewording the assertion in place
(Disposition A): future edits to `narrative.html` (e.g., the Clinical
Safety Concerns expansion that Grahame agreed to in the Zulip thread)
will not silently re-introduce a contradiction here. Trade-off vs. A:
readers who only land on `comparison-v2.html` lose the inline summary
and have to follow a link, which is a minor UX hit on a page already
densely populated with cross-links.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Close the ticket as "Not Persuasive" on the basis that the paragraph is
giving informative V2-conversion guidance rather than restating
conformance, and that readers are expected to consult `narrative.html`
for the actual rules.

#### Justification

`comparison-v2.html` is non-normative implementation guidance, and a
strict reading of the wider spec architecture would say that any
appearance of disagreement with `narrative.html` is resolved in favour
of `narrative.html`. From that angle, no instance is non-conformant
because of this prose, and no implementer who reads the full spec is
materially misled.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

Disposition B fixes the reporter's concern (the inaccurate "FHIR
requires" wording goes away) while also addressing the structural cause
of the bug — a non-normative page asserting a normative rule that has
since become more nuanced than the one-liner can capture. The Zulip
thread shows that the narrative-conformance story is genuinely subtle
(`Narrative.status` semantics, the FHIR Documents exception, the
"safe to ignore" caveats around `additional`); any inline restatement
on the V2 page is likely to drift again. Pointing at `narrative.html`
keeps the V2 page focused on V2-specific implementation advice, which
is what its readers actually need. Disposition A is an acceptable
fallback if the work group prefers to keep an inline summary on the
conversion page; Disposition C should be rejected because the current
wording is demonstrably wrong, not merely informal, and a reader of
just the V2 page can reasonably draw the wrong conclusion.
