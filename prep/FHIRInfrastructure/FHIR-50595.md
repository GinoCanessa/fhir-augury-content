## Summary

The reporter questions whether the Maturity Model (FMM) level assigned to the
`TestScript` resource accurately reflects real-world usage and review. Internal
reviewers at the reporter's organization were surprised to see `TestScript`
marked at FMM 4, because they are not aware of organizations in their network
actively using it, and some portions of the specification feel underdeveloped.
The reporter also highlights an internal inconsistency: closely related
specification pages — "Testing FHIR", `TestReport`, and `TestPlan` — are marked
at FMM 1 or 2. They ask whether FMM 4 is an accurate indication of the current
adoption level of `TestScript`.

No Jira tickets are cross-referenced to this issue in the Augury index, but a
related Zulip thread in the `#inferno` stream ("TestReport FHIR resource as an
Inferno Framework report") discusses the tight coupling between `TestReport`
and `TestScript` and notes that `TestReport`'s low maturity level is precisely
what makes it a candidate for redesign — implicitly reinforcing the reporter's
concern about the mismatch in maturity between `TestScript` and its companion
resources.

This thread, from November 2024, discusses whether the Inferno framework
should emit FHIR `TestReport` resources. Pavel Rozhkov proposes adopting
`TestReport` and notes that the resource's low maturity level may be an
opportunity to influence its design. Robert Scanlon (Inferno maintainer)
pushes back on the feasibility: he explains that `TestReport` is tightly
coupled to `TestScript` ("The TestReport resource defines how systems should
encode the summarized results of executing a TestScript"), and that mapping
Inferno's more dynamic test model into `TestReport`/`TestScript` would
require "a huge mess of extensions and implementation-specific choices." He
floats the idea of redefining `TestScript` as a more abstract artifact so
that non-TestScript test systems can still emit interoperable `TestReport`s,
but acknowledges this idea has not generated traction. No consensus was
reached. The thread is directly relevant to FHIR-50595 because it provides
concrete, first-hand implementer evidence that a widely-used FHIR test
framework (Inferno) does **not** use `TestScript` and finds its current
definition unworkable for real tests — which is evidence against the current
FMM 4 designation.

Participants: Pavel Rozhkov, Robert Scanlon.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Lower the Maturity Model level of `TestScript` from FMM 4 to a value
consistent with observed implementer adoption and with the related resources
in the Testing family. Concretely, reduce `TestScript.maturity` to **FMM 2**
(matching `TestReport`/`TestPlan`'s current levels) in the resource's
authoring metadata, and update the "Testing FHIR" narrative page to reflect
the revised level. Require the FHIR Infrastructure (FHIR-I) work group to
document, as part of the change, which of the FMM 4 criteria (multiple
implementations, test coverage, formal review, cross-implementer connectathon
testing) `TestScript` fails to meet today.

#### Justification

FMM levels are intended to communicate implementer-facing confidence, and
the FMM criteria require demonstrable multi-party implementation and review.
The reporter provides direct evidence of limited adoption in their network;
the Zulip thread provides corroborating evidence that a major FHIR test
framework (Inferno) explicitly avoids `TestScript` because it is too
prescriptive. An FMM of 4 that is inconsistent with (a) observable adoption
and (b) the maturity of closely-related resources in the same family is
misleading to implementers and undermines the signal the FMM is intended to
send. Aligning `TestScript`'s level with `TestReport` and `TestPlan` is
consistent with how the FMM treats tightly-coupled resource families
elsewhere (e.g., Medication* resources were rebalanced for the same reason).

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Rather than change the FMM level as a bare number, treat this as a
**maturity review** of the whole Testing resource family. FHIR-I should
(1) publish the evidence that was used to justify `TestScript`'s FMM 4
rating (connectathon results, implementation survey, formal reviews); (2)
run an open call for implementer feedback on the Testing family via a Zulip
thread in `#FHIR-I` and a connectathon track; and (3) adjust the FMM level
— up, down, or unchanged — on the basis of that evidence. If adjustment is
warranted, update `TestScript`, `TestReport`, `TestPlan`, and the "Testing
FHIR" page together so the family's maturity story is internally consistent.

#### Justification

FMM downgrades are rare and carry a cost: implementers who have invested in
an FMM 4 resource read a downgrade as a signal that the spec is less stable
than they thought. A structured review produces a defensible, documented
decision rather than a single data-point downgrade, and — importantly —
addresses the reporter's underlying concern (the spec family's maturity
story is incoherent) regardless of what the final numbers turn out to be.
It also surfaces the Inferno-style feedback (that `TestScript`'s opinionated
model blocks adoption), which a pure FMM re-labeling would not address.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline to change the Maturity Model level of `TestScript`. Close the ticket
as a "Question — Answered" with a response summarising the evidence that
supported the FMM 4 designation (e.g., implementer testimony, connectathon
testing history, IHE/Touchstone usage) and pointing the reporter at the
governance process for disputing an FMM level if they have counter-evidence
to offer.

#### Justification

Defensible only if FHIR-I can produce the evidence trail for `TestScript`'s
FMM 4 rating and that evidence genuinely meets the FMM 4 criteria
(e.g., Touchstone-driven TestScript usage by HL7 and regulatory testing
programs). In that case the reporter's observation reflects the reporter's
own network rather than the broader ecosystem, and a single unsupported
question ticket is not a sufficient basis to downgrade a resource that has
passed the formal criteria. Declining also avoids the implementer-confidence
cost of a downgrade.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

Disposition B is recommended. The reporter's ticket is framed as a question
("Is FMM 4 an accurate indication of the current usage level of this spec?")
rather than a concrete change request, and the right response to a well-posed
question is to produce evidence — not to either rubber-stamp a downgrade
(A) or dismiss the concern (C). The Zulip conversation with the Inferno team
shows that the underlying issue is not just a labeling dispute but a genuine
design question about whether `TestScript` models the space correctly;
bundling the FMM review with a structured implementer-feedback pass gives
FHIR-I a defensible outcome and surfaces information the work group will
need regardless of which FMM value is ultimately chosen. If the review
confirms the reporter's intuition, the resulting downgrade is backed by
evidence; if it does not, the reporter receives a clear, documented answer.
Disposition A should remain on the table as the outcome B is most likely to
produce.
