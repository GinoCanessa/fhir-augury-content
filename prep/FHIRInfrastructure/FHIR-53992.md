## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Remove the duplicate (penultimate) paragraph from
`ImplementationGuide` section **5.9.4.3** so that only the final
paragraph — the one whose inline link points to the correct target
section — remains. The change is purely to the authored narrative for
the `ImplementationGuide` resource page; no element definitions,
bindings, invariants, or examples are modified. The reporter should be
asked to confirm (or the editor should verify at resolution time) which
of the two duplicate paragraphs carries the correct linked section
name, so the wrong paragraph is the one deleted.

#### Justification

The ticket describes a verbatim duplication where only the name of the
linked section differs between the two paragraphs. Keeping both adds no
reader value and actively invites confusion (readers may assume the two
links are intentionally distinct). Deleting the paragraph with the
incorrect / superseded link preserves the author's intent while
removing clutter. This is the minimal, targeted fix that matches what
was reported, is scoped to a single `ImplementationGuide` narrative
file, and carries no normative risk (editorial correction in a
Technical Correction ticket).

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Instead of simply deleting the duplicate paragraph, **merge** the two
paragraphs into a single paragraph that references both linked sections
explicitly — e.g., "See also &lt;Section X&gt; and &lt;Section Y&gt;
for related guidance." This preserves both cross-references in case
each was genuinely intended to point readers to a different part of
the specification, while eliminating the repeated prose. If, on
review, the two linked sections turn out to be the same target reached
via different anchor names, fall back to Disposition A.

#### Justification

A verbatim duplication sometimes signals a merge conflict or an
incomplete edit where the original author intended to point to **two**
different follow-on sections but accidentally copied the same prose
twice. In that case, removing one paragraph outright (Disposition A)
silently drops a cross-reference that readers may have found useful.
Merging is only slightly more work for the editor, is still an
editorial-only change, and preserves navigability until the work group
can confirm intent with the reporter.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline the change on the grounds that it is a cosmetic / redundant
narrative issue that does not affect the normative content of
`ImplementationGuide` and can be left to a later editorial sweep.

#### Justification

This is hard to defend. The fix is genuinely trivial (removing a
single paragraph), the reporter has correctly identified the anomaly,
and Technical Correction tickets exist precisely to capture this class
of low-risk editorial cleanup. Declining would leave confusing prose
in a prominent resource page with no offsetting benefit. The only
plausible grounds for decline would be a formal freeze on narrative
edits to the affected publication, which is not indicated anywhere in
the ticket or cross-references.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** A

The reported issue is an unambiguous, self-contained editorial defect
in the `ImplementationGuide` resource page at section 5.9.4.3, has no
downstream impact beyond that single narrative location, and is
exactly the shape of fix that a "Technical Correction" ticket is meant
to capture. Disposition A applies it as requested with minimal work
and zero risk. Disposition B is worth falling back to **only** if the
resolving editor, on opening the file, determines that the two
linked-section names point to meaningfully different targets and both
cross-references should be preserved; in practice, the reporter's own
framing ("the last paragraph links to the appropriate section")
strongly implies the other link is simply wrong, which is the case A
is optimized for. Disposition C has no real justification and should
not be adopted.
