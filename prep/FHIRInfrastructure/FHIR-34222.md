## Summary

The reporter (Eric Haas) is asking the SMART on FHIR authors to clarify the
narrative in the "App Launch: Scopes and Launch Context" section of the
SMART App Launch IG, specifically the subsection "SMART's scopes are used
to delegate access." As written, the text shifts between two different
notions of where authority comes from — "underlying system policies and
permissions" versus "the user's underlying permissions" — without making
clear who "the user" is in each scenario or how that user actually
communicates their permissions to the client. The reporter notes that
because the phrase "underlying system policies and permissions" sits
proximally to the bullet describing `user/*.cruds`, a reader is led to
believe that even user-launched scopes are bounded by *system* policies
rather than by the launching user's own privileges. The proposal is to
rewrite the surrounding prose so that the user role(s) implied by
`user/*.cruds` versus `system/*.cruds` are explicit, including how
delegation actually flows from the user to the client during App Launch.

There are no cross-referenced Jira tickets, Zulip threads, or GitHub
items recorded in Augury for this ticket, so the request stands on its
own.

## Proposed Dispositions

### Disposition A: Accept As Requested

#### Proposal

Rewrite the prose in the SMART App Launch IG section
**"SMART's scopes are used to delegate access"** (within
`scopes-and-launch-context.html`) to make the actor model explicit.
Concretely:

1. Define the term "user" up front for this section. Use language such
   as: *"In SMART App Launch, the **user** is the human end-user who
   authenticates at the authorization server (typically via the EHR's
   login) and who, during the authorization step, consents to delegate
   some subset of their own access rights to the client app."*
2. Disambiguate the opening sentence so that "underlying system
   policies and permissions" is replaced by two distinct phrases:
   - For interactive launches: *"…limited by the authorizing user's
     permissions in the underlying system, as further constrained by
     server policy and by what the user consents to grant."*
   - For backend launches: *"…limited by the pre-configured policy for
     the client in the underlying system."*
3. Rework the two bullets so each one names its actor:
   - `user/*.cruds` — *"granted by the authorizing end-user during
     the App Launch consent step; effective access is the
     intersection of the user's own permissions, server policy, and
     the consent the user gave."*
   - `system/*.cruds` — *"granted by an administrator out-of-band as
     a pre-configured client policy; no end-user is involved at
     runtime."*
4. Add a one-line forward reference to whatever section of the IG
   describes the user-facing consent / authorization step, so the
   reader can see *how* the user "communicates their permission" to
   the client.

This is a documentation-only change — no scope strings, conformance
statements, or normative requirements are modified.

#### Justification

The reporter has correctly identified a real ambiguity: the section
mixes "system policies" and "user permissions" in adjacent sentences
without naming who "the user" is or how delegation occurs at runtime.
Resolving the ambiguity by naming the actor (authorizing end-user vs.
pre-configured client policy administrator) is consistent with how the
rest of the SMART App Launch IG already distinguishes the App Launch
flow from the Backend Services flow, and it aligns with OAuth 2.0's
own actor model (resource owner vs. client). Because the change is
purely editorial, it carries essentially no implementer cost and no
backward-compatibility risk.

---

### Disposition B: Alternative Approach

#### Proposal

Rather than only patching the prose in place, restructure the section
into a small **"Who grants what"** table immediately above the existing
bullets, and let the surrounding prose stay short. The table would have
one row per launch mode and columns for: scope family
(`user/*`, `patient/*`, `system/*`), who grants the access at runtime
(authorizing end-user / EHR launch context / pre-configured client
policy), what bounds the effective access (user's own permissions /
launch context / client policy), and where in the IG the granting
mechanism is specified (link to the consent / launch step). The
existing bullets become illustrative examples below the table rather
than the primary explanation.

Optionally, factor "Who is 'the user'?" into a short shared definition
near the top of the Scopes and Launch Context page so that subsequent
sections (`user/*` scopes, `launch/patient`, refresh-token semantics)
can refer back to it instead of re-defining the actor each time.

#### Justification

The current text fails the reporter not just locally but structurally —
the actor is implicit across several adjacent paragraphs. A table makes
the App Launch ↔ Backend Services symmetry visually obvious and
prevents a future reader from re-introducing the same ambiguity when
the prose is edited. The trade-off vs. Disposition A is more editorial
work and a slightly larger diff, in exchange for a section that is
self-explanatory without depending on careful reading of the
surrounding paragraphs. It also gives downstream IGs (e.g., US Core,
SMART Health IT guidance) a clean anchor to link to when explaining
delegation.

---

### Disposition C: Decline

#### Proposal

Decline the change on the grounds that the section, read in the full
context of the SMART App Launch IG, already establishes that
`user/*.cruds` is granted by an end-user during App Launch and that
`system/*.cruds` is granted via a pre-configured client policy. Close
the ticket as "Not Persuasive" with a comment pointing to the existing
authorization / consent narrative elsewhere in the IG and noting that
the highlighted phrases ("granted … by a user", "relative to the
user's underlying permissions") are intended to convey exactly the
distinction the reporter is asking for.

#### Justification

The phrases the reporter highlighted *do* name the user and *do* tie
effective access to the user's underlying permissions; the perceived
ambiguity may be a local-reading artifact rather than a substantive
gap. Declining avoids editorial churn on a stable, widely-implemented
IG section and keeps the wording aligned with prior published
versions, which implementers have already internalized. This option
is defensible only if the workgroup judges that the existing wording
is unambiguous to its target audience.

---

### Recommendation

**Recommended disposition:** B

The reporter's confusion is a real signal — "underlying system
policies and permissions" and "the user's underlying permissions"
appear in adjacent sentences and do not obviously refer to the same
or different things, and the section never names who "the user" is at
runtime in the App Launch flow. Disposition A fixes the immediate
wording, but the underlying problem is structural: the section
explains delegation prose-first when the natural shape of the content
is a two-mode comparison (App Launch vs. Backend Services). A small
"who grants what" table, paired with a one-paragraph definition of
"user" at the top of the page, addresses the ticket's literal request
*and* prevents the same ambiguity from re-emerging in future edits,
at modest editorial cost and no normative impact. Disposition C is
not recommended because the ticket's evidence — a careful reader
arriving at the wrong mental model — is exactly the kind of feedback
this section should be edited to prevent.
